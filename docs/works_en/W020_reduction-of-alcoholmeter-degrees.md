# In Memory of the Late Domainenrath Pogge

I.
In Memory of the Late
Domainenrath Pogge.

(Submitted from the Teterow District.)

From our midst has departed the man who enlivened earlier assemblies through his presence and who lent the proceedings a high degree of interest through the sharing of his views and experiences.

But even more effective than at these proceedings was his influence on the improvement of practical agriculture in our fatherland. Given the extraordinary advances that the cultivation of the soil has made in recent times, he was, in nearly all branches of agriculture, the forerunner for the past forty years, and in part the inventor. His example and his communications have contributed to the higher cultivation of the soil across a wide circle, and perhaps there is not a single person in this assembly who has not been advanced in knowledge, directly or indirectly, through him, and spurred by him to higher intellectual activity.

Admirably great was the productivity of his mind, the creative power of his intellect — a power that perhaps becomes all the rarer as our present methods of upbringing and instruction prescribe the mind its course, fill nearly the entire period of youth with learning what others have thought, and leave no time for the free intellectual activity that springs from inner inclination and one's own particular talents. With this free, keen intellect he combined an extraordinary gift of observation, and since his mind was not fettered by any learned system, he saw nature as it was, not as others imagined it, and so it revealed to him many secrets that it often keeps veiled from those laden with positive knowledge.

History has rarely recorded the names of men who, through discoveries and improvements in agriculture, founded the prosperity of a state; rather, it reports almost exclusively only the great deeds of a people in war. But each time in earlier history when a people emerges from obscurity and unexpectedly appears with numerous armies, a flourishing of prosperity — without which no rise in population can take place — must have preceded it, and this flourishing is the cause of that vigorous emergence. Now, however, since history — especially the older history — remains silent on this matter and recounts events whose causes remain hidden, it often becomes enigmatic, and the serious instruction that otherwise lies within it for succeeding generations is to a great extent lost.

Shall this always remain so, and shall the names of men who had a beneficial effect on the prosperity and thus on the happiness of an entire people not belong to history just as much as the names of heroes? Shall they not, like the latter, receive an outward token of veneration?

The memory of our revered departed one lives on in his family; it lives on in the hearts of those who had the good fortune to be his friend. His work, too, has not been extinguished by his death; it endures on the estates that he transformed from a raw state into fruitful fields; it endures in those whom he enriched with his views and his knowledge; it endures in his subordinates, whom he raised to be upright people through goodwill, justice, and adequate reward for their labor — and even when, after several generations, his name would no longer be spoken, his work would nevertheless endure no less.

For him, for the revered departed one himself, no earthly memorial is therefore needed. But the contemporaries of a deserving man honor themselves when they honor his memory, and — what is above all important — in outward recognition lies the incentive for coming generations to emulate him.

History teaches us that in all states where the national character of their inhabitants has attained a high degree of perfection, the memory of great men has also been honored in a distinguished manner and transmitted to posterity through monuments, but that where people watched such men depart with indifference, they became rarer and ever rarer.

The mere thought of a distinguished man already elevates a person; the contemplation of his features has even greater power to do so.

May therefore the marble bust of the departed one adorn our assembly hall in the future, and after he himself no longer dwells among us, may his image summon us to serious accomplishments! May finally the coming generations, who will perhaps one day assemble here, find in the bust a spur to make themselves worthy of a similar distinction.
