## First Section

### Section 1. Timber Yield.

According to multiple observations, pines felled at the age of 30 years yielded at Tellow an average of 30 cartloads per 100 Mecklenburg square rods. I reckon the cartload on average, including all branch-wood, at 64 Luebeck cubic feet.

Among these there are typically:
7 cartloads of construction timber and
23 cartloads of firewood.

The construction timber consists of straw-roof laths and hurdle-poles.

*) The Mecklenburg rod measures 16 Luebeck feet of 129 Paris lines each. The Magdeburg Morgen equals 117 Mecklenburg square rods. (100 Luebeck cubic feet = 79 Rhineland cubic feet, and 100 Rhineland cubic feet = 126 Luebeck cubic feet.) The timber yield of 100 Luebeck cubic feet per 100 Mecklenburg square rods equals a yield of 93 Rhineland cubic feet per Magdeburg Morgen, and conversely, the yield of 100 Rhineland cubic feet per Magdeburg Morgen equals 107.5 Luebeck cubic feet per 100 Mecklenburg square rods.

The cartload of firewood contains approximately half a cord of log-wood and half a cord of thin brush or branch-wood, the cord reckoned at 196 Luebeck cubic feet of stacked volume.

On the basis of the views and findings of Mr. Chief Forester Nagel at Diekhof, I make the following assumptions regarding timber increment:

With a proper thinning, by means of which the individual trees are steadily given the space at which the increment of the whole forest reaches its maximum, the increment of a pine forest stand remains constant from the sixth year onward, and the timber stock therefore grows — though with the exception of advanced age — in arithmetic proportion. The first 5 years I regard, together with Mr. Chief Forester Nagel, as required for the formation of the plant body, and I set the timber stock of the 6-year-old pine forest on the local soil equal to one year's timber increment in the immediately following years.

We have calculated the timber stock of the 30-year-old pine forest for Tellow at 30 cartloads of 64 cubic feet = 1,920 cubic feet per 100 square rods. This arises from the increment of 30 - 5 = 25 years. This gives an annual increment of 76.8 cubic feet per 100 square rods.

The area required to deliver an annual increment of 100 cubic feet I call, for brevity's sake, a "forest Morgen." The size of the forest Morgen here, where 100 square rods yield 76.8 cubic feet, amounts to

100/76.8 x 100 = 130 square rods.

### Section 2. Timber Value.

The cartload of firewood contains:

a. Half a cord of log-wood, at the low local timber prices*) of 2 Thalers 4 shillings N2/3 = 1 Thaler 2 shillings N2/3
b. Half a cord of brush-wood at 18 shillings = 18 shillings

Total: 1 Thaler 11 shillings N2/3

The costs of felling, hauling, and stacking per cartload are reckoned at: [deducted]

Remaining value for 1 cartload of firewood: 38 shillings N2/3

The 23 cartloads of firewood thus have a value of 23 x 38 shillings = 18 Thalers 6 shillings.

The cartload of construction timber has, at the price of 4 shillings for a hurdle-pole and 2 shillings for a straw-roof lath, the value of 2.5 Thalers or 120 shillings; this amounts for 7 cartloads to 840 shillings.

The value of 30 cartloads is = 1,714 shillings.

This amounts for one cartload of 64 cubic feet on average = 57 shillings, for 1 cubic foot = 0.9 shillings, and for 100 cubic feet = 90 shillings.

*) When Thalers are mentioned in this text without further qualification, Thalers N2/3 are always meant, of which 6 equal 7 Thalers Prussian current. The Thaler N2/3 has 48 shillings.

**Value of 100-year-old pine timber.**

The price of heavy construction timber in the local area is currently 4 shillings per cubic foot.

If one now reckons that of the 100-year-old pines two-thirds of the mass is suitable for construction timber and one-third is used as firewood, the following average value results:

2 cubic feet at 4 shillings = 8 shillings
1 cubic foot at 0.9 shillings = 0.9 shillings
3 cubic feet = 8.9 shillings

1 cubic foot on average = 2.97 shillings N2/3.

**Increase in value of timber with age.**

Of the 100-year-old pines, the cubic foot is worth 2.97 shillings.
Of the 30-year-old, 0.9 shillings.
Difference over 70 years: 2.07 shillings.

This gives an annual increase in value of 0.03 shillings per cubic foot and of 3 shillings N2/3 for 100 cubic feet.

In the pine stand of 30 years age, 100 cubic feet have a value of 90 shillings.

If one now applies the principle derived from the value difference between 30-year-old and 100-year-old stands — "that each year the value of 100 cubic feet rises by 3 shillings N2/3" — backwards to the younger stands, one obtains for 100 cubic feet the value:

of 29-year-old timber = 87 shillings
of 28-year-old = 84 shillings
of 27-year-old = 81 shillings
of 16-year-old = 48 shillings

Remarkably, then, from the price relationship prevailing in the local area between younger and older timber, the principle emerges:

"that the value of 100 cubic feet of pine timber here stands in direct proportion to the age of the trees."

In the annual increase in value, or the annual return of a forest, two components are therefore contained:

1) the increment in timber mass,
2) the increase in value of the already existing timber stock.

### Section 3. Valuation of Pine Stands of a Given Age.

From the foregoing positions, the value of stands of every age can now be calculated.

Let the age of the stand be = x + 5 years, then the timber stock = 100x cubic feet per forest Morgen.

The value of 100 cubic feet is 3 shillings multiplied by the age of the trees, here thus 3(x + 5) shillings. The stand of 100x cubic feet accordingly has the value of 3x(x + 5) = 3x^2 + 15x, where the shilling N2/3 is taken as the unit.

**Example.**

| Age of timber stand | Timber stock (cubic feet) | Value of 100 cubic feet | Value of the timber stand (shillings) | Cumulative sum of values (shillings) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 6 years | 100 | 18 | 18 | |
| 7 | 200 | 21 | 42 | 60 |
| 8 | 300 | 24 | 72 | 132 |
| 9 | 400 | 27 | 108 | 240 |
| 10 | 500 | 30 | 150 | 390 |
| 11 | 600 | 33 | 198 | 588 |
| 12 | 700 | 36 | 252 | 840 |
| 13 | 800 | 39 | 312 | 1,152 |
| 14 | 900 | 42 | 378 | 1,530 |
| 15 | 1,000 | 45 | 450 | 1,980 |

By continuing this calculation, the sum of the value of all stands for every rotation period can be found.

But it is important for us to present a formula by which we can express this sum without calculating all the terms of the series.

To this end we examine more closely the pattern that shows itself in the increase in value of the timber stands.

For a series whose differences ultimately become constant, the sum can be found by a general formula.

According to Euler (Differential Calculus, Part 1, Chapter 2, p. 56), for a series whose first term = a, first difference = b, and second difference = c, and the number of terms = x:

The sum = ax + x(x-1)/2 * b + x(x-1)(x-2)/6 * c.

For our series giving the value of the stands:
a = 18,
b = 24,
c = 6.

For these values of a, b, and c:

Sum = x^3 + 9x^2 + 8x.

**Check:** Let x = 10, then x^3 = 1,000, 9x^2 = 900, 8x = 80; the sum = 1,980, in agreement with the above table.

### Section 5. The Rent of Forest Land.

If we for the time being abstract from, on the one hand, the yield of thinnings as well as subsidiary uses from hunting, etc., and on the other hand from the costs of supervision: then the ground rent of the forest equals the value of the final felling stand, minus the interest on the value of all timber stands and the costs of re-seeding the felled stand.

The interest on the value of the timber stands can be calculated from the foregoing propositions.

Since pine seedings frequently fail entirely, and even the successful ones require replanting in individual spots, I cannot, following local experience, estimate the costs of seeding at less than 2 shillings per square rod, i.e. 5 Thalers 20 shillings per forest Morgen of 130 square rods.

With a rotation of x + 5 years, the final felling stand has 100x cubic feet of timber mass; the value of 100 cubic feet is = 3(x + 5) shillings, and the revenue from this stand amounts to:

3x(x + 5) = 3x^2 + 15x.

**Examples.**

For x = 21 - 5 = 16, the value of the final felling stand is:
3x^2 = 768
15x = 240
Total: 1,008 shillings

The value of the timber stock is, according to the preceding calculation, 6,528 shillings.
Interest thereon at 4 percent = 261 shillings.
Seeding costs on 130 square rods at 2 shillings = 260 shillings.
Deduction: 521 shillings.
Surplus remaining: 487 shillings.

21 forest Morgen yield 487 shillings rent.
This amounts to per forest Morgen: 23.2 shillings.
Per 100 square rods: 17.8 shillings.

### Section 6. Problems Regarding the Rotation Period.

**Problem 1.** To express by a general formula the rotation period at which the forest land yields the maximum rent.

From the preceding examples it follows that extending the rotation from 21 years to 28, 35, and 42 years is associated with an increase in ground rent, but that the further extension to 49 years results in a reduction of rent.

It follows from this that there must be a point at which the maximum rent occurs.

This point can indeed be found approximately through laborious and tedious trial and error; but we do not thereby recognize the law itself, and the presentation of a general formula expressing this law would therefore have both scientific and practical interest.

Through the combination of the propositions set forth in the foregoing, such a general formula can indeed be presented, as the following calculation shows.

The final felling stand yields revenue of 3x^2 + 15x.

From this must be deducted:

1) the interest on the value of all timber stands; the value of the timber stands is x^3 + 9x^2 + 8x; interest thereon at 4 percent is 0.04x^3 + 0.36x^2 + 0.32x.

2) the costs of seeding one forest Morgen of 130 square rods at 2 shillings: 260.

After deducting these expenditures, the rent for one forest Morgen remains:

(-0.04x^3 + 2.64x^2 + 14.68x - 260) / (x + 5).

To find the value of x at which this function reaches its maximum, we must, as is well known, take the differential and set it equal to zero.

The differential is:

(x + 5)(-0.12x^2 + 5.28x + 14.68)dx - (-0.04x^3 + 2.64x^2 + 14.68x - 260)dx.

The development of this formula gives:

(-0.08x^3 + 2.04x^2 + 26.40x + 333.40)dx = 0.

This gives:

x^3 - 25.5x^2 - 330x - 4,167 = 0.

To solve this equation, the second term must be eliminated, and to this end we set x = y + 8.5. Then:

y^3 - 546.75y - 8,200 = 0.

By Cardano's rule, for y^3 = fy + g:

f = 546.75
g = 8,200

The calculation yields y = 28.83 and x = y + 8.5 = 28.83 + 8.5 = 37.33.

The age of the final felling stand equals the rotation period = x + 5 years.

We thus arrive at the result that, at the given price ratio of timber of different ages, **if the thinnings had no value, the maximum rent would occur at a rotation period of 37.33 + 5 = 42.33 years.**

**Problem 2.** To determine the age of a timber stand at which the value of the increment just covers the interest on the value of that timber stand.

For the stand of age x + 5 years, the value of the timber stand is = 3(x + 5)x = 3x^2 + 15x.

The value of the stand one year younger is 3(x - 1)(x + 4) = 3x^2 + 9x - 12.

The annual increment thus amounts to 3x^2 + 15x - (3x^2 + 9x - 12) = 6x + 12.

The interest on the value of the (x + 5)-year stand amounts to (3x^2 + 15x) x 0.04 = 0.12x^2 + 0.6x.

Setting this interest equal to the value of the increment gives:

0.12x^2 + 0.6x = 6x + 12
0.12x^2 - 5.4x = 12
x^2 - 45x = 100
x - 22.5 = sqrt(606.25) = 24.62
x = 47.12.

The age of the timber stand at which the interest on the value of the timber stock of this stand consumes the value of the increment, and the land yields no more rent, is x + 5 = **52.12 years**.

**Problem 3.** At what age of the timber stand does the timber increment, after deduction of interest on the timber value of that stand, yield the highest surplus; or at what age of the trees does the timber stand yield the highest rent?

As in the previous problem:

the value of the increment = 6x + 12
the timber stock of the (x + 5)-year stand = 3x^2 + 15x
the interest thereon = 0.12x^2 + 0.6x.

After deducting this interest from the value of the timber in the (x + 5)-year stand, the rent is:
= -0.12x^2 + 5.4x + 12.

Setting the differential of this function equal to zero gives:
(-0.24x + 5.4)dx = 0
thus 0.24x = 5.4
and x = 22.5.

For x = 22.5, i.e. for a stand age of 27.5 years, the rent of the individual stand thus reaches its maximum. But this does not yet give a norm for the most advantageous rotation period; for only from the sum of the rent so calculated for all stands, divided by the number of stands, does it emerge at which rotation period the entire forest area yields the highest rent. Since the younger stands yield a much smaller rent than the 27-year-old one, the point where all stands on average yield the maximum rent must also lie beyond the point at which a single stand gives the highest rent.

**Numerical examples for individual years.**

| Age of stand (x+5) | Value of increment (6x + 12) | Interest on timber value (0.12x^2 + 0.6x) | Surplus |
|---|---|---|---|
| x = 1 | 18 | 0.72 | 17.28 |
| x = 10 | 72 | 18.00 | 54 |
| x = 21 | 138 | 65.52 | 72.48 |
| x = 22.5 | 147 | 74.25 | 72.75 |
| x = 24 | 156 | 83.52 | 72.48 |
| x = 30 | 192 | 126.00 | 66.0 |
| x = 42.5 | 266 | 240.44 | 25.56 |
| x = 47 | 294 | 293.28 | 0.72 |

From the surplus thus determined, the seeding costs must still be deducted to arrive at the rent.

The question may arise whether the procedure followed here, of already charging interest on the current stock — that is, on the timber increment itself — is correct, or whether, as with loaned capital from which interest is paid only in the following year, only the interest on the timber value of the preceding year should have been charged.

But just as, in the strict separation of wages from the use of capital, we must assume that wages are paid daily — because in wages paid at the end of the year, interest is already contained — so we must also assume here that the interest on timber value is paid daily or taken into account, because in interest paid at year's end, compound interest is already contained.

### Section 7. Thinning.

In regard to thinning, forestry science presents interesting problems.

By means of thinning, one can give individual trees any desired amount of space. The greater this space, the greater the increment of individual trees; but at the same time, the number of trees standing on a given forest area decreases. There must therefore be a point in the spacing of trees at which the increment of the entire forest area reaches its maximum.

**Question 1.** Where is this point, and what is the measure for the spacing that the individual stems must have from one another, if this maximum is to be attained?

**Question 2.** Does the diameter or the timber mass of the tree serve as the measure for the spacing number?

**Question 3.** How does the increment of individual trees and the increment on the entire forest area change with the relative spacing?

As far as I know, these questions have barely been raised in forestry science, much less resolved.

In the investigation immediately before us, we are particularly interested in the question:

How is the value of the thinnings to be determined, and what is the most advantageous rotation period when the thinnings are taken into account?

Regarding the value of the thinnings in proportion to the value of the final felling stand, or also of the remaining stock, the data from the most reputable foresters diverge so widely that no point of reference can be derived from them.

The yield and value of the thinnings depend essentially on the greater or lesser spacing given to the trees during thinning, and the diversity of the principles held by foresters on this subject is probably the source of the diversity of data on the value of the timber obtained through thinning.

My own experience is far too insufficient to draw firm conclusions from it.

What matters here, however, is far less the correctness of the numbers than the presentation of the method by means of which the question of the most advantageous rotation period can be resolved. For once the method is found, the desired result can be derived for every case in which the — admittedly different for every locality — numbers are given.

In this spirit, I now adopt the following assumptions.

1) In pine stands, no thinning takes place in the first years after seeding, because on one hand no advantage to timber growth would result from it, and on the other hand the costs of thinning would exceed the value of the timber obtained.

2) The increment of pines from age 6 to 10 inclusive I take as two-thirds of the increment of the following years. If, for example, the annual total increment from year 11 onward is 150 cubic feet, then that from year 6 to 10 inclusive = 2/3 x 150 = 100 cubic feet.

If from the total increment 100 cubic feet annually pass into the remaining stand, the said 5 years yield no thinning timber at all. If, however, only 75 cubic feet are to pass annually into the remaining stand, then from the increment of the 5 years 25 cubic feet annually, and from the increment of the following years 75 cubic feet annually, fall to the next thinning.

3) If, as assumed here, from year 11 onward the crowns of the pines are closed, and the individual stems occupy the space at which the entire area yields the maximum increment, then by the growth of the trees in year 11 the space is already constricted, and in year 12 the highest increment will no longer occur unless a portion of the stems is removed by thinning. The same applies in every subsequent year.

Such a thinning would indeed be possible in pine stands that have arisen not by planting but by seeding — in which the stems come to stand at unequal distances from one another; but the costs of annual thinning, especially in younger stands, would exceed the value of the obtained material, and therefore in practice thinnings are not carried out annually but at intervals of several years.

For the presentation of a general formula, however, we need the assumption that the individual trees always occupy the space at which the whole forest attains the maximum value increment. This presupposes that in every year a portion of the annual timber increment is removed by means of thinning.

Since, however, annually recurring thinning is not practicable, we must ask ourselves whether the revenue from periodically recurring thinnings can be represented by a regular series connected with the value of the remaining timber stock.

The following serves as an answer to this:

The present capital value of a future revenue can, if the interest rate is given, be calculated precisely, and thus also the sum of the capital values of all revenues occurring periodically in a rotation period. The calculus can also represent the capital value of a rent growing from year to year according to a definite law. Conversely, the value of a capital can also be resolved into an annually rising rent. Consequently, the present capital value of all future thinnings can also be represented with mathematical precision by a growing series in which the terms are proportional to the value of the remaining timber stock.

Another objection that can be raised against representing the value of the thinnings by a regularly increasing series is this:

that in the periodically undertaken thinnings, the stems do not permanently but only in individual years receive the normal space, so that the annual increment in timber value cannot be the highest — which we nevertheless assume as the basis for the formula to be developed.

This objection is entirely justified, and the resulting deviation can become very significant for long thinning periods. To confine this deviation within the narrowest possible limits, we must assume for the following calculation:

that the thinnings are carried out at closely spaced intervals, and that the trees are given the space at which the normal spacing prevails at the midpoint of the period. Under this assumption, the deviation between the result of practice and our calculation will not be considerable, and I believe I may therefore abstract from it for the time being.

The immediate purpose of this investigation is to explore whether and to what extent the rotation period at which the maximum ground rent occurs changes when the value of the thinnings is taken into account.

We can also arrive at this understanding when we — for lack of sufficient data — conduct the calculation with hypothetically assumed numbers regarding timber yield and value of thinning timber.

Accordingly, I here base the analysis on the following assumptions:

1) From year 11 onward, one-third of the annual timber increment is removed by thinning each year, and two-thirds of this increment pass into the remaining stand. Since we have assumed the annual increase in the remaining stand to be 100 cubic feet per forest Morgen, the total annual increment = 150 cubic feet, and the timber yield from thinning amounts to 50 cubic feet annually.

2) The value of the thinning timber per cubic foot is less than that of the remaining stand for the following two reasons:

a. In forests that have arisen through seeding — which we assume here — the stems removed in thinning are those which are already overtopped, and beyond these also the stems which will foreseeably be overtopped by the next thinning. But this almost always affects the weaker trees, which with a smaller diameter also have a smaller value per cubic foot.

b. The felling, extraction, and hauling of thinning timber is much more time-consuming and costly than the felling and removal of timber from the final felling stand. Since, however, we always have in view only the value of the timber after deduction of felling and preparation costs, the thinning timber must be valued lower for this reason alone.

For both reasons together, I take the value of the thinning timber per cubic foot as two-thirds of the remaining timber.

From this the following calculations follow.

From year 11 onward, the annual total increment per forest Morgen is 150 cubic feet; of this, 50 cubic feet are removed by thinning.

These have a value of 50 x 2/3 = 33.33 cubic feet of the remaining stand.

The annual value yield of the thinning thus amounts to 33.33/100 = one-third of the annual increase in value of the remaining timber.

A glance at this table immediately shows that the cumulative value increase is always 150 less than the value of the timber stand itself. Nor can this be otherwise; for the value of the stand consists of the cumulative value increment of all stands from year 6 to x + 5, and since the increment from year 6 to 11, which amounts to 150, drops out here because no thinning yield occurs in these 5 years when the stand is to increase annually by 100 cubic feet, the sum considered here must also be 150 less than the value of the stand.

The value of the (x + 5)-year stand is, according to Section 4, = 3x^2 + 15x. The value increase in the stands from age 11 to (x + 5) is therefore = 3x^2 + 15x - 150.

The value of all thinnings amounts to one-third of this sum, thus x^2 + 5x - 50.

The total revenue is therefore:
1) from the final felling stand = 3x^2 + 15x
2) from the thinnings = x^2 + 5x - 50
Sum = 4x^2 + 20x - 50

From this must be deducted:
1) the interest on the value of all timber stands: 0.04x^3 + 0.36x^2 + 0.32x
2) the costs of seeding one forest Morgen of 130 square rods at 2 shillings: 260

The surplus is = -0.04x^3 + 3.64x^2 + 19.68x - 310.

This surplus divided by the number of stands gives the rent per forest Morgen.

For x = 30: the calculation gives a rent per forest Morgen of 130 square rods of 70.8 shillings, or per 100 square rods: 54.5 shillings.

For x = 40: rent per forest Morgen: 83.1 shillings, per 100 square rods: 63.9 shillings.

For x = 50: rent per forest Morgen: 86.8 shillings, per 100 square rods: 66.8 shillings.

For x = 60: rent per forest Morgen: 82.1 shillings, per 100 square rods: 63.1 shillings.

| Rotation | Rent per forest Morgen | Rent per 100 sq. rods |
|---|---|---|
| x + 5 = 35 years | 70.8 shillings | 54.5 shillings |
| 45 years | 83.1 | 63.9 |
| 55 years | 86.8 | 66.8 |
| 65 years | 82.1 | 63.1 |

We can conclude from this, even without performing the algebraic calculation as in Section 6, that the maximum rent falls at a rotation period between 54 and 55 years.

This rent, however, does not yet constitute the actual ground rent. For to it must still be added the yield of subsidiary uses from hunting, pasture, etc., and against it the costs of supervision and administration required by forestry must be deducted, in order to arrive at the ground rent itself. The supervision and administration costs vary greatly according to the size of the forest and its location, which exposes it more or less to timber theft. Here I estimate them, after deducting the insignificant subsidiary uses, at 8 shillings per forest Morgen, or 6.2 shillings per 100 square rods.

### Section 8. Forest Rent.

Just as in agriculture we distinguish estate rent from land rent — the former constituting the joint product of capital and land, the latter consisting of that part of estate rent remaining after deduction of interest on the capital invested in the erection of buildings, etc. — so we can also in forestry combine the yield from land and from the capital contained in the timber stock under the name "forest rent" and distinguish it from ground rent.

**Calculation of the forest rent.**

Revenue from the final felling stand: 3x^2 + 15x
Revenue from the thinnings: x^2 + 5x - 50
Total revenue: 4x^2 + 20x - 50

Expenditures:
Seeding costs: 260
Administration costs for x + 5 forest Morgen at 8 shillings: 8x + 40
Expenditure: 8x + 300

After deduction of this expenditure, the forest rent remains: 4x^2 + 12x - 350.

For x = 30: forest rent for 35 Morgen = 3,610. Per forest Morgen: 103.1 shillings.
For x = 40: forest rent for 45 Morgen = 6,530. Per forest Morgen: 145.1 shillings.
For x = 50: forest rent for 55 Morgen = 10,250. Per forest Morgen: 186.4 shillings.
For x = 60: forest rent for 65 Morgen = 14,770. Per forest Morgen: 227.2 shillings.

**Comparison between forest rent and ground rent.**

The ground rent results from Section 5, if one deducts 8 shillings per forest Morgen for administration costs from the rent found there.

| Rotation | Forest rent per forest Morgen | Ground rent per forest Morgen | Ratio of forest to ground rent |
|---|---|---|---|
| 35 years | 103.1 shillings | — | — |
| 45 years | 145.1 shillings | 75.1 shillings | 100 : 52 |
| 55 years | 186.4 shillings | 78.8 shillings | 100 : 42 |
| 65 years | 227.2 shillings | 74.1 shillings | 100 : 33 |

It is apparent here that with the extension of the rotation period, the forest rent grows, but the ground rent constitutes an ever smaller share of the forest rent and already reaches its maximum at the 55-year rotation.

At the rotation period yielding the highest ground rent, the forest Morgen gives 78.8 shillings. This amounts to 60.6 shillings per 100 square rods and for the Mecklenburg area measure of one Last of arable land = 6,000 square rods (ca. 50 Magdeburg Morgen), 75.7 Thalers N2/3. It follows that, for a situation where the assumed positions on timber yield and timber price apply, the land that through arable farming yields less than 56.5 Thalers N2/3 in land rent (not to be confused with lease rent) is utilized more highly through the cultivation of pines than through arable farming.

For the larger landowner, the question of where the natural boundary lies between forest land and grain land is of practical significance. The numbers with which the calculation for deciding this question is conducted are, of course, highly variable depending on the different locality and can differ even on two adjacent estates. But the method of how the calculation is to be conducted finds universal applicability.

The natural boundary between forest land and arable land is, however, essentially altered when the production of firewood becomes the primary purpose of forestry, which is why we must still cast a glance at such a situation.

### Section 9. Coppice or Brush-wood.

Meadow bottoms and low-lying fields not suited to high forest yield, apart from some construction timber, only firewood.

Here the rotation must be much shorter than on the upland for two different reasons:

1) the value of firewood increases with the greater thickness — hence with the age — of the stems in a much smaller proportion than with construction timber, and

2) at a more advanced age, the stump re-sprouting becomes uncertain and deficient.

For these reasons, a 20-year rotation is usually maintained here.

According to my observations, I have drawn up the following calculation for the yield of the brush or coppice wood at Tellow:

With a 20-year rotation, 100 square rods of regularly stocked coppice wood yield upon felling on average:
14 four-horse cartloads of firewood
and 1 four-horse cartload of construction timber.

These give:
6.5 cords of split and log-wood at 2 Thalers 4 shillings: 13 Thalers 26 shillings N2/3
8 cords of brush-wood at 9 shillings: 3 Thalers 16 shillings
0.5 cords of construction timber as pole shafts, ladder beams, hurdle-stakes: 3 Thalers 0 shillings

For 15 cartloads the revenue amounts to: 19 Thalers 42 shillings N2/3.
This makes per cartload: 1 Thaler 15.6 shillings.

The felling, extraction, hauling, and stacking costs according to my calculation per cartload: 21 shillings.
After deduction of these costs, the value of one cartload remains: 42.6 shillings.

Considering the firewood by itself, 14 cartloads give revenue of 16 Thalers 42 shillings.
This gives per cartload: 1 Thaler 7.8 shillings N2/3.
And after deduction of costs of 21 shillings, value of one cartload remains: 36.8 shillings.

For 2,000 square rods of coppice wood, of which 100 square rods are felled annually, the surplus revenue for construction and firewood combined from 15 cartloads at 42.6 shillings amounts to: 13 Thalers 15 shillings.

This makes for one Last of 6,000 square rods: 39 Thalers 45 shillings.

To find the forest rent, the following expenditures must be deducted from this revenue:

1) costs of replanting to replace stumps that have not re-sprouted, on 6,000 square rods: 3 Thalers.
2) cutting of hops in the younger stands to protect the young re-growth against suppression: 2 Thalers.
3) administration and supervision costs per 100 square rods at 6.2 shillings, for 6,000 square rods: 7 Thalers 36 shillings.

Expenditures: 12 Thalers 36 shillings.
After deduction, there results for 6,000 square rods a forest rent of: 27 Thalers 9 shillings.

The ground rent results when one deducts from the forest rent the interest on the capital invested in the timber stands.

For 100 square rods, the timber stock of the final felling stand = 15 cartloads. If now, as I believe can be assumed without significant error, the annual timber increment from year 1 to 20 is equal, the average timber stock of all stands = 7.5 cartloads. This gives the total timber stock of all 20 stands on 2,000 square rods = 150 cartloads. The value of a cartload in the final felling stand is = 42.6 shillings. The average value of the timber from all stands may amount to approximately two-thirds of this sum, thus 28.4 shillings per cartload.

Accordingly, on 20 stands of 100 square rods = 2,000 square rods, the value of all timber stands is 150 x 28.4 shillings = 88 Thalers 36 shillings.

For an area of 6,000 square rods, the capital value of the timber stock is thus: 266 Thalers 12 shillings.
Interest thereon at 4 percent: 10 Thalers 31 shillings.
From the forest rent of: 27 Thalers 9 shillings
deducting this interest gives for 6,000 square rods of coppice wood a ground rent of: 16 Thalers 26 shillings.

This rent from 6,000 square rods of regularly stocked coppice wood is so small that one is tempted to conclude that all coppice wood on an estate should be cleared from land that has any value as arable land, meadow, or pasture.

But at the moment when the timber requirement is no longer produced on the estate but must be purchased, a remarkable change occurs in the price of timber on the estate itself.

If timber must be purchased, it costs not only the price paid for it but also the amount of the hauling costs. At the same time, great uncertainty arises about satisfying the annually recurring need for timber. One becomes dependent on the whims of one's neighbors, and if they do not care to sell timber, or if they prefer to clear their surplus timber, one can find oneself in the position of having to fetch timber from miles away.

Through this, however, the price of timber on the estate itself, as determined by procurement costs, can rise to double or even triple the former selling price.

From this consideration it may be expedient — if the need for firewood is not covered by the waste wood from the high forest — to cultivate coppice wood on land that, used as meadow or pasture, yields a land rent per 6,000 square rods of 40 Thalers and above.

In any case, under these conditions, it is advisable to limit firewood production to the minimum of one's own timber needs.

But since all estate owners have an equal interest in clearing their surplus firewood, a shortage of firewood must necessarily arise in the long run, and the contradiction between the production price — which includes the rent the forest land could yield as arable or meadow — and the selling price can persist only so long as remnants of primeval forests, not laid out by the hand of man but by nature itself, are still in existence.

### Section 10. Influence of the Value Yield of Thinnings on the Most Advantageous Rotation Period and on Ground Rent.

Our investigation is directed principally at determining the rotation period at which, for a given timber price, forestry yields the highest ground rent.

If this problem is solved, this investigation can pave the way for solving the general and higher problem:

"to determine the rotation period at which the production price of timber reaches its minimum."

In order to shed light on the question whether and how the value yield of thinnings affects the most advantageous rotation period, we assumed at the beginning of this investigation that thinnings take place — because without them the requirement that the annual value increment reach its maximum cannot be met — but we abstracted from their value yield; or in other words: we assumed that the yield from thinnings was absorbed by the felling and preparation costs of the obtained timber.

We then built the further investigation on the assumption that the thinnings yield a net return, and assumed numbers for this net return that may in many cases come close to reality.

The comparison of the results from these calculations now provides a contribution to answering the above question.

**a. When the thinnings yield no net return,**

the most advantageous rotation period is, according to Section 5, = 42 years.

The ground rent is then, according to Section 5, for the forest Morgen: 44.5 shillings minus 8 shillings for administration costs = 36.5 shillings.

**b. When the thinnings have the value stated above,**

the most advantageous rotation period is = 55 years, and the ground rent is then per forest Morgen = 78.8 shillings.

From this comparison we thus see that through the added net return from thinnings:

1) the rotation period extends from 42 to 55 years, and
2) the ground rent rises from 36.5 to 78.8 shillings per forest Morgen.

Most striking and interesting, however, is the fact that even with the strong increase in timber value per cubic foot assumed here, standing in direct proportion to the age of the trees, a rotation longer than 55 years proves disadvantageous.

But if all forest owners in an entire region, recognizing their own interest, allow no longer rotation, then the production of heavy construction timber ceases entirely. Since, however, heavy construction timber absolutely cannot be dispensed with, its production must be called forth and made profitable by raising the price.

It thus appears as though the timber price per cubic foot must rise in an even stronger proportion to the age of the trees than we have assumed, if the production costs of heavy construction timber are to be covered.

We have, however, through our investigation, arrived at the knowledge that when the value of the thinnings rises from zero to one-third of the value increment of the remaining stand, the rotation period extends from 42 to 55 years without any increase in the prices of older timber.

Now the value of the thinnings relative to the value of the remaining stand is by no means a constant quantity, but depends entirely on the thinning method.

The question thus naturally arises whether there is not a method of thinning that, by raising the value of the thinnings relative to the value of the stand, extends the most advantageous rotation period so far that even heavy construction timber can be produced with advantage without any change in the price ratio between younger and older timber.

### Section 11. The Thinning Method of Mr. Chief Forester Nagel.

In the Mecklenburg Annals of Agriculture, 12th year, second half, 1825, Mr. Chief Forester Nagel*) of Diekhof — a sharp-thinking, practical forester — supplied a valuable forest valuation calculation that has great personal value for me, because it first gave me insight into the yield of the land through timber cultivation and the method of thinning.

What I have drawn from this treatise and learned through oral explanations and communications from the author about his thinning method, I share here all the more willingly, since given the advanced age of this forester, the results of his manifold researches will probably be lost to the public.

*) Since deceased. — Ed.

This thinning method rests on the following principles and positions:

1) The calculation is based on a pine forest that has arisen not through seeding but through planting in straight rows.

2) At the 1st thinning, undertaken when the pines are 15 years old, every other row of trees is removed. At the 2nd thinning at age 21, every other tree in each row is removed. This procedure is maintained in the following thinnings.

When the trees standing in squares have grown so far that the spacing of the trees is only 10 times their diameter, a thinning is undertaken in which half the stems are felled. According to this principle:

The 3rd thinning occurs at age 30
The 4th at age 42
The 5th at age 60
The 6th at age 85
And the final felling at age 120.

The timber increment under this thinning method is equally large in the younger and older stands, and the timber stock grows in arithmetic progression. For the initial development of the plants, however, a number of years, varying with the soil, are regarded as yielding no increment and are accounted for accordingly.

---

We now turn to the question:

**What influence does this thinning method exert on ground rent and the most advantageous rotation period?**

The question whether this type of thinning can also deliver the highest annual increment, we leave for now unanswered, and assume as before a total annual increment of 150 cubic feet per forest Morgen.

Here, however, instead of one-third, half of the annual timber increment is removed by thinning — thus 75 instead of 50 cubic feet — and the remaining stand increases annually not by 100 but by 75 cubic feet.

Since here it is not the weaker, threatened stems that are removed, but rather the place occupied by the stems determines the selection, the thinning timber must have the same selling price as the remaining timber.

Furthermore, since under this thinning method passable roads quickly form everywhere, and the extraction of the timber is thus spared, we may assume without significant departure from reality that the thinning timber has not only the same selling price but also the same value per cubic foot as the remaining timber.

According to these determinations, the following general formula results for the rent:

For a stand age of x + 5 years, the timber stock is 75x cubic feet.
100 cubic feet have a value of 3x + 15.
This gives for 75x cubic feet a value of 2.25x^2 + 11.25x.

The thinnings deliver the same value as the final felling stand, with the only difference that for stands from age 6 to 10 inclusive, as in Section 7, no thinning value is counted, and the equivalent must therefore be deducted from the value of the final felling stand.

This deduction amounts here to 150 x 100/120 = 125.

The value of all thinnings is accordingly 2.25x^2 + 11.25x - 112.5. *)

Final felling stand and thinnings together give 4.50x^2 + 22.50x - 112.5.

From this must be deducted:

1) The interest on the value of all timber stands. At an annual increase of the timber stock of 100 cubic feet, this amounted to 0.04x^3 + 0.36x^2 + 0.32x. At an annual increase of 75 cubic feet, the interest is only three-quarters as large and thus amounts to 0.03x^3 + 0.27x^2 + 0.24x.

2) Planting costs, which we for now assume equal to the seeding costs at 260.

*) Here there is an error that I only recognized later and which has therefore carried over into the following sections; the more precise calculation as conducted in Section 21 gives the thinning yield = 2.25x^2 + 11.25x - 75. The resulting deviation is, however, insignificant and nowhere in the following calculations reaches one shilling per forest Morgen.

After deducting these two expenditures, the rent for x + 5 forest Morgen remains:
-0.03x^3 + 4.23x^2 + 22.26x - 372.5.

If one now sets x successively to 50, 60, 70, 80, and 90, and deducts 8 shillings per forest Morgen for administration costs, the completed calculation yields a ground rent per forest Morgen:

at the rotation of 55 years: 129.6 shillings
at 65 years: 141.4 shillings
at 75 years: 147.0 shillings
at 85 years: 143.8 shillings
at 95 years: 129.5 shillings

**The maximum ground rent will here fall between the 75th and 76th year.**

Under the first thinning method considered (Section 7), we found at the 55-year rotation the highest ground rent per forest Morgen = 78.8.

Here we find this at the 55-year rotation = 129.6 and at the 75-year rotation = 147.0.

Nagel's thinning method thus appears to result in an enormous increase in ground rent.

However, this comparison cannot serve as the norm for our investigation, for the following reasons:

1) If a thinning is to be profitable as early as age 15, the planting must be extraordinarily dense, and then the costs will far exceed the seeding costs assumed here. But if the planting is done widely, the first years yield far less than the calculated increment, and the first profitable thinning may then perhaps not be undertaken until age 30.

2) Since under this method the epochs of thinning in the later age of the stands fall so far apart, the position of the trees must be too crowded shortly before thinning, and after thinning — which gives each tree double the area — far too spacious to deliver the maximum increment assumed in the calculation.

3) If, to maintain the regular position of the trees, thinning considers only the space the tree occupies, then some stunted or poorly grown trees must be left standing, and at final felling more firewood and less construction timber will be found than we have assumed in the calculation.

By how much the calculated ground rent is diminished by these circumstances, I am also unable to indicate even approximately.

Undeniably, however, the circumstance that here the capital invested in the timber stock, and thus also the interest to be calculated thereon, is one-quarter less than under the first thinning method (Section 7), has a very significant share in the higher ground rent that results for Nagel's method.

But in order to bring the influence that the reduction of the timber capital exerts on ground rent purely to recognition — i.e. without the effect of disturbing secondary circumstances — we must also apply the principle of removing half the increment through thinning to seeded stands.

### Section 12. Ground Rent and Rotation Period in Seeded Stands, when the Thinnings Remove Half the Timber Increment.

Although in seeded stands the perfectly regular positioning of the stems in squares cannot be achieved, there is surely no doubt that through a stronger thinning, half the increment can be removed here just as well as in planted stands.

The disadvantage of Nagel's method — that the stems sometimes stand too closely and sometimes have too much space — can almost be eliminated here if short thinning periods are adopted. In what follows I therefore assume that the 1st thinning is carried out at age 15, the 2nd at 25, the 3rd at 35, and so on, recurring every 10 years.

On the other hand, the advantage that Nagel's method offers — namely, that the thinning timber achieves equal value per cubic foot with the remaining stand — cannot be achieved in seeded stands. For, as already mentioned, the less valuable stems are preferentially taken in thinning, and at the same time the extraction of the timber from the forest significantly increases costs. We shall therefore, as before, value the thinning timber per cubic foot at only two-thirds of the remaining timber.

The value of the thinnings therefore amounts to 2/3(2.25x^2 + 11.25x - 112.5) = 1.50x^2 + 7.50x - 75, thus 0.75x^2 + 3.75x - 37.5 less than under Nagel's method.

The revenue from the final felling stand and the expenditures for interest and seeding costs remain unchanged.

In the previous section, the rent was = -0.03x^3 + 4.23x^2 + 22.26x - 372.5.

For the lesser yield of the thinnings, deduct: 0.75x^2 + 3.75x - 37.5.

The rent for the seeded stands thus remains = -0.03x^3 + 3.48x^2 + 18.51x - 335.

Setting various values for x and deducting 8 shillings administration costs per forest Morgen yields:

The ground rent is:

**The highest ground rent occurs at the rotation of 67 years.**

### Section 13. Comparison of the Two Methods, in which A. the thinning removes one-third of the annual timber increment, B. half of this increment.

**1. In regard to the portion of the stand removed by thinning.**

If the first thinning is carried out when the trees are 15 years old and is repeated every 10 years, the proportional share of thinning timber from the stock is as follows:

**For case A, when one-third of the increment is taken by thinning:**

At age 15 the stand is 5 x 100 + 5 x 150 = 500 + 750 = 1,250 cubic feet, since the annual increment from year 5 to 10 = 100 cubic feet and from year 10 to 15 = 150 cubic feet.

The thinning removes 5 x 50 = 250 cubic feet; thus one-fifth of the stock.

The remaining stand is 1,000 cubic feet. Adding the increment in 10 years of 1,500, the stand at age 25 = 2,500 cubic feet.

The second thinning removes 10 x 50 = 500 cubic feet, thus one-fifth of the stock.

The remaining stand is 2,000 cubic feet; adding the increment of 1,500 in 10 years, the stand at age 35 = 3,500 cubic feet.

The thinning again removes 500 cubic feet, thus one-seventh of the stock.

Continuing this calculation shows that the thinning removes from the stock:

at age 45: one-ninth
at age 55: one-eleventh
at age 65: one-thirteenth
at age 75: one-fifteenth

**For case B, when the thinning takes half the increment:**

At age 15 the stand = 1,250 cubic feet. The thinning yield is 5 x 25 + 5 x 75 = 125 + 375 = 500, thus two-fifths of the stock.

The remaining stand is 750 cubic feet. Increment in 10 years: 1,500. Stand at age 25: 2,250 cubic feet.

The thinning removes 10 x 75 = 750, thus one-third of the stock.

In this way it emerges that:
the 3rd thinning at age 35 removes one-quarter of the stock,
the 4th at age 45: one-fifth,
the 5th at age 55: one-sixth,
the 6th at age 65: one-seventh,
the 7th at age 75: one-eighth.

**2. In regard to forest rent.**

The forest rent per forest Morgen:

| Rotation | Method A | Method B |
|---|---|---|
| 35 years | 103.1 | — |
| 45 years | 145.1 | — |
| 55 years | 186.4 | 173.1 |
| 65 years | 227.2 | 211.8 |
| 75 years | — | 250.0 |
| 105 years | 389.0 | 363.8 |

From this it follows:

1) that method A yields a larger forest rent than method B.

2) that the forest rent rises continuously and indeed very considerably with the longer rotation period.

From this result, which can also be found by a simpler calculation, it becomes understandable why one has held so long and so persistently to the weaker thinning and the longer rotation period.

In truth, however, from such a calculation only one truth emerges:

**that a large capital brings more interest than a small one!**

For with the longer rotation, by far the largest part of the forest rent arises from the interest on the capital invested in the timber stock.

For A (Section 7), the interest on the value of all timber stands = 0.04x^3 + 0.36x^2 + 0.32x.

For x = 100 and the rotation of 105 years, this gives per forest Morgen interest of 415.5.

The interest on the capital invested in the timber stock thus here exceeds the entire forest rent, considerable as this is — and the forest owner would, if he felled all timber, put the proceeds at interest, and left the land lying waste, have more income than before from operating the forest.

**3. In regard to ground rent.**

This amounts per forest Morgen:

| Rotation | For A | For B |
|---|---|---|
| 35 years | 62.8 | — |
| 45 years | 75.1 | — |
| 55 years | 78.8 | 92.7 |
| 65 years | 74.1 | 97.0 |
| 67 years | — | 97.1 |
| 70 years | — | 95.0 |
| 105 years | -26.5 | 52.2 |

It follows from this that the highest ground rent that method A can deliver amounts to 78.8 shillings per forest Morgen of 130 square rods, which for an area of 6,000 square rods amounts to 75.7 Thalers; the highest ground rent of method B is, on the other hand, 97.1 shillings per forest Morgen = 93.3 Thalers for 6,000 square rods. The ratio between A and B is as 78.8 : 97.1 = 100 : 123; or **B yields 23 percent more ground rent than A.**

Method B has, however, yet another advantage not yet considered here. With the greater spacing of the trees, the increment of individual stems becomes larger, even though the total increment of the forest remains the same. The increasing value of the timber per cubic foot with the age of the trees is, however, based solely on the larger diameter of the older stems, and when under different thinning methods the annual increase in the diameter of the trees differs, then instead of the age of the trees, the diameter must be taken as the measure for the value of the timber per cubic foot.

Suppose it were found that under method A the diameter of individual trees increases annually by one-sixth of an inch, under B by one-fifth of an inch, then under A the tree would already at age 72, under B at age 60, attain a thickness of 1 foot in diameter, and despite the different age, both trees would have equal value.

Suppose that for A the assumed annual value increase of the timber of 0.03 shillings per cubic foot is correct; then for B this value increase would have to be calculated at 0.036 shillings per cubic foot.

If one were to take the numbers here included merely as an example as the norm, then the additional yield of B would rise to almost 90 percent.

But to set up a well-founded calculation, one would need to know the law according to which the diameter of the trees grows under different degrees of spacing.

In any case, however, the result found here deserves attention. For if through an improved thinning method the net yield of all pine forests can be raised by 23 to 90 percent, this benefits not only the forest owner, but the national income itself is thereby increased to a significant degree.

Here I must, however, myself declare this investigation to be an unfinished one.

For it is assumed, for both B and A, that the annual increment per forest Morgen is 150 cubic feet. But there is surely no doubt that the greater or lesser spacing affects not only the increment of individual trees but also the total increment of the entire forest, so that for two different thinning methods one and the same annual increment may not be assumed.

The question, however, whether for A or B the annual total increment is greater, and in what proportion the two stand to each other, is insoluble as long as we do not know **in what proportion the increment of the individual tree stands to the space given to it.**

Thus here too it appears that the advance in knowledge leads not to complete satisfaction but to ever higher problems.

**4. In regard to the rotation period.**

From the investigations so far, we have seen that the most advantageous rotation period changes with the value and yield of the thinnings, and that the land yields the highest rent:

a) When the thinning timber is without value: at a rotation of 42 years.
b) When the thinning takes one-third of the timber increment and this timber has two-thirds the value of the remaining timber: at 55 years.
c) When half the increment is removed by thinning, and this timber has two-thirds the value of the remaining stand: at 67 years.

It follows from this quite clearly that the rotation period yielding the highest profit extends the more, the more productive the thinnings become.

Striking, however, is the fact that even with the strong thinning that takes half the increment, the most advantageous rotation period does not exceed 67 years. At the age of 67 years, pines do not yet yield heavy construction timber, and thus the view earlier expressed seems to be confirmed: that the price of construction timber per cubic foot must rise in an even stronger proportion than that of the age of the trees, if, after the disappearance of the primeval forests, heavy construction timber is to be produced at all.

Let us now examine more closely what obstructs the longer rotation and makes it unprofitable: we find the reason in the large capital that is contained in the timber stock under the long rotation. Strikingly this shows itself under the 105-year rotation, where under method A the interest on the standing capital not only swallows the entire forest rent but exceeds it by 26.5 shillings per forest Morgen.

On the other hand, the same rotation under method B, where the standing capital is one-quarter less, still yields a ground rent of 52.2 shillings. This teaches us that the disadvantages of the high rotation can be mitigated by reducing the stock, that is, by stronger thinning.

To what degree this thinning may be carried is now the subject of the following investigation.

## Second Section

### Section 14. How Large Must the Spacing of Trees Relative to Their Diameter Be, If the Annual Value Increment of the Whole Forest Is to Reach Its Maximum?

This ratio will certainly be very different for different tree species and even for the same tree species on different soils. It thus appears as if a rule is entirely out of the question here.

But it is not at all a question here of a rule encompassing all species of trees and soils; rather, it is only a matter of finding the law for a given tree species (here the pine) on a specific soil and of building further conclusions upon it.

If one wishes to maintain that even for this concrete case no law exists, I pose the question: would nature indeed remain silent if one undertook the following experiment?

Divide a field of entirely uniform soil into eight equal parts, sow the whole uniformly with pines, and through annually recurring thinning, as far as approximately possible, give the stems a spacing from one another, from their youth to felling, which amounts:

in the 1st section to 8 times,
in the 2nd to 9 times,
in the 3rd to 10 times,
in the 4th to 11 times,
in the 5th to 12 times,
in the 6th to 13 times,
in the 7th to 14 times,
in the 8th to 15 times
their diameter.

Certainly nature would not remain silent here — and if we repeated this experiment on the same soil and under the same conditions, in order to be able to compensate for possible disturbances, the law would be found for this concrete case.

Since, however, there is no absolute obstacle to conducting such an experiment with every tree species, on every soil type, and under all climatic conditions, the possibility of researching this natural law is, apart from the great difficulty of the experiment, not to be denied.

Conducting such experiments requires, however, great attentiveness on the part of the observer, a considerable expenditure of cost, and, what is worst, a duration that exceeds the lifespan of the individual human being. It is therefore not surprising if this question has not yet been solved in practice. But I was struck by the fact that in the forestry writings known to me, I have found nowhere a numerical specification, even if only hypothetical, of the ratio of the required space to the diameter of the trees — even though every foundation of a thinning principle leads back to this question.

The only point of reference on this subject I find in the thinning method of Mr. Chief Forester Nagel.

He undertakes, as already mentioned in Section 11, the thinning when the spacing of the trees is ten times their diameter, and then by removing half the trees gives the remaining stems double the floor space.

If we take here the square of the diameter = d^2 as the unit and measure of floor space, each tree has shortly before thinning 100d^2, after thinning a space of 200 such units.

The relative space of 200d^2 for each tree, with which the thinning period begins, becomes, however, smaller with each year, because the unit by which we measure — the diameter — steadily grows, and at the close of the thinning period this relative space has sunk to 100d^2.

Under this procedure, however, the trees initially have too much and at the close of the period too little space, and we can only conclude from this that at the midpoint of the period the trees have the normal spacing.

At the midpoint of the thinning period, however, the tree has a floor space of (200d^2 + 100d^2)/2 = 150d^2.

At a floor space of 150d^2, the spacing of the trees is = sqrt(150d^2) = 12.2d.

On this basis I now adopt the following assumption for further consideration:

**The spacing of trees from one another — from the center of the base of one tree to the center of the base of the surrounding trees standing in a square — must be twelve times the diameter, if the forest area is to yield the highest annual value increment.**

The testing and possible correction of this assumption I must leave to practical foresters.

### Section 15. The Increment of Individual Trees in Diameter and Physical Volume.

Mr. Chief Forester Nagel assumes that with the normal spacing of trees, the annual increase in diameter proceeds in arithmetic proportion and, with the omission of the first years required for forming the plant body and with the exception of the advanced age of the trees, is equally large in every year.

**Example:** The number of years required for body formation, for which no increment is counted, is = 5, and the increase in diameter from year 6 onward amounts to one-sixth inch or 1/60 foot annually; then the diameter of the tree:

at age 25 = 20 x 1/5 = 4 inches
at age 35 = 6 inches
at age 45 = 8 inches
at age 50 = 10 inches
at age 65 = 12 inches

**Calculation of the physical volume of a tree of 1 foot diameter and 71 feet height.**

The tree forms a truncated cone furnished with branches, which, however, in the middle is usually somewhat thicker than the pure conical form requires.

Cotta has in his auxiliary tables presented the ratio between the volume of a tree and that of the mathematical cone, at equal height and base area — thereby greatly facilitating our calculation.

According to Table 1 p. 5 and Table 11 p. 23, the volume of the cone of 1 foot diameter and 71 feet height = 18.59 cubic feet.

According to Table IV p. 32, for pines the average ratio between the volume of the cone and the volume of the de-branched tree is as 100 : 129. Accordingly, the volume of a de-branched pine of 71 feet height and 1 foot diameter = 18.59 x 1.29 = 23.98 cubic feet.

The calculation is simplified considerably, however, if instead of the cone one takes the four-sided pyramid as the measure for comparison.

Let the ratio of diameter to height be as 1 : h, then for diameter = d the height = hd. The pyramid, whose base forms a square of which each side equals the diameter of the tree, i.e. = d^2, has a volume of (1/3)hd x d^2 = (1/3)hd^3.

For h = 71 and d = 1, the volume of the pyramid = 1/3 x 71 = 23.67 cubic feet.
The volume of the tree is = 23.98 cubic feet.

Here there is only a difference of 0.31 cubic feet, and to find the volume of the de-branched tree, one must multiply the volume of the pyramid by 1.013.

For stems with full utilization, i.e. of the shaft with all branches, the volume of the whole tree relates to that of the cone, according to Cotta's 4th table p. 32, as 167 : 100.

The volume of the cone is 18.59 cubic feet.
The volume of the whole tree is thus 18.59 x 1.67 = 31.05 cubic feet.

Comparing this with the volume of the pyramid, the ratio between the two is 23.67 : 31.05 = 100 : 131.2.

One thus finds the volume of the tree with all branches by multiplying the volume of the corresponding pyramid by 1.312.

The volume of the pyramid is (1/3)h x d^2 = (1/3)hd^3.
The volume of the tree is then = (1/3) x 1.312 x hd^3.
For h = 71: 1/3 x 1.312 x 71 = 31, and

we obtain for the volume of a tree the supremely simple expression **31d^3**.

This gives for d = 2, the volume = 248 cubic feet.
For d = 1/2, the volume = 31/8.
For d = 1/3, the volume = 31/27.

If the annual increase in diameter is one-fifth inch = 1/60 foot, how large is then the increment of a tree of 1 foot diameter in physical volume?

The tree, which in spring had a thickness of 1 foot, then has in autumn a diameter of 1 + 1/72 = 73/72 feet.

The volume of this tree is 31(73/72)^3 = 32.31 cubic feet.
The volume of the tree has risen from 31 to 32.31 cubic feet, and the increment amounts to 1.31 cubic feet or 4.2 percent of the stock.

In general terms, let the annual increment of the diameter be *a* feet, then over the course of the summer the diameter grows from d to d + a.

The volume of the tree whose diameter = d + a is 31(d + a)^3 = 31(d^3 + 3d^2a + 3da^2 + a^3).

In spring the volume was = 31d^3.

The increase in physical volume thus amounts to **31(3ad^2 + 3a^2d + a^3)**.

### Section 16. Calculation of the Portion of Increment Which Must Be Removed Through Thinning.

Given the great diversity of foresters' views on the thinning yield, and the complete lack of a demonstrably correct thinning principle, I have in the foregoing been able to assume the yield of thinnings in proportion to the increment only hypothetically.

Given this gap in the science regarding one of the most essential subjects of forestry, it is equally impossible to determine the most advantageous rotation period or to calculate precisely the yield of the land through timber cultivation.

The desire and the need therefore press themselves upon us to discover a scientifically founded principle for thinning.

The question now is whether the investigation of such a law is at all possible, and if so, whether the available data are sufficient for it.

Let us now test whether the propositions I have drawn and derived from the forest valuation of Chief Forester Nagel are sufficient for this purpose.

For ease of overview, I here assemble in sequence the propositions already individually cited above.

**Proposition 1.** The annual value increment of a forest reaches its maximum when the spacing of the trees is twelve times their diameter.

**Proposition 2.** With the exception of the first years required for forming the plant body and of the advanced age of the trees, the annual increase in diameter of the individual trees, at the normal spacing, remains constant.

**Proposition 3.** Under the same restrictions and conditions as in Proposition 2, the annual increment of the timber mass of the entire forest is also constant.

I now lay these propositions as the foundation for the following reflections, to test whether the sought law can be derived from them.

Based on the calculation in the preceding section founded on Proposition 2, we know that the increment of individual trees in physical volume amounts to 31(3ad^2 + 3a^2d + a^3) cubic feet. According to Proposition 1, each tree requires a space of 12 x 12 x d^2 = 144d^2.

If now the forest area = w square feet, there stand in spring w/(144d^2) trees on this area. This number multiplied by the increment of individual trees gives the total increment of the forest. In autumn the diameter of the trees has grown from d to d + a. Each tree then requires 144(d + a)^2 space. On the whole forest area, w/(144(d + a)^2) trees then have room. But there are w/(144d^2) trees standing there, and to create the normal space, w/(144d^2) - w/(144(d + a)^2) trees must be felled, each of which has a volume of 31(d + a)^3 cubic feet. This timber mass compared with the total increment shows in what proportion increment and thinning yield stand to each other.

The possibility of solving the stated problem through the calculus is thus demonstrated, if the value of d is given.

[The detailed mathematical derivation follows, which through integration and analysis of limiting cases establishes that:]

The floor space that the tree whose diameter in spring = d requires during the entire summer = (d^2 + ad + 1/3 a^2) x 144.

**1. Increment of individual trees.**

The diameter of each tree is:
in spring: d
in autumn: d + a

The volume of the tree is:
in spring: 31d^3
in autumn: 31(d + a)^3 = 31(d^3 + 3ad^2 + 3a^2d + a^3)

The increment is: 31(3ad^2 + 3a^2d + a^3).

**2. Increment of the entire forest area.**

The tree requires during the whole summer on average a space of 144(d^2 + ad + 1/3 a^2).

On the forest area w there can thus stand w / [144(d^2 + ad + 1/3 a^2)] trees. This number multiplied by the increment of individual trees gives the total increment:

= (31w / 144) x 3a = (31w / 48) x a.

**3. Increment of the remaining stand.**

At the beginning of spring each tree has diameter d, volume 31d^3, and requires space 144d^2.

On the forest area w there can stand w/(144d^2) trees.
The timber stock of the forest is thus (31w)/(144) x d.

In autumn the diameter is d + a, the volume 31(d + a)^3, and the space required is 144(d + a)^2.

On the area w there can stand w/(144(d + a)^2) trees.

The timber stock of the remaining stand is then (31w)/(144) x (d + a).

The increment of the remaining stand is (31w)/(144) x a.

The total increment we found to be (31w)/(48) x a.

The thinnings have thus removed (31w)/(48) x a - (31w)/(144) x a = (31w)/(72) x 2a.

**We thus obtain the highly remarkable result that at the normal spacing of the trees, the yield of the thinning in timber mass amounts to double the increment of the remaining stand.**

**Example:** Let d = 1 foot, a = 1/72 foot, w = 1,440,000 square feet = 5,625 square rods.

The total increment = 12,919 cubic feet.

Through the steadily continued thinning, 272 stems are removed in the course of the summer.
Thinning yield: 8,789 cubic feet.
Remaining increment: 4,311 cubic feet.

The increment in remaining stock relates to the thinning yield as 4,311 : 8,789 = 100 : 204.

The yield from thinning here thus amounts to over two-thirds of the total increment.

### Section 17. Critique.

The essential content of the preceding section I conceived and wrote down as early as 1828.

The result of this investigation was, however, so surprising to me and stood in such glaring contradiction with practice and the views of proven foresters, that I suspected an error in the conclusions and gave no credence to the result itself.

Since I then lacked the leisure for a renewed examination, this investigation remained entirely at rest and had almost fallen into oblivion for me.

In more recent times, however, I was induced to seek clarity about the value of timber stands of different ages and about the rent that the land yields through the cultivation of pines. But the ground rent depends essentially on the rotation period and the yield of thinnings. The continued investigation very soon showed, however, that the most advantageous rotation period itself is in turn dependent on the yield of the thinnings — and this subject thereby became the pivot of the investigation.

Since I lack data on the yield of thinnings, and know no authority I can follow in this respect, I had to content myself with hypothetically assuming various ratios for the yield of thinnings relative to the remaining stand, in order to approach the truth more closely through comparing the effects of different thinning methods on the rotation period and ground rent.

Thus the present first section came into being. But every deeper penetration into the subject led to questions that cannot be solved by the hypothetical propositions, and with each further step, the need for knowledge of the law prevailing in nature became ever clearer.

Involuntarily I was thereby led back to my earlier investigation of 1828.

With all care I have now subjected what was then written down to a renewed examination; but I have not succeeded in discovering the supposed error in the conclusions themselves.

I must therefore leave it to others to attempt to demonstrate an inconsistency in the conclusions.

If this does not succeed, however, the criticism must turn against the Nagel propositions that underlie our calculation — insofar as it is conducted with numbers rather than letters — and whose correctness I certainly cannot guarantee, in order to bring to a decision whether the result found, or the prevailing view, is an untruth.

[The section continues with a detailed mathematical proof showing that the result — two-thirds of total increment going to thinning — holds regardless of whether the Nagel proposition about constant diameter increase is correct, and regardless of whether the spacing number is 10, 12, 15, or any other number. The result is shown to be a mathematical consequence of the assumption that the required space is proportional to the square of the diameter.]

The section also demonstrates that if instead the required space were proportional to the timber mass of the tree, the entire increment would have to be removed by thinning and no increase in the forest's timber stock would occur — an absurdity. This confirms that the diameter, not the mass, must serve as the measure for the required space.

The chapter concludes:

These investigations directed at discovering the natural laws governing forestry relate to practical forestry as pure geometry relates to applied geometry.

Pure geometry rests on nothing but fictions; it assumes points without extension, lines without breadth, which are nowhere to be found in reality. Yet it is the unassailable foundation of practical geometry, and the latter would without the former be mere groping in the dark.

### Section 19. In What Ratio Does the Increment of the Tree Stand to the Space Given to It?

We must first consider the space that the tree of given diameter is capable of penetrating with its roots.

[The section describes observations of root spread in willows (4 rods from the tree row), birches (16 feet), and discusses the concept of "subsistence space" — the minimum spacing at which tree growth ceases entirely.]

The space that trees occupy when, due to their crowded position, their growth ceases and their increment equals zero, I call **the space required for the subsistence of the trees.**

I now establish the following proposition:

**If trees standing in a square require for their subsistence a spacing of md, and at a spacing of (m+1)d the increment of the diameter = g, then at a spacing of (m+2)d the increment = 2g, at (m+3)d = 3g, and so forth, until the tree's ability to spread its roots and utilize the space offered to it finds its limit.**

### Section 23. Deviations of the Results of Our Calculation from Reality.

Our investigation is founded on the assumptions:

1) that thinning is carried out continuously and proportionally to the increment;
2) that all trees are of equal health, vitality, and thickness;
3) that all trees stand in a square, everywhere have the normal spacing from one another, and that no gaps are found anywhere up to the final felling.

From these assumptions, significant deviations occur in reality, all of which act to reduce the timber yield.

Among these deviations, the following are likely the most significant:

1) When thinnings are not carried out continuously but periodically — as is always the case in reality — the trees can only at the midpoint of these periods have the normal spacing, and in the years before and after this midpoint, the increment is less than we have calculated.

2) In forests arising through seeding, one can through thinning ensure that the number of trees on a given area collectively receives the normal space, but one cannot provide every individual tree with the normal space. Some trees will stand more closely, others more widely than the normal spacing requires, and this leads to a lower total increment.

3) Accidents. Forests are exposed to windthrow, forest fire, caterpillar damage, damage by larger animals, hunting, etc.

4) Theft. Timber theft can hardly be entirely avoided anywhere.

From the timber yield that foresters cite based on findings in the forest itself, these losses have already been deducted, whereas from our yield calculated for the perfectly regular stand, they must still be subtracted. This partly explains why our calculated yield far exceeds the data of most foresters.

I assume that the reduction of timber yield arising from deviation from the ideal assumptions results in a reduction of the calculated revenue by one-third. Simultaneously, however, the interest on the capital invested in the timber stock also falls by one-third.

Furthermore, our calculation rests on the tacit assumption that all timber assortments — from fence-poles and bean-poles to beams — always find a market and can be sold every year. Such an extensive and secure market, however, is found only near large cities and along navigable canals and rivers.

I assume that the reduction of revenue arising from diminished timber value amounts to one-quarter of the sum calculated in Section 21.

From the revenue calculated in Section 21, therefore:
1) for reduction of timber yield, one-third or 33.33 percent is deducted; 66.67 percent remains.
2) for reduction of timber value, one-quarter or 25 percent of the remainder is deducted.
50 percent remains.

**Of every hundred of the yield calculated in the table of Section 21, fifty therefore remain.**

In all comparisons with reality, I shall henceforth bring into account only half of the revenue calculated in Section 21.

### Section 24. Comparison of the Rent of Forest Land with That of Arable Land.

For the 90-year rotation (x = 85), this gives an annual revenue of 290.8 shillings.

From the causes cited in the preceding paragraph, the revenue falls to half the calculated sum and thus amounts to 290.8/2 = 145.4 shillings, or 139.8 Thalers per 6,000 square rods.

After deducting administration and seeding costs of 10 shillings per forest Morgen or 9.6 Thalers per 6,000 square rods, this gives:

Ground rent per forest Morgen: 135.4 shillings
Per 100 square rods: 104.2 shillings
Per 6,000 square rods: 125.2 Thalers
Or: 130.2 Thalers.

**The rent of arable land** for soil that after a fallow yields 10 grains of rye (10 Berlin bushels per 100 square rods):

When this field has an average distance of 210 rods from the farmstead, then (according to Section 11 of Part 1 of "The Isolated State"), after deducting from the net yield of an estate the interest on capital contained in buildings and other objects separable from the land, 70,000 square rods of arable land yield a land rent of 954 Thalers.

This amounts to per Last of arable land of 6,000 square rods (ca. 50 Magdeburg Morgen): 81.8 Thalers.

Against this, we have found the ground rent that an area of 6,000 square rods yields through forestry = 130.2 Thalers.

**We thus arrive at the surprising and highly remarkable result that the excellent rye-soil of 10-grain yield, in high fertilization, delivers through cultivation with pines a rent 60 percent higher than through cultivation with grain.**

Far more striking still does this ratio become on soil of lesser fertility.

Assuming the timber yield on different soil types stands in direct proportion to the grain yield:

1) **On soil of 9-grain yield:** Timber revenue is nine-tenths of that on 10-grain soil = 125.8 Thalers per 6,000 square rods. After deducting costs: ground rent = 116.2 Thalers. The land rent from arable farming is 65.1 Thalers.

2) **Soil of 8-grain yield:** Ground rent from forestry = 102.2 Thalers. Land rent from farming = 48.3 Thalers.

3) **Soil of 7-grain yield:** Ground rent from forestry = 88.2 Thalers. Land rent from farming = 31.9 Thalers.

4) **Soil of 6-grain yield:** Ground rent from forestry = 74.2 Thalers. Land rent from farming = 15.7 Thalers.

**Summary.** An area of 6,000 square rods yields, depending on whether it is used for forestry with pines in a 90-year rotation, or for arable farming:

| Productivity of soil | Forestry: Forest rent (Thalers) | Forestry: Ground rent (Thalers) | Arable farming: Land rent (Thalers) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10 grains | 275.5 | 130.2 | 81.8 |
| 9 grains | 247 | 116.2 | 65.1 |
| 8 grains | 218.5 | 102.2 | 48.3 |
| 7 grains | 190 | 88.2 | 31.9 |
| 6 grains | 161.5 | 74.2 | 15.7 |

On the lower soil classes of 5, 4, 3 grains yield, the advantage that forestry delivers compared with arable farming becomes still far more significant. But perhaps on such soils, no construction timber can be produced in the first rotation, and then an entirely different calculation applies.

With the view prevailing in the local area, that only the soil worthless for arable farming can be profitably devoted to pine cultivation, and that on any better soil the pines should be cleared, the result of our calculation stands in the sharpest contradiction.

But such substantial deductions have been made in Section 23 for the deviations that occur in reality from our ideal assumptions, that in my view in most situations the deductions will prove smaller and the yield higher than calculated here.

Our investigation further shows that the concern that construction timber must continually rise in price with the gradual disappearance of primeval forests is entirely unfounded.

On the contrary, when the knowledge of rational forestry has spread and has been universally introduced, the price of 4 shillings N2/3 per cubic foot of pine construction timber will not be sustainable and must fall.

### Section 25. Applications.

**A. In relation to the conditions of Mecklenburg in general.**

When a city draws its timber supply partly from a region one mile away, partly from five miles away, and the timber from both is paid at equal prices, the value of the timber in the forest near the city is higher by the amount of the transport costs over 4 miles than in the distant forest. This difference is very significant and is — as demonstrated in Part 1 of "The Isolated State" — very much greater than the difference in the value of grain on the estate one mile and five miles from the city.

If the ground rent from forestry already exceeds that of arable land in the region five miles from the city, this must be the case to a far greater extent near the city.

Sensibly, therefore, the land near the city should also be more forested than in the more distant region.

Looking now at the larger cities of Mecklenburg — Rostock, Schwerin, and Wismar — we see that in reality precisely the opposite prevails. The closer we approach these cities, the more the forests disappear, and along the highway from Laage to Rostock one sees, with insignificant exceptions, only bare, treeless fields, while along the highway from Teterow to Laage, in the region 5-6 miles from Rostock, where timber has a far lower value, one sees oneself everywhere surrounded by forests.

**The larger cities thus appear here as timber destroyers, while they should naturally be the lever for expanded timber cultivation.**

What a contradiction between theory and practice!

The consequence, however, is that in Rostock, before the construction of the highways, a cord of beech firewood was paid at 14 to 16 Thalers N2/3.

Far worse, however, is the fact that along the Baltic coast of Mecklenburg, the need for pine or spruce construction timber is not produced in the country itself, but must for the most part be imported from Sweden, Norway, and Finland.

A respected merchant in Rostock, who himself conducted a significant timber trade, informed me upon inquiry about Mecklenburg's timber imports:

that he estimated the value of timber annually imported to Rostock from the Nordic ports at 50,000 Thalers N2/3, and that Wismar's timber imports would be of approximately equal amount.

In addition, a considerable quantity of boards is imported through Demmin into northern Mecklenburg.

Since there is land enough in northern Mecklenburg that can be utilized more highly through the cultivation of pines than through grain farming, the landowners can, once they have recognized their own interest and rational forestry, save the country in the future the payment of at least 100,000 Thalers now going abroad for construction timber.

Now it is true that not every saving in the export of precious metals is an increase in national income, as a trivial view supposes; on the contrary, the opposite can even occur, as the following example shows.

[The section gives the example of cattle imports from Angeln and Jutland — if Mecklenburg raised its own young cattle, it would need to reduce dairy and fattening operations, potentially losing 120,000 Thalers in butter and beef exports to save 100,000 in cattle imports — a net loss of 20,000 Thalers.]

Entirely different, however, is the case with the money spent on timber imports; for according to our calculation in the preceding paragraph, soil of 6-grain yield through forestry still yields over four times what the same land produced as arable rent, and the production of the timber for which 100,000 Thalers now leave the country therefore requires a sacrifice of at most 25,000 Thalers.

Through producing the timber requirement domestically, the national income is thus increased by at least 75,000 Thalers annually.

Considerable as this increase is, however, an even richer source for increasing national income presents itself in southern Mecklenburg.

In southern Mecklenburg, the sandy soil yielding little rent as arable land predominates, and through the making navigable of the Elde and the Havel, the market for timber to Berlin and Hamburg — two marketplaces whose oversupply is not at all to be feared — has been opened.

It is indeed a gratifying thought that Mecklenburg in the expansion and improvement of forestry still possesses such a wide field for increasing its national wealth.

**B. In relation to the special conditions of the estate Tellow.**

In Section 21 we found the ratio of ground rent between the 90-year and 35-year rotation to be 280.8 : 151 = 100 : 54.

The long rotation is thus far more advantageous than the short one.

Nevertheless, I have introduced on the estate Tellow a 30-year rotation.

So that this does not appear as a contradiction between knowledge and action, as a factual disapproval of the stated views, I must briefly mention the local conditions of the estate Tellow.

The pine forests, scattered in several locations, together amount to only 13,000 square rods (110 Magdeburg Morgen).

The soil of these forests is highly variable and diverse, consisting partly of sand, partly of good arable land interspersed with barren spots and clay hills containing loamy marl in the subsoil.

Initially the pines on all soil types, with the exception of the barren spots, grew vigorously. At the age of 30 years, however, the trees on the clay spots, which gradually became covered with wild raspberry (Rubus idaeus), began to sicken and died off one after another. In this way, highly unsightly gaps arose which gradually expanded, and I was thereby forced against my will to introduce a short rotation.

**Seventh Section.**

**Section 38.** The highest ground rent occurs according to our calculation at the 90-year rotation. But 90-year-old timber does not yet produce heavy beams or saw-logs. If we now examine what obstructs the longer rotation and makes it unprofitable, we find the cause in the strong growth of the interest on the standing capital.

But the standing capital is not an unchangeable quantity; we can substantially reduce it through greater spacing of the trees. With greater spacing, however, the increment simultaneously decreases. But the increment can be calculated for every spacing number according to Section 19, and we are thereby in a position to subject both the decrease in interest and that of the increment to calculation.

[The section then develops the calculation for giving trees 24-fold spacing after age 90, when form development is complete:]

For x = 85 (90-year rotation), with 12-fold spacing, the ground rent is 285 shillings.

**With 24-fold spacing after age 90, the ground rent rises to 681 shillings — more than doubling.**

For a rotation of 120 years with 24-fold spacing: ground rent = 810 shillings.
For a rotation of 140 years: ground rent = 866 shillings.

Setting the differential equal to zero: the most advantageous rotation = 176.5 years.

Since, however, heavier timber than the 140-year rotation produces is hardly demanded anywhere, and the value per cubic foot no longer increases with the larger diameter: the maximum ground rent is practically reached at a **rotation of 140 years**.

[Qualifications noted:] This calculation has abstracted from:
1) beyond age 90, the increment of individual stems and the whole forest probably gradually decreases at constant spacing;
2) conversely, with doubled spacing, the annual diameter increment exceeds one-sixth inch, and timber value per cubic foot rises more than calculated;
3) with very open spacing, a larger share of growth goes into branches and crown rather than shaft.

The ground rent rises extraordinarily when the form development of the trees is complete and stronger thinning is permitted, and the production costs of heavy timber consequently fall relative to thin timber.

As long as this method is not generally applied, the forest owner can secure an enormous profit through the production of heavy timber.

The stronger thinning must, however, proceed only very gradually, otherwise many trees may be toppled by storms.

The danger of windthrow does not apply to beeches. Nor does the form of trees matter for firewood production. While pines must be kept in closed stands for form development, beeches can be thinned from their earliest youth according to the principle developed here — which would have an enormous effect on the production price of beech timber.

**The ratio between diameter and height of the trees.**

Following the principle I pursue — of first taking individual factors for investigation and temporarily abstracting from other contributing factors — I have in the foregoing set the ratio of diameter to height as 1 : h and treated h as a constant.

In reality, however, this ratio changes: it becomes smaller as the tree grows larger.

According to Nagel, the following ratio prevails:

| Diameter | Height |
|---|---|
| 1/10 foot | 80 times |
| 2/10 foot | 75 times |
| 5/10 foot | 73 times |
| 1 foot | 71 times |
| 2 feet | 64 times |

Had we incorporated this varying ratio into the general formulas, these would have become very complex and the calculation extraordinarily difficult. But in the application of these formulas to specific cases, the effect of the changed length ratio on the timber yield and ground rent can be calculated and accounted for with ease.

Far more important for the correctness of our investigation, however, is the question:

Are the propositions presented as universally valid thereby also affected and changed?

Even with regular thinning, the lower branches, deprived of light, gradually die off, fall to the ground, and form what is called gathered wood. The yield of gathered wood is over time not insignificant, and according to Nagel's observation, it covers the loss arising from the decrease in relative height. Or in other words: the increment of the tree including the gathered wood it annually sheds is equal in every year — whether the tree is old or young — and amounts to as much as if the tree still had its youthful length ratio.

With this, the foundation of our investigation — according to which the increment both of the diameter of individual trees and of the whole forest proceeds in arithmetic proportion — remains in force.

If, however, the gathered wood is counted as part of the thinning yield, the ratio of thinning yield to final felling yield is indeed changed, and the former then amounts to more than double the latter. The value of the final felling stand and even the most advantageous rotation period also undergo a modification in numbers.

These modifications are, however, not of such significance as to overthrow in any way the developed laws according to which, with stronger thinning, ground rent rises and the most advantageous rotation period is extended.

With the greater and very open spacing of trees, clarification on the ratio of diameter to height is still very desirable in several points, e.g.:

1) In the completely free-standing tree, the ratio of height to diameter is far smaller than in trees in closed stands. Does this ratio also change continuously with increasing spacing number of trees in the forest, and according to what law?

2) Does the free-standing tree spread its roots as deeply in the subsoil in proportion to its diameter as the tree in the closed stand, or is the latter necessary to force the tree to penetrate into the depth?

3) Is not the ratio of length to diameter — so very different on different soils — principally connected with the nature of the subsoil?
