VII.

Some Questions Concerning the Statics of Agriculture, and Their Answers.

By an esteemed member of our association, the following six very comprehensive questions were raised. Two of our most insightful agriculturalists, Dr. von Thunen of Tellow and Mr. Pogge of Roggow bei Striesenow, have had the kindness to undertake the answering of these questions forthwith, as set out below.

Though one may boldly assert that these two answers are as thorough and comprehensive as possible, the content of the above questions is of such great interest, and the views, as is usual, can differ so widely here too, that one cannot urge strongly enough not only all the members of our patriotic association, but also every true agriculturalist to whom the perfection of his science lies close to heart, that several more gentlemen might undertake to answer the above questions.

The Editors.

Questions.

I. For the various crops which are as a rule cultivated in Mecklenburg -- such as wheat, rye, barley, oats, buckwheat, rapeseed, potatoes, large turnips, etc. -- is the production greater than the consumption? Or more clearly: through the production of the designated crops, if nothing is taken out of the estate but everything is used therein for the feeding and nourishment of people and livestock, is the consumption of manure that these crops have drawn from the field replaced?

II. In what proportion does the utilization of production within the estate, as described in question I, stand to the consumption of the above-designated crops? And to the diminished or increased fertility of the estate?

III. How would, in the case given in question I, expanded potato cultivation relate in terms of the manure consumption drawn from the field to its manure production, when used as livestock feed? Or, what greater or diminished productive capacity for the estate would result if annually one field, or the seventh part of the arable land, is manured and planted with potatoes for livestock feed and so utilized?

IV. In what proportion will the productive capacity of an estate be increased or diminished, which consists of medium-quality, varied soil of about 90 to 120 square rods per assessed bushel, is exhausted, thus partly bearing clover, and is managed in 7 fields with bare fallow, 3 grain crops, and 2 to 3 pasture fields, of which the last approximately half a pasture field is planted with potatoes, and which has only moderate meadows of about the size of one field, but otherwise possesses no means of soil improvement: if all grain crops which are not absolutely required for consumption within the estate are sold externally?

V. How would the productive capacity of the estate described in question IV be increased or diminished, if the same were managed in 7 fields in the following order, and of the entire production nothing at all were sold, but everything were consumed by people and fed to livestock within the estate? The utilization and crop rotation would be:

1st field, manured, potatoes;
2nd field, peas;
3rd field, rye;
4th field, barley, oats;
5th field, oats;
6th and 7th fields, pasture for cattle, with half stall-feeding, and sheep.

Finally:

VI. Which crop rotation and utilization would be the most advantageous for the estate described in question IV, of about 63,000 square rods of arable land and 8,000 square rods of meadows, and would annually increase the productive capacity of the estate, and by how many degrees, if the requirements for the keeping of about 10 luxury horses and about 10 domestic servants who contribute nothing to the field operations are taken into account alongside the needs of the proprietor's household, and the entire production, as noted in question I, is used within the estate for the nourishment of people and the feeding of livestock?

Further: What team of horses would be required, and how much livestock could be kept?

Answer I.

Relationship between the exhaustion and the replacement of the various grain types.

Based on my observations and experience to date, I adopt the following propositions on this matter:

a. When the cultivated grain -- excluding what is consumed within the farm itself -- is sold and thus withdrawn from manure production.

On good barley soil, which in Mecklenburg is assessed at approximately 100 square rods per bushel, the production of 8.2 Berlin bushels of rye costs the field 1 cartload of manure.

The cartload of manure here taken as the unit arises from the feeding of 870 pounds of dry fodder, which consists of approximately two-thirds hay and grain and one-third straw.

In terms of the exhaustion they cause:

100 bushels of wheat = 120 bushels of rye,
100 bushels of barley = 75 bushels of rye,
100 level bushels of oats = 50 bushels of rye,
100 bushels of peas = 90 bushels of rye.

If one designates the quantity of plant nourishment which is drawn from the soil by the production of 1 Berlin bushel of rye as "1 degree," then the cartload of manure on the soil here taken as the norm has the value of 3.2 degrees.

200 pounds of hay and 300 pounds of straw yield, through feeding to cattle, 1,150 pounds of manure. The factor for manure yield is thus 2.3. From 870 pounds of fodder, at 2.3, there result 2,000 pounds of manure.

The more nutritious the fodder is, the greater the portion that passes into the animal body, and it follows from this -- apart from the deceptive experiments that have been conducted on this subject -- that 100 pounds of hay yield less manure than 100 pounds of straw, and that grain feed in this respect again falls behind hay. But the manure from more nutritious fodder is decidedly of higher effectiveness. Since in statics it is not so much the quantity of manure that matters as the value it has for the reproduction of grain, clover, and so forth, we need a factor that indicates the value of the manure resulting from different feeding materials.

According to a law derived from empirical propositions, I assume that this factor amounts to:

for straw . . . . . 2.28
for hay . . . . . 2.4
for rye . . . . . 2.66
for wheat . . . . . 2.66
for barley . . . . . 2.87
for oats . . . . . 2.87
for peas . . . . . 3.07
for pea straw . . . . . 2.8

The case where all produced grain is fed to livestock does not occur in practice; for at a minimum the seed must be taken from the harvest. Also, the above calculation was undertaken only for the purpose of obtaining data to answer the following question:

"How does the wealth of the soil change when the grain that was previously sold is now entirely fed to livestock within the estate?"

In answering this question, the first thing to consider is what portion of the grain harvest was previously sold, and this in turn depends on the state of cultivation of the fields. For on soil yielding 8 to 10 grains (8 to 10 Berlin bushels of winter grain per 100 square rods), approximately half of the harvest suffices to cover the seed, the horse feed, the maintenance of the village people, the household, and so forth, so that half of the harvest is available for sale; whereas on an estate yielding only 4 grains, consumption takes away at least three-quarters of the harvest.

The question therefore cannot be answered in general terms, and I shall accordingly base the following calculations on a ratio where half of the total grain production was previously sold outside the estate.

Relationship of exhaustion and replacement for potatoes.

The field that possesses the productive capacity to deliver 10 Berlin bushels of rye per 100 square rods bears, with careful cultivation of potatoes, according to my observations, 110 bushels of fodder potatoes, or 60 bushels of fine eating potatoes.

I reckon the exhaustion of fodder potatoes at one-ninth of a degree per Berlin bushel; this makes for 110 bushels = 12.22 degrees, or (since 8.2 degrees = 1 cartload) = 3.82 cartloads of manure.

Of the harvest of . . . . . 110 bushels
deduct: seed . . . . . 10 bushels
excess through drying out and sifted-out earth, approx. . . . . . 5 bushels
remaining for manure production . . . . . 95 bushels

I take the factor for the value of manure at 0.9 degrees; a bushel of potatoes of 100 pounds thus yields 90 pounds of manure. 95 bushels at 90 pounds accordingly yield 8,550 pounds of manure, or . . . 4.27 cartloads.

The manure consumption amounts to: 3.82 cartloads
The surplus thus amounts to: 0.45 cartloads.

This makes for 1,000 square rods planted with potatoes a surplus of 4.5 cartloads.

Enrichment of the soil through lying fallow as pasture.

On this subject I adopt the following propositions:

1) The exhaustion caused by grass and clover production is replaced by the roots and stubble of the grass and clover plants that remain in the soil upon plowing up the pasture and decompose. The quantity of manure falling on the pasture is therefore the measure of the enrichment of the soil through pasture.

2) The productivity of the pasture decreases with each year, and on soil yielding 10 grains, which in the seven-field system has borne 3 grain crops, I reckon for one cow that grazes for 140 days on the fallow pasture and daily consumes 18 pounds of grass (reduced to hay equivalent), the pasture requirement in the 1st pasture year as 220 square rods.

3) Of the manure that falls on the pasture and lies on the surface, a considerable portion is lost over time through volatilization and is delivered to the atmosphere in gaseous form. For the manure yield it is therefore of great importance to apply the greatest possible portion of pasture manure directly to the fertilization of the arable land, which can be accomplished through hurdle folding, nightly housing of the livestock, and so forth.

Of the manure that falls on the pasture in the first fallow year, a greater portion is lost through volatilization the longer it remains exposed to the air -- that is, the later the fallow is plowed up. Diminished productivity of the pasture and increased volatilization of manure thus work together to cause the manure production of the fallow pasture to decrease with each year.

If half of the pasture manure is gained for the manure yard or for direct fertilization of the arable land through hurdle folding, nightly housing of the livestock, and so forth, then according to my calculation, the manure production amounts to:

of the 1st pasture year . . . 13.74 cartloads
of the 2nd . . . 10.96 cartloads
of the 3rd . . . 7.02 cartloads

Total . . . 31.72 cartloads.
Average . . . 10.57 cartloads.

The manure production of the fallow bare period through the grasses and weeds that grow up in it, which are partly grazed off and partly plowed under, I estimate as equal to one-third of the manure production of the preceding pasture year.

Relative exhaustion.

The ratio between what the harvest has drawn from the field in plant nourishment in one year, and the total wealth of the field, I call the relative exhaustion, which is recognized by the decline in yield of successive sowings made under identical conditions. The magnitude of the relative exhaustion depends on the physical properties of the soil and probably also on the greater or lesser wealth of the soil.

For the following calculations I take as my basis a soil whose relative exhaustion for rye is one-sixth, and whose yield after bare fallow is 10 Berlin bushels of rye per 100 square rods. If, then, the harvest of 10 bushels of rye has cost the field one-sixth of its content of plant nourishment, then in 100 square rods there is nourishment for 6 times 10 = 60 bushels of rye, or, in other words, the wealth of this soil is, in 100 square rods, = 60 degrees, and in 1,000 square rods therefore = 600 degrees.

Question: "How will the productive capacity of the estate be increased or diminished, if instead of the ordinary seven-field system it is managed in the following order, and of the entire production nothing at all is sold, but everything is consumed by people and fed to livestock within the estate?"

Crop rotation:

1st field, potatoes, manured;
2nd field, barley;
3rd field, peas, manured;
4th field, rye;
5th field, oats;
6th field, pasture;
7th field, pasture.

As already noted above, in the ordinary seven-field system, with a yield of 10 grains, approximately half of the grain production is used for seed and for the nourishment of people and livestock within the estate itself, while the other half is sold.

Since a comparison between this system and one where all grain is fed to livestock is to be made here, only the half of the grain production which was previously sold but is now fed to livestock may be counted as a supplement to the manure yield in the static calculation.

In the former system, rye comes after bare fallow; in the latter, after peas. Now it is well known that, under ordinary conditions, with equal wealth of the soil, rye after bare fallow gives a higher yield than after peas; or, in other words, that the preceding crop diminishes the activity of the soil. In the following calculation, I assume this diminution of activity to be one-tenth, so that where 10 bushels of rye would grow after bare fallow, only 9 bushels grow after peas. On the other hand, I assume the activity of the soil for barley after potatoes to be 5 percent higher than after rye.

Manure supplement from the meadows.

One cartload of hay of 1,800 pounds yields through feeding 1,800 times 2.4 = 4,320 pounds, or 2.16 cartloads of manure. Since 1 cartload of manure = 3.2 degrees of wealth, the feeding of one cartload of hay yields 2.16 times 3.2 = 6.9 degrees of wealth.

If now such a hay yield is associated with the arable land that for every 1,000 square rods of arable land one cartload of meadow hay is available, and the arable land receives all the manure arising therefrom, then the manure supplement for 7,000 square rods of arable land amounts to 7 times 6.9 = 48.3 degrees.

The wealth of the field at the end of the rotation was: 649.4 degrees.
Add the manure supplement from the meadows: 48.3 degrees.
Total: 697.7 degrees.

With respect to the yield to be expected in the second rotation, it must be noted that with rising wealth of the soil, the grain yield -- even if lodging of the grain set no limit -- most probably does not increase in direct proportion to the wealth.

If one compares on two fields of identical soil but unequal wealth the yield of the third grain sowing with the first sowing after manuring, one will, in my observation, always find that the proportional decrease in yield is greater on the poorer soil than on the rich. From this it follows that the relative exhaustion on the poorer soil is greater than on the rich, or that plants appropriate a proportionally greater share of the plant nourishment present in the soil, the less of it there is.

As a hypothesis, which however still requires multifaceted testing, I would like to put forward the proposition: "that on two fields of identical soil but unequal wealth, the yield is proportional to the square root of the wealth."

According to this, the yield of soil with 700 degrees of wealth would be -- apart from lodging of the grain -- 10.8 grains; for the ratio is as the square root of 600 to the square root of 700, that is, as 10 to 10.8. The relative exhaustion would then amount to 15.1 percent.

At the moment, however, when the transition from farming system A to farming system B takes place, the wealth of the soil is obviously entirely identical in both systems. Therefore, for every 1,000 square rods, 9 cartloads of manure must be purchased; or the yield declines by about 6 percent compared to the yield calculated in the tableau.

That the grain yield under these conditions must decline is evident even without any static calculation from the fact that the same stock of manure, which was previously applied to the manuring of one field, is now distributed over two fields.

This decline in grain yield occurs to an even far greater extent when a seven-field enclosed-field system transitions to a crop-alternation system with the following succession of crops: 1. Potatoes, manured; 2. Barley; 3. Clover; 4. Rye; 5. Green vetches, manured; 6. Rye -- and amounts here to approximately 12 percent.

The failure to heed this circumstance is the reef on which so many prematurely introduced crop-alternation systems have foundered.

If in the chosen crop rotation the ratio between exhausting and enriching crops was such that the system remained in a steady state with respect to soil wealth, one continually cultivated poorer grain than before; if the system was an enriching one, one arrived only late and at great cost back at the former grain yield.

Since a large part of the costs associated with grain production is proportional to the size of the area -- for example, seed, plowing, harrowing -- and is equally high for poor and large harvests, only from large harvests can a significant net return emerge. One should therefore never purchase greater soil wealth through inferior grain types and the resulting reduced exhaustion; rather, one should enrich the soil through reduction of the grain sowing area to such an extent that one can transition to a crop-alternation system without a setback in grain yield.

In the present case this can be easily accomplished. One need only, at the initial transition, let the 5th field bear clover instead of oats; then, with the increased manure yield, plant part of the field with oats, gradually enlarge this portion, and then, once all fields have attained the normal level of wealth, let the whole field bear oats.

Given the uncommonly rapidly rising soil wealth under these conditions, not many years will be required for this.

Against the introduction of this system, however, another difficulty presents itself. The potatoes namely come into the freshly plowed fallow pasture, and here a couch-grass infestation of the field is very much to be feared. At a minimum, two careful harrowings of the fallow in the preceding autumn are required, and on soil that is not favorable to couch-grass growth, the field may thereby be kept clean in dry years; but in wet years, I fear this will not suffice.

The yield stated in the static tableau for this system presupposes a complete freedom of the field from couch grass; on couch-grass-infested fields, the yield sinks to a point that lies beyond calculation.

Among the crops known to me, whose cultivation on a large scale is feasible, none so completely replaces the fallow period with respect to keeping the field clean as buckwheat. This is, however, only the case when, upon plowing up the fallow in autumn, no unplowed strips have been left standing, and the field is carefully harrowed and cultivated three more times in spring. Thus, for example, this year here, with three-furrow plowing and with the vegetation of the buckwheat interrupted in spring by night frosts, several couch-grass-infested patches appeared; which would surely have been avoided if the field, as in earlier years, had received two furrows instead of three. According to my experience of the past two years, buckwheat sown on wheat soil tolerates a manuring of 8 cartloads per 100 square rods without growing too rank and without losing in grain set. Up to now, buckwheat has shown here the remarkable property that it greatly diminishes the activity of the soil, although it leaves the field in a loosened, couch-grass-free condition. Wheat after buckwheat that had been manured with 8 cartloads per 100 square rods remained standing, while after bare fallow manured with 5 cartloads per 100 square rods, the wheat almost entirely lodged. Now the fallow receives only 4 cartloads, but buckwheat continues to receive 8 cartloads of manure per 100 square rods.

Also, buckwheat straw, when brought in dry and fully desiccated, has proven here to be an excellent, very nutritious sheep feed.

If these experiences are confirmed over a longer period and on more estates, then for those estates where there is an excess of manure and the wheat frequently lodges, the means has been found in buckwheat cultivation to make a portion of the hitherto entirely barren fallow productive and at the same time to prevent the lodging of wheat -- which one now frequently seeks to achieve by the tempting but most pernicious means of debilitating the field through many grain sowings, and which one will undoubtedly achieve.

On poor soil, however, where even after bare fallow no luxuriant grain grows, and where the diminished activity of the soil cannot be offset by stronger manuring, the planting of fallow with buckwheat will only deliver inferior winter grain and diminish the net return of the estate.

Tellow, in September 1835.
J. H. von Thunen.

Answer II.

Re I. With judicious management of an estate in all its parts, through the consumption of its products on the estate and through the return of the residues to field and meadow in the form of manure, the substance which the estate had to give up for the production of those products may not only be preserved, but there will probably also be an increase with respect to the fertility of the soil.

Re II. In what proportion the fertility of the soil can be increased solely through this consumption, without extraordinary supplements, I lack the experience to say.

Re III. The potato, among all field crops known to me, brings the highest possible mass of nutritive substance from a given area, and deserves an expanded cultivation; yet I hardly believe that its residues in the form of foliage and manure mass, without the addition of other substances, will replace what it has drawn from the soil. However, through its expanded cultivation, the cultural condition of the estate will be indirectly elevated.

Re IV. The more grain and other products are sold from the estate, the more the fertility substance of the estate will be diminished. But how much a bushel of grain, used as livestock feed, returns to the estate in degrees of fertility -- on this too I lack the experience, and I am not in a position to state in numbers or degrees the effect on fertility substance of a system that sells a great deal.

Re V. Only exceptional measures will be capable of successfully carrying out the stated crop rotation; for it costs much labor to manure the 7th or last pasture field from autumn until May of the following year and to prepare it sufficiently for potatoes. Also, I consider peas too uncertain a crop to allot them a seventh of the entire field. If, however, one succeeds in cultivating the field well according to that order, it will probably deliver a significant yield of products, and through their feeding to livestock will increase the wealth of the arable land.

Re VI. It is a difficult problem to find the most advantageous crop rotation for an estate whose characteristics are fully known; still more difficult, however, for one whose soil, land, and other conditions are not precisely known.

Furthermore, it is to be assumed that the proposed crop rotation has resulted from careful deliberation, and I therefore content myself with adding the following in reference to it.

If the soil of the said estate is rich enough that it bears potatoes throughout with success, and if the working forces are proportionate, then the potato field will provide much livestock feed, and with the operation of a potato distillery will also deliver more return.

To the peas in the second field I would for the most part add an admixture of oats, because a mixture of this kind on average produces good feed in grain, and as a preceding crop before rye is likewise not to be rejected.

Fields 3, 4, and 5 bear grain crops in immediate succession. Whether here a partial sowing of red clover for one or two mowings in a single year, which could then be well prepared for spring grain in autumn and spring, might not be worth considering -- this the experienced proponent of the crop rotation will best judge.

How now this crop rotation, modified or unmodified, affects the enrichment of the soil -- to determine this lies beyond my powers.

As working animals I would recommend horses, and 10 to 12 head might suffice. Solely for the needs of the farm, cows should be kept, 20 to 30 head; in addition, 300 to 400 sheep, and for both types of animal half stall-feeding would be advisable.

It is assumed that gypsum is applied annually to peas (mixed grain), clover, and pasture, before the end of April at 1 pound per square rod, and one can then count all the more certainly on an increase in the fertility substance of the soil. Greater wealth and increased fertility of the soil will, however, perhaps change the crop rotation again and permit the cultivation of saleable, profitable products such as rapeseed, and so forth.

Striesenow, in August 1835.
