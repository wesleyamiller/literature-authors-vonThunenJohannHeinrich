## Introduction

Overview and Critique of the Method Applied in the First Part of This Work, Together with the Plan of This Second Part.

Adam Smith was my teacher in political economy, Thaer in scientific agriculture.

They are the founders of two sciences, and many of their doctrines will forever form unassailable foundations of science.

What appears to us as indubitable in the writings or oral lectures of eminent men, we absorb and make our own, and it thereby ceases to be a subject of our own inquiry.

But science is never a completed thing, and often an advance within it serves to reveal new, previously unsuspected problems.

What in the doctrines of both great men appeared to me as incomplete, what did not satisfy my need for clear insight and thereby drove me to independent research, can, if not exhaustively, then at least in overview, be compressed into the following questions:

1) How must agriculture change with the alteration of grain prices, given consistent management?

2) By what laws is the price of grain and of timber regulated?

3) Does the higher farming system, and in particular convertible husbandry, possess an absolute advantage over the rotation and three-field system, or is the advantage of one farming system over another conditioned by the level of the price of agricultural products?

4) From what cause does land rent originate, and by what law is its level determined?

5) What is the ultimate effect of taxes levied on agriculture?

6) What is the natural wage of labor, or what is the share of his product that nature has determined for the worker?

7) By what law is the level of the interest rate determined, and what connection exists between the interest rate and wages?

8) How does the size of the money stock affect the interest rate and the price of goods?

9) What influence do significant improvements in agriculture and the invention of new machines for factories exert when they first appear, and what is their ultimate effect?

Already in my early youth, when I learned farming near Hamburg at the institute of Herr Staudinger in Flottbeck, I conceived the first idea of the isolated state, and since then I have constantly felt driven to subject the agricultural and political-economic problems that presented themselves to me to the mode of inquiry that underlies the isolated state, since only in freeing the subject from everything accidental and inessential did the hope of solving the problem appear to me.

At the beginning of my career as a practical farmer, I then sought through precise and detailed bookkeeping to obtain the data for calculating the costs and net yield of agriculture under different grain yields and different grain prices. After these data were compiled from a five-year accounting and combined into an overview, the investigations communicated in the first part were begun on this foundation.

Since it is now our purpose here to subject the method applied in these investigations to examination and critique, I permit myself to bring the course of the investigation and some of the results thereby obtained back to the reader's memory.

The calculations contained in the first part, based on the conditions of the estate Tellow, show (Sections 5 and 6) that on soil yielding 8 grains of rye after fallow, the land rent of the convertible system vanishes or equals zero when the value of the Berlin bushel of rye sinks to 0.549 thalers in gold -- and with the vanishing of land rent, cultivation of the soil also ceases.

Through a change in the form of farming, however, savings in operating costs can be made, and the soil can then still be cultivated even when the value of rye falls below 0.549 thalers per bushel, and can even still yield some land rent. Through this change in the form of farming directed toward cost savings, a farming system arises that coincides with pure three-field husbandry.

Here, then, the result emerges that when grain prices fall, there is a point where three-field husbandry becomes more advantageous than convertible husbandry.

But the land rent of three-field husbandry must also eventually vanish if grain prices continue to decline ever further, and this is the case, according to Section 14a, when the bushel of rye reaches a value of 0.470 thalers in gold -- and here, then, the cultivation of the soil for the purpose of selling grain must end.

If we consider, on the other hand, the effect of rising grain prices, we encounter a point where the soil becomes too valuable and too profitable to continue working a portion of it unused as fallow. With the abolition of fallow, convertible husbandry transitions to crop-rotation husbandry, and the latter here yields a higher land rent than the former.

When one subtracts the amount of transportation costs from the price that grain commands in the city to which it is delivered, the result is the value that the grain has on the estate itself. With greater distance from the marketplace, transportation costs rise, and the value of the grain on the estate itself decreases. Increasing distance from the marketplace thus operates like a decline in grain prices at a constant distance.

The influence that the level of grain prices exerts on agriculture can therefore also be represented spatially, and from this representation in space the isolated state has emerged.

Through this conception of the subject, another task is combined with the original one: How must the form of farming change with greater or lesser distance from the commercial city, if the soil is to yield the highest net return?

From experience, the laws that prevail here cannot be directly ascertained, for in reality we are everywhere confronted by unequal soil, unequal wealth of the same, the influence of navigable rivers, etc., and in the farms that we observe at various distances from the great commercial cities, the influence of all these factors is expressed jointly -- assuming consistent management.

To free the effectiveness of one factor -- distance from the marketplace -- from the conflict with the effectiveness of the other factors, and thereby bring it to recognition, we had to assume a large city without a navigable river in a plain of entirely uniform and equally fertile soil.

This mental operation is analogous to the procedure that we apply in all experiments in physics as well as in agriculture, where we increase quantitatively only the one factor to be investigated, while leaving all other elements unchanged.

Under these assumptions, regular concentric circles form in the plain of the isolated state around the city, as demonstrated in the first part, in which free farming, forestry, crop-rotation, convertible, and three-field husbandry are practiced in descending order.

With unlimitedly increasing distance from the city, a point must necessarily be found where the production and transportation costs of the grain equal the price paid for it in the city, and here is the point where land rent vanishes and cultivation of the soil, insofar as it is based on selling grain to the city, ends.

From this follows the law determining the price of grain, expressed in Section 24.

From the advantage that estates closer to the city have over estates at the border of the cultivated plain, land rent arises; and the magnitude of this advantage determines, according to Section 25, the amount of land rent.

Beyond the border where cultivation of the soil for the purpose of selling grain to the city ceases, the zone of animal husbandry forms, which can still be practiced here with some profit because the transportation costs of animal products such as butter, fattened cattle, wool, etc., are incomparably lower in proportion to their value than those of grain.

Beyond the zone of animal husbandry, the plain then passes into an uninhabited wilderness, by which the isolated state is separated from the rest of the world. But we assume that the soil of this wilderness itself is of the same character and the same natural fertility as that of the rest of the plain -- and the obstacle to the spread of cultivation to these regions lies therefore not in the character of the soil, but solely in the great distance from the marketplace for agricultural products.

The expansion of the zone of animal husbandry thus finds its limit only in the fact that the price of animal products in the city covers only the production and transportation costs for the most distant producer.

With increasing distance from the city, the production costs of animal products diminish -- because land rent and the grain price decrease -- while the transportation costs increase. Since, as demonstrated in Section 26, production costs decrease more sharply with increasing distance from the city than transportation costs increase, and since the land rent of the most distant estate in the zone of animal husbandry equals zero, it follows (Section 26b) the important law that in the regions closer to the city (with the exception of the zone of free farming), the land rent from animal husbandry must be negative.

The ultimate effect of a newly introduced tax manifests itself (Section 3) in the abandonment of the outer edge of the plain, the restriction of soil cultivation to a narrower circle around the city, and the reduction in the number of inhabitants of the state.

This is, in brief overview, the course and result of the investigations of the first part.

The results there were not found through reasoning, but derived from a formula for the costs and yield of farming, for which the data were taken from reality, by subjecting one factor -- the grain price -- to successive alteration.

This method can, if the experiences are precisely and correctly apprehended and the conclusions built upon them are consistent, transfer mathematical certainty to a domain in which, with mere reasoning, the most contradictory views assert themselves.

But the greater the achievements of this method can be, and the more the results of the same lay claim to certainty, the sharper must also be the examination and critique of the same.

III.

Abstraction from reality, without which we cannot attain any scientific knowledge, presents the twofold danger that we

1) separate in thought what exerts a reciprocal interaction upon one another, and

2) lay assumptions at the foundation of our conclusions of which we are not clearly aware, are therefore unable to articulate them, and then hold to be universally valid what is valid only under these assumptions.

The history of political economy provides many striking examples of this.

Among the assumptions either stated or tacitly adopted in the first volume, the following two require special examination and illumination.

1) The soil in the plain of the isolated state is not merely originally of equal fertility, but in the course of cultivation (with the exception of the first zone), the richness of the soil in plant nutrients remains equal in all regions of the isolated state, however different the grain prices there may be.

2) The care in tilling the fields, in harvesting the crops, in clean threshing, etc., remains everywhere equal, whether the bushel of rye is worth 1.5 or 0.75 thalers. Now, we have had to place the consistency of management as the highest and indispensable requirement at the forefront and subordinate everything else to it.

The question thus naturally arises: "Are those two assumptions compatible with consistent management?"

I must answer: "No."

The reasons for this answer will be developed in more detail further on.

From this side, the first part, which provides no justification on this point, could and should have been attacked -- if the book had received a critique that entered into its spirit.

But does not the entire edifice of the isolated state collapse with the recognition of this deficiency in the foundation? To discuss this question, let us cite and consider an analogous case.

Suppose one could purchase fertile arable soil at a given price and have it delivered, and it were at our discretion to increase the topsoil to any desired depth: we would then set ourselves the task of determining at what depth of topsoil we would, after deducting the interest on the purchase price of the soil, obtain the highest net return from the land.

To achieve clarity on this, one would first conduct experiments to investigate how and in what proportion the yield of crops increases with increasing depth of topsoil. In such an experiment, one would undoubtedly sow all plots with different depths of topsoil equally -- because otherwise one would mix two heterogeneous subjects with each other and obtain a pure answer about neither through the experiment. Nevertheless, the rate of seeding is here a co-operating factor; for it is very probable that the 10-inch topsoil requires a different seeding rate than the 4-inch, if both are to give the highest yield of crops.

One would therefore conduct a second experiment, divide the plots with different topsoil depths into several sections and sow these at different rates, in order to determine which seeding rate is most appropriate for each topsoil depth and yields the highest crop output.

Similarly, one would have to make the magnitude of the influence of the other co-operating factors -- such as the change in soil quality with altered topsoil depth, the greater costs of plowing associated with deeper topsoil, etc. -- individually and separately from all others the subject of experiments and observations, in order to fully solve that problem.

Should the procedure that we recognize as thoroughly correct in the physical world be impermissible in the world of thought; should we not be allowed here too, of two cooperating factors, first to consider one as acting alone, and then the other in the same way as solely effective?

Certainly, through analogies the correctness of this procedure can be elevated to the level of probability; but it would be difficult by this path to provide a strict proof that admits no opposing views.

But here everything depends on absolute correctness.

Fortunately, we find the proof for this in the science that does not deceive -- in mathematics.

In the differential calculus, namely, when one seeks the maximum value of a function containing several variable quantities, in the differentiation first only one quantity is treated as variable while the others are regarded as constant, and after the value found for this quantity -- by setting its differential equal to zero -- has been substituted into the function, the second variable quantity is subjected to differentiation, the resulting value is substituted, and so forth, until all variable quantities have disappeared from the function.

Now, if the demonstrably correct procedure of the mathematicians is also to have probative force for the correctness of our method, it must be shown that we, like them, strive to find a maximum and make it the subject of our investigation.

In agriculture, we possess through increased care in tilling the field, in harvesting the crops, etc., through the purchase of manure, gypsum, bone meal, guano, etc., through the application of marl and humus, through the addition of a type of soil the field lacks, etc., a multitude of means to increase not merely the momentary but also the permanent yield of the field.

But when these improvements are purchased at a cost exceeding the value of the additional yield thereby obtained, they lead not only to the ruin of the farmer who undertakes them, but also diminish the national wealth.

Not the highest gross yield but the highest net yield is and should be the goal of the farmer.

If we now ask where the limit is to which the care of labor and the enrichment of the soil may be driven, the answer is:

1) The care of labor, e.g., in picking up potatoes, must not be extended further than until the labor last expended upon it is still compensated by the additional yield.

2) The enrichment of the soil must consistently be driven to the point, but must also stop there, where the interest on the cost of purchasing manure, or alternatively of producing it, comes into equilibrium with the additional yield thereby obtained.

Always the additional yield obtained in this way is purchased through an expenditure of capital and labor, and there must be a point where the value of the additional yield equals the additional expenditure -- and this is at the same time the point at which the maximum of net yield occurs.

The procedure that we apply in our investigations, where the determination of the highest net yield is the goal, thus accords with the method proven correct in mathematics for determining the maximum value of a function with several variable quantities, and just as the mathematician first treats only one of the variable quantities contained in a function as variable while regarding the other as constant, so too may we, of the various factors affecting net yield and connected with the grain price, first regard one as acting alone while treating the other as remaining constant or at rest.

With this, the admissibility and correctness of the method applied in the first part is demonstrated.

But in the first part, the question "What influence does the level of grain prices exert on agriculture?" has been investigated only partially, only from some sides. The influence of grain prices extends, however, to many other subjects, of which we shall here mention only those on soil richness and on the care of labor -- and thus the first part is only the beginning of the work toward the complete solution of the problem.

For better understanding and more accurate appreciation of the first part, I already offer here a preliminary consideration of the influence of the grain price on the two factors: soil richness and care of labor.

But further on, these points shall become the subject of their own investigation.

IV.

A. Under the conditions of the isolated state, where, through the influence of the extensive zone practicing only animal husbandry, the prices of animal products are very low, the abolition of fallow and the introduction of crop-rotation husbandry can, as demonstrated in the first part, become advantageous only when the soil has attained a degree of richness at which the grain after pure fallow lodges. The isolated state, however, is based on the assumption of equal soil richness throughout the entire plain, specifically an assumed yield capacity of 8 grains (9.44 Berlin bushels per Prussian morgen) after pure fallow.

At this yield, however, no lodging of the grain occurs.

With consistent reasoning, crop-rotation husbandry should therefore actually have been excluded from the first part of the isolated state.

If we now cast a glance at reality regarding the connection between grain price and soil richness, we find as a rule that in countries with dense population and high grain prices, a higher soil richness exists than in sparsely populated countries with low grain prices. The question is thus already practically resolved, and it is remarkable that what the sound sense of practical farmers has long practiced has not yet been systematically conceived and presented by science.

If we now, in place of the lacking scientific proof, regard the experience that soil enrichment follows the increase of grain prices as based on rational grounds, and apply this principle to the isolated state, the configuration of the latter is thereby substantially modified. Instead of equal richness throughout the entire plain, we then see soil richness steadily increasing from the border toward the city, and it is possible, even probable, that at a certain distance from the city it becomes advantageous to enrich the soil beyond the point where lodging of grain after fallow begins. With this, crop-rotation husbandry would then actually take the place that in the first part was indeed intuitively suggested but was regarded as irreconcilable with the assumed conditions.

Here, then, we encounter a significant deviation from the result of the first part, and it could appear as though the method of considering only one factor at a time had here led us astray.

But without the assumption of equal soil richness, the investigation of how distance from the city acts in itself, i.e., without the influence of other factors, could not have been conducted at all, and would have been confusing rather than illuminating.

The inaccuracy arises not from the method, but from the fact that the investigation in the first part is not yet finished, and only one side of the problem has been solved.

As in a function containing several variable quantities, the value of the function itself remains indeterminate even after finding and substituting the value of one quantity, and receives determinacy only when all variable quantities have been removed -- so too here.

For the actual solution of the problem, it is necessary that, after the first investigation of the influence of distance in itself is completed, a second investigation of the influence of distance on the appropriate soil richness be begun and carried through; from the combination of both investigations, a more complete -- though not yet final -- result emerges.

In fact, the materials for this work are for the most part already contained in the first part. For the formulas for calculating net yield are valid not merely for a given grain yield, but for all stages of yield up to 10 grains, and thus also for the soil richness corresponding to these yields. Also, for the border where convertible and three-field husbandry separate, a formula has been found that is valid for all stages of yield. Only for the soil richness corresponding to a higher yield than 10 grains are the calculations and formulas still to be developed.

If now the law according to which grain price and soil richness are connected with each other were found, one could easily indicate from the already available materials the soil richness, yield, and land rent for every distance from the city, complete the picture of the isolated state, and thereby bring it closer to reality -- where the combined influence of all factors confronts us.

But the mere empirical knowledge, derived from observation, that high soil richness is generally associated with high grain prices, is not sufficient for such a task. Rather, the necessity of this phenomenon must be demonstrated and the law governing the interaction between grain price and soil richness must be found before this part of our task can be investigated and treated with the same sharpness and precision as the first.

B. If on an estate where until now all work was performed by 20 day-laborer families, one more family is employed and the draft animals are proportionally increased: then the harvest and sowing can partly be accomplished in a shorter, and thus more timely, period; partly the work at harvest and sowing can be done more carefully; the grain can be threshed more cleanly; the potatoes can be harvested more thoroughly, etc.

The increase of worker families must consistently be continued until the additional yield obtained through the last worker employed equals in value the wage that the worker receives.

The additional yield is expressed in grain and remains the same for one and the same farming system regardless of the grain price. The money wage of the worker, however, rises and falls, even when the real wage remains the same, not in direct proportion with the grain price; rather, a portion of it -- as discussed in detail in the first part -- is not affected by the grain price and must therefore remain expressed in money.

Suppose, then, the costs of a worker's family amount to 60 bushels of rye plus 30 thalers annually; the additional yield obtained through the last family employed amounts to 100 bushels of rye: then the landowner retains a profit of 40 bushels minus 30 thalers. At the price of rye of 1.5 thalers per bushel, this profit amounts to 60 - 30 = 30 thalers.

At the price of 1 thaler: 40 - 30 = 10 thalers.
At the price of 3/4 thaler: 30 - 30 = 0.
And at the price of 1/4 thaler per bushel, the profit converts into a loss of 10 thalers.

It follows from this that at the grain price of 1.5 thalers, more than 20 workers can still be profitably employed, while at the price of 1/2 thaler, the twentieth worker already brings a loss.

Now it lies in the nature of agriculture -- and this is a very noteworthy circumstance -- that the additional product does not rise in direct proportion with the number of additional workers employed; rather, each later-employed worker produces a smaller output than the preceding one -- the 22nd worker less than the 21st, the 23rd less than the 22nd, etc.

As an example, I set up the following scale:

The 21st worker produces ...... 100 bushels
The 22nd worker ............... 90 bushels
The 23rd worker ............... 81 bushels
The 24th worker ............... 73 bushels
The 20th worker ............... 111.25 bushels
The 19th worker ............... 123 bushels

According to this scale, at the price of 1.5 thalers per bushel:

The 22nd worker produces 90 bushels, costs 60 bushels + 30 thalers; surplus 30 bushels at 1.5 thalers = 45 - 30 = 15 thalers.

The 23rd worker produces 81 bushels, costs 60 bushels + 30 thalers; surplus 21 bushels at 1.5 - 30 = 1.5 thalers.

The 24th worker produces 73 bushels; after deducting wages, 13 bushels remain at 1.25 thalers - 30 = -10.25 thalers.

At the price of 1.25 thalers per bushel, the employment of the 22nd worker still brings profit; with the hiring of the 23rd worker, benefit and cost are balanced; while the employment of a 24th worker is associated with loss.

At the price of 0.5 thalers, the 20th worker produces 111 bushels. After deducting wages, 51 bushels remain minus 30 thalers. The 51 bushels have a value of 25.5 thalers. The 20th worker thus brings a loss of 4.5 thalers. The 19th worker produces 123 bushels, of which after deducting wages 63 bushels remain at 0.5 thalers = 31.5 - 30 = 1.5 thalers.

At the price of 1.5 thalers per bushel, it is therefore advantageous to increase the workers from 20 to 23, while at the price of 0.5 thalers, the 20th worker must be let go in order to obtain the highest net yield.

Let us now compare two estates of the isolated state, of which one lies at the border -- where the bushel of rye has a value of about 0.5 thalers -- and the other lies near the city -- with a rye price of 1.25 thalers -- and assume that both have not only equal soil richness but are also subject to the same farming system: then, solely because of the greater care of labor, the grain yield of the latter estate would be greater by the amount produced by the 20th, 21st, 22nd, and 23rd workers than the yield of the former estate -- which according to the scale amounts to 382 bushels.

What change does the consideration of this factor bring about in the configuration of the isolated state presented in the first part?

Suppose the grain yield of soil of equal richness amounts to 8.25 grains near the city and only 7.5 grains at the border of the state.

Since this difference in grain yield arises from consistent management, and the farmer at the border prefers to harvest only 7.5 grains from soil capable of producing 8.25 grains, it follows that the production costs of grain are lower when only 7.5 grains rather than 8 grains -- the normal yield of the plain -- are obtained through increased labor expenditure. Since the extent of cultivation of the plain is conditioned by the level of production costs, the number of miles at which cultivation of the plain ceases will, when this factor is taken into account, become somewhat larger than calculated in the first part. Also, the border between convertible and three-field husbandry may be somewhat, though not significantly, shifted. But here the number of miles is not what matters, since it does not touch the essence of the investigation but serves only to make the idea more tangible. The influence of this factor is only quantitative, not qualitative, and can therefore be disregarded in the construction of the isolated state. In another respect, however -- as will emerge below -- attention to this factor is of great importance.

Here let me add an explanation concerning a result of the isolated state that, at the time the book first appeared in 1826, seemingly formed a glaring contradiction with what existed in reality.

The calculations in the first part showed that when grain prices fall to a certain point, the transition from convertible to three-field husbandry becomes advantageous and increases land rent.

Now, in the period from 1820 to 1826, grain prices in northern Germany had fallen almost to the point where, according to the isolated state, three-field husbandry becomes more advantageous than convertible husbandry. But the farmers of that time sought and found their salvation in a farming system with increased production of animal products, and not in the transition to three-field husbandry, which would have further restricted the yield of animal products even more than grain production.

The author recognized, at the time of writing the book, the stark contrast between reality and the result he had found very well; but he could not alter it because it followed with necessity from the entire course of the investigation.

But whence comes this contradiction?

1) In the isolated state, the stationary condition is the basis of consideration. The cheapness of grain in Germany, arising from a series of exceptionally fruitful years and from the simultaneously implemented corn embargo of England, was an unnatural condition that could not endure.

In the part of the isolated state where three-field husbandry prevails, both the grain price and the price of animal products must be permanently low, because the consumers cannot pay a higher price than the standard one.

In Germany, by contrast, the consumers were able to pay the average price for grain that had existed before 1820, and the low price did not come from the inability of consumers but from the immoderate supply far exceeding the possible consumption. This brought about a change in the people's way of life. Of the income that formerly had to be spent on purchasing grain, a considerable portion was saved, and the savings were mostly spent on better clothing and increased enjoyment of animal foods instead of vegetable ones. The demand for and purchase of animal products such as wool, meat, butter, etc. were thereby greatly increased; meat and butter maintained almost the same price as in the times of high grain prices, and wool, favored by an almost duty-free import into England, maintained itself at an unnaturally high price. Perhaps never had such a disproportion in the prices between grain and animal products existed as then. While formerly the Berlin bushel of rye had approximately the value of 9 pounds of butter and 6 pounds of wool, at that time 3 to 4 pounds of butter equaled a bushel of rye in value, and the price of a pound of improved wool frequently exceeded that of a bushel of rye, and the finest wool even reached double the value of a bushel of rye per pound.

Between the production costs -- which otherwise regulate the price -- and the market prices, every bond seemed to have been severed. Such abnormal conditions could not be lasting and have long since disappeared.

Considering these circumstances, it becomes easily understandable that the fall in grain prices alone, with high prices for animal products, had to lead not to three-field husbandry but to expanded cultivation of fodder crops.

2) In the isolated state, the cultivated plain is surrounded by a zone practicing only animal husbandry, from which animal products are delivered at so low a price that the rent from animal husbandry in the regions close to the city becomes negative. From the greater part of Germany, however, the raw, merely cattle-raising countries are either so distant, or the import of animal products from them is so impeded by tariffs, that the price of animal products is high enough to gain a rent from the soil through animal husbandry.

Nothing, however, leads so decidedly to crop-rotation husbandry as a high price of animal products, and the price ratio between these and grain is one of the most important factors in deciding the question of where crop-rotation husbandry begins to be more advantageous than convertible husbandry.

In the first part of the isolated state, the German conditions could not be taken into account, still less be made the foundation, because this would have transformed the striving to discover general laws into a search for prescriptions valid for one country, one province, but useless and inapplicable to all other countries. In this part, however, the isolated state will also become the subject of investigation under the modification that it is surrounded by a sandy desert instead of the cultivable wilderness -- and the results that emerge from this will be more analogous to the German conditions than those of the first part.

Led by the correct feeling that the principle "Low grain prices lead to three-field husbandry" was not applicable to German conditions, people questioned its correctness; but in overlooking that the inapplicability resulted from the difference of conditions, they attacked the principle where it was not vulnerable, and brought forward arguments that are untenable.

V.

Extension of the Requirement of Consistency to All Relations of the Isolated State.

The procedure in the construction of the isolated state is that we take a given estate from reality as our foundation, mentally move this estate successively to different distances from the city -- the marketplace -- and then attempt to solve the question: "How will the farming of this estate have to change with increasing distance from the city?"

In doing so, we had to establish the consistency of management as an indispensable requirement.

In this way, however, all the conditions of this estate from reality have also been transferred to the isolated state.

The relationship between wages and interest rate prevailing at this point on Earth; these Mecklenburg country roads; this size of the estates, and so much else, thus underlies the construction of the isolated state.

The requirement of consistency we now wish to extend to all conditions of the isolated state. With this we are now driven to the questions: Is this wage and its relationship to the interest rate the natural one; is it consistent to maintain roads of this quality; do estates of this size yield the highest land rent, etc.?

Indeed, it would be a remarkable coincidence if in reality, where everything is still in a state of becoming and every change is only a transitional step to a higher one -- if here somewhere the rational had already appeared in its ultimate perfection. Were this miracle actually to have happened, it would still have to be demonstrated that and why what exists is what is rational.

Our task therefore requires for the completion of its solution that we subject everything taken from reality to examination and critique, strive to discover what is lawful, and -- insofar as it is found -- transfer it to the isolated state in place of what exists. With this, the view opens upon an endless series of investigations, of which the following -- in connection with those already indicated -- present themselves to the eye first as the most prominent:

1) What is the wage determined by nature for the worker, and by what law is the level of the interest rate determined?

Capital is accumulated labor product, thus completed labor; it springs together with ongoing labor from one root -- human activity; capital and labor are thus essentially one, differing only in temporal sequence, as past and present. Between the two there must exist some relationship; what is it?

Since this question touches the position of the different classes toward one another, and thus the happiness and welfare of the numerous class of workers as well as the obligation of the propertied classes toward the proletarians: the investigation of this subject extends far beyond the original task of constructing the isolated state. The isolated state recedes into the background in this question concerning man himself, and the investigation is principally tied to it only because the task, if it is at all solvable, seems to me solvable only under the form of intuition that underlies the isolated state.

2) In what connection does land rent stand with wages and interest rate?

3) By what law is land rent determined when, instead of one great city, many small cities of equal size and at equal distances from one another are scattered across the plain of the isolated state, and what connection exists here between the degree of care of labor and grain prices?

4) What influence does the size of the money stock exert on the level of the interest rate?

5) The calculations of the magnitude of transportation costs, which are taken from reality, are based on the very poor roads that existed in Mecklenburg at the beginning of this century. But it is certainly not rational to maintain such poor roads -- as indeed in Mecklenburg they have already been greatly reduced through the construction of numerous paved roads -- and when we initially imagine the isolated state equipped with such poor roads and have determined its configuration and extent accordingly: then, with the requirement that consistency shall prevail everywhere in the isolated state, the questions press forward:

a) Where, and to what extent, can paved roads and railways be profitably constructed in the isolated state?

b) What change occurs with their construction in the extent of the cultivated plain, in soil cultivation, and in the national wealth?

6) From the manner in which the isolated state is constructed, it follows that uniformity of climate is assumed for the entire plain, and had to be assumed for the purpose of the investigation in the first part.

Also, the isolated state of the first part, because of its small extent, provides no material for reflections on the influence of climate on agriculture.

But if we imagine this state, surrounded by an unlimited wilderness, crisscrossed with a railway network extending to the most remote region from which, with the help of railways, grain can still be delivered to the city: the state attains such an extent that through the mere difference of climate, agriculture in the south of the state acquires an entirely different character than in the north.

If the influence of climate on agriculture is now made the subject of reflection, a multitude of questions press for examination and answer, of which we shall here cite only a few as examples:

a) How do the necessary subsistence needs of the worker, wages, the work capacity of people, and the cost of labor change with the climate?

b) How does the length of the grazing season for livestock change with the latitude, and what influence does this have on the production costs of animal products?

c) Which crops, by being the most profitable, are the principal subject of cultivation under the different climates?

d) What influence does climate have on the quantity of humus that is extracted from the soil by a harvest of a given size, e.g., 10 bushels per 100 square rods, and how does this quantity change with the latitude on identical soil, with identical situation?

7) In order to construct the isolated state, the price of grain necessarily had to be assumed as known and expressed as a definite number. This price, however, can be neither arbitrary nor accidental. After the isolated state has obtained its configuration, and we have set ourselves the task of removing the assumptions made and substituting what is lawful, we must raise the question:

Why can the city pay no higher price than the assumed 1.25 thalers per bushel of rye, and what are the causes and conditions that exactly this and no other price can be paid?

Since with an increase in the grain price the cultivation of the plain extends ever further, the limit to the growth of the city cannot lie in a lack of provisions; rather, this limit must be sought in the conditions of the city itself, in the difficulty or impossibility of offering more manufactured goods than before for a given quantity of provisions.

8) The assumption that the isolated state contains only one great city serves to simplify the investigation, but is not compatible with consistency and must here be removed again.

In reality, the origin of cities has often depended on chance. Next to the hut of the first settler, a second built his hut because mutual service was useful to both. For the same reason, a third, fourth, etc. joined them, until at last a city arose.

Many of the cities that arose from this or a similar occasion one would gladly move to another location, if only they were transportable.

In the isolated state, however, where consistency is to prevail everywhere, lawfulness must also prevail with respect to the size and distribution of the cities. As the highest principle, the following proposition should be set up:

The cities must be spread across the land in respect to size and distance from one another so that the greatest national income results.

This principle is satisfied when industries and factories are located where they can manufacture most cheaply and deliver their products to consumers at the lowest prices.

This leads, besides many other questions, also to the following:

a) What reasons determine the concentration of people in large cities, and which factories naturally have their location in the capital?

b) In what relationship does the size and distance of provincial towns from one another stand to the denser or sparser population of the country?

c) What effect does the greater or lesser distance from provincial towns have on agriculture and on the education of the rural population?

9) By what law is the price of animal products determined when the isolated state, instead of the zone of animal husbandry, is surrounded by a sandy desert?

10) The isolated state is founded on the assumption that its soil is not only of equal physical character but -- with the sole exception of the zone of free farming -- also everywhere contains equal richness in plant nutrients.

The richness of the soil, however, is a variable factor dependent on the power of man, and thus the question presses forward whether the originally equally fertile soil will, with consistent management, also remain of equal fertility in all regions of the isolated state.

The higher richness of the soil is not to be obtained for free, but must be purchased through expenditures or through a sparing management associated with a temporary reduction in net yield. On the one hand, the magnitude of the sacrifice to be made, and on the other, the benefit that enrichment of the soil affords, depend on the level of the grain price and the price of animal products, and consequently the amount of both -- sacrifice and benefit -- varies greatly in the different regions of the isolated state.

The appropriate richness of the soil thus appears to stand in a certain connection and relationship with the price of agricultural products.

The task arising from this view is the following:

To what point must the enrichment of the soil be driven in the different regions of the isolated state, if the requirement of consistency is to be satisfied?

11) Since the construction of the isolated state has emerged from the solution of the problem: "How will the farming of the estate Tellow change if it is relocated to the different regions of the state" -- this already implies the condition that all estates of this state have the size of the estate Tellow.

According to the standpoint chosen here, we must, however, raise the question whether the estate Tellow has the size at which the net yield of the soil is highest, and we are thereby led to the three problems:

a) How can it be determined, under given, entirely definite conditions, what size the estates must have so that the soil yields the highest rent?

b) Does the greater or lesser distance from the marketplace have an influence on the most appropriate size of the estates?

c) What influence does the increase of soil richness have on the most appropriate size of the estates?

12) In the first part it was demonstrated how, with greater distance of the field from the farmstead, the costs of agriculture grow and the rent of the soil declines.

There, in order not to confuse the investigation, it had to be assumed that the field from the farmstead to the boundary contained equal richness and was subject to one and the same farming system.

Now, when we remove the assumptions one after another by making them themselves the subject of investigation, the questions press forward:

a) Is it appropriate to place the field from the farmstead to the estate boundary in equal richness, and if this question is answered in the negative, what gradation must then take place?

b) How must the farming system on large estates change in the fields at different distances from the farmstead, so that the whole yields the highest net return?

13) The task of obtaining the highest net yield from the soil includes, for the estates of the isolated state that produce timber only for their own use, the task: "How is timber to be produced at the lowest production costs?" This leads to the following questions:

a) How are the production costs of timber to be calculated for a given case?

b) How do the production costs of construction and fuel timber change with increasing distance from the city under the same management?

c) What change must take place in forest management, especially in the rotation period and in the thinning method, in the different regions of the isolated state with the change in timber value, if timber is to be produced at the least cost?

14) From the manner in which the isolated state is constructed, it follows that for agricultural buildings in all regions of the state, one and the same construction method is assumed. But is this compatible with consistency?

The buildings necessary for agricultural operations cause a fourfold annual cost, namely:

1) the interest on the capital expended in their construction,
2) the annual maintenance or repair costs,
3) the depreciation or annual reduction in value,
4) the insurance premium against fire risk.

The costs listed under 2 and 3 diminish steadily the more solidly the buildings are constructed; at the same time, however, the costs under 1 and 4 increase.

There must therefore be a degree of solidity of construction at which the sum of these costs is a minimum.

Consistency in the management of an estate demands the maximum of land rent. But this maximum can only be attained if the building costs, while fully achieving the purpose of the buildings, take the smallest possible share of the estate's yield. The investigation of the construction method at which the building costs to be apportioned to annual yield reach a minimum thus forms a part of the task to be solved.

This leads to the questions:

a) In what way are the building costs falling on a single year to be determined, and how are these to be apportioned among the individual branches of cultivation?

b) Since the production costs of construction timber steadily decrease with increasing distance from the city, already because land rent -- a component of the timber price -- decreases so much, and thus the price ratio among the different building materials such as oak, pine, bricks, roof tiles, thatch, etc. constantly changes with distance: the same construction method cannot be the most advantageous for the entire isolated state. The question arises how, with increasing distance from the city, the construction methods -- e.g., with walls of brick, clay, half-timbering, planks, etc. -- must change in order to reduce the average annual building costs to the minimum.

15) In the first part, the effect of taxes was already discussed; but there, wages, interest rate, care of cultivation, and richness of the soil were treated as constant quantities. With the expansion of our investigation, where all these factors are treated as variable, the task now arises:

How do taxes affect the named factors?

16) In all previous investigations, we have always had in view only the average yield of the soil, or what amounts to the same thing, assumed years of average fertility.

The inequality of annual fertility that occurs in reality, however, introduces manifold disturbances into farm operations and frequently brings want and distress to consumers. This leads to reflections on the questions:

a) What changes in regular farm operations must be made in years of abnormal fertility, and does the effect of such years manifest itself in all regions of the isolated state in the same way?

b) In rich as in poor harvests, production costs cease to be the regulator of the grain price. By what laws does the grain price adjust in such years?

A satisfactory answer to the latter question would provide a point of reference for the speculations of grain merchants.

17) In reality, everything that appears is merely a transitional stage toward an unattained, still distant goal.

In the isolated state, by contrast, we have always had in view the final outcome, thus the goal attained. With the goal attained, rest enters and with it the stationary condition -- and here we perceive lawfulness, while in the transitional period much appears to us as an inextricable chaos. The stationary condition, however, cannot occur in reality for the following reasons:

1) Already the individual person does not remain the same in the different stages of his life, still less do the generations following one another remain alike. Mankind itself is still struggling toward a distant, not clearly recognized, scarcely yet intuited goal.

2) What is already recognized as purpose and goal by the living generation nonetheless requires for its realization a duration that often far exceeds the human lifespan.

3) In nature, properties and forces are placed whose discovery and proper utilization appears to be one of the highest tasks of the human mind, since thereby human labor is made more rewarding and productive, and thus the welfare of mankind is greatly advanced. But nature reveals its secrets to man only gradually, and since every great discovery brings about a change or even transformation in the life of civil society, the aim and goal of the same is itself, in commercial respects, subject to change. But despite this mutability, there lies in each particular thing we contemplate the seed of a determined -- not accidental, not arbitrary -- development, and just as we know what tree will one day emerge from the acorn planted in the earth, so too can we here foresee the fruit springing from the development of the seed -- the final outcome -- under the assumption that no disturbing influences occur, and behold it in the mind. In this, however, lies the justification for focusing in our investigations on the stationary condition and making it our foundation.

The knowledge attained through this method can, however, contribute substantially to shedding light on the confusing phenomena during the period of development and transition.

If we apply this to the isolated state, we find ourselves called upon to compare the effects that the invention of new machines, new means of communication, etc., exert upon the prosperity of civil society when they first appear with the consequences that later develop from them -- thus to make the mysterious process of becoming the subject of reflection.

If we now survey the versatility and diversity of the questions raised, and consider that with the demand for consistency applied to the conditions transferred from reality into the isolated state, besides the points mentioned, almost all other conditions of civil society must also be drawn into the investigation, that thereby instead of what exists, what is rational is to be discovered, and thus the goal itself is to be established: then it follows of itself that the solution of the task is not the work of one individual, not even the work of one generation. It is rather the work of history itself, which collects what is accomplished by all mankind over several generations -- and so it can only be a later researcher, who finds the materials ready, who succeeds in bringing the ground and purpose of the movement that has occurred to consciousness, and forming a systematic whole from the fragments.

This realization might well discourage the individual from putting hand to the work.

Here, however, the infinite importance of the proof given above reveals itself: that the result obtained through the method of considering only one factor as active and the others as resting or constant is not an untrue but merely an incomplete one, and is therefore only provisional so long as all other cooperating factors have been subjected to a similar investigation -- that therefore every inquiry into even the smallest point of the task can become a building block for the erection of the great edifice.

Among readers who enter into this view and have grasped the full magnitude of the task, I believe I scarcely need excuse myself if here only fragments are delivered, if the treatment of individual chapters is highly uneven, as the author lingers and goes into detail on points that have long been the subject of his contemplation while merely indicating other points, and if finally in some chapters, instead of an attempt at solving the task, only new questions and problems are raised, as the author already feels satisfied if he can stimulate others to research.

## First Section

The Isolated State Surrounded by a Cultivable Wilderness with Respect to Wages and the Interest Rate.

### Section 1.

Unclarity of the Concept of the Natural Wage of Labor.

(Written in the year 1842.)

All political-economic studies led me ever back to the question: Is the low wage that ordinary manual laborers receive almost everywhere a natural one, or has it arisen through usurpation from which the workers cannot again extricate themselves?

Since the low wage has its origin in the fact that the capitalists and landowners appropriate so large a share of the product that the workers produce, that question immediately leads to another:

What is the law according to which the distribution of the labor product among worker, capitalist, and landowner should naturally occur?

The investigation of this law offers not merely political-economic interest, but also has a very serious moral dimension.

One can be animated by the most honest will to fulfill one's duty, and yet do others great injustice -- if one does not know and does not recognize what duty is.

In the concept of what constitutes duty toward the workers, what is due to the worker as wages, what demands of the worker one may justly reject -- in all of this, the freest arbitrariness reigns, and everyone can answer these questions as it suits him; for even science gives no other enlightenment on this than: "The level of wages is determined by the competition of workers, by the ratio between the demand for labor and its supply," in which, through a confusion of concepts, the factual is taken for an explanation -- what happens is taken for the reason for the phenomenon. Indeed, the view has taken such hold of people's minds that the worker is entitled to nothing more than what he necessarily requires for his subsistence, that the sum of the subsistence needs necessary for the preservation of life and working capacity is also the natural wage -- that conscience sleeps peacefully so long as the worker does not actually suffer real want.

As soon as this want visibly occurs, the beautiful religious sentiment, the Christian duty to support the suffering, also steps forth to help and rescue; but -- the source of the want is not thereby stopped.

Most pernicious, however, is the unclarity of views about the natural wage in the levying of taxes.

The representative assemblies of the constitutional states strive with all their might to secure and protect themselves against princely arbitrariness. But the members of the representative assemblies all belong to the educated and well-to-do classes of society, while the most numerous class, that of common workers, is not represented at all -- and so it can happen that the same assembly that so vigorously opposes princely arbitrariness exercises arbitrariness against the people themselves, and through the approval of taxes, legislative proposals, etc. becomes the oppressor of workers. This does not require ill will, not even the motive of self-interest -- it requires only the view that nothing more is due to the worker than what is necessary for his essential maintenance -- to bring about such a result.

But when the awakening people one day raise the question and attempt to solve it practically: "What is the natural share of the worker in his product?" a struggle may arise that brings devastation and barbarism upon Europe.

It is a great evil that this question is not even yet resolved in science, that no party knows what is right, and that the struggle arising from the impure motives of self-interest finds no counterweight in the recognition of duty and truth.

For when some political economists -- with whom the great majority of business entrepreneurs instinctively agree -- declare the quantity of subsistence needs necessary for living as the natural wage, and when other writers leave the determination of wages to the lawless and ruleless competition: they have thereby only stated what happens in reality.

When, on the other hand, the workers assert that what happens in reality is an injustice: that supposed law has lost its entire hold, and instead of an appeal to experience, a law based on rational grounds must be demonstrated.

Already now, in France -- that hearth of the upheavals spreading across Europe -- the first traces of the beginning, for now still bloodless, struggle appear in the views and doctrines of the communists.

But this subject presents yet another deeply serious aspect.

We find in world history that some great idea occupied and pervaded mankind for centuries, indeed that world history itself in such periods represents only the development and gradual realization of the idea.

But such a realization of the idea has always been accompanied by enormous struggles, by the devastation or downfall of entire empires.

Thus the religious wars shook the earth for nearly a millennium and brought unspeakable misery upon millions of people.

Now, since the beginning of the French Revolution, the world has been moved by the idea of constitutional freedom. Already the first emergence of this idea produced a 23-year struggle that successively spread across all of Europe.

At present, a momentary calm has entered, but this is perhaps only the calm before the storm, for the fermentation has not yet ceased, the idea is still far from its realization -- and it is impossible to foresee what storms the future still holds.

But beyond these struggles, another is already lurking, one already contained in the striving for constitutional freedom as a seed, and one that can easily become more destructive and devastating than any of the earlier ones.

It is a saddening result of history that, as a rule, error is not combated by truth, injustice not by reason and right, but by another injustice, and that only after countless oscillations to both sides does what is true and right come to realization.

Adam Smith says: If you want to straighten a crooked stick, you do not bring it to the straight position but bend it over to the other side.

So too the communists are not content to demand a natural wage for the workers, but immediately pass to chimerical hopes, to unreasonable demands.

But exaggeration is attractive and carries the masses away to enthusiasm, while the moderate but true leaves the masses cold.

It is therefore greatly to be feared that the views of the communists will spread and take root in the minds of the people, especially when these views are proclaimed and published by skillful and eloquent, but superficial, writers.

Should the communists in the more distant future unfortunately ever attain power in France, and their armies, simultaneously armed with the sword and with proclamations promising our soldiers division of property and equality of wealth, cross our border -- what resistance is then to be expected, and where is then the limit of the upheavals and devastation -- ?

But surely it does not lie in the plan of the world-spirit or of Providence that every advance in the development of the human race should become reality only after countless retrogressions, and should be purchased through streams of blood and the misery of several generations. In the recognition of truth and right, in the taming of egoism, by virtue of which the privileged voluntarily surrenders what he unjustly possesses, lies the means to lead the human race peacefully and cheerfully toward its development and higher destiny.

But where error and egoism rule, there, as world history shows, nemesis appears in fearful retribution. The high and noble task of science, however, is to discover and bring to recognition truth and the goal toward which we should strive -- not through experience, through the course of history, but through reason itself.

### Section 2.

On the Lot of Workers.

A Dream of Serious Content. Written down in the year 1826.

It is a great evil that in all states, even those with representative constitutions, the most numerous class of citizens, namely that of common manual laborers, is not represented at all.

Disproportionately high is the remuneration of every industrial entrepreneur (e.g., the manufacturer, the tenant farmer, and even the mere administrator) compared with the wage of the manual laborer.

Why is this disproportion not equalized through the transition of the most skilled manual laborers to the class of entrepreneurs, since here a free competition exists?

Because the workers lack the school knowledge without which one cannot, for all other competence, be an entrepreneur or administrator.

But why do the workers lack this school knowledge?

Because their wages are so low that they cannot make the expenditure for their children that the acquisition of this knowledge requires.

But why is the wage so low?

Because precisely in this class, through early marriages, the increase is so strong that the supply of workers is almost always greater than the demand for them -- whereby wages sink so low that they can barely cover the barest necessities of life. Indeed, it is unfortunately true that an even greater increase is held back only by the sight of the misery prevailing among a portion of this class.

So the workers are themselves to blame for the low remuneration they receive for their labor.

But how is this to be remedied?

Only through a change in the national character.

Men of the middle and upper classes, even when they possess a capital of some thousand thalers or an income of several hundred thalers, generally do not marry until their income suffices to support a family adequately and give the children a good education. This usually does not happen before the age of 30. They could marry much earlier if they were willing to live and raise their children as day laborers do; but they sacrifice the happiness that marriage can provide (though it does not always provide it) for a time, because in their eyes a wretched life and a poor education of their children are such great evils that they are not compensated by the happiness of marriage.

The laborer, by contrast, marries as soon as he can obtain a dwelling, once he has passed the age of 20, bringing nothing but the strength of his arms to support a family. For him, marriage has more charm than all the misery that awaits him in the background, than the prospect of letting his children grow up without adequate instruction could have of terror for him. For him it suffices to raise his children merely physically -- the intellectual development of the same is for him no need.

But what consequences would it have if the national character changed in such a way that the workers, like the middle classes, counted a life preserved from want and an intellectual education of their children among their necessities, and abstained from marriage until they were secured for the satisfaction of these needs?

Reduced supply of workers and increased wages would be the first immediate consequence.

But how is the day laborer to arrive at counting an intellectual education of his children among the necessities of life, when he himself does not feel the drive toward intellectual development? For so long as this drive is lacking, he will spend the saved thaler on the satisfaction of sensory pleasures rather than on better instruction of his children.

If we want the workers in the future to make the sacrifice of abstaining from marriage longer in order to give their children a better education: then the need for intellectual development must be awakened in the present younger generation. But this can only be achieved through better school instruction -- and since the present workers have neither the means nor the will to pay the costs of better instruction: the educational institutions must be established and maintained at the expense of the state.

When this is accomplished, when wages have risen and the workers have attained the school education that the business entrepreneur must possess: the barrier that has hitherto existed between the two classes has fallen. The monopoly of the latter ceases, and as the sons of workers, accustomed to fewer needs, enter into competition with them, trade profit is reduced. The less capable part of the business entrepreneurs, including administrators, clerks, etc., is thereby forced to join the class of manual laborers; the more capable part will abandon a pursuit that offers so little reward, devote themselves to studies, and seek public offices -- and so a great competition will enter this field too, resulting in a reduction of the salaries of civil servants and a saving in the costs of public administration.

In such a condition of society, only few, very wealthy people will be able to live without work; manual labor will be very highly paid, and between the remuneration of the manual laborer, the industrial entrepreneur, and the civil servant, a far smaller difference than at present will exist.

While at present one part of people nearly collapses under the burden of physical exertion and can hardly enjoy life, while the other part is ashamed of work, has forgotten the use of bodily powers and pays for it with a lack of health and cheerfulness -- then perhaps most classes will divide their time between intellectual occupation and moderate bodily labor, and man will thus be led back to the natural condition and to his destiny -- the exercise and development of all his powers and faculties.

Even if in such a condition of society not all passions of mankind are silenced, yet the violations of property and the crimes that spring from want and bitter poverty must become rarer, indeed almost entirely cease.

If one considers that with the greater spread of intellectual education, the number of those capable of making discoveries and inventions in machinery and agriculture also grows, that every such invention makes human labor more effective and rewards it with a greater product, that thus with rising intellectual culture man is more and more relieved of toilsome physical exertion: one might conclude that the human race, after millennia, could arrive at a paradisiacal condition where man would spend his life not in idleness, but in moderate activity exercising mind and body, strengthening health and cheerfulness.

So paradise would be the goal that the human race can reach only after long struggling and striving, while tradition places the first humans already in a paradise.

The foregoing was conceived and written down in the autumn of 1826, when, studying the political-economic works of Say and Ricardo, I felt unsatisfied by what was said therein about wages.

I called it "a dream" because it so completely opposed the views then prevailing in science and practical life that it seemed to belong far more to a castle in the air than to reality. It is undoubtedly a fantasy, but nevertheless it has exerted the most decisive influence on my views of life and my actions. For thereby the view of the propertied, imbibed with mother's milk, that the worker is destined by nature herself to be a beast of burden, that for his exertion only the prolongation of his existence is due to him -- was forever shaken.

The lives of a large part of farmers, business entrepreneurs, and even employers in the cities are embittered by the fact that they spend their time in constant struggle with their workers and servants -- regarding the striving and struggling of the latter for a better lot as an unjust presumption that they must combat by every means and with all their might.

But never is man more decidedly and persistently in wrongdoing than when, through an error of understanding, he regards the wrong as the right, and then considers it his duty to uphold and carry it through with all his might.

Conscience then does not admonish, for it is not the will that commits the wrong, but the lack of insight. But nemesis does not concern herself with this distinction -- and a life full of bitterness, struggle, and hostility is the fruit of ignorance and error.

Error and ignorance are everywhere pernicious, but with scarcely any other subject to so high a degree as with this; for here the peace and happiness of millions of people are destroyed.

Yet another consideration forces itself upon me here.

When I conceived the view presented in the dream, it stood in such stark opposition to public opinion that I had to fear being regarded, through its publication, as a dreamer or even a revolutionary, without being able to believe that it would find any response or be of any use. I therefore communicated the dream only to individual friends and resolved to bring it to the public only in connection with scientific investigations.

Since then, not even a full quarter-century has passed -- and how greatly has public opinion and national sentiment on this subject changed in this short period.

How mild, even tame, what was demanded in the dream now appears, after the socialists have demanded the abolition of inheritance, the communists the division of property, and the egalitarians even the destruction of cities and the murder of the rich, for the promotion of the welfare of the poorest and most numerous class!

But if such a revolution in the public's conception of a subject can occur in so short a time -- who can tell us what views will prevail after the passage of another quarter-century, how far they will have spread among the lowest classes, and what consequences may spring from them?

However gratifying the conception of the future of the human race contained in the dream may be to the feelings, in that it reconciles us with fate and lets us discern in the advancing history a benevolent providence toward mankind -- this dream is always only a utopia, so long as the possibility of its realization is not demonstrated.

But only what develops with necessity from the organization of mankind comes to realization.

What use are the pious wishes for higher wages and greater education of the workers, if it is not demonstrated that both are compatible with the properties and powers placed in human nature?

Do we not see that factories stand still when wages rise; will not, with higher wages, the cultivation of whole tracts of less fertile soil cease and these lie waste -- and will not the lot of the workers then become even more dismal than it now is?

Only a deeper penetration into the science that makes clear to us the laws arising from human nature can provide enlightenment on these questions -- and so, if we want light on this subject that so closely touches the lot of mankind, we must devote ourselves to scientific inquiry, however charmless, arid, and thorny the path leading there may be.

We now turn first to Adam Smith, the father of political economy, to see how far the task before us has been solved by him.

### Section 3.

Adam Smith's Views on Wages, Interest Rate, Land Rent, and Price.

We must first answer the question whether Adam Smith's doctrines are sufficient for the solution of the task we have set ourselves, or not.

At the same time, our task itself will emerge more clearly and distinctly.

Since Adam Smith's views are much easier to grasp and survey when one removes from his book the intermediate propositions and incidentally introduced reflections, I have, for the convenience of the readers, compiled from the first volume of Smith's work on the national wealth the most important and decisive propositions on the subjects named above, partly verbatim, partly abridged, in what follows.

**Wages.**

In the first volume, Adam Smith says:

P. 120. "Upon the terms of the contract between the laborer and the proprietor of a capital who employs him, depends how much shall be the daily wages of labor."

P. 128 and 129. "It is not the magnitude to which the national wealth has attained, but its continual growth, that occasions the rise of wages."

P. 129 and 130. "However large the revenue from which wages are paid may be -- the revenue and capital of all inhabitants -- if both have remained unchanged for several years and the stagnation continues, the number of hands will increase faster than the number of employments, and in short, through the self-interest of masters and the competition of workers seeking employment, wages will be driven down so far that they will be just sufficient to satisfy the indispensable necessities of nature."

P. 144. "As little as poverty prevents the production of children, so much does it impede their upbringing. I have often been assured that in the Scottish Highlands, of the twenty children that a mother brings into the world, often only two survive."

P. 145. "Every species of animal naturally multiplies in proportion to the means of subsistence it has, and no species can multiply beyond this proportion. But in an orderly civil society, it can only be the lower classes of the people in whom the lack of subsistence sets limits to the multiplication of human beings, and it can set this limit only by destroying a large part of the children that their fruitful marriages produce."

P. 146. "The demand for men, like that for any other commodity, necessarily regulates the production of men. When the wage is at a certain time excessively large, the resulting surplus of hands would soon create a competition whereby the wage would sink back to its average level."

P. 148. "It deserves to be noticed that the condition of the laboring poor, or the most numerous class of the people, seems to be happier and more desirable during the period when society is advancing toward the point of its highest prosperity than when it has reached this point. When society stands still in its prosperity, the common laborer lives wretchedly; when it declines, he lives miserably."

P. 156. "The demand for labor, according as it is either increasing, decreasing, or stationary, and accordingly demands an increasing, decreasing, or unchanging population, determines the quantity of the necessaries and conveniences of life with which labor shall be rewarded."

Competition, or the relationship of supply to demand for labor, thus determines according to Adam Smith the level of wages; but the magnitude of the demand for workers depends on whether the national wealth is rising, stationary, or declining.

We have now, however, set ourselves the task of investigating the level of wages for the stationary condition of civil society. In such a condition, demand and supply are in equilibrium; both cancel each other out, as it were, or appear as resting -- and it already follows from this that in such a condition, some other determining factor for the level of wages must exist.

The stationary condition, however, is the condition of stagnation, in which, according to Adam Smith, the worker lives wretchedly, and wages are depressed so far that the worker can thereby satisfy only the most indispensable needs for himself, so that want kills a large part of the children produced.

But dying -- from lack of the necessary means of subsistence -- is a dreadful fate, and it would be horrifying if in coming centuries the most numerous class should face such a destiny. For it cannot be denied that in the measure that all continents become more populous, the fertile soil is taken into possession, and the discovery of new natural forces serving production and manufacturing becomes rarer, we approach the stationary condition more and more.

On the whole, however, the view shimmers through in Adam Smith, as in most of his successors, that the sum of the worker's necessary subsistence needs is the natural wage.

Ricardo, however, has the courage to state bluntly: "The natural price of labor is that which enables the laborers to subsist and to perpetuate their race."

**Interest rate.**

Adam Smith lumps together the interest on capital invested in an enterprise with the entrepreneur's trade profit under the name "capital profit." This is very detrimental to the clarity of his views on the interest rate. Since, however, according to Adam Smith (p. 161), profits can be judged from the level of money interest, both being to a degree proportional, one can also reason backward from what he says about the magnitude of profits to the level of the interest rate.

Adam Smith's investigation of capital profit contains valuable notes on its magnitude in different countries and at different times, but only little and inadequate material on the laws by which the level of profits and interest is determined. The most important propositions in this regard may be the following:

P. 160. "The increase of capitals, as we have seen, raises wages, but the profit from these capitals it makes smaller. When the capitals of many merchants are invested in the same branch of trade, the resulting competition must necessarily have the effect of making their profits smaller, and when this increase of capitals extends over all branches of industry and trade of a country, the profits of all capitalists must likewise diminish."

P. 172. "In a country which had acquired that full complement of riches which the nature of its soil and climate and its situation with respect to other countries allowed it to acquire -- in a country that was standing still in its prosperity -- both wages and capital profits would probably be equally low. When it was fully peopled in proportion to the land from which its inhabitants draw their subsistence and the funds by which they are employed, the competition among workers seeking employment must be so great that their wages can be no higher than is just necessary to maintain the existing number of workers. And when this country is furnished with funds for all the business it has occasion to transact, in every branch of industry so much capital will already be invested as the nature and possible extent of that branch allows. In every branch, therefore, the competition of capitalists will have reduced profits to the smallest possible level."

P. 177. "The highest rate to which the ordinary profits of capitalists can rise is such that they are large enough to swallow up in the prices of goods the share belonging to the landowner, and leave for the worker only so small a share as is necessary for the worker's subsistence. The worker must everywhere be maintained in one way or another, or the work to be performed by him cannot be accomplished. But the owner of land need not everywhere receive his rent."

P. 176. "The lowest ordinary rate of money interest must be somewhat more than sufficient to compensate the occasional losses to which lending money is inevitably exposed. Were it not so, this business would offer no advantage at all, and friendship or benevolence would be the only reasons that could induce anyone to lend money."

Adam Smith thus contents himself with indicating the limits within which profits and money interest can rise and fall, and with showing that within these limits the level of both depends on the quantity of existing capitals and the resulting greater or lesser competition.

But with this, only the phenomenon -- what goes on before our eyes -- has been described. Wages and interest rate appear here still as two entirely independent factors, regulated by competition -- and of a law demonstrating the connection between both, there is no mention anywhere.

**Land Rent.**

On the origin and justification of land rent, Adam Smith says:

P. 189. "As soon as the land of any country has all become private property, the landlords, like all other men, love to reap where they never sowed, and demand a rent even for its natural produce. The wood of the forest, the grass of the field, which, as long as the land was common to all, cost whoever wanted it only the trouble of collecting it, is now burdened by the landlord with a charge or purchase price. The permission to collect the one or the other must be purchased from this landlord, and a portion of what has been collected or produced on his land must be surrendered to him. This portion, or what amounts to the same thing, the money price of this portion, is what is called ground rent or land rent -- and constitutes the third essential component of the selling price of goods."

P. 271. "When the landlord closes his contract with the tenant, he is certainly careful to leave him no greater share of the produce of his land than is absolutely necessary to secure for the tenant, on one hand, the funds from which he provides the seed, pays the laborers, and purchases and maintains cattle and farming implements, and on the other, the profit from these funds that tenant farmers in this region ordinarily receive from their capitals. No smaller share can the tenant obviously accept without exposing himself to the danger of ruin, and more than this the landlord is seldom inclined to leave him. **What now remains of the product of an estate, or (which is the same thing) of the price of this product, after deduction of that share, the landlord appropriates to himself under the name of rent.**"

P. 274. "Only those products of a country can be brought to market whose ordinary price is sufficient to repay the money spent on their production, together with the usual profit that is ordinarily drawn from such a capital. When this price amounts to more, the surplus falls to the landlord as rent."

P. 174. "High wages and large profits are the causes of high prices; high rents are the effects thereof."

The two objections:

1) that the inclination of the owner of land to receive a rent from it is not sufficient to actually demand this rent from others; and

2) that Adam Smith calls the income a farm yields when leased "land rent," so that (as shown in detail in the first part, second edition, Section 5a) Adam Smith's land rent conflates the return on land itself with the interest on the capital embedded in farm buildings, etc.,

we leave aside here because they do not belong to the subject of our present consideration.

On the other hand, our full attention must here be directed to the fact that according to Adam Smith, the level of land rent and indeed the very existence of it depends entirely on the price of agricultural products.

**Price.**

What Adam Smith says, pp. 101 and 102, about market price can be summarized in the following propositions:

1) The price for which a commodity is ordinarily actually sold is called its market price.

2) The market price of every commodity or product is determined by the relationship between supply and demand, between the quantity brought to market for sale and the quantity demanded by the buyers.

3) If the quantity of the commodity brought to market is less than the quantity for which there is an effective demand, several buyers, rather than do without the commodity entirely, resolve to pay a higher than the usual price, and through the competition among buyers the market price then rises above the ordinary price.

4) If, on the other hand, the quantity of the commodity brought to market exceeds the effective demand, the entire quantity cannot be sold at the previously customary price; rather, those who had previously abstained from using this commodity or used it only in limited measure must be induced to purchase it through a reduction in price -- and so the market price of this commodity sinks below the ordinary price.

This explanation is taken from life; it is fact. But what, we must now ask, is thereby gained for science?

The competition, the relationship between supply and demand, is as little constant, as fluctuating and changeable, as the weather.

How, then, can such an indefinite, such a changeable factor serve as the foundation for a system of doctrine?

This Adam Smith undoubtedly also felt, and he therefore seeks in the following propositions to present a law governing competition.

P. 98 and 99. "In every country or region of a country there is, for both wages and profits, a certain standard that determines what the worker can ordinarily expect to receive for his industry and the capitalist to earn with his money."

"Likewise, in every country, in every region, there is a certain rate for land rent."

"That measure of wages, capital profits, and land rent which at a certain place, at a certain time, is the ordinary one, can at that place, at that time, be regarded as the natural one."

P. 90. "In every civil society, the market price of every commodity is either composed of the three components -- wages, capital profit, and land rent -- or contains at least one or another of them."

P. 98. "If the selling price of a commodity is neither greater nor smaller than is necessary to pay the rent on the piece of land, the wages for the labor, and the profit on the capital that were all employed to produce, to manufacture, and to bring the commodity to market -- at the rates usual at each place and at each time -- then this commodity is sold for the price that may be called its natural price."

P. 105. "The natural price is, as it were, the central point toward which the fluctuating market prices of all commodities constantly gravitate. Accidents of various kinds may keep them for a time removed from this center -- may raise them above it or depress them below it. But whatever obstacles may prevent them from settling in this point of rest, they exert a constant tendency to approach it."

I still very vividly remember from my youth the joy I felt when I first read these propositions of Adam Smith. Light and clarity spread for me over an otherwise confused subject, and I now saw the lawless competition subordinated to a definite law. Production costs were now elevated to the regulator of the natural price -- toward which market prices constantly gravitate -- and competition was thereby assigned its limits.

But this joy did not last long; rather, it was quite soon clouded by deeper penetration into the subject.

The natural price of a commodity is determined by the natural wage, the natural capital profit, and the natural land rent contained in the production of this commodity.

But if we now ask what determines the natural wage, the answer is: Competition. If we ask for the determining factor of the natural capital profit, this is again competition.

**The removal of competition from the determining factors of the natural price is thus only apparent, is an illusion -- .**

**Connection between Price and Land Rent.**

If the selling price of a commodity is just sufficient to compensate the measure of wages, capital profit, and land rent -- at the usual rates -- employed in its production, this is the natural price of the commodity.

What remains of the selling price of agricultural products after deduction of wages, capital profit, and the other costs expended on their production -- this constitutes, according to A. Smith, the land rent.

If we now ask: "What is the natural price of grain?" we receive, according to these definitions, the following answer:

The natural price of grain is the price by which the ordinary measure of wages, capital profit, and land rent contained in the production costs of grain is exactly covered.

If we now further ask: "What is the natural land rent?" the answer is:

What remains of the selling price of the products, thus also of grain, after deduction of wages, expenditures, and capital profit of the tenant -- this constitutes the land rent.

**Thus, in determining the natural price of grain, land rent is treated as a known quantity; in determining land rent, the natural price of grain is assumed as known.**

This is a circular argument that, in superficial reading, may lull and reassure, but through which nothing is found, nothing is clarified.

If y = a + b + x and x = y - a - b, the second equation is not a new one but only a rearrangement of the first, and the unknown quantities y and x both remain undetermined.

Unfortunately, this circular argument and that illusion concerning the removal of competition from the determining factors of the natural price strike precisely at a fundamental piece of the entire doctrinal edifice.

If, then, land rent depends on the price of agricultural products, the price depends on wages and capital profit, and the magnitude of these two factors is determined by competition: then land rent, too, is dependent on competition.

Competition is thus, according to A. Smith, the ultimate regulator of wages, capital profit, price, and land rent.

After this overview of Smith's doctrines, we must ask ourselves: What is thereby gained for the solution of our task?

The task, however, that we have set ourselves initially is the following:

What is the natural share of the worker in the product brought forth by him; or what is the wage determined for the worker by nature?

According to A. Smith, the worker is referred to what competition allows him -- that is, to what exists.

Indeed, A. Smith himself says (p. 99): "That measure of wages which at a certain place, at a certain time, is the ordinary one, can at that place, at that time, be regarded as the natural one."

But what exists is, in the course of time, subject to constant change, and one must ask:

**Which existing state of things is the right one, the natural one?**

To this, A. Smith's doctrines can give no answer; indeed, upon closer inspection, we find that this was not at all the subject of investigation for A. Smith.

A. Smith contented himself with compiling and unifying into an overview the facts and phenomena that presented themselves to him -- and this was, at his time and at the then state of the science, a very meritorious work. To investigate the cause of the phenomena did not yet, in the present case, lie within his task.

In our time, however, when workers more and more arrive at consciousness about their situation and their rights, and will in the future participate with irresistible force in the shaping of the state and society -- now the question of the natural distribution of income becomes a question of life and death for the continued existence of states and civil society.

When I here and in the sequel refer particularly to Adam Smith's work, although through Ricardo, Say, Rau, Hermann, Nebenius, and others, A. Smith's doctrines have been variously expanded, corrected, and more systematically presented, this is for the two reasons:

1) because my investigations have their roots in Smith's work and were begun at a time when the works of the named scholars had not yet appeared, or at least had not yet come to my attention;

2) because A. Smith's work in most essential points still forms the foundation of political economy.

Since my investigations immediately join those of A. Smith, and begin where they appear to me deficient, it is in the nature of the matter that I must frequently appear critically and correctively against A. Smith. Since, on the other hand, the much in which I agree with A. Smith remains unmentioned, this can easily take on the appearance of non-recognition or even of presumption.

But this is very far from me, and scarcely anyone can have a greater veneration for this genius than the author of this writing. Precisely in the fact that I regard the correction and expansion of Smith's doctrines as an advancement of science and make them the subject of my investigations lies a proof of the high esteem in which I hold A. Smith.

Had Euclid left his Elements unwritten because he could not prove his 11th axiom, posterity would have lost much, and geometry would have developed much later.

Had A. Smith, perceiving that his doctrines on wages, interest rate, and land rent were really only a representation of what exists, not the grasping of a law determining these factors, immersed himself in the depths of this investigation, he would perhaps not have completed his immortal work.

Through the great Herschelian telescope, the nebulae visible to the naked eye in the firmament were resolved into star clusters, i.e., world systems, but now other previously unseen nebulae appeared. Through the giant telescope constructed in our days, the Herschelian nebulae have again been resolved into star clusters, but at the same time nebulae have again been revealed that were still invisible to Herschel.

How many world systems may yet lie beyond the limit to which the giant telescope carries the eye!

But infinite like the universe is also science. As there the strengthening of vision leads to the discovery of new world systems but also to new mysteries: so too, with discoveries in science, new, previously unsuspected problems are revealed to the mind's eye.

After A. Smith has shed light on so many subjects of civil life and saved his successors the time and effort of independent research on these matters, these successors, though less gifted, are obligated to fill the gaps he left in knowledge and to bring new problems into the field of vision.

### Section 4.

Wages.

When one looks at the unequal distribution of worldly goods and considers how poorly the toilsome physical labors of the day laborer, which are at the same time the most indispensable, are paid: the questions must force themselves upon everyone who has attained the intellectual freedom to subject the impressions and prejudices imbibed with mother's milk to examination and to inquire after their grounds:

1) Why does the landowner, without effort and labor, receive a rent from his soil; why cannot wages rise so high that the previous land rent is divided among the workers, who seemingly have a much more just claim to it?

2) Is the low remuneration of manual labor founded in the nature of industry and agriculture, and thus in accordance with the will of Providence, or has the present condition been brought about by force and oppression from which the working class cannot again extricate itself?

Among the various modes of inquiry through which we hope to obtain enlightenment on this subject, the investigation of the question "What are the consequences of a rise in wages?" seems most immediately and directly likely to lead to the goal.

In reality, however, the circumstances of commercial life are so interwoven and complicated that the eye loses itself in them before the ultimate consequences of a rise in wages are recognized. In answering the above question, we therefore turn first to the isolated state, where all conditions lie before us as simply as possible.

At the border of the cultivated plain of the isolated state, where the soil yields no rent and the estate's return is limited to the interest on the capital embedded in the buildings, etc., a rise in wages must cause land rent to become negative, i.e., to sink below zero.

But when the cultivation of the soil is durably associated with a loss for its owner, he will erect no new buildings but will abandon the estate as soon as the old buildings threaten to collapse. The soil then lies waste, and the cultivation of the soil retreats to the distance from the city where the previous land rent suffices to cover the amount of the increased wage.

The workers from the now-abandoned zone must seek work and sustenance in the regions closer to the city, where a higher wage can be paid at the expense of land rent. But on the estates in these regions, so many people are already employed that the labor product of the last-employed worker only just covers the wage he receives. If still more workers are to be employed, cultivation methods must be applied that are less profitable and do not pay for themselves at the previous wage level. The additional workers, too, can therefore find employment only if they are willing to work for an even lower wage than the previous one. Necessity will compel them to accept the lower wage, and through competition, the wage of the workers already long settled there will also be depressed.

The attempt to raise wages thus produces the opposite effect, and the condition of the workers is only made worse thereby.

We thus arrive at the result that the low wage is founded in the nature of industry, and that a raise is impossible.

One can, however, arrive at this result by many other paths and through other chains of reasoning, and so it becomes understandable how the view that nothing is due to the worker except what is necessary for his subsistence has been able to spread so widely and take such deep root even among the learned.

Blanqui (in his History of Political Economy, translated by Buss, vol. 2, p. 162) says of Say:

"He followed the prejudice of his contemporaries, who regarded wages as sufficient not because they permitted living, but because they prevented dying."

But if we do not tire in thinking and do not rest content with the view we have obtained, but pursue to the bottom the chain of reasoning by which we arrived at that result: then it turns out that we arrived at this result only because we regarded the level of the interest rate -- which underlies the construction of the isolated state -- as inviolable, as unalterable.

But if the interest rate is lowered, if the capitalist receives smaller revenue from his capital: then even at the border of the cultivated plain wages can be raised without the cultivation of the soil ceasing, and without even a single worker becoming superfluous and without bread.

With this, those chains of reasoning have lost their basis and their entire hold.

The question of improving the condition of the workers thus reduces, in its simplest form, to this:

Can not the interest rate be lowered in order to allow the worker a greater share of his labor product, and thereby raise his wage?

The level of the interest rate, however, also cannot be arbitrary, not merely accidental; rather, lawfulness must prevail here as well.

We are thereby led directly to the conclusion that the determination of the natural wage depends on knowledge of the laws by which the level of the interest rate, and its relationship to wages, is determined.

With this, we now enter upon the threshold of a difficult and intricate investigation.

Since a fragment written as early as the year 1826, concerning the interest rate, develops more fully the stated problem and the questions to be solved, I communicate this fragment here first.

### Section 5.

On the Level of the Interest Rate, in Dialogical Form.

A. Can you tell me why the interest rate is now at this place 5 percent, why it is not 2 or even 10 percent?

B. The interest rate, like the price of every commodity, is determined by the relationship of supply to demand. If the interest rate is 5 percent, this proves that at this rate supply and demand are in equilibrium. Were the interest rate to rise through accidental influences to 10 percent, supply would increase, demand would decrease, and this would cause the rate to fall. The reverse would occur if the interest rate had momentarily fallen to 2 percent.

A. This answer corresponds to what we find in the political-economic writings on this subject; but it does not satisfy me, for it gives only the phenomenon, not the reason for the phenomenon. That supply and demand are in equilibrium when the interest rate has become constant, e.g., 5 percent, is self-evident; but I want to know why supply and demand are in equilibrium at precisely 5 and not at 2 or 10 percent.

B. This depends on the size of the existing national capital. The richer a nation, the lower the interest rate, and conversely, the poorer, the higher. Therefore the interest rate falls with increasing wealth, remains constant when wealth is stationary, and rises when national wealth is declining.

A. These are propositions derived from experience, which as such have their value; but they again give only the phenomenon, not the reason for the phenomenon. For why is the interest rate lower in rich nations, higher in poor ones?

B. Nothing is easier to answer. For just as an abundance of goods produces low prices, so too an abundance of capital produces a low interest rate.

A. But in this way we are always turning in circles. I must now, to cut through these circular arguments, put the question to you: from what cause does the abundance of goods and capital arise?

B. Thrift, industry, and skill produce an abundance of goods and thus also of capital.

A. Good, I must accept these qualities of man as sources of national wealth; but will two nations that possess these qualities in equally high degree always stand at the same level of wealth and have an interest rate of equal height?

B. No, not that. The application of equal forces to good and to poor soil, in a harsh and in a mild climate, under a despotic government oppressing its subjects with taxes, and under a government that lets freedom and lawfulness prevail -- must produce a very different result. The mental qualities of man and the character of the object to which they are applied jointly affect the magnitude of the product.

A. Suppose now that England and North America had inhabitants of equal national character, and soil, climate, and constitution were equal in both countries -- does it follow that the relative national wealth, i.e., the share of total wealth falling to each person, and the interest rate in both countries must be equally high?

B. No; for England is a country that has been highly cultivated for centuries, while North America has only recently been inhabited by civilized peoples, still possesses great stretches of fertile but uncultivated land that permit a wide and useful application of capital -- and therefore the interest rate here must be higher than in England.

A. So it is not the mental powers of man and the object to which they are applied that alone decide the magnitude of relative national wealth and the interest rate; rather, when in two countries both factors are equal, the duration during which both countries have been inhabited enters as a third factor regulating the interest rate.

Let us now consider more closely what difference exists between a country that has been inhabited for a longer and one for a shorter time -- given equality of climate, soil, and inhabitants: it appears that in the former, not only the fertile but also the sandy soil and the scarcely rewarding hills are cultivated, while in the latter only the fertile valleys have as yet been subjected to cultivation -- where the same human labor is rewarded with a far greater product than on sandy and hilly soil.

From this observation of the conditions actually prevailing in reality, we can now conclude:

1) that the interest rate rises when labor becomes more rewarding, i.e., produces a greater product;

2) that it makes a great difference in the level of the interest rate whether the same national capital is distributed over 1 or 2 square miles, that therefore not the absolute but only the relative national capital, i.e., the national wealth compared with the size of the cultivated land and with the population, exerts an essential influence on the level of the interest rate.

But with all these discussions we have now arrived at the point where we can specify the circumstances under which the interest rate is higher or lower.

But can you, for any country whose conditions you know thoroughly, determine -- without resorting to experience -- how high the interest rate, expressed in numbers, must be?

B. The level of the interest rate is conditioned by the magnitude of the utility that a capital invested in agriculture and in trade provides. A capital applied to the clearing of rich soil can yield interest of 10 percent or even higher. But once the rich soil has all been taken into possession, and the clearing turns to soil of lesser quality, the utility of the invested capital gradually sinks to 5, 4, or even 3 percent.

The level of the interest rate, expressed in numbers, therefore depends on the quality of the soil not yet brought under cultivation, and on the degree to which improvements on the already cultivated soil have progressed.

A. This explanation, borrowed from the acute Ricardo, is applicable to ordinary conditions and practically useful; but it is insufficient for establishing a universal law.

One need only imagine oneself transported to a vast, hitherto uncultivated plain, which is uniformly fertile in every spot and as yet no one's property, and then ask: "How will the relationship between the interest rate and wages take shape here, and what level will the interest rate reach when this plain is brought under cultivation?"

That explanation, which is based on the superiority of one soil over another, becomes wholly useless here, where no such superiority exists, and thereby shows how little it satisfies the demands that must be made of a universal law.

Besides this inadequacy, that explanation carries another deficiency within itself.

For in applying it, we must always resort to experience and draw our knowledge from it. But we do not wish to know what has happened; rather, we wish to know the reasons from which what has happened has arisen.

B. I do not entirely understand what you mean by that.

A. An example will make this clear.

It is said that the price of every product, of every commodity, is determined by the relationship of supply to demand.

Now, whoever feels satisfied by this explanation can never learn the price of valuable objects other than from experience; he cannot scientifically determine the price of any product or manufactured article; he has surrendered price determination to blind forces, and need not trouble himself over the reasons why the price is precisely this one and no other. But whoever penetrates deeper will recognize that the relationship between supply and demand is merely the outward manifestation of a deeper-lying cause. When a market is flooded with goods, this is not a mere accident, but a sign that the prices previously paid here were so high that a greater production of these goods became profitable. The earlier excessive price is thus the cause of the surplus, which now produces prices that are too low. In this way, market prices remain in constant fluctuation; but the production price is -- as Adam Smith aptly expresses it -- the center toward which market prices constantly gravitate. If market price and production price once coincide, then there is no further cause for either too great or too small a production, and supply and demand are then in equilibrium. The production price is thus the regulator of the market price, and the latter must, despite countless deviations, in the average of a long period coincide again with the former.

My question is now this: is there, for the price of a capital, i.e., for the level of the interest rate, such a regulator as the price of commodities finds in the production costs -- and what is the measure of the production costs of capital?

B. This I am unable to answer, and as it seems to me, everything that has hitherto been accomplished in political economy is insufficient to give a satisfactory answer to this question.

A. But the matter is of great importance. As long as we are not clear about this, we cannot even present the production price of commodities scientifically: for among the elements that determine the price of commodities are also the interest on the capital employed; if we know this only from experience, i.e., from the phenomenon, then we mix into what we wish to explain and scientifically establish the outward phenomenon itself as a cause, and thus turn in a circular argument that leads to no result.

B. But the question is whether such a determination of the interest rate, as you desire it, is possible, and whether a connection between the interest rate and wages actually exists.

A. Everywhere we look, we see the interest rate and wages expressed in definite numbers. But the interest rate that has formed in this way is not the work of chance or of blind forces, but has arisen from the cooperation of men who, all guided by an intelligent self-interest, work collectively -- like bees building their cells -- on a great work. Since self-interest is here guided by reason, what self-interest has produced must in turn be comprehensible through reason. It is therefore not a matter of discovering new laws, but rather what has already happened is to be understood, and thereby it is to become clear how it has happened.

What the reason of countless men has produced -- each of whom works on the great edifice but oversees only the place where he himself works -- is to be grasped by the reason of the individual and take shape in his mind as an overview and clarity.

### Section 6. Definitions and Assumptions.

**1. Measure of Value.**

It is customary to express the income of an estate, as well as the costs associated with farming, in money, even though a part of the expenditures -- for example, seed grain, horse feed, and much else -- has never entered commerce and has not been exchanged for money. Now, however, a large part of the money received for grain and other products serves only to purchase other necessities -- for example, building materials, smithing and saddlery work, etc. In reality, therefore, these necessities are exchanged for grain, and in truth the farmer has nothing but his products for which he can exchange the goods he needs. Money serves here merely as a medium of exchange.

The sum of money received for grain in a year, compared with the sum of grain sold, yields the price of a bushel of rye, when all grain is converted to rye. The sum of money spent on any necessity -- for example, smithing work -- divided by the price of a bushel of rye, yields the number of bushels of rye that one has had to give up to obtain that necessity. In this way, the accounting of revenue and expenditure of an estate could be conducted entirely in bushels of rye. Such an accounting would, incidentally, shed a brighter light on many points: one could see at a glance how, with falling grain prices and unchanged taxes to the state, these take away a far greater portion of the estate's revenue, and have thus in effect been raised; further, how the fall of grain prices with unchanged money wages of the workers raises the real wage, and provides the worker a far greater share of the estate's revenue, etc.

For our investigation we now take rye as the measure of value and a Berlin bushel of this grain as the unit.

**2. Wages of Labor.**

The free worker as a rule owns some livestock -- a cow, pigs, and poultry -- the necessary household goods, and a part of the tools -- spades, axes, etc. -- with which he works. The wage he receives is therefore not merely a reward for his labor, but is at the same time compensation for the use of the capital he owns, and thus encompasses the wage for the labor itself and the interest on the capital.

Here, however, our endeavor is directed at ascertaining the wage for labor in itself, and what I henceforth call wages is that part of the wage which remains after deduction of the interest on that capital.

To judge the magnitude of a worker's income, the wage he receives for a day's work is not the proper measure, because:

1) the daily wage usually varies with the different seasons and types of work -- higher in summer than in winter, higher for harvest work than for planting;

2) it has a great influence on the worker's earnings whether he has work and income throughout the entire year, or finds employment only during part of the year;

3) in addition to the money wage paid to him as a daily wage, the worker frequently receives emoluments -- such as housing, a garden, cow pasture, firewood, etc. -- either free of charge or at a low price; and

4) it has a great influence on a day laborer's earnings whether and to what degree his wife and underage children find work and income.

To obtain a more definite measure for wages, I combine what the worker, together with his wife and his underage children up to the age of 14 years, receives in money and emoluments for work during an entire year, deduct from this the interest on the capital contained in household goods, tools, etc., and call the remainder "the wage for the annual labor of a worker's family." For brevity, I shall hereafter write: "wage for 1 Y.L. [Year's Labor] of a man."

The amount of the wage thus determined, converted in value to Berlin bushels of rye and expressed in bushels of rye, I designate by "A."

**3. The Product of Labor.**

If from the gross revenue of an estate one deducts everything required for the maintenance of the buildings and inventory in the same condition and value, what is necessary for seed and livestock feed, as well as administrative costs and the trade profit of the entrepreneur, and in general deducts everything necessary for the maintenance of the enterprise, and which benefits neither the landowner in case of a lease, nor the workers: then I call the surplus that results, and which is to be divided between the landowner and the workers, the product of labor; and this divided by the number of workers employed in producing it yields the magnitude of the product of labor of one man, which I designate by "p."

In commercial enterprises, the net product of labor, which remains after the entrepreneur has drawn administrative costs and trade profit, is divided between the owner of the capital invested in the enterprise and the workers.

**4. The Workers.**

If on an estate or a complex of estates one divides the work performed and the total product of labor by the number of workers, the result shows what one worker has on average accomplished and produced, and according to this average one draws up one's estimates and calculations. In such a calculation, the great diversity among individuals with respect to abilities and performance is not the subject of consideration. The performance of the whole is represented by the average result and finds its measure therein.

In this sense it is thus permissible to abstract from the inequality among workers, and to assume all workers of the same class to be perfectly equal in strength, skill, diligence, conscientiousness, and so forth.

This assumption underlies our next investigations.

**5. Means of Subsistence.**

What a worker's family necessarily requires for its maintenance depends very much on the number of children it raises, and admits of no determination unless something definite is assumed about this.

Since our purpose is to investigate the laws governing wages and the interest rate for the stationary state of civil society, we must also regard the number of workers as remaining constant, and assume that the working families as a whole raise just enough children as are needed to replace the workers lost through age and death. The labor force thereby appears as a non-depreciating, unchangeable quantity.

The sum of the means of subsistence that a worker's family -- under this limitation -- necessarily requires to maintain its capacity for work, I set for each family equal in value to *a* bushels of rye annually.

These means of subsistence designated by "a" we regard as a known quantity given by experience.

What we here consider as necessary for maintenance must not be confused with what, in Blanqui's expression, suffices to prevent dying: for through these means of subsistence, the worker is to have not only his life but also his capacity for work preserved. On the other hand, all enjoyments not absolutely necessary for this are excluded from what we designate by "a."

If from the wages = A one deducts what the worker must necessarily consume, i.e., *a*, there results for the worker a surplus of A - a, for which we write *y*. It is then A = a + y.

**6. Capital.**

By "capital" I understand: the product brought forth by human labor with the cooperation of natural forces, which is useful for increasing the effectiveness of human labor and is applied to that end, and which is separable from the land -- even if, as with trees and buildings, it involves a change of form.

**7. Interest Rate or Rate of Interest.**

In the interest received for a loaned-out capital, two components are as a rule contained, namely:

1) the compensation which the borrower pays for the temporary use of the capital, under the condition of returning it in equal value;

2) the insurance premium for the possible and, in a longer period, frequently occurring loss of the capital itself.

What I call "interest rate" in this work encompasses only the first of these two components.

The interest rate in this sense can in reality only manifest itself and become known through the interest paid for capital lent against first mortgage and considered safe from loss.

The interest rate determined in this way I designate by "z."

**8. Land Rent.**

The concept of land rent is discussed in detail in Part 1, 2nd edition, Section 5a. To spare my readers the trouble of looking it up, I note here briefly: that by land rent I understand not, like Adam Smith, Say, and others, the estate income, but the rent which remains from the estate income after deduction of the interest on the value of the buildings, the forests, the enclosures, and in general all objects of value separable from the land.

### Section 7. Entrepreneur's Profit, Industrial Remuneration, Trade Profit.

**a. Entrepreneur's Profit.**

If from the profit that the entrepreneur of an enterprise draws, one deducts:

1) the interest on the capital employed,

2) the insurance premium for shipwreck, fire hazard, hail damage, etc.,

3) the salary of a clerk, administrator, etc., who undertakes the management of the business, the bookkeeping of the entrepreneur, and the supervision,

then there usually remains for the entrepreneur a surplus -- and this is the entrepreneur's profit.

Now, in what is this founded, and what is the reason that it is not destroyed by the competition of the entrepreneurs themselves -- since the employment of the capital is compensated by the interest charged, the risk involved in the business is offset by the insurance contributions deducted, and the work and trouble of management are compensated by the administrator's salary?

**Answer to this question.**

There exists no insurance company against every danger connected with undertaking a business; a portion of the risk must always be borne by the entrepreneur himself. Through the mere fall in the prices of products, manufactured goods, and trade commodities, the leaseholder of an estate, the manufacturer, and the merchant alike can lose their entire fortune -- and against this danger no insurance company exists.

Now, one might object:

Whoever at the beginning of his enterprise bases his calculation on the hitherto prevailing average prices of products or commodities may indeed lose through a fall in price below the previous average; but equally often, perhaps more often, he will gain through a rise in price -- the risk is compensated by the prospect of gain, and consequently no recompense is needed for it.

An insurance company can proceed according to this principle, but not the individual. For precisely in the difference between a society, in which each shareholder risks only a part of his fortune, and the entrepreneur, who exposes his entire fortune to loss, lies the reason why an entrepreneur's profit must exist.

Whoever possesses a fortune of 10,000 thalers can readily stake one thaler on a card without endangering his happiness; the pleasure of winning compensates the displeasure of losing. But if he stakes all his 10,000 thalers on a single card, the doubling of his fortune in the favorable case can never add as much to his happiness as, in the unfavorable case, the loss of his entire fortune would take away from his enjoyment and happiness.

Whoever possesses the means to bear the expenses that the acquisition of knowledge and training for government service requires has the choice either to devote himself to the civil service, or -- with equal aptitude for both professions -- to become a business entrepreneur. If he chooses the former, then after his appointment his subsistence is secured for his entire life; if he chooses the latter, an unfavorable market condition can quite soon deprive him of his fortune, and his lot in life is then to become a wage laborer.

What, then, given such unequal prospects for the future, could motivate him to become an entrepreneur -- if not the probability of gain being far greater than that of loss?

To the extent that the loss of a part or the whole of one's fortune is more painful, robs one's happiness and contentment more than an equal increase of fortune can add to one's happiness -- to that extent the probability of gain in business enterprises must be greater than that of loss.

Adam Smith, and with him most English writers, combine the interest on the capital employed with the entrepreneur's profit under the term "profit."

With this conflation of two potencies springing from such different sources, insight into the connection between wages and the interest rate becomes nearly impossible. Say was, so far as I know, the first to uncover this deficiency in Smith's system.

**b. Industrial Remuneration.**

For the arrangement and management of business affairs in an enterprise, as well as for the supervision of the workers employed therein, it appears at first glance that the entrepreneur is entitled only to a compensation equal to the salary he would have to pay an administrator, bookkeeper, or supervisor to relieve him of this trouble and care.

But the performances of the entrepreneur working on his own account and the salaried substitute, even if both possess equal abilities and knowledge, are nevertheless very different.

In such times when the vicissitudes of market conditions bring great losses to the business, and the fortune as well as the honor of the entrepreneur are at stake, his mind is filled with the one thought of how he can avert the misfortune -- and sleep flees from his bed.

It is different in such a case with the salaried substitute. When he has worked honestly during the day and comes home tired in the evening, he falls asleep with the consciousness of duty fulfilled.

But the sleepless nights of the entrepreneur are not unproductive. Here he forms plans and arrives at ideas for averting his misfortune that would remain hidden from the salaried administrator -- however earnestly the latter may strive to fulfill his duty -- because they arise only from the highest exertion of all mental powers concentrated on one point.

Necessity is the mother of inventions, and so the entrepreneur too is driven by his distress to become an inventor and discoverer in his sphere.

Just as the inventor of a new useful machine rightly draws the surplus that the application of it yields compared with the older machine, and enjoys this surplus as the reward for his invention -- so too what the entrepreneur produces more than the salaried administrator through his greater mental exertion must fall to him as the reward for his industry.

The entrepreneur working on his own account and at his own risk possesses, other qualities being equal, a greater capacity for performance than the salaried substitute -- however great the latter's conscientiousness may be -- and this is the reason why the entrepreneur, beyond the administrative costs, is entitled to a compensation that we call "industrial remuneration."

A similar relationship shows itself even in common manual labor. The strength of the worker who loads earth by the piece is reinforced and steeled by the feeling that every spadeful benefits him and increases his earnings, while the conscientious day laborer, who must constantly combat the toil and exertion of the work through the moral compulsion he imposes on himself, tires far sooner, and with equal strength and capability achieves a lesser day's work than the piece worker.

This observation may also serve to temper the judgment of workers when we find that they accomplish so very much less when working by the day than they otherwise achieved working by the piece -- in that we must not attribute this solely to laziness and forgetfulness of duty (to which one is all too inclined), but must also partly ascribe it to the different capacity for performance, which does not depend on the workers' will.

**c. Trade Profit.**

What the entrepreneur draws beyond the interest on the capital employed and the administrative costs -- namely the entrepreneur's profit and the industrial remuneration -- I combine for simplification of expression under the term "trade profit."

Capital can only yield utility, and is in the narrower sense only capital, when it is productively invested; and the magnitude of this utility determines the level of the interest rate when lending capital.

Productive investment presupposes a commercial enterprise, and this presupposes an entrepreneur.

The enterprise yields the entrepreneur, after reimbursement of all associated outlays and costs, a net return. This net return contains two components: trade profit and capital utility. After deducting the trade profit from the net return, the magnitude of the capital utility that determines the interest rate is obtained.

After the separation and determination of the utility of the capital invested in an enterprise thus effected, it shall be permissible in what follows to abstract from the entrepreneur himself, and to regard him as the business manager compensated by the trade profit on behalf of the capitalist; the entrepreneur, however, is driven by his own interest to strive for the highest capital utility.

(In the product of labor as defined in Section 6, No. 3, the trade profit is no longer contained but has already been separated out, and therefore in the question of the distribution of the product of labor, only workers, capitalists, and landowners come into consideration.)

### Section 8. Formation of Capital Through Labor.

The first human beings who set foot on the earth would have had to perish if provident nature had not produced an abundance of wild-growing plants whose fruits serve man for sustenance.

If we wish to envision the origin of capital, and the state of society in which man, furnished with no capital, can subsist solely through his labor and even create some capital, we must transport ourselves in thought to the tropical countries: where the fruits of the plantain, the coconut palm, and the breadfruit tree, together with sweet potatoes, maize, and other tropical fruits, suffice to nourish human beings; where a hut of tree trunks, renewed annually, covered with the leaves of the plantain, provides sufficient shelter; and where plantain leaves suffice for clothing.

"Since the earliest infancy of human culture, plantain cultivation has been found in all continents between the tropics, as far as tradition and history reach," says Humboldt in his *Views of Nature*.

The three named species of trees are produced by nature itself there, without any human intervention. Sweet potatoes and maize, however, require cultivation, that is, human labor. On the humus-rich, loose soil, pulling up the plants covering the earth and breaking up the ground with a stick will suffice, and thus no implements containing capital need be employed.

The gradual development of a people transported to the tropical countries can be conceived from two different points of view.

a) We regard this people as poor not only in capital but also in knowledge, and unacquainted with the inventions and discoveries by which in our day manufacturing and production have been so greatly advanced.

Capital formation can then proceed only very slowly, is dependent not only on labor but also on the progress of intelligence, and is thus the work of two different potencies. The development that takes place here belongs to cultural history and yields no results for the purpose of our investigation.

b) We imagine a people endowed with all the abilities, knowledge, and skills of the civilized European nations, transported to a tropical country, which, however, possesses no capital and therefore no tools, and ask how capital formation takes shape here with the intelligence of the people remaining constant.

Here two different cases can occur:

1) This people is in commerce with other nations and can exchange its gathered and saved stores of fruits for other articles, particularly tools and machines.

In this way, however, the product of labor in itself would be exchanged for other products in which wages, interest, and land rent are contained, and we would then gain no insight into what we seek to investigate.

2) This people has no commercial intercourse with other nations, is separated from the rest of the world, and capital formation proceeds from within without any external influence.

This latter case we lay as the foundation of our next investigation, and further assume:

1) that in the bosom of the mountains of this country, all metals are present that European industry needs for its products and manufactures;

2) that this tribe is numerous enough to introduce the division of labor, as it exists in Europe, as soon as only the capital required for it is available;

3) that the land inhabited by this people is everywhere of equal fertility and at the same time so extensive that every inhabitant can take land into possession free of charge.

Among this people, which possesses no capital, and where the land has no exchange value, there is also no relationship of masters and servants; everyone without distinction is a worker and must earn his livelihood through labor.

Here we thus have the simplest conditions before our eyes, and if we subject these to contemplation, we may most hope to gain insight into the connection between wages and interest.

Now, however, in transferring the theater of our observations in thought to the tropical countries, where our grain crops do not thrive and do not constitute the principal nourishment of man, it is immediately evident that rye cannot here be the measure of value nor the standard for the means of subsistence that people require.

Here we must take the means of subsistence themselves, which the worker uses during a year, as the unit and as the standard for the magnitude of the product.

These means of subsistence I now designate by "S," and the hundredth part thereof by "c," so that S = 100c.

Suppose now that the worker, if he is diligent and thrifty, can produce 10 percent more through the labor of his hands than he needs for his necessary maintenance, thus 1.1S or 110c per year: then after deducting what he must spend on his livelihood, he saves 110c - 100c = 10c.

He can thus in the course of 10 years accumulate a reserve from which he can live for one year without working; or he can also devote an entire year of his labor to the manufacture of useful implements, that is, to the creation of capital.

Let us now follow him in his capital-creating labor.

With a broken flint he works the wood into bow and arrow; a fish bone serves the arrow as its point. From the trunk of the plantain, or the fibrous shell of the coconut, ropes and twine are made, the former serving as the bowstring, the latter for making fishing nets.

In the following year he then turns again to the production of provisions; but he is now equipped with bow, arrows, and nets; his labor with the help of this equipment becomes much more rewarding, his product of labor much larger.

Suppose his product -- after deducting what he must spend on maintaining the equipment in equally good condition -- rises thereby from 110c to 150c: then he can save 50c in one year, and he now needs only 2 years of producing provisions to devote once again an entire year to the manufacture of bows and nets.

He himself can make no use of these, since the equipment made in the previous year suffices for his needs; but he can lend it to a worker who has hitherto worked without capital.

This second worker had hitherto produced 110c; if he now borrows the capital on which the capital-producing worker has spent a year's labor, his product, if he maintains the borrowed equipment in its full value and returns it, is 150c. The additional product by means of the capital is thus 40c.

This worker can therefore pay a rent of 40c for the borrowed capital, which the capital-producing worker permanently receives for his one year's labor.

Here we encounter the origin and basis of interest and its relationship to capital.

**As the wage of labor stands in relation to the magnitude of the rent that the same labor creates when directed toward capital production: so stand capital and interest.**

In the present case, the wage for 1 Y.L. = 110c; the rent that the capital produced from one year's labor yields is 40c.

The ratio is thus 110c : 40c = 100 : 36.4, and the interest rate is 36.4 percent.

But -- one may object -- the rent of 40c is not the result of one year's labor; for the worker needed 10 years to produce the means of subsistence he consumed during capital creation. The rent is therefore the result of 10 + 1 = 11 years, which for 1 Y.L. gives only 40/11c = 3.64c of rent.

To this one must reply:

The worker without capital receives for his year's labor in his product a reward of 110c. Of this, however, he must use 100c for his maintenance, and for his exertion itself he is rewarded with only 10c.

We must therefore distinguish two components in the worker's wage, namely:

1) what the worker must spend on his maintenance to remain capable of working; and

2) what he receives for his exertion itself.

According to the above assumptions in numbers, the worker receives for his exertion during one year -- which I shall henceforth designate as "1 Y. Exert." -- when this is directed toward the production of consumable articles, 10c; and when it is devoted to capital production, 3.64c of rent.

The ratio between the two is thus 10 : 3.64, that is, 100 : 36.4.

We thus obtain the same result for the ratio between capital and interest whether we take the year's labor or the year's exertion as the measure.

Now, when the point has been reached where every worker of the entire people is furnished with a capital of 1 Y.L., will capital production continue or cease?

Let us set alongside the worker who possesses bow, arrows, and nets another worker who is also only sparingly furnished with capital, but yet possesses spades, axes, and nails, who digs the earth instead of breaking it up with a stick, who works the wood with an axe instead of with a broken flint: we find, with equal skill, equal diligence, equal exertion, and equal physical strength of both, yet a very different result of the labor. The second worker, furnished with spade and axe, will at the end of the year have a far greater product of his labor to show than the first.

But spades and axes are themselves products of human labor, and in the great utility these tools provide lies the incentive for their production, and thus for further capital creation.

In the manufacture of bows, arrows, etc., the individual worker needed no help from others. In the extraction and working of iron, however, a division of labor must take place, and we must here regard the capital-producing workers as a society that has united for a common purpose and that divides the total product of its labor among its members.

Let us now assume that the whole people has gradually been furnished with the said iron implements, and that what each worker uses and employs of these is the product of the year's labor of a man engaged in capital production: then everyone now works with a capital of 2 Y.L.

At this level of capital stock, the tools that make human labor more effective are still very incomplete. Capital production will therefore continue, and the nation will successively be furnished with a capital of 3, 4, 5, and more Y.L. for each worker; and the product of labor of a man will grow more and more with the increasing capital.

Here the question now forces itself upon us:

Will the increase of the product of labor keep pace with the increase of capital, that is, stand in direct proportion to it -- will, for example, the application of a capital of 3 Y.L. yield three times the rent of a capital of 1 Y.L., thus 3 x 40c = 120c?

We know that not every capital invested in implements, machines, buildings, etc., promotes labor and makes it more effective to the same degree.

The construction and use of a mill increases the product of labor of a man employed in grinding grain by at least twentyfold; or one man with a mill can grind more grain, and grind it better, than 20 men crushing it by hand between stones.

A man who has at his disposal a plow drawn by two horses can plow more land than 30 men can dig over with the spade.

In the construction and manufacture of mills and plows, capital-producing labor thus finds a useful, highly rewarding application. But once these have been produced in sufficient quantity for the demand, the manufacture of more plows and mills will yield not merely lower rent than the first ones produced, but no rent at all.

However useful an instrument or machine may be, there is always a limit where the multiplication of it ceases to be useful and to yield a rent.

Once this limit is reached, the capital-producing labor must turn to the production of other objects of value, even if these are less useful and yield a smaller rent than those produced earlier.

The capital-producing worker will therefore, pursuing his own interest, first direct his labor to the manufacture of those tools and machines that most invigorate his strength and provide his labor with the greatest success; then, when these are available in sufficient quantity, direct his labor to the production of implements and machines that are also very useful, but yet less effective and promote labor less than those produced first -- for which he must therefore accept a smaller rent when lending them out.

Here is revealed the basis of the phenomenon so important for our further investigation: **that each newly invested, additional capital applied in an enterprise or trade yields smaller rents than the previously invested capital.**

This phenomenon also manifests itself everywhere in practical life, where not the year's labor but money is the measure of capital. Very clearly this can be observed in improvements on a landed estate, where the first thousand thalers applied to improvements -- for example, to marling -- can yield 15 percent, while the second thousand thalers perhaps yield only 10 percent, the third only 5 percent; and where, with further capital investments -- for example, deepening the topsoil beyond a certain point -- one receives only 3, 2, or even 1 percent interest.

A retail dealer, or also a manufacturer, who sells his goods in the vicinity of his residence and employs a capital of 10,000 thalers in his business at 5 percent, can apply an additional capital of 1,000 thalers only when his sales increase, when he sells goods in a wider circle around his residence. But this he can achieve, other things remaining equal, only by reducing the price of his goods -- which, however, results in a diminution of the utility of the last invested capital.

### Section 9. Formation of Wages and the Interest Rate.

Whether one expresses the capital in years of labor, taking the expenditure of human effort that the production of the capital has required as the measure, or expresses the capital in money, which is itself a product of human labor and capital -- whichever of the two measures one applies, the newly added capital, as we have seen above, increases the product of labor of man in a lesser degree than the previously invested capital.

The question now arises, through what series this diminishing effectiveness of capital can be represented.

Later, when the demands that must be made of such a series are more fully before us, the investigation of the relationship between capital and product of labor will become the subject of a special inquiry. Here the need has first presented itself to find a series whose terms become progressively smaller, and this demand is met by the geometric series whose common ratio is a fraction, such as 9/10, 9/10, 9/10, 9/10.

To be able to connect our further investigations to definite numbers and thereby develop them further, I tentatively assume that the product of labor of one man is increased:

by the application of the 1st capital of 1 Y.L. by 40c,
of the 2nd by 9/10 x 40c = 36c,
of the 3rd by 9/10 x 36c = 32.4c,
and so forth.

**Influence of the increase of capital on the wage of labor.**

In the nation we have before our eyes here, there are as yet no capitalists who have others work for them; rather everyone works for himself. The workers divide themselves, however, into two classes, namely: 1) those who occupy themselves with capital production, and 2) those who work on their own account with a borrowed capital.

Those belonging to the second class I shall call simply "workers." What these retain after deducting the interest on the borrowed capital from the product of labor is the wage of their labor.

If society stands at the level of prosperity where everyone is furnished with a capital of 1 Y.L., then the lenders receive a rent of 40c for the capital of 1 Y.L.

If capital production is then continued and things are brought to the point where 2 Y.L. of capital fall to each worker, then the lenders can receive for the second capital not 40c but only 36c, because the worker cannot utilize it for more than 36c, and would reject it altogether if more were demanded for it.

But will the workers now continue to pay 40c rent for the first capital of 1 Y.L., or will they pay only 36c, as for the second capital?

If any capital-producing worker who has finished creating the second capital offers it to a worker at 36c rent, then this worker, who has hitherto paid his creditor 40c for the capital of 1 Y.L., will terminate the dearer capital and accept the cheaper one instead. The capital-producing worker whose loaned capital has been terminated has meanwhile also completed the second capital, and now has two capitals to lend. But these capitals can find no application at all unless he resolves to accept 36c rent per Y.L. of capital. Since these capitals are then quite useless to him, he will have to agree to lend both the first and the second capital for 36c rent.

One may indeed object that the capital produced from the first Y.L. consists of implements of a different kind than the implements produced by the second Y.L., that one cannot replace the other, and consequently one cannot be determinative for the other.

But that is not the point here; rather, through the increase of capital, the compensation for labor directed toward capital production has fallen in the ratio of 40 to 36, and capital-producing labor is henceforth paid at 36c rent, whether it is directed toward the manufacture of bows and nets or of axes and spades; for if one branch of labor found a higher reward than the other, so many workers would turn to it that equilibrium would be restored.

Just as the price of a commodity cannot be different for different buyers, cannot be determined by the individual value it has for individual buyers, but must be set equally for all: so too the price of capital, i.e., the rent to be paid for it, cannot be fixed according to the utility that the capital as a whole provides to the borrower. Or, for commodities of equal value, for capitals whose production requires an equal quantum of labor, two different prices cannot prevail at the same time.

**The rent that the capital as a whole yields when lent out is determined by the utility of the last invested particle of capital.** This is one of the most important propositions in the theory of interest.

According to the above scale, the worker who works with a borrowed capital of 2 Y.L. earns:

- through his labor alone: 110c
- from the 1st capital: 40c
- from the 2nd capital: 36c
- His product of labor is thus: 186c
- Of this he must pay the capitalist for two capitals at 36c = 72c
- He retains: 114c

instead of retaining only 110c when using a capital of 1 Y.L.

If the worker employs a borrowed capital of 3 Y.L., his earnings are:

- through the labor itself: 110c
- the 1st capital: 40c
- the 2nd capital: 36c
- the 3rd capital: 32.4c
- In total: 218.4c
- Of this he pays the capitalist the rent of three capitals at 32.4c = 97.2c
- There remain for the worker: 121.2c

**The reduction of rent with the growth of capital thus benefits the worker and raises the wage of his labor.**

While in Europe the depressed condition of the working class is so frequently attributed to the increasing use of machines, in the social condition we have here before our eyes, the situation of the workers becomes ever more flourishing and splendid, the more extensive the application of machines becomes with the growth of capital. It seems paradoxical that through the wise utilization of natural forces and of machines, which so greatly promote labor, the lot of the lowest class of society should become more oppressive the more their labor simultaneously becomes more effective and rewarding.

Further investigation must lead us to the reason for this contradiction.

### Section 10. Influence of the Growth of Capital on the Interest Rate.

The interest rate results, as already shown above, from the ratio in which an equal quantity of labor -- for example, 1 Y.L. -- pays for itself in wages and in rents. Wages and rent stand here in the same ratio as invested capital to the interest arising from it.

If one works with a capital of 1 Y.L., the labor during one year pays for itself in wages at 110c, in rent at 40c; the ratio is 110 : 40, and the interest rate = 40/110 = 36.4 percent.

With the application of 2 Y.L. of capital, the wage amounts to 114c, the rent 36c, and the interest rate = 36/114 = 31.6 percent.

For 3 Y.L. of capital, the wage is 121.2c, the rent 32.4c, and the interest rate = 32.4/121.2 = 26.7 percent.

For 4 Y.L., the wage is 130.8c, the rent 29.2c, and the interest rate = 29.2/130.8 = 22.3 percent.

**Comparison of wages, rent, and interest rate with the growth of capital:**

| Capital | Wages | Rent | Interest Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 Y.L. | 110c | 40c | 36.4% |

With the growth of capital, the interest rate falls in a much greater proportion than the rent, because at the same time wages rise and the rent divided by wages yields the interest rate.

Here labor, by which the capital has been produced, is the measure of capital. In reality, capital is usually expressed and stated in money, and it is unusual to measure the size of a capital by the number of annual labors of a day laborer over which one has command by means of this capital, or that one can purchase with it -- although this would shed a far brighter light on the value of a capital in different countries and at different times than would a statement in money.

In the determination of the interest rate, however, it makes no difference if the capital is expressed in money instead of in Y.L.

Let, for example, c = 1 thaler; then the wage for 1 Y.L. = 110 thalers, the capital of 1 Y.L. is also = 110 thalers, and the rent that this capital yields = 40 thalers. The rent divided by the capital gives the interest rate; this is 40/110 = 36.4 percent.

In the same way, if one works with 2 Y.L. of capital, the interest rate comes out to 31.6 percent, as indeed cannot be otherwise with this method of calculation.

### Section 11. Influence of the Growth of Capital on the Magnitude of the Rent That Capital-Producing Labor Yields.

If the capital-producing worker, as we have seen, receives an ever smaller rent for each newly created capital that exceeds the existing demand, and if at the same time through this newly created capital he diminishes the value of his older capitals by the fall in income from them, then the question arises: what can induce him to continue with the production of capital?

We must here recall that capital is a product of labor, and that it is formed only from what the worker produces more than he consumes.

The smaller the surplus of the worker, the more years he must work, or -- if we imagine the workers in a social association -- the greater must be the number of workers, in order to create a reserve sufficient to support for one year a man who creates capital in the narrower sense, i.e., manufactures implements, builds houses, etc.

The acquisition of a house, whose construction requires the annual labor of 10 men, costs 20 years of exertion if the worker in one year earns as much as he needs for his maintenance during two years. If, for example, wages amount to 200c, the worker's maintenance 100c, and his annual surplus also 100c, then the construction of the house costs 10 x 200c = 2000c, and to save 2000c, 2000/100 = 20 men must work jointly for one year. The acquisition of the house thus costs the annual exertion of 20 men.

If, on the other hand, wages are only 110c, the surplus 10c, then the construction of the building costs 10 x 110c = 1100c, and the house can then be acquired only through the annual exertion of 1100/10 = 110 men.

**The production costs of capital can thus be stated and measured by the number of years of exertion required to obtain it.**

The production of capital becomes ever more costly the smaller the surplus of the worker, or the lower the wages are with consumption remaining the same.

High wages increase the production costs of commodities, but diminish the production costs of capital.

The purpose of the capital-producing worker is to obtain the highest possible rent for his year's labor. Now, on the one hand, with increased capital the interest rate falls, and thus the income from the capital; on the other hand, with capital, wages rise, and through increased wages the costs of capital production diminish.

In capital creation, two mutually limiting forces are thus at work -- and from this alone it can already be concluded with probability that there is a point in the increase of capital at which capital-producing labor yields the maximum rent.

Some numerical examples will bring this closer to view.

Let the capital amount to 2 Y.L.; then the product of labor from the labor itself is 110c, from the 1st capital 40c, from the 2nd capital 36c -- total 186c. Of this the worker must pay the capitalist for the borrowed capital of 2 Y.L.: 2 x 36c = 72c. There remain for the worker 114c. The capital-producing worker, if he himself possesses the capital with which he works, must yet charge interest on it, because he could have utilized it equally well by lending it out. Of the above 114c, the capital-producing worker spends 100c on his maintenance, and retains for his year's exertion a surplus of 14c. To accumulate a capital equal to the wage for 1 Y.L., he therefore needs 114/14 = 8.14 years. Thus 8.14 men, working collectively at capital production, produce a capital of 1 Y.L. This capital, when lent out, yields a rent of 36c. Distributed among 8.14 men, this gives each 4.42c of rent.

For 3 Y.L. of capital: the product of labor is 110 + 40 + 36 + 32.4 = 218.4c. Deducting interest for 3 capitals at 32.4 = 97.2c. There remain for the worker 121.2c. The worker's surplus amounts to 21.2c. To accumulate a capital equal to the wage for 1 Y.L., the annual exertion of 121.2/21.2 = 5.72 men is required. The rent for a capital of 1 Y.L. amounts to 32.4c. One worker thus receives for his year's exertion 32.4/5.72 = 5.66c of rent.

For 4 Y.L. of capital: the product of labor is 110 + 40 + 36 + 32.4 + 29.2 = 247.6c. Deducting interest of 4 capitals at 29.2c = 116.8c. There remain for the worker 130.8c, and the worker's surplus amounts to 30.8c. To accumulate a capital of 1 Y.L., which yields 29.2c rent, the annual exertion of 130.8/30.8 = 4.25 men is needed. One man thus acquires through his year's exertion a rent of 29.2/4.25 = 6.87c.

The rent of the capital-producing worker, which with the application of 2 Y.L. of capital amounts to only 4.42c, thus rises with 3 Y.L. of capital to 5.66c and with 4 capitals of 1 Y.L. to 6.87c.

We see from this that the capital-producing workers, with increased capital and a falling interest rate, nevertheless earn a greater rent through their labor than with small capital and a high interest rate, and that they are thus driven by their own interest to increase capital, even though the product of their labor -- namely capital -- thereby fetches a lower price through the fall of the interest rate.

Were one to object that, although the capital-producing workers secure a greater rent for themselves through the increase of capital, their interest requires them to apply the greater capital only in their own labor but to let none of it reach the other workers so that the interest rate would retain its former height: then one must consider that the capital-producing workers possess no monopoly, and that the other workers would immediately turn to capital production if the labor devoted to it were more highly rewarded than other labor.

This crossing over of workers from the second class to the first would continue until equilibrium is restored, i.e., until both kinds of labor are equally highly rewarded.

Here the question now comes up: what common measure is there for comparing both kinds of labor, since the reward for one kind is expressed in a permanent rent, while that for the other is expressed in the product itself?

To this one must reply: when the worker lends out his surplus at interest, his reward for his year's exertion transforms into a permanent interest income, which can be compared with the rent of the capital-producing worker and measured by the same standard -- for example, in thalers or in bushels of rye.

Suppose now the two classes of workers employ a different capital: those of the first class, for example, 3 Y.L., while those of the second class work with only a capital of 2 Y.L.

The rent of the capital-producing worker then amounts, as shown above, to 5.66c. With the application of 2 Y.L. of capital, wages are 114c, the surplus 14c, and the interest rate 36/114 = 31.6 percent. The worker thus receives for his surplus of 14c: 14 x 31.6/100 = 4.42c, while the worker of the first class receives 5.66c of rent.

If, on the other hand, the workers likewise employ a capital of 3 Y.L., then wages = 121.2c, the surplus = 21.2c, the interest rate = 32.4/121.2 = 26.7 percent, and the interest on the surplus then amounts to 21.2 x 26.7/100 = 5.66c -- exactly as much as the rent of the capital-producing worker. With equal capital investment, therefore, the equilibrium in the reward of both kinds of labor prevails, and there is then no reason for workers to cross over from one class to the other.

### Section 12. Influence of the Fertility of the Soil and Climate on the Level of Wages and the Interest Rate.

If, as a consequence of the lesser fertility of the soil, the worker furnished with the same capital produces a product of labor one-quarter smaller than in Table A, then interest amount and wages also decline by one-quarter, as immediately appears when one applies the same calculation according to which Table A was constructed to the case where the product of labor of a man without capital is 3/4 x 110 = 82.5 and the increment through the first capital is 3/4 x 40 = 30.

In that case, however, wages with the application of 1, 2, 3, and even 4 Y.L. of capital do not yet reach the amount of the necessary means of subsistence of the worker. Still less can capital be created through labor under these conditions. Only when the relative capital has risen to 5 Y.L. does labor yield a wage of 3/4 x 142.4 = 106.8, providing a surplus of 6.8 that can be applied to capital formation.

Capital must therefore precede man if he is to subsist at all.

This condition, however, is the one prevailing throughout all Europe; for even in our mildest climates, in the south of Italy and Greece, a people without any capital -- that is, without clothing, shelter, implements, etc. -- would have to perish miserably.

But capital has not arisen (like the world according to Feuerbach) from and through itself, from internal necessity; rather it is -- the product of human labor.

So capital is the condition of man's subsistence, but has not existed from the beginning; rather it arose from the labor of men who possessed no capital.

Here we encounter a circular argument, an apparently insoluble contradiction.

If I am not mistaken, this contradiction is everywhere reflected in science wherever wages and the interest rate are discussed, and perhaps in the failure to resolve it lies the reason why what has been said on this subject is so unsatisfactory.

Indeed, for more than 20 years I have endeavored to find a law for the connection between capital and the product of labor through which that contradiction would be resolved -- but always in vain.

To be sure, it is not difficult, for the higher levels of relative capital, to construct a scale representing the relationship between it and the product of labor that approximately corresponds to reality; but if the series thus formed is continued to the lower levels of capital, or even to zero -- i.e., to the origin of capital -- the same contradiction reappears.

The product of labor *p* is a function of *q*, where *q* denotes the magnitude of the capital employed; but none of the equations I have set up in almost every algebraic form illuminates the darkness that prevails here.

Only late -- too late on account of the time and effort lost -- has the reason for the insolubility of the problem, which I have found in the following reflections, become clear to me.

Only where nature spontaneously -- that is, without human intervention -- produces plantains and coconut palms, where the warmth of the climate makes neither clothing nor shelter among the absolute necessities of man -- only there could the cradle of humanity have stood, and only there can capital grow from labor alone.

After in this paradisal land capitals have been accumulated, but at the same time the population has also so increased that space becomes cramped, all fertile land having become the property of individuals, individual tribes can separate, emigrate, and with the help of the acquired capital -- livestock, provisions, implements, etc. -- find ample sustenance even in such countries where man cannot live without capital, earning more than if they had hired themselves out for wages in their homeland.

After at this new habitation new capitals have again been accumulated, after the population has again so increased that space again becomes cramped, emigrants furnished with sufficient capital can proceed to inhospitable regions where even the so undemanding savages cannot live, regions that are in themselves uninhabitable, and yet find a fully adequate livelihood there.

Indeed, we can further conclude that countries we now still consider uninhabitable on account of their infertile soil or unfavorable climate will one day, when capitals through further increase have become yet cheaper, be brought under cultivation and sustain human beings. The cheaper capital becomes -- that is, the lower the interest at which it is to be had -- the more the habitability of the earth expands.

Europe too belongs to those countries that could only be populated through the immigration of people who were furnished with capital.

The insolubility of the above problem is now explained by the fact that the primordial capital was not created in Europe but originates from countries where other laws of capital formation prevail than here.

The original capital in Europe is an immigrant one, and does not follow the laws that we can survey from our standpoint.

With this insight, however, the contradiction also ceases, since we now give up trying to find one and the same law encompassing both the origin of the primordial capital and the higher levels.

Might not, analogous to this case, many a problem in other and still higher connections appear insoluble to us because we seek to explain and comprehend through a single law what is of entirely different origin -- what only partly belongs to our stage, but partly may have sprung not merely from another continent but even from another world?

**Application.**

It may be permitted, even with partial repetition of what has already been said, to append the following reflection that presses itself upon me in connection with this subject.

Only in such regions of the earth as lie, like southern India, central Africa, Peru, in the region of the plantain and the coconut palm, could the human race have had its origin.

Here in these countries so richly endowed by nature, human beings lived in abundance so long as for the growing population ownerless land was still to be found. But after all fertile land had been taken into possession and become the property of individuals, with further growing population, a part of the people had to hire themselves out and work for wages. These wages then gradually sink to a point where it becomes more advantageous to emigrate to other less fertile and by nature less favored countries that still contain ownerless land, and there to cultivate the soil with the help of the acquired and brought-along capital.

This course of development is so firmly grounded in all the mental endowments of man, in the striving for the advancement of his well-being that nature has given man as instinct, and finally also in the constitution of the physical world, and is so natural, that we may regard the gradual spread of the human race over the entire earth, effected by emigration, as corresponding to the world plan.

If, on the other hand, we look at those states from which emigration occurs, this is by no means gratifying for them. The state loses the productive power of the emigrants; it loses the capital that was spent on their upbringing; it loses the capital they take with them.

If such emigration becomes regular and permanent, it can happen that this state, despite all its useful institutions and arrangements, works only for another state, while itself gaining neither in power nor wealth.

This becomes all the more painful when the emigration takes the direction toward a state that may one day come into hostile contact with one's own. This state then itself works toward having to succumb one day in the struggle with the other state.

But this cannot be prevented; for man at the present level of civilization will not let the right of free movement be taken from him -- and even if a government could accomplish this, overpopulation, misery, and revolt would yet be the eventual consequence.

Even the mightiest and most absolute monarch on earth is powerless when he sets himself against the fulfillment of the world plan.

So the states remain, in relation to the world-spirit, in a condition of constraint, and unreconciled with the fate that governs them.

Is then -- so we must ask -- this contradiction natural and therefore irreconcilable?

Individuals too are subject to a constraint imposed by the laws of the state. But to them is given the power to free themselves from constraint and to attain freedom if they renounce the egoistic striving directed at their own interest, make the welfare of the state the aim of their actions, and through deeper recognition of their higher calling voluntarily set for themselves the limits that the state, through its laws directed at the welfare of the whole, imposes as constraint.

Is there then for states and their leaders no such reconciliation with fate, no such elevation to freedom as is granted to individuals -- must they forever persist in the condition of constraint and opposition to the world plan?

This reconciliation can hardly take place other than if the states give up regarding themselves as the center of the earth and the other nations as instruments for their benefit.

The reconciliation can and will take place when states make the welfare of humanity the aim of their striving, when they relate to humanity as those individuals who have attained freedom relate to the state.

Walking on this path certainly requires firm courage and at first also the offering of sacrifices. But just as individuals who act in accordance with their calling are also rewarded for it unsought, so too the reward would not fail to come for states. The government that has won the trust that it will persevere steadily on this path would make the other peoples intellectually subject to it, and thereby gain more in influence and power than can be gained through increase of population and wealth or through territorial expansion.

England has already shown traces of such a direction -- in the emancipation of slaves, in Canning's efforts, in the peace with China, and more recently also in its trade policy. Were England to strip off all egoism toward foreign countries and to walk the path it has momentarily entered upon permanently, its material, but still more its intellectual influence, could attain a height never before reached by any state.

### Section 13. Reduction of the Effectiveness of Capital to Labor.

We now leave the tropical world with our observations and turn to European conditions, where man without the cooperation of capital can produce nothing and without the aid of capital cannot even subsist.

Here every product is the joint work of labor and capital, and the question now arises whether the share that each of these potencies has in the common product can be recognized and separated out.

To resolve this question we make the following observations.

If the capital Q is stated in bushels of rye, or in thalers, or in any other measure of value, and the wages a + y are expressed in the same measure of value and assumed as known: then, by dividing Q by a + y, one obtains how large the capital is when expressed in years of labor of a worker's family, or over how many Y.L. of a family the capitalist commands with the capital Q.

Let this number of workers be = n; then Q/n = q(a + y), and Q = nq(a + y).

Now if the capitalist turns this capital over to an entrepreneur who invests it in a trade, or in agriculture in a region where no land rent exists, and this entrepreneur employs n workers, then each of them works with a capital of Q/(n(a + y)) = q Y.L. of capital.

If one now deducts from the gross revenue of the trade or of farming in the region where no land rent exists, all expenses of the enterprise with the sole exception of wages and the interest to be paid to the capitalist, and from the resulting surplus further deducts the trade profit of the entrepreneur (according to Section 7), then there remains the portion of the revenue that we (in Section 6, No. 3) called the product of labor, and for the worker who works with a capital of q Y.L. designated by "p."

It is immaterial in what measure of value p is stated, whether in rye or money, etc.; only the measure of value must be the same in which Q and a + y are stated.

This product of labor p is the joint product of labor and capital, and since every other business expense has already been deducted, comes solely between the capitalist and the worker for division.

In what way does this division take place?

The n workers employed in the enterprise produce a product of np. Of this, the n workers receive in wages n(a + y). After deduction of these wages, there remains for the capitalist a rent of n(p - (a + y)).

The capital employed amounts to nq(a + y).

**The rent divided by the invested capital yields the interest rate, which we designate by z.**

Thus z = n(p - (a + y)) / (nq(a + y)) = (p - (a + y)) / (q(a + y)).

This expression for the interest rate is (given the concepts we associate with the symbols p, q, and a + y) of universal, absolute validity. Equally decisive, however, must also be the conclusions that can be mathematically derived from this equation.

From z = (p - (a + y)) / (q(a + y)), it follows that qz(a + y) = p - (a + y), and (1 + qz)(a + y) = p.

Thus **a + y = p / (1 + qz)**.

That is, **wages equal the product of labor divided by one plus the capital expressed in years of labor multiplied by the interest rate**.

The rent that the capitalist draws is obtained by subtracting wages from the product of labor; this amounts to:

p - p/(1 + qz) = p(1 - 1/(1 + qz)) = pqz/(1 + qz).

The ratio in which the reward of labor stands to that of capital is thus:

p/(1 + qz) : pqz/(1 + qz), or 1 : qz.

If one sets the worker's wage = A, then the capitalist's rent = Aqz.

The rent from q Y.L. of capital is thus equal to the wages of qz workers, and the rent from 1 Y.L. of capital is equal to the wages of z workers.

Now since, as will be shown further below, in the production of one and the same product p, a part of the capital can be replaced by increased labor, and conversely a part of the labor can be replaced by additional capital, capital appears as a co-worker that enters into competition with the wage laborer. Now it is in the power of the entrepreneur, who employs n workers with the capital Q, to give the relative capital q, with which one man works, any desired magnitude by enlarging or reducing n. The entrepreneur, knowing and pursuing his interest, will raise the relative capital q precisely so far until the costs of the labor of capital and those of human labor stand in direct proportion to the effectiveness of both in production.

The effectiveness of capital must be the measure of its compensation: for if the labor of capital were cheaper than that of human beings, the entrepreneur would dismiss workers; in the opposite case, he would increase them.

Accordingly, the effectiveness of capital relative to that of human labor must stand, like their compensation, in the ratio of z to 1 -- and the compensation of capital through the interest to be paid for it is therefore neither accidental nor unjust.

We thereby arrive at the insight, so highly important for our investigation, that when capital and human labor are measured by one and the same measure -- namely the year's labor of a man --

**the interest rate z is the factor by which the ratio of the effectiveness of capital to that of human labor is expressed.**

We are thereby now in a position to reduce the cooperation of capital in the production of an exchangeable good to labor.

Through this reduction it is then possible to express the production costs of a product, insofar as no land rent is contained in it, entirely in labor, and labor thereby truly becomes the measure of value for exchangeable goods.

But we can also conversely reduce a capital stated in products -- for example, rye -- to Y.L., by dividing this capital by the wage for one year's labor, which wage here equals the value of labor, namely by p/(1 + qz).

Adam Smith has already designated labor as the true, original measure of the value of exchangeable goods. But Adam Smith at once limits the application of this measure to the first rude state of human society, where little or no capital existed and the land did not yet yield a rent.

Ricardo -- and after him McCulloch -- regards labor, on the other hand, as the sole, always valid measure of the value of exchangeable goods. According to Ricardo, the price of exchangeable goods contains neither capital utility nor land rent, but only labor.

He regards the capital contained in buildings, machines, etc., as itself a product of labor, and accordingly -- since no capital utility is charged -- it would only need to be calculated how much of this labor passes into the product in proportion to the duration of this fixed capital, in order to determine the quantum of labor that, including the presently performed labor, is contained in the product.

This otherwise so acute writer overlooks, however:

1) that for the production of fixed capital, not only labor but also capital utility has been employed;

2) that in the use of machines, not only their wear and tear but also the interest on their purchase price must be compensated.

In general, the subject of capital is extremely difficult to understand in Ricardo. Upon closer analysis, however, it turns out that the reason lies in the fact that Ricardo does not remain true to himself; for while on page 21 of his work he makes no mention of capital interest in the price determination of exchangeable goods and recognizes labor alone as the measure of value, on page 28, where his principles come to be applied, he charges for the use of machines an annuity in which not only the repayment of wear and tear but also the interest on the acquisition capital is contained -- and thus, without an explanation and apparently unconsciously, he again gives up recognizing labor as the sole measure of value.

Very remarkable, however, is the fact that Ricardo on the last page of the chapter on value himself admits that what he has said is fully correct only for the first rude state of society, and thus himself revokes what he has set up as universal laws.

Of a measure by which capital utility might be reduced to labor, there can therefore be no question in Ricardo. But this is in general also not possible so long as one confuses trade profit with capital interest, and does not in wages separate the wage for labor in itself from the interest that the worker receives on his capital contained in clothing, household goods, housing, etc.

To illustrate the foregoing propositions, it may be useful to add a numerical example.

For this purpose we assume for the time being -- and since this does not agree with our earlier assumption, only for the present case -- that the silver mines in the isolated state are scattered about, and that the least productive silver mine whose exploitation is still necessary to satisfy the demand is situated at the border of the cultivated plain. If we now imagine that silver mines of equal productivity with the latter extend even deeper into the wilderness, but that these mines are not worked: then this non-utilization can have no other reason than that the value of the silver to be obtained from them no longer covers the extraction costs.

The extension of mining thus finds, just as the extension of grain cultivation, a limit where the value of the product comes into equilibrium with the production costs of the same.

For this reason, the last worked mine, just as the last cultivated grain land, can yield no rent.

Now since in this region -- provided no state monopoly interposes itself -- capital and labor can turn equally well to mining as to farming, capital and labor must also yield equally high returns in both applications.

According to the formula a + y = p/(1 + qz), wages are expressed as a share of the product. In one case, however, the product consists of silver, in the other of grain. If the quantum of silver falling to the worker is to be a compensation for the quantum of grain that he could have earned in farming, then both quanta must have equal exchange value. Here, therefore, is the place where the exchange value between silver and grain is formed.

Now let the product of labor of a man in mining = 7.5 pounds of silver, in farming = 240 bushels of rye; then the worker's share that constitutes his wage is, in the first case, 7.5/(1 + 20z) pounds of silver, in the second, 240/(1 + 12z) bushels of rye.

The interest rate z, which must be equally high in both applications of capital, is 1/20 or 5 percent.

The capital q with which a man works is, however, since different trades require very different capital investments, of unequal size. Suppose q in farming = 12, in mining = 20; then the wage of labor in mining = 7.5/(1 + 20 x 0.05) = 7.5/2 = 3.75 pounds of silver, in farming = 240/(1 + 12 x 0.05) = 240/1.6 = 150 bushels of rye.

Here, then, 3.75 pounds of silver is an equivalent for 150 bushels of rye, that is, with 3.75 pounds of silver the worker can satisfy just as many needs through exchange as with 150 bushels of rye. Hence 3.75 pounds of silver has equal exchange value with 150 bushels of rye. The exchange value of a product expressed in gold or precious metals is usually called the price thereof. Accordingly, the price of one bushel of rye = 3.75/150 = 0.025 pounds of silver.

This value-relationship between silver and grain formed at the border of the cultivated plain is the foundation for the price determination of grain throughout the whole isolated state. But to this foundation another element is added, through whose cooperation the price of grain in the various regions of the isolated state becomes quite different from what it is at the border. This element is grounded in the different mobility of silver and grain.

The costs of transporting the precious metals over 30 miles are, in proportion to their value, so trifling that we may set them equal to zero.

By contrast, the transport costs of grain over 30 miles are, in proportion to its value, most significant.

In Part One, Section 4, the principles are developed according to which these transport costs are to be calculated, and their application to the present case yields the following results. [Details of the transport cost calculation follow, showing that 150 bushels of rye at the border, worth 3.75 pounds of silver, have a value in the city of 10.98 pounds of silver due to transport costs over 31.5 miles.]

Taking silver as the measure, grain in the city has almost triple the value of grain at the border; and taking grain as the measure of value, silver in the city has sunk to almost one-third of the value it had at the border.

It is erroneous, however, when one, as Locke does, measures the value of precious metals in different countries solely by grain prices. In Moscow one can undoubtedly buy far more grain with one pound of silver than in London; but in London one obtains for the same quantum of silver a greater quantum of colonial, factory, and manufactured goods than in Moscow. Similarly in the isolated state, the prices of most manufactured goods stated in silver are lower in the city than at the border.

The foregoing calculation of transport costs is based on the former very poor Mecklenburg country roads. On paved roads, railways, and canals, the transport costs are naturally very much lower. But the more or less is not the point here; rather, what matters is only the principle from which the value-relationship between silver and grain arises. It is self-evident that as the means of communication improve, the differences in the value-relationship between silver and grain at various locations also diminish.

Entire books have been written on the theory of price without achieving unanimity of views.

Since in the foregoing it is adopted as a principle that production costs are the measure of the exchange value of products, this subject requires further discussion here.

Adam Smith calls the price that corresponds to the production costs the natural price of a product.

Say, on the other hand, declares Adam Smith's distinction between natural price and market price to be chimerical, and regards competition or the relationship between supply and demand as the sole regulator of price.

If we observe on a market how prices are formed, we indeed see that the scarcity or surplus of a commodity and the related ratio of supply to demand is decisive. The production costs of the commodity are so little considered that the seller only makes himself ridiculous if he appeals to them.

But competition is merely the outward manifestation of a deeper-lying cause, and one must not, like Say, content oneself with grasping the phenomenon, but must seek to investigate the reason.

What is the cause that at a given time the market is flooded with a certain commodity? Answer: In the preceding period, the production of this commodity afforded an unusual advantage, and in consequence thereof production expanded.

What is the cause of the deficient supply of a commodity on the market? Answer: Its production was attended with loss in the preceding period, and as a result of this loss, production was curtailed.

The fluctuation of market prices is, however, unavoidable because the individual producers cannot foresee the future demand, and are first taught by the market price itself whether there is a deficiency or surplus of their commodity.

What has been said applies to commodities that can be produced at any time in any desired quantity. It is different with grain, where the deficiency or surplus depends on the lesser or greater annual fertility.

But if one looks at longer periods, in which the influence of weather on vegetation appears almost as a constant potency, then here too the excess of average market prices over production costs brings about a greater production and increased supply of grain; conversely, the fall of market prices below production costs causes a diminished production of grain.

For the reasons cited, a constant striving toward the equalization of market prices with production costs, proceeding from the self-interest of the entrepreneurs, must therefore be at work. Very beautifully and aptly Adam Smith says on this subject:

"The natural price is, as it were, the central point toward which the changeable market prices are constantly gravitating."

In the average of a longer period, market prices will therefore closely coincide with the production prices regulated by costs.

Between the price of a commodity and its production costs, equilibrium prevails when the trade by which this commodity is produced yields neither loss nor unusual profit.

But by what standard -- one must now ask -- is profit and loss to be measured?

I answer: when through the price of commodities, labor of equal quality is equally highly rewarded in all trades, then equilibrium prevails, and this average reward is the measure of production costs as well as of profit and loss.

That in most commodities, capital utility and land rent are also contained as elements of price, changes nothing essential in this proposition; for if one deducts land rent and capital interest as expenses, it becomes apparent how highly the producer is rewarded for his labor.

The proposition "production costs determine the average price of a commodity" is, however, true only with the qualification that the use-value or utility of the commodity is esteemed at least equal to the costs of its production.

Whoever devotes his labor to trifles -- for example, makes a clock in a nutshell, or a Great Mogul of gold, and the like -- cannot count on a recompense for his labor, because the use-value of his manufactures stands far below the manufacturing costs. But curiosities of this sort never come permanently to market, and only such commodities whose use-value at least covers the production costs can become objects of regular trade.

Commodities and implements whose production can be expanded without limit at constant costs, which includes most manufactured goods, can never permanently stand above the production price, however far their use-value may exceed it.

A striking example of this is the plow. If this instrument did not exist and the soil had to be worked with the spade, Europe could probably scarcely feed half its present population. But one pays in the plow not for the utility it provides, but only for the trifling manufacturing costs.

For products, on the other hand, that can be produced in larger quantity only with increased costs -- such as grain -- the price rises so high until production costs and use-value are in equilibrium.

Herein lies, incidentally, a reason why with growing population the exchange value of grain relative to manufactured goods must steadily rise.

The gold and silver mines belong in this respect in the same category as grain. For if no new, rich mines are discovered, and the demand for gold and silver can be met only from mines already long worked, then the extraction of these precious metals, since they must be taken from ever greater depth, is also attended with ever increasing costs. Mining must then, just as the cultivation of grain, find its limit when the extraction costs of the precious metals reach the use-value of the same, conditioned by the purchasing power of the buyers.

In the assumption, taken as a fact, that the least productive silver mine at the border of the isolated state is indeed being worked, the proof is already contained that the production costs of silver from this mine do not exceed its use-value -- and that we are therefore also justified in taking the production costs of silver as the measure of its exchange value. Higher than these production costs, however, the exchange value of silver cannot be -- for otherwise the mines lying further in the wilderness would not remain unworked.

Our observations are thus based on the simplest possible conditions. Neither mining nor farming yields a rent here, and for both silver and grain, production costs and use-value are in equilibrium.

Through the foregoing observations we have gained some insight into the nature of the interest rate and of wages, in that we

1) have arrived at the recognition that z denotes the ratio of the effectiveness of capital to the effectiveness of presently performed labor; and

2) have found for wages the universally valid expression

a + y = p / (1 + qz).

But we have thereby only arrived at the gates of the actual investigation. For in that expression, a + y is dependent on z, so that we must always assume z as known if we wish to determine a + y. But p is also not a constant magnitude; rather it rises and falls with q, and is therefore dependent on q. On the value of p, however, the values of y and z in turn depend. Accordingly, p, y, and z are functions of q. The task is therefore this: for a given q, to find the values of p, y, and z.

While in most sciences the investigation begins with individual fixed propositions taken as given, we are dealing here with forces that stand in a constant interrelation with one another, and of which not a single one may be taken as given.

This, however, makes our investigation so difficult and complicated -- and the question arises whether as many equations can be found as are required for the determination of the unknown quantities.

### Section 14

**At the border of the isolated state lies the workshop for the formation of the relationship between wages and the interest rate.**

To investigate how wages and the interest rate arise from one another, and to express wages independently of the interest rate, we must take as the basis of our observations the simplest possible case, where the entire product of labor is divided between the worker and the capitalist, and where the third factor in price determination, land rent, does not make the task still more complicated.

This is precisely the case at the border of the cultivated plain of the isolated state, where beyond the circle of three-field husbandry, land of equal fertility with that of the cultivated plain is to be had for nothing.

It is true that the land in the circle of stock-raising, beyond the cultivated plain, still yields some rent; but this is so small that it may be regarded as vanishing -- and since taking it into account would indeed make the investigation more complicated but would change nothing in the result, we abstract from it entirely and set the land rent of the soil beyond the circle of three-field husbandry equal to zero.

At the border of the cultivated plain, it is left to the worker's choice whether he will continue to work for wages, or whether with the aid of his accumulated savings he will clear a piece of land, erect buildings, etc., and acquire a property on which he will henceforth work on his own account.

If the workers in this area are to be deterred from establishing colonist holdings or small farms, and induced to continue working for their previous masters for wages, then these wages, together with the interest they could obtain by lending out the capital required for establishing a colonist holding, must be equal to the product of labor that they could produce on the colonist holding, which can be worked by one laborer's family.

Now if the wages = a + y bushels of rye, the product of labor = p, the capital required for establishing the small farm = 1 times q Y.A. [years of labor], which expressed in bushels of rye = q(a + y), and finally the interest rate = z percent;

then, if equilibrium is to prevail here,

a + y + q(a + y)z = p.

This gives a + y = p / (1 + qz), and z = (p - (a + y)) / (q(a + y)).

Here a, p, and q are determinate, but y and z are indeterminate quantities.

Everything now depends on finding an equation between y and z, for the determination of the relationship between wages and the interest rate depends on the solution of this problem.

The attempt at solving this problem shall be made in the next section.

But so as not to have to interrupt the train of thought there too often and too long by raising doubts and objections against the correctness of the procedure, we wish to state and dispose of in advance the misgivings that arise from comparison with reality.

**I.**

We maintain that the wages and interest rate forming at the border of the isolated state are normative for the entire state, and must justify this claim here.

**A. Wages.**

Not money wages, but real wages -- i.e., the sum of necessities and amenities that the worker can procure for his wages -- must be equally high throughout the entire isolated state; for if at one place these real wages were higher than at another, the influx of workers from areas of lower wages would very soon restore equilibrium.

At the border of the cultivated plain of the isolated state, where ownerless land is available in unlimited quantity, neither the caprice of capitalists, nor the competition of workers, nor the magnitude of necessary subsistence determines the level of wages; rather, the product of labor itself is the measure of the reward of labor. Here, then, must also be the workshop for the formation of the natural wage of labor, which becomes normative for the entire isolated state.

In reality, this is of course quite different; for there we find enormous differences in the level of wages -- for example, between Poland and North America.

But here the diversity of language, customs, laws, the effects of climate on health, etc., and the costliness of emigrating to a distant land are the reasons why the difference in wages is not equalized.

These hindrances to equalization are, on the other hand, entirely absent in the isolated state.

**B. Interest rate.**

The interest rate forming at the border of the isolated state must become normative for the entire state, since the easily mobile capital always flows to where it yields the highest return, and the interest rate thereby equalizes everywhere.

In reality, the deviations in the interest rate in different countries are almost as significant as those in wages.

While in England and Holland the ordinary interest rate is 3 to 4 percent, in Russia and several North American states it is 6 to 7 percent. That this difference is not equalized by the flow of capital from one country to another is easily explained when one considers that capitalists cannot be inclined to lend their money to countries where the administration of justice is defective and partial, and where judges are even corruptible -- since they can obtain there neither security for the correct payment of interest nor for the repayment of capital.

Striking and worthy of closer investigation is the fact, however, that in the various provinces of one and the same monarchy, where the same legal code prevails and the administration of justice is strict and impartial, so different an interest rate can nonetheless prevail, as is the case in the Prussian state. For while in the Province of Brandenburg and in Western Pomerania the interest rate has fallen to 3-1/4 to 4 percent, in the Province of East Prussia the interest rate on loans to private persons has remained at 5 percent.

Here it might be difficult to decide whether the higher interest rate in East Prussia is a consequence of a higher utility of capital or of a lesser security for creditors, if the price of mortgage bonds did not shed light on this. According to the "Allgemeine preussische Zeitung," on January 13, 1846, at the Berlin Exchange, the price of

East Prussian mortgage bonds was 96-3/4 percent,
Pomeranian mortgage bonds 96-3/4 percent,
Kurmark and Neumark mortgage bonds 98 percent.

The mortgage bonds of these three provinces bear the same amount, namely 3-1/2 percent interest.

For the security of the mortgage bonds, all estates that have joined the credit association are jointly and severally liable, and mortgage bonds are issued as first mortgages on only a part of the value of the estates. The security of the mortgage bonds is thus far greater than that of private loans.

Since, then, in the price and value of East Prussian and Kurmark mortgage bonds at the same interest rate there is only an insignificant difference -- namely from 96-3/4 to 98 percent -- while in the interest rate on private loans so significant a deviation appears, we must conclude that the high interest rate in East Prussia is caused and maintained by the insecurity of loans on estates there.

Whether this greater insecurity of private loans in East Prussia, compared with other provinces, arises from the national character of the inhabitants, or from the greater fluctuations in estate prices (because the income of this land depends almost entirely on the conjunctures of the grain trade), or from the greater danger of becoming a theater of war, or whether these causes operate jointly -- this I must leave to others to judge and answer. Besides these causes, however, the greater distance from Berlin -- that seat of the great capitalists -- may also contribute to the raising of the interest rate in East Prussia. For where the land and soil do not provide complete security for a loan, and credit is based more on the person of the debtor, the capitalist will not willingly lose him from sight, so as to be able, when danger arises, to call in and collect his capital. In such a case, therefore, the capitalist will lend his money in the vicinity of his residence somewhat more cheaply than at a great distance.

However that may be, the difference in the interest received on mortgage bonds and private loans is always to be regarded as an insurance premium for the risk associated with lending capital in the latter manner.

Since, then, in the isolated state we understand by "interest rate" only the interest received after deduction of the insurance premium, no argument against the equality of the interest rate in all parts of the isolated state can be drawn from the fact that in one and the same monarchy, in different provinces, interest of very different amounts is paid for lent capital.

**III.**

Our investigations rest on the assumption that the isolated state is in a stationary condition. Accordingly, its size and extent must also be unchangeable. But when we here in thought establish new farms in the circle of stock-raising, we thereby apparently act against our own assumption.

Now, however, the individual farm is to be regarded in relation to the whole as an infinitely small point -- and if despite this increment we regard the whole as still in the stationary condition, our procedure is analogous to that in the calculus of the infinite, and can be justified in the same way.

If namely x transforms into x + dx, then this quantity is still reckoned as equal in value to x, so that dx = 0. The differential, dx, however, acquires its significance when it is combined as a factor with another finite quantity. In the parabola, whose abscissa = x, parameter = a, and ordinate = y, we have y-squared = ax and y = sqrt(ax). If here x grows by dx, then the element of the area, or the infinitely small increment that the area receives, = dx times sqrt(ax). In this element the law is reflected according to which the figure is constructed -- and from the integral of this element = 2/3 xy, the area of the figure is obtained.

Here dx has again vanished, and we find through this calculation not the area of a parabola whose abscissa = x + dx, but that of the parabola for the abscissa = x.

But even without recourse to the differential calculus, this procedure can perhaps be made intuitively clear.

Imagine that, as a consequence of wages being too low, not a few but very many workers devote their surplus to the establishment of new farms, and substantially extend the cultivated plain. But since the number of workers, according to our assumption, is constant, a shortage of workers will arise on the already existing farms, and to put a stop to further emigration to the wilderness, the owners will have to raise wages high enough that emigration becomes unprofitable. But if a significant extension of the cultivated plain has already occurred, then more grain than before is brought to the city, and since the number of consumers has not increased, the price of grain in the city, and thereby also in the entire cultivated area, must fall. With it, however, the land rent of the newly established farms also falls below zero. The final result of the sinking of land rent below zero, however, is that the settlements are again abandoned when the buildings have decayed.

Thus the cultivated plain is once more restricted to its former extent, and the stationary state returns.

But as soon as the estate owners attempt to push wages below the measure of what the workers can earn by working on their own account in the wilderness, the same game begins anew. But since this is attended with great disadvantage for the estate owners, on account of the resulting shortage of workers, the mere possibility for the workers of settling in the wilderness, without this actually occurring, suffices to compel the estate owners to pay a wage that is in equilibrium with what the worker can obtain through settlement and work on his own account.

The stationary state can therefore only prevail at the normal wage that forms in this way.

**IV.**

We base our following investigation of capital creation through labor on the assumption that the workers devote their surplus, or that part of their wages remaining after deduction of necessary subsistence, to the stated purpose.

Looking at reality, one might readily object that the wages of workers in the greater part of Europe amount to no more than what they necessarily need for the maintenance of their families, that their surplus is zero, and that consequently no capital creation by the workers can take place.

This objection, however, loses its force for the present investigation for the following two reasons:

1) In the construction of the isolated state, a wage is assumed as the basis that does indeed permit the workers to make savings.

2) In recent decades, the population in almost all European countries has increased by approximately one percent annually. In the working class, the increase has been proportionally at least as great as in the class of the well-to-do. The wages of the workers, however small they may be, have therefore sufficed to raise more children than were necessary for maintaining the population.

But our investigation is based on the assumption of a stationary state of the population, and under this condition the workers, even at their present low wages, would have had a surplus that could have been devoted to capital creation.

### Section 15

**Capital creation through labor.**

We imagine that a number of workers unite into an association to establish, at the border of the cultivated plain of the isolated state, a new farm of the same size as the older farms of this state.

The workers united for this purpose divide themselves into two divisions -- of which one occupies itself with clearing the fields, erecting buildings, manufacturing implements, etc.; while the other, in the meantime, remains at work for wages and, through its surplus expressed in rye, furnishes the subsistence that the workers engaged in establishing the farm consume.

Under these conditions, nothing of the already existing national capital is consumed in establishing the farm; the sum of these valuable objects is, after the completed creation of the farm, still exactly as great as before.

The newly established farm costs only labor, and nothing other than labor.

The rent that the farm yields falls solely and exclusively to the capital-creating workers who have created the farm through their labor -- and this rent is the reward of their labor.

This association of capital-creating workers hires, after the completed establishment of the farm, a number of wage laborers to work and manage the new farm.

The wages of these workers cannot, however, be determined arbitrarily, nor according to the wages customary on the older farms. These wages must rather be so high that the surplus of the worker, placed at interest -- that is, yz -- equals the rent of the capital-creating worker; for were this not the case, then -- since we assume workers of equal strength, knowledge, and skill -- the wage laborers would immediately switch to capital creation.

We have here, then, a twofold linkage between labor and capital: first, in that capital arises directly from labor, and second, in that the capital-creating workers now assume the position of the capitalist vis-a-vis the wage laborer.

Here, under the simplest conditions, where no land rent acts as a confusing third force -- here must the connection between wages and the interest rate reveal itself, if the task we have set ourselves is to be solvable at all.

The determination of wages is here placed in the hands of the workers themselves, and the wages arising from the workers' determination are, as previously demonstrated, normative for the entire isolated state.

The caprice of the workers finds, in this determination of their wages, no other limit than that of their own interest.

In capital creation, however, the worker can have no other aim than to obtain the highest possible rent for his labor.

That wage which brings the maximum of rent must therefore be the goal of striving, and since nothing stands in the way of this striving, that wage must also become the actual one.

Thus we are led to the question: at what level of wages does the worker obtain the maximum of rent for his exertion?

To answer this question, we adopt the following propositions:

The working of the newly founded farm requires the continued labor of n day-laborer families.

The establishment of the farm required the annual labor of nq men (nq worker families). To the creation of a new farm belongs unquestionably not merely labor, but also the application of capital. But according to Section 13, we can reduce the participation of capital to labor, and thus express the establishment costs entirely in labor.

Each of the day laborers working the fields then works with a capital of q Y.A. (q annual labors of a worker family).

The worker equipped with a capital of q Y.A. produces an annual product of p (bushels of rye).

The total product of the n workers is accordingly = np.

The subsistence that the worker necessarily requires to maintain his working capacity amounts to a bushels of rye or its equivalent.

The nq workers occupied with establishing the farm during one year have consumed nqa (bushels of rye).

Of the division of the association engaged in producing provisions, each worker retains from his wages, after deduction of his consumption, a surplus of y bushels of rye, or its equivalent.

For producing the nqa bushels consumed during the establishment of the farm, nqa/y workers engaged in production are therefore required.

The number of worker families from whose combined labor the farm has arisen amounts accordingly to

nq + nqa/y = nq(a + y)/y.

The n day laborers who work the fields each receive a + y (bushels of rye) in wages. The total expenditure on wages thus amounts to n(a + y).

If one subtracts this expenditure from the total product = np, there remains a farm rent of np - n(a + y).

This enduring farm rent is the property of the nq capital-creating workers [note: the total number is nq(a+y)/y].

The annual labor of one worker engaged in capital creation is thus rewarded with a rent of:

(p - (a + y)) * y / (q(a + y)).

**Remark:** Since in this formula for the rent, n has vanished, we shall henceforth consider only the farm portion falling to one worker, and the capital with which one man works. But we must always remember that what is meant here is not a colonist holding that can be worked by one family, but rather a farm equal in size to the other farms of the isolated state. For otherwise we would introduce a disturbing and confusing element into our investigations, namely the influence that the different sizes of farms exert on the product of labor and on the farm rent.

At what value of y does the above function for the magnitude of the rent reach its maximum?

We wish, in order to investigate this approximately, and at the same time to make the influence of the different values of y on the magnitude of the rent vivid, first to give a numerical example.

Let a = 100c; p = 300c; q = 12 Y.A.

First, let y = 20c.

The workers engaged in establishing the farm consume aq = 1,200c. Since each worker engaged in producing provisions delivers a surplus of y = 20c, 1,200/20 = 60 other workers are required to produce the provisions consumed during the establishment.

The creation of the farm thus costs the annual labor of 12 + 60 = 72 men.

From the product of the day laborer working the field = 300c, his wages are deducted at 120c.

The rent of this farm portion therefore amounts to 180c.

This rent distributed among 72 men gives for one capital-creating worker 180/72 = 2.50c rent.

Second, let y = 50c.

For the production of the 1,200c consumed during the establishment, 1,200/50 = 24 men are required.

The creation of the farm then costs only 12 + 24 = 36 Y.A. The rent of the farm portion amounts to 300 - 150 = 150c. This distributed among 36 men gives each capital-creating worker 150/36 = 4.166c rent.

It thus appears here that the rent of the capital-creating worker initially rises with increasing wages, but with further increase in wages falls again, and even becomes zero when wages absorb the entire product.

The unbounded increase of wages therefore lies by no means in the interest of the capital-creating workers.

From the initial rise of the rent falling to one man as wages grow, and the subsequent fall of the rent as wages continue to grow, it follows that there is a level of wages at which the rent reaches its maximum.

### Section 16

**At what interest rate does the wage laborer obtain the highest amount of interest for his surplus?**

The rent divided by the capital from which it arose gives the interest rate.

The rent from the farm portion we have before us amounts to p - (a + y) bushels.

The capital contained in this farm portion amounts to q Y.A., which at a wage of a + y = q(a + y) bushels.

The interest rate is therefore z = (p - (a + y)) / (q(a + y)).

And a + y = p / (1 + qz), as already shown in Section 13.

The surplus y is therefore p / (1 + qz) - a.

When lent out, this surplus yields an interest of yz = z * (p / (1 + qz) - a).

At what value of z does this function reach its maximum?

Setting the differential of this function equal to zero gives:

[*After the standard optimization:*]

a + y = sqrt(ap).

Thus the wage laborer receives the highest interest for his surplus when the wage = sqrt(ap), and his interest therefore coincides with that of the capital-creating worker.

### Section 17

**Capital as replacing labor.**

Suppose that on a farm there is a peat bog from which water must be pumped every year in order to cut peat, and this requires the annual labor of one man.

If a canal is now dug through which the peat bog is drained, then the capital expended on the construction of the canal replaces the annually recurring labor of one man.

Here, then, labor is literally saved through capital; the capital now performs the work that was otherwise performed by one man.

If the digging of the canal had required, for example, 20 Y.A., then the invested capital yields interest at 5 percent.

The utilization of capital here expresses itself not in bushels of rye or thalers of money, but in annual labors.

The interest rate arising here is independent of the level of wages, and independent of the fertility of the soil and the associated magnitude of the product of labor.

If, however, wages and the product of labor here appear to have no influence on the interest rate, this must lead to the question whether there are not entirely different determinants for the formation of the interest rate than those we have so far considered.

In agriculture there are many improvements and operations whereby annual recurring labor can be saved through a capital investment: for example, by erecting barns instead of grain ricks, by removing stones that impede plowing, by purchasing threshing machines, etc. But these operations do not all pay equally well. While there may be some where the annually recurring labor of one man is already replaced by a capital investment of 10 Y.A., there are others where this effect arises only from a capital investment of 20, 30, or even 50 Y.A.

The question then is where the farmer should stop on this ladder of improvements, which ones he should undertake in his interest, and which he should omit. The answer is: he will profitably undertake all improvements in which the effect, compared with the capital investment, is greater than the interest rate at which he can obtain capital on loan. If this interest rate is, for example, 5 percent, he will carry out all improvements in which the annual labor of one man is replaced by a capital investment of 15, 16, 17, 18, 19 Y.A.; but he will omit those in which he must expend 21, 22, 23, etc. Y.A. to achieve this effect.

This use of capital therefore already presupposes knowledge of the interest rate -- and it follows that the birthplace of the interest rate is not here, but must be sought elsewhere.

Capital has, on the one hand, the property of replacing labor, and on the other hand, capital is the product of human labor. How is unity and clarity to be found in this interaction?

To attempt the solution of this problem, we bring labor-saving through capital into connection with capital creation through labor.

Suppose the capital investment of k Y.A. replaces the annually recurring labor of one man. The farm, whose operation formerly required n workers, each working with a capital of q Y.A., can after the increase of capital by k Y.A. dispense with one day laborer, whereby a + y bushels are saved in wages. The total capital investment is then nq + k Y.A. The total product, which for n workers amounted to np bushels, remains unchanged = np.

[*The mathematical derivation proceeds through setting the two expressions for yz equal, yielding:*]

k = q(a + y) / (p - (a + y)).

We thus obtain here again the result already found in Section 13, namely: **the interest rate indicates the ratio in which the performance of 1 Y.A. of capital stands to a recurring annual labor.**

While with the construction of the canal it appeared as if it were immaterial whether wages were high or low, the soil fertile or infertile -- since the same improvement always yields the same percentage -- it now follows from the equation k = q(a + y) / (p - (a + y)) that k depends both on p and on y, and that it depends on the level of the interest rate determined by p, y, and q how far improvement aimed at labor-saving can be profitably pursued.

In establishing a new farm, it is in the interest of the capital-creating workers to increase the number of wage laborers to be hired until the additional product brought forth by the last hired worker is absorbed by the wages he receives. Likewise, it is in the interest of the capital-creating workers to raise the capital investment until no increased rent for them arises from further capital increase. But since a portion of the workers can be replaced by capital, and conversely a portion of capital by more hired workers, the costs of labor by humans must, at the boundary to which capital and labor can be profitably employed, be in equilibrium with the costs of labor through capital -- and this equilibrium prevails when k = 1/z.

For q = 12, p = 300c, and y = 73.2c, we found in the preceding section z = 6.1 percent. In that case, 1/z = 1/0.061 = 16.4. In this case, all improvements in which the capital investment of 12, 14, 15 to 16.4 Y.A. saves the labor of one man are profitable, and must consequently be carried out already when the farm is established. The costs of these improvements are thus already contained in the establishment capital of the farm = nq Y.A. By contrast, improvements in which the labor of one man is replaced only by a capital investment of 17, 18, etc. Y.A. would diminish the rent of the capital-creating workers.

Through our investigations we have obtained the result that when the already existing capital nq is increased by k Y.A., then the same total product np, for whose production n workers were formerly required, is produced by n - 1 workers.

The capital of k Y.A., combined with the capital of q Y.A. freed by the departure of the one worker, thus delivers a product of p bushels -- equal to the product of a worker equipped with a capital of q Y.A.

From 1 Y.A. of capital there thus arises a product of p / (q + k) bushels.

Here capital itself appears as a worker. Capital in itself, however, is a dead thing, and can become effective only through the hand of man; but in that it enhances the effectiveness of man, it appears as a co-worker.

In this sense it is to be taken when here and in what follows the labor of capital is spoken of.

### Section 18

**The utility of the last applied particle of capital determines the level of the interest rate.**

In our earlier investigations on the origin of capital, the justification of this proposition is found. It is also shown there that with the increase of capital investment, each later invested capital yields a lesser utility than the earlier invested one.

The utility of the last invested capital expresses itself in the increment that the product of labor of the man who works with the aid of this capital receives.

The increase of the relative national capital does not occur in leaps -- for example from 6 to 7 Y.A. -- but is a continuous growth traversing all intermediate stages.

It follows from this that we must assume the last created and invested particle of capital, through whose utility the interest rate is to be determined, to be very small -- strictly speaking, infinitely small.

Accordingly, we divide the capital of 1 Y.A. into n parts -- where n can signify any, and thus also a very large, number -- and regard the increment of capital by 1/n Y.A. as that particle of capital through whose ratio to the increment of a man's product of labor the interest rate is regulated.

With the application of a capital of q Y.A., let the product be p; with the application of a capital of q - 1/n Y.A., let the product be p - delta. The latter subtracted from the former gives, for 1/n Y.A. of capital, the increment to the product = delta.

1/n Y.A. of capital gives a rent of delta, and since the rent of the entire capital is governed by this, the rent payable for 1 Y.A. of capital = n times delta. If we now set n times delta = alpha, then the rent payable for the entire capital of q Y.A. = alpha times q.

By p we understand, as explained in detail in the assumptions, that part of the total product remaining after deduction of all costs connected with the business operation, as well as of the administration costs and the entrepreneurial profit, and which comes to be divided between capitalist and worker.

The worker who operates with a borrowed capital of q Y.A. produces a product of p. Of this he has to pay in interest alpha times q; for his labor there remains p - alpha times q.

We thus obtain for wages the new expression A = p - alpha*q.

At the wage of p - alpha*q, the capital q has the value of q(p - alpha*q) bushels. The rent that this capital yields amounts to alpha*q bushels. The rent divided by the capital gives the interest rate.

Therefore z = alpha / (p - alpha*q).

Here we have to investigate whether the two methods, according to which we found 1) wages = sqrt(ap), and 2) wages = p - alpha*q, are in accord or in contradiction with each other.

[*Von Thunen then presents an extended numerical comparison using Table B, showing that for capital levels from 6 to 12 Y.A., the wages and labor-rents computed by both formulas diverge at low and high capital levels but converge at a specific capital level (between 10 and 11 Y.A. in the example), at which point p - alpha*q = sqrt(ap), and this is the point of the unconditional maximum of labor-rent.*]

The following results emerge from the comparison:

1) that at the lower levels of capital investment, both wages and labor-rent according to sqrt(ap) are significantly higher than according to p - alpha*q;

2) that this difference diminishes as the capital investment rises;

3) that in this example the labor-rent calculated by both formulas becomes equal at a capital investment falling between 10 and 11 Y.A.;

4) that when this equality prevails, wages p - alpha*q = sqrt(ap);

5) that if the capital grows beyond this point, the labor-rent according to both formulas again decreases;

6) that the labor-rent at the wage p - alpha*q, when this is greater or less than sqrt(ap), is always less than at the wage sqrt(ap), and that if we think of q as continually growing, there is only one moment when both formulas yield equal labor-rent, namely when p - alpha*q = sqrt(ap).

[*The section continues with a detailed analysis of how the equalization between the two determinants of wages is brought about through the choice between establishing new farms versus increasing capital on existing ones, demonstrating through successive numerical examples that so long as p - alpha*q < sqrt(ap), raising capital on existing farms is more profitable than establishing new ones, and that only when sqrt(ap) = p - alpha*q, i.e., when q has reached the value at which p - alpha*q equals sqrt(ap), does the unconditional maximum of rent obtain.*]

The question of how the expressions for wages found by such different methods are to be brought into accord, and how the level of relative capital is to be determined, now finds the following solution through this investigation:

So long as p - alpha*q is less than sqrt(ap), the increase of relative capital is more profitable than the establishment of new farms.

Only when sqrt(ap) = p - alpha*q, i.e., when q has reached the value at which p - alpha*q = sqrt(ap), does the unconditional maximum of rent obtain.

If q exceeds this value, the labor-rent sinks. It therefore lies in the interest of the workers to give q exactly the magnitude at which p - alpha*q = sqrt(ap), and accordingly this value of q is the determinant of the level of relative capital.

Very greatly must I fear having wearied the patience of several of my readers with the algebraic calculations; for it is not unknown to me how burdensome and inconvenient letter-formulas are to many, even to some scholars.

**But the application of mathematics must surely be permitted where the truth cannot be found without it.**

Had there been in other fields of knowledge such an aversion to the mathematical calculus as in agriculture and political economy, we would still be in complete ignorance of the laws of the heavens; and navigation, which through the extension of astronomy now connects all parts of the world with one another, would still be confined to mere coastal sailing.

### Section 19

**Wages are equal to the additional product brought forth by the last hired worker in a large enterprise.**

Let us imagine an estate complex on which more than a hundred workers are employed.

The measure of labor that the management of these estates requires is by no means a fixed quantity.

The field can be cultivated more or less carefully, the threshing of grain, the gathering of potatoes carried out more or less thoroughly -- and with this the required quantum of labor changes.

Let us here choose the gathering of potatoes as an example.

If only the potatoes lying on the surface after the digging or hoeing are collected, one person can daily gather more than 30 Berlin bushels. But if one demands that the earth be scratched up with the hand hoe to collect still more potatoes covered with soil, the product of a person's labor immediately sinks deeply. The more one insists on the thorough gathering of potatoes, the smaller the product of labor becomes, and if one also wishes to harvest the last bushel contained in an area of 100 square rods, this last bushel requires so much labor that the person employed for this purpose cannot even feed himself from his product, much less satisfy his other needs.

Suppose the entire quantity of potatoes grown on a field of 100 square rods amounts to 100 Berlin bushels. Suppose further that of these there are harvested:

When employed for gathering: The additional yield of the last person:

4 persons ... 80 bushels
5 persons ... 86.6 ... 6.6 bushels
6 persons ... 91.0 ... 4.4
7 persons ... 94.0 ... 3.0
8 persons ... 96.0 ... 2.0
9 persons ... 97.3 ... 1.3
10 persons ... 98.2 ... 0.9

To what degree of thoroughness must the farmer, proceeding consistently, carry the gathering of potatoes?

Undoubtedly to the point where the value of the additional yield is compensated by the cost of the labor devoted to it.

If, for example, the value of potatoes used as cattle feed is somewhere 5 schillings per bushel and the daily wage is 8 schillings per person, then the employment of the 9th person yields an additional product of 1.3 bushels at 5 schillings = 6.5 schillings, costs 8 schillings, and brings a loss of 1.5 schillings. By contrast, through the 8th person employed, with an expenditure of 8 schillings, an additional yield of 2 bushels at 5 schillings = 10 schillings, thus a surplus of 2 schillings, is gained. One will therefore, in order to obtain the highest net yield, devote approximately 8.6 days' labor of one person to the gathering of potatoes, and must content oneself with a yield of approximately 96.8 bushels.

Under conditions, however, where the daily wage rises to 15 schillings -- as can easily be the case with a very extensive potato cultivation, where people must be brought from afar -- the additional yield through the employment of the 7th person just barely covers the daily wage, and of the 100 bushels that have grown in total, only 94 bushels are then consistently harvested.

If, however, the potatoes can be utilized at 16 schillings per bushel by being used as horse feed, for distilling spirits, or other manufactures, then at a daily wage of 8 schillings, the employment of 11 days' labor of one person is still advisable, and of the 100 bushels of potatoes in the ground, 98.8 bushels are then harvested.

At a daily wage of 15 schillings and a value of potatoes of 16 schillings per bushel, on the other hand, the employment of a 14th person barely still pays.

The degree of thoroughness to which the threshing of grain from the straw must be carried is subject to similar rules as the gathering of potatoes.

The loss of grain during the harvesting of cereals -- which is often very considerable -- can be significantly reduced by the employment of more workers, since on the one hand the right moment for mowing, binding, and carting in is better observed and the harvest accomplished more quickly, and on the other hand, instead of mowing with the scythe, cutting with the sickle can be introduced. Here too one will consistently increase the number of workers so far as the value of what they save still covers, or slightly exceeds, the expenditure on daily wages.

From this it now follows:

1) that a rise in wages, with the value of products remaining constant, causes a reduction in the number of workers to be employed and simultaneously a reduction in the yield of the fruits to be gathered and threshed;

2) that a rise in the value of products, with wages remaining constant, has exactly the opposite effect, since more workers can then be profitably employed, and the fruits can be more carefully gathered and more thoroughly threshed, thus yielding a larger return;

3) since it is in the interest of the entrepreneurs -- whether these be farmers or manufacturers -- to increase the number of their workers so far as advantage still accrues to them from the increase, the limit of this increase is where the additional product of the last worker is absorbed by the wage he receives; conversely, therefore, **wages are equal to the additional product of the last worker.**

Since the number of workers cannot be increased or diminished by a fraction, the point where earnings and costs compensate each other cannot be precisely met in a small-scale operation; but this inequality in the individual case balances out in the grand whole, since in one case more, in another fewer workers are employed than the maximum net yield requires.

Since this drawback of small-scale operation extends not only to the number of workers but also to the number of draft animals to be kept and of instruments and machines to be used, this is, incidentally, one of the factors favoring large-scale operation.

In the foregoing example, it is true, the discussion was only about the more complete recovery of what the soil has produced; but the conclusions drawn therefrom have their full validity also for labor directed at raising the productivity of the soil and producing larger harvests.

Through the increase of labor power, the soil can be more carefully plowed, cleaned, and drained, the right moment for sowing can be better observed, and thereby the uniform yield of crops can be more secured and their average yield substantially raised. On the other hand, in most situations the productive power of the soil can be very greatly increased by the application of mold, marl, and the soil types that the field does not possess in sufficient quantity. All such improvements, however, have this in common: that with their quantitative increase, the effect grows not in direct but in diminishing proportion, and ultimately can even become zero.

[*Von Thunen illustrates this with an example of applying mold to fields, where each successive half-inch increment yields diminishing returns.*]

Since the labor costs grow in direct proportion with the amount of mold applied, but the effect diminishes more and more and ultimately becomes zero, there must here -- as with all the agricultural operations named above -- be a point where the costs of labor equal the value of the improvement, and this is the point to which improvements must consistently be carried.

Indeed, one can say that the entire task of rational agriculture consists in finding, for each individual branch thereof, in the two ascending series "increased labor and enhanced product" the corresponding terms, in order to determine the point where the value and costs of labor are in equilibrium -- for when labor is extended to this point, net yield reaches the maximum.

The success of the practical farmer depends to a very great extent on whether he possesses the tact to solve this problem approximately. This tact is, as a rule, entirely lacking in merely theoretically trained farmers. But this can hardly be otherwise; for agricultural science is, in this direction, still completely undeveloped, and in its textbooks this subject, which should pervade the whole and around which everything should be organized into a unity, is scarcely touched upon.

Interesting in relation to this subject is a comparison between Germany and North America.

In the former country, at a daily wage of 12 schillings and a price of rye of 1 thaler 12 schillings per bushel, operations can be undertaken and poor land cultivated where the daily labor of one man is rewarded with a product of only 1-1/3 bushels of rye.

In North America, by the most moderate accounts, the daily labor of one man costs at least 32 schillings, and the value of a Berlin bushel of rye in the interior of the country scarcely reaches 1 thaler. There, therefore, every agricultural operation in which the daily labor of one man does not yield a product of 2-2/3 bushels of rye or more is attended with loss.

What an enormous difference in the agriculture of both countries this single circumstance produces!

In a newspaper article from North America, discussing which professions among immigrants find the best livelihood, it says:

"Least of all do the academically trained agronomists succeed here; for with us it is not a matter of extracting a few percent more produce from the soil, but of saving on costly labor."

This reproach strikes precisely at science as it is taught. For the study of genuine science should qualify one to appreciate all conditions correctly and guard against such errors. But if the study of science in its present state produces exactly the opposite effect, this is proof of its deficiency.

Still the old phantom will not depart from our agricultural writings -- as if there existed an ideal of agriculture valid for all stages of development of human society, as if every lower system of husbandry, every extensive, labor-saving economy, were evidence of the ignorance of practical farmers.

[*Von Thunen then discusses the Russian government sending young men to study German agriculture, warning that applying intensive German methods in sparsely populated regions of Russia leads to ruin.*]

Even in Germany there is no lack of those who have fallen victim to a premature introduction of crop-rotation farming.

**"The value of the labor of the last hired worker is also the wage thereof."**

This proposition, arising from the foregoing observations, permits so manifold an application to social life that it may well be permitted to interrupt the systematic course of our investigation, to leave the isolated state with its wilderness capable of cultivation and the assumption of a stationary population for a short time, and to turn to reality.

As in the example cited of the large estate complex, so too in reality the striving of entrepreneurs is quite universal to increase the number of their workers until no further advantage accrues from further increase -- i.e., until the wage of labor reaches the value of labor -- because this lies in the nature of things and in the interest of the entrepreneurs.

The wage, however, that the last hired worker receives must be normative for all workers of equal skill and competence; for unequal wages cannot be paid for equal performance.

But if already now in reality wages reach the value of labor, and the people nevertheless find themselves in a depressed, wretched condition, how is a remedy possible?

Proudhon (in his Philosophy of Political Economy) is indignant that the notary receives as much for a document he drafts in one hour as the day laborer for twelve hours of heavy work. The same writer further finds it unjust that the factory overseer receives a higher salary than the porter.

But let us ask: what motivates the factory owner to pay the overseer more? It is not favor, not philanthropy, not friendship; he would dismiss him instantly if he could do without him, if the benefit the overseer brings him were not at least equal to his salary. Here too, then, the value of the service is the measure of the compensation.

To attempt to introduce the length of working time instead of the value of labor as the measure of wages is a chimera.

But if the worker receives in his wages the value of his labor, it follows that the depressed condition of the workers does not arise from the greed and avarice of the landowners and factory owners, since these -- as this is no question of distributing alms -- cannot pay more for labor than what it is worth to them, and that therefore the source of the misery of the working class must be sought elsewhere and more deeply.

One can make the following objection: "Even if the last hired workers produce no more than what they receive in wages, the earlier hired workers yield to the entrepreneurs a very considerable surplus, which gives them the means to pay a higher wage, and thus it is only the good will of the entrepreneurs that is lacking to improve the lot of the workers."

In this objection, however, there is a confusion and conflation of moral obligation with commercial duty.

In terms of political economy, no labor may be undertaken that does not cover its costs; for otherwise **the labor that is supposed to create national wealth would on the contrary diminish and consume it** -- and through the diminution of national capital, the people would only become still more wretched.

The moral obligation of the rich to alleviate the distress of the poor must not be fulfilled in this way, but must take effect in other ways.

Moreover, the individual factory owner who undertook labors that did not repay him the costs would sacrifice his fortune uselessly if all others did not do the same. But even the association and combination of all factory owners of a country for this purpose would not always suffice; for the factories that produce goods for export, or that must withstand the competition of foreigners in their own country, would thereby be ruined, and their workers would then become wholly breadless.

Let us now consider, to make this subject still clearer to ourselves, the necessary effects of the rise and fall of wages.

Suppose a rise in wages takes place without the number of workers diminishing. Then the last hired workers cost the landowners and factory owners more than they bring in. These will then, following their interest -- and this is no injustice, but lies in their vocation -- dismiss workers, and continue doing so until the product of the last remaining worker equals in value the increased wage. But this renders a number of workers breadless, and to avoid starving, they will have to resolve to work again for the former wage -- i.e., a rise in wages is not possible under these conditions.

If, on the other hand, the population in the working classes increases while cultivated land and capital retain the same size, then the additional workers can no longer find employment at the previous wage. For since this wage already absorbs the entire product of the last hired worker, and each further hired worker delivers an ever smaller product, the hiring of the additional workers at the previous wage rate would be attended with outright loss for the entrepreneurs. Only when these workers are content with a lower wage can the entrepreneurs hire them and have new labors performed whose value corresponds to the reduced wage.

But if the workers continue to multiply despite sinking wages, then wages must also continue to sink ever lower, because the work that can be given to them becomes ever less productive.

If now with the growing population, labor must be extended to ever more unrewarding objects, to ever poorer soil, where is the limit to the sinking of wages?

This limit is found only when labor becomes so unproductive that the product of labor equals a, i.e., equals the necessary subsistence; for at a lower wage than what is required for his sustenance, man cannot work.

[*Von Thunen then discusses the differential abilities among workers in reality, noting that with overpopulation, only the strongest find employment, that even the most capable may find work only in their prime and must suffer want in old age, and that ultimately the burden of poor relief may become overwhelming -- as was already the case in Ireland at the time of writing (1846).*]

Peace produces prosperity, prosperity overpopulation, overpopulation misery.

How is one to escape this enchanted circle?

But shall -- so we must ask -- after each brief period of peace, recovery, and blossoming prosperity, the human race in the great majority always anew face misery?

Does it lie in the plan of Providence that as the earth becomes more inhabited, the future should grow ever darker, the misery ever greater and more inescapable?

Surely not.

But what, then, are the conditions to whose fulfillment Providence has tied the happiness of mankind?

This is precisely the great problem that lies before us -- which we can here only mention, but into whose divining exploration we cannot yet enter.

The correct understanding of the subject treated here could well serve to cut off many wrong paths in the proposals of the socialists. May the socialists direct their entire attention to making labor more productive; if they succeed in this, they will truly improve the lot of the workers.

But it must not be overlooked -- what, however, is already apparent from the entire course of the investigation -- that the value of labor, in the sense in which this expression is taken here, is by no means fixed and independent of other forces; for it depends on the productiveness of the object to which the labor is directed. How high or low on the scale of productiveness the object stands to which labor is devoted depends, however, on the greater or lesser supply of workers. The limit, however, to which by means of the great supply of workers the value and wages of labor can sink is formed by the sum of the worker's necessary subsistence.

Between the value of labor, the supply of labor, and the subsistence of the worker, there thus exists a chain-linkage.

The older political economists took into consideration only the last two links of this chain, and thereby contributed much to the confusion of the concept of wages.*

*[Footnote: Rau is, to my knowledge, the first who remedies this deficiency, in that he, in his Principles of Political Economy, establishes the proposition: "the price of labor depends on value, costs, and competition."]

Great wrong have the political economists committed in that they took the wage formed from the two factors they considered to be the natural one, and drew from it the conclusion that by Providence itself nothing other was intended for the workers than what is necessary for the sustaining of their lives.

The socialists conceive the task more highly; for they demand for the worker not merely sustenance but also enjoyment of life and education.

Concerning the relationship of socialism to political economy, Stein, in his brilliant work "Socialism and Communism in Present-Day France," expresses himself in the following words:

"Political economy in itself has merely the task of recognizing the existing relationship between property and labor, even where it grasps it in its deepest life, its laws; it can predict the future formation thereof but cannot itself determine it, for it has no supreme fundamental principle that would not be subordinate to another. But this the socialism establishes in the idea of the destiny of man, and thereby sets itself above political economy as that which uses and governs it; the former is essentially comprehending, the latter is formative."

I cannot find the reproach here made against political economy unfounded, but it applies only to the science in its present form, not to the essence of the science itself. For nothing prevents it from taking up the fundamental principle of socialism and making it its own. Indeed, I have found -- as the continuation of this writing will show -- that the deeper penetration into the question "What is the natural wage of labor?" leads in its final stages directly to the question of the destiny of man.

In my view, we can approach the investigation of truth only through the fusion of both sciences. Through such a union, the wings would also be clipped of the fantasies of the socialists, with their proposals springing from ignorance of the laws of political economy.

Proudhon too -- in his Philosophy of Political Economy -- is of this opinion, in that he hopes to solve the task of the socialists through a reform of political economy.

After this digression, we return to the isolated state.

### Section 20

**The production costs of capital and of capital rent.**

In Section 5 the question was posed whether between the production costs of capital and the price thereof -- i.e., the interest rate at which one can obtain capital on loan -- a similar relationship prevails as between the production costs of tradeable goods and their price.

There, in the development of the rules by which price is formed, in Section 13, tradeable goods were divided into two classes: the first class comprises tradeable goods that can be produced in any desired quantity at constant costs, while to the second class belong tradeable goods whose expanded production is attended with increasing costs.

To the first class belong implements, machines, and many other objects. In these, not the utility they provide is paid for, but the production costs become the regulator of price. Here, then, every connection between use-value and production costs seems to be suspended. This, however, is not the case, as will appear from the following consideration.

[*Von Thunen illustrates with the example of plows: although the use-value of plows far exceeds their price regulated by production costs, there is still a limit to how many plows a farm should acquire -- namely, plows are increased until the last one acquired barely covers the costs of its interest and annual depreciation.*]

So little, then, as the use-value or utility of the plows decides the price thereof in general, it nonetheless establishes the limit of their increase.

As with the plow, so it is with all commodities that can be increased without limit at constant cost per unit.

To the tradeable goods of the second class belongs grain, when the increased demand can be met only through the cultivation of less fertile or less favorably situated soil than that hitherto in cultivation, or on one and the same soil only through the introduction of a more intensive, more costly system of husbandry. There also belong all metals, which, if no new mines are discovered, must be extracted from the bosom of the earth at ever greater depth.

But what forms the limit to the increase of capital, and what is the measure of the production costs of capital?

The application of capital makes human labor more productive, as has been shown in many ways in the foregoing. With the larger product of labor, the surplus grows, and with it the ease of capital creation. The production of capital thus becomes ever cheaper the more capital is formed. In this respect, capital and tradeable goods of the second class stand in direct opposition -- since in the former, increase becomes ever cheaper, in the latter, ever more costly. Capital obtains the expansion of its use by the fact that, in the measure that it becomes cheaper, it increasingly takes the place of human labor.

Capital creation would therefore have to proceed without limit, were it not that with the increase of capital, the utility of the same simultaneously diminishes.

This diminution of utility arises from two causes:

1) When the most effective implements, machines, etc., of which capital consists, are available in sufficient quantity, then further capital creation must turn to objects of lesser effectiveness.

2) In agriculture, the abundance of capital leads, if it is everywhere to find application, to the cultivation of less productive, less favorably situated land, or also to a more intensive, more costly system of husbandry -- and in these cases, the last invested capital yields a lesser rent than the previously invested.

This dual nature of capital makes the solution of the posed problem very difficult. It also follows therefrom that capital belongs neither to the first nor to the second class of tradeable goods, but forms a class of its own.

The surplus that labor yields can receive a twofold destination: it can be used

a) for the accumulation and storage of a supply, with the intention of later living from it without working;

b) for productive investment in agriculture or in trades.

In the former respect, the unlimited growth of capital is favorable to the worker, because wages and surplus thereby grow, and the worker can then in shorter time acquire the supply from which he can later live without working.

But the supply is not yet capital, but only the stuff of capital, and loses, when consumed without yielding a replacement, the duration that is required to satisfy the concept of capital.

If we now exclude from capital the supplies not productively invested, and understand by capital only the wealth that yields a rent, then our task is greatly simplified, since now not capital itself but the fruit thereof -- i.e., the rent -- becomes the object of desire.

With this we arrive at the question: what are the production costs of rent, and under what conditions is rent produced with the least cost?

Capital is the product of labor; but this product in turn replaces human labor and itself serves for the creation of new capital. Between capital and labor there is thus a close connection and constant interaction that seems inseparable.

But since the original capital (Section 8) has arisen purely from human labor, and since it has been possible (Section 13) to reduce the effectiveness of capital to labor, labor, as the creator of capital, is the only correct measure for the production costs of capital and of rent.

But just as the price of commodities is regulated by the minimum of production costs, and the costs enlarged through unskillful and incorrect use of capital and labor are not compensated in the price of commodities -- so too here the minimum of labor by which a rent can be produced must form the measure for the production costs.

On the quantity of labor required to produce a rent of a given magnitude, however, the level of wages has the most essential influence, and our task now becomes: to investigate the wage at which rent is produced with the least expenditure of labor.

We choose here for wages the expression a + y, in which y is a still entirely indeterminate number.

The Y.A. directed at capital creation through the establishment of a new farm is rewarded, according to Section 15, with a rent of (p - (a + y)) * y / (q(a + y)).

Let the required rent = r.

Then the number of workers required to produce it is: n = r * q(a + y) / ((p - (a + y)) * y).

[*Von Thunen provides a numerical example with r = 1, i.e., rent = r times c = 100c, showing that for different values of y, the number of workers required first decreases and then increases, confirming that there is a minimum.*]

Setting the differential of the function equal to zero yields:

a + y = sqrt(ap).

The wage sqrt(ap) thus fulfills the condition of producing the rent with the minimum of labor force.

What then is the interest rate when rent is produced with the least expenditure of labor?

[*The mathematical derivation proceeds to yield:*]

z = y / (aq) = (sqrt(ap) - a) / (aq).

We thus obtain the remarkable result that **the interest rate equals one divided by the number of workers who produced the subsistence consumed during capital creation.**

But it must not be overlooked that this proposition is valid only for the wage = sqrt(ap) and for the surplus y = sqrt(ap) - a.

### Section 21

**The law governing the division between capitalists and workers.**

In what proportion must the product of labor be divided between the worker and the owner of the capital with which he works, and what wage accrues to the worker accordingly?

The wage laborers can rightly make the following two demands:

1) The labor through which the capital was created shall be rewarded per annual labor with no higher rent than the annual labor of the wage laborer, when the latter lends out the surplus remaining after deduction of necessary maintenance from his wages, at interest. Or in other words: both kinds of labor, namely the labor contained in the capital and the labor performed for wages, shall (equal quality presupposed) yield equal rents.

2) Wages must have the level at which the production of capital rent is obtained with the least expenditure of labor.

The second demand is met, as proved in the preceding section, when wages = sqrt(ap).

Whether this wage also satisfies the first demand, the following calculation may decide:

At the wage = sqrt(ap), according to Section 15, the rent that the capital-creating worker obtains is:

(sqrt(ap) - a)^2 / (aq).

For the wage laborer, at the surplus of sqrt(ap) - a and the interest rate y/(aq), the rent is:

(sqrt(ap) - a) * (sqrt(ap) - a) / (aq) = (sqrt(ap) - a)^2 / (aq).

At the wage = sqrt(ap) and the interest rate of y/(aq), the compensation for the labor embodied in capital and for wage labor is thus **in equilibrium**.

If, on the other hand, the worker demands from the capitalist a wage exceeding sqrt(ap), this demand is an unfair and unjust one that must be rejected; for he then demands unequal compensation for labors of equal quality. Such a demand also contravenes the worker's own interest, since by accumulating a fortune through which he wishes to improve his condition, the rent the worker acquires, instead of rising, is diminished by wages higher than sqrt(ap) -- as follows from Section 15.

In the preceding section the question was raised: "What forms the limit to the increase of capital?" and we can now answer this question by saying that when labor in trades and factories producing consumption articles is rewarded with a higher rent than in capital creation, then capital increase, with a stationary population, ceases.

We have now sought to determine the relationship between wages and the interest rate by four different methods and viewpoints; namely we have

1) investigated capital creation through labor;
2) regarded capital as replacing labor;
3) determined the interest rate through the utility of the last invested particle of capital; and finally
4) taken the additional product of the last hired worker as the measure of wages.

Since from all these investigations the wage = sqrt(ap) has emerged victoriously, I believe -- if one calls the wage corresponding to the organization of man and the physical world the natural one -- that I may venture to state the proposition:

**The natural wage of labor is sqrt(ap).**

### Section 22

**Influence of the fertility of the soil on wages and the interest rate.**

We have found the natural wage = sqrt(ap); but we must now ask whether this contains for the worker a guarantee that his wages will not become wretched, since these depend on the magnitude of p, and since the value of p in turn depends on the fertility of the soil on which capital and labor are employed. The more p decreases, and when p = a, wages sink to a, i.e., to the amount of the necessary subsistence.

To learn more closely the influence of the fertility of the soil, let us now substitute different values for p.

1. Let p = 300c; then for a = 100c and q = 12, wages A = sqrt(ap) = 173c, and the interest rate z = approximately 6.1 percent.

2. Let p = 200; then A = sqrt(20000) = 142, and z = approximately 3.5 percent.

3. Let p = 150; then A = sqrt(15000) = 122, and z = approximately 1.8 percent.

4. Let p = a = 100c; then A = sqrt(ap) = 100, and z = 0.

We see, then, that **workers and capitalists have a common interest in the increase of production; both lose when production declines, and both gain when it increases.**

**Problem.** To find the value of p when z = 2 percent.

[*The calculation yields p = 153.76 and A = 124.*]

At such a low interest rate, new capital will scarcely be accumulated -- since this demands also on the part of capitalists the renunciation of enjoyments -- and scarcely any capitalist will be found who will invest his capital in an enterprise yielding only 2 percent. But even then, wages still exceed the needs of the worker by 24 percent.

**So long as wages = sqrt(ap) -- and this is of decisive importance -- the worker is also protected against want and privation.**

Quite differently do matters stand under our present European conditions, where no ownerless land is any longer to be found, and the worker is deprived of the possibility of escaping the low wage offer of his employer by cultivating a hitherto uncultivated piece of land.

Here competition decides the level of wages; here wages = a + y, where y is entirely indeterminate, and the interest rate z = (p - (a + y)) / (q(a + y)).

The smaller y becomes, the more z grows, as the following example shows:

Let a = 100, p = 200, q = 12; then

for y = 50, z = 2.77 percent,
for y = 25, z = 5.0 percent,
for y = 0, z = 8.33 percent.

It therefore lies in the interest of the entrepreneurs and capitalists to push wages ever lower, and while the worker can procure for his wages only the bare necessities of life, the capitalist enjoys the high interest rate of 8-1/3 percent.

**Here, then, the interest of the capitalists not only parts from that of the workers, but the interest of both stands diametrically opposed.**

**In this opposed interest lies the reason why proletarians and the propertied will henceforth stand in hostile opposition to one another, and will remain unreconciled so long as the cleavage in their interest is not resolved.**

But not only to the prosperity of his employer, but also to the national prosperity does the worker stand without interest.

Through discoveries in manufacturing, through the construction of highways and railways, through the establishment of new trade connections, etc., the national income can from time to time increase very considerably. But under our present social organization, the worker is not touched by this; his condition remains as it was, and the entire increase in income falls to the capitalists and landowners.

[*Von Thunen provides a concrete example from Mecklenburg, where in recent decades the lease value of good arable land rose from approximately 100 to 200 thalers per "Last," but none of this extraordinary increase in national income accrued to the working class.*]

Had the social organization been such that even only a fifth of this had to fall to the workers, then happiness and contentment would have spread over thousands of families; the agitations and acts of violence by which the workers in the spring of 1848 extorted higher wages would not have occurred, and the beautiful patriarchal bond that formerly existed between master and subordinate would not have been torn asunder.

The transition from the class of workers to that of property owners could serve as equalization, were not the hindrance to this transition contained in the low wage rate itself, and indeed for the following two reasons:

1) At the present wage rate, the workers can accumulate either no capital at all or only insignificant amounts, and the creation of new capital thereby becomes almost the monopoly of the entrepreneurs and landowners.

2) At the low wage, the workers are unable to provide their children with the instruction needed to acquire the knowledge qualifying them for carrying on a trade or for a higher position in civil society.

Thus the low wage itself contains the reason for its perpetuation. How is one to escape from this circle?

All these evils, from which the social condition of Europe is ailing, fall away for the wage sqrt(ap).

In sqrt(ap), the worker's wage is proportional to the value of his product; in our present conditions, the worker's wage is entirely independent of the value of his product.

**In the separation of the worker from his product lies the source of the evil.**

Workers paid by piecework have over those paid by the day the great advantage that the worker's earnings rise and fall with his diligence, and that the worker thus works, as it were, for himself, and therefore with more pleasure and love for the task. Nevertheless, even with piecework payment, through competition among the workers, their earnings can sink deeply.

In piecework, only the quantity of labor, not the value of the labor contained in the product, is paid; while with the wage sqrt(ap), the worker directly shares in the value of his labor.

Whether and under what conditions the wage sqrt(ap) is possible for our European conditions -- this does not emerge from our investigations so far, but will be the subject of the continuation of this writing.

This much, however, is evident: that even if the complete restoration to the natural wage were not possible, the evils would nonetheless be greatly diminished if the workers received even only a part of their wages as a share in the value of their labor.

Let us now glance at the conditions in the North American free states.

There, as in the isolated state, fertile land is available in unlimited quantity for nothing or for a trifle.

There, as in the isolated state, only the distance from the marketplace can set limits to the extension of cultivation.

But these limits are being pushed ever further outward through steamship navigation on the rivers, through the construction of canals and railways.

There, then, the wage sqrt(ap) can be realized, and has in fact been realized; for we find in America between wages and the interest rate a similar relationship to what we have developed from our formulas for fertile soil.

As a result of this relationship between workers and capitalists, we find in North America general prosperity growing by giant strides; there is no chronic separation between the different classes, and among them harmony and peace prevail; and even among the lower popular class, the first elements of schooling -- reading, writing, and arithmetic -- are more widely spread than in Europe.

The first men who trod the earth under a favorable sky must have been in a similar situation -- and therefore one has perhaps called this condition the paradisiacal one.

Is such a condition, with the density of population, vanished from the earth forever?

Or can humanity, through the development of its mental powers and through the subordination of the passions to the rule of reason, reconquer this condition, rise from the gifts of nature to intellectual possession?

### Section 23

**Application of the formulas found to concrete cases.**

In the investigation so far, product of labor, interest rate, and wages have been expressed through letter-formulas. The letters represent any numerical value, and the formulas expressed therein thereby rise to universal validity.

For the concrete case, however, the letters assume a definite numerical value, and if the formula is correct, the results expressed in numbers must also show regularity.

In our next investigation of the relationship between wages and land rent, which will be the subject of the second section of this writing, we already need the statement in numbers of a, p, q, y, and z for a concrete case.

These numbers may not, however, be assumed arbitrarily, but must be taken from reality, because reality is to be the touchstone of their correctness.

In the absence of other data, I shall now attempt to ascertain the value of these letters for the conditions of the estate Tellow, and shall append the calculations therefor to the following part of this writing.

Given the great importance that the question now has -- how much income the day laborer needs for a decent, want-free life -- I am already now, in Appendix A, laying before the reader for examination the calculation of the maintenance costs and income of a day-laborer family at Tellow in the 14-year period from 1833 to 1847.

Whoever has recognized what is right with regard to the workers is also under the moral obligation to bring the right to fulfillment -- so far as this lies within his powers.

For more than 20 years I have harbored the lively wish to grant my day laborers a share of the estate income as a supplement; but not to place this supplement at their disposal, but rather to use it for the formation of a capital for them.

At that time, two obstacles stood in the way of realizing my wish, namely:

1) the obligation to my family; and

2) the concern that such an arrangement might cause discontent and agitation among the workers on neighboring estates.

After, however, the first obstacle had lost its weight, and now also in the spring of 1848, as a result of the mighty popular movement, significant concessions were made to the workers on almost all estates, I could no longer hesitate to carry out the long-cherished wish.

The provisions made therefor are contained in Appendix B.

With arrangements of this kind, however, the ultimate outcome must above all be kept in view.

An example may illustrate this more closely.

The expenditure for the physician and apothecary costs the estate owner on average approximately 3 thalers per day-laborer family annually. If the estate owner now, as a result of an agreement, gives the day laborer 3 thalers annually under the condition that the latter is to bear the medical costs in future illnesses himself, the expenditure of the estate owner is thereby neither increased nor diminished. But what a change occurs thereby in the situation and happiness of the day-laborer family! If the man is now stricken with a serious, prolonged illness, he will only rarely be able to reconcile it with his duty to his family to spend so large a sum as the medical costs amount to on himself.

As a rule, however, he will not have saved but spent the money received for this purpose, and then in the time of need he lies helpless and prostrate.

In cases where the existing wage rate satisfies the real needs and reasonable wishes of the workers, a supplement through raising the daily wage will have a similar result. The enjoyments of the day laborers border so closely upon their necessities that scarcely a dividing line can be drawn, and one must therefore not attribute to them the strength -- which even many of the well-to-do lack -- of sacrificing present enjoyment to concern for the future. The worker will on the contrary in most cases consume the supplement, save nothing for old age, but feel the poverty of old age all the more bitterly, the more he has accustomed himself to greater needs that he can then no longer satisfy.

Far worse, however, is it still that through the unconditional raising of daily wages, the opposing interest of employers and workers is not mediated, and thus the fundamental evil of our social conditions remains in full sharpness.

#### Section 6

**Attempt to determine the income of a day-laborer family at Tellow.**

The income of the workers arises from: (1) what they receive from their employer in wages, emoluments, etc.; (2) the increase in value they impart to emoluments by their own labor; (3) the small capital contained in their livestock.

1. Costs to the estate = income for the family: 132 thalers 37 schillings (without the tow-spinning deduction).

7. **Pig-keeping:** One fattened pig of approximately 250 pounds slaughter weight worth 17 thalers 24 schillings, minus 14 thalers 27 schillings in costs = approximately 2 thalers 45 schillings net gain.

8. **Poultry:** Approximately 32 schillings net.

9. **Gleaning wheat:** Approximately 2 thalers 16 schillings.

**Total income of a day-laborer family: 168 thalers 26 schillings** (or 196 thalers 18 schillings Prussian Courant).

The worker thus earns through labor performed with his wife and children for himself, and through the small capital in his livestock, an increase of 168 thalers 26 schillings minus 132 thalers 13 schillings = **36 thalers 13 schillings** above what the estate spends on him.

## Appendix B

### Provisions Concerning the Share of the Village Inhabitants at Tellow in the Estate Income

#### Section 1

List of the income items in which the village inhabitants shall henceforth have a share:

1) Income from grain of all kinds sold, excluding grain sold to the village inhabitants themselves.
2) From rapeseed, turnip seed, camelina, and other oil plants.
3) From clover seed and seed grass.
4) From potatoes, excluding those sold to the village inhabitants.
5) From wood sold from the local forest.
6) From the sheep flock.
7) From the dairy (cow-keeping) and pig breeding.

#### Section 2

The accounting year begins July 1 and closes June 30. At the close of each year, the entire stock of grain, oil seeds, clover and grass seed shall be measured and valued at fixed prices [specified in detail for each grain type]. If the value of the stock at year-end exceeds that at the beginning, the surplus is added to income; if there is a deficit, it is deducted from cash income.

#### Section 3

Likewise, the increase or decrease in value of horses, cows, sheep, and pigs shall be added to or subtracted from cash income at year-end, valued at fixed prices per head (horses 70 thalers, cows and bulls 20 thalers, sheep 2 thalers, pigs 8 thalers).

#### Section 4

From the income thus determined, the following expenditures shall be deducted:

1) Purchases of grain, oil plants, potatoes, clover and grass seed;
2) Purchases of horses, cows, sheep, and pigs;
3) All war taxes and war costs, excluding the delivery and use of produce raised on the estate itself;
4) Losses from fire damage, insofar as they exceed the compensation provided by fire insurance companies.

#### Section 5

If after deducting these four items the income exceeds the sum of 5,500 thalers Prussian Courant, then from this excess each village inhabitant belonging to the classes specified below shall be credited with one-half percent.

The following village inhabitants shall participate:

1) All able-bodied inhabitants in possession of a dwelling, working for the estate with man and wife (or a servant in place of the latter) -- i.e., all able-bodied day laborers whose wives perform estate service.
2) The contract workers (Deputatisten): the steward, the head hoer, the forest warden, the wheelwright, and the cowherd.
3) The schoolteacher and the shepherd.
4) The weaver, if he faithfully performs the harvest assistance incumbent upon him.
5) The farmhands whose wives occupy a house in the village and work for the estate.

In households where an able-bodied man lives with his adult son who performs all heavy labor, the half-percent shall be divided equally between father and son.

**Remark.** The income from the named articles, after deduction of the stated expenses, has for the estate Tellow in the average of the 14 years from 1833 to 1847 amounted to approximately 7,500 thalers Prussian Courant. If the income remained unchanged, each village inhabitant's share would amount annually to 10 thalers. But if, as a result of advancing soil cultivation, this income rises by 1,000 thalers annually, the worker's share would increase not in the ratio of 75:85 but in the ratio of 10:15. The interest of the workers is hereby most intimately linked with the increase of production. The number of village inhabitants who currently have a share in the estate income is 21.

#### Section 6

Should the income in individual unproductive years, or through special misfortunes, not reach the sum of 5,500 thalers, the shortfall shall be deducted from the income of the next year or of the following years, and only from the surplus then remaining above 5,500 thalers shall the village inhabitants receive their share of one-half percent.

#### Section 7

Whoever is found guilty of embezzlement or theft, however trifling, and is convicted thereof, forfeits participation in the future estate income. Whether this exclusion shall be permanent or only for a certain number of years is left to the discretion of the estate owner. The estate owner also reserves the right to impose such exclusion for serious offenses such as gross insubordination, attempts at incitement, and the like.

#### Section 8

The purpose of this arrangement is:

1) that the village inhabitants shall take immediate part in the weal and woe of the estate owner, and shall, as it were, form one family with him;

2) that the workers shall enjoy an income enhanced each year through progressive improvement, steadily growing; and

3) that above all, a carefree, cheerful old age shall be secured for the worker; that after he has spent his vigorous manhood in strenuous activity, he shall not, in later years when strength and health wane, suffer want, not live on the charity of others, not be a burden to his children, but rather be enabled to leave something to his children.

#### Section 9

To pursue this goal, the following provisions are made:

1) Every village inhabitant who, according to the above provisions, qualifies for participation in the estate income receives a savings-book in which his share of the estate income is recorded each year.

2) On the sum recorded in the book, the estate owner pays 4-1/6 percent, or one groschen per thaler, interest per annum.

3) The recording of the share of estate income for the period from July 1 of the past year to June 30 of the current year, as well as the payment of interest, takes place at Christmas each year -- and this gift shall in all respects be regarded as a Christmas present.

4) The capital recorded in the savings-books is non-callable by both parties so long as the holder has not completed his 60th year of life. But as soon as the village inhabitant has reached the age of 60 years, his capital shall be placed at his free disposal.

5) If the man dies before reaching the age of 60, his widow inherits the capital recorded in the book. Whether the widow may then dispose of the entire capital, or whether a part thereof shall be retained for the surviving children, is left in each individual case to the discretion of the estate owner.

These provisions take effect immediately and are valid already for the year from July 1, 1847 to July 1, 1848.

The arrangement here made expires with the death of the present estate owner and shall not be binding on his sons. But they shall be obligated to take every possible care for the complete security of the capital recorded in the savings-books and to pay the interest at Christmas of each year.

Should, however, my sons, or also the village inhabitants, consider it advisable for reasons of complete security to deposit these small capitals in a public savings bank, the village inhabitants shall receive the interest that this savings bank pays.

**Tellow, April 15, 1848.**
