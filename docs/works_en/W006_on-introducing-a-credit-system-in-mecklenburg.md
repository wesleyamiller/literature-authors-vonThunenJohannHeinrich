# On the Introduction of a Credit System in Mecklenburg

*Original: Ueber die Einführung eines Kreditsystems in Mecklenburg (1817)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

## On the Introduction of a Credit System in Mecklenburg and on the Determination of the Collateral Value of Mecklenburg Landed Estates

### Foreword

The need for a credit system suited to Mecklenburg has been felt long enough. The efforts of so many men knowledgeable in this subject, to reconcile the manifold and partly contradictory proposals that have been made public from various sides, are proof that people are convinced of the necessity of an institution through which our credit can be secured. The greatest difficulty at present seems to lie in the fact that there is not yet full agreement on a reliable determination of the collateral value of our landed estates. If these can give the creditor security and win the confidence of the public, then the foundation is laid upon which this edifice can be erected with safety.

The author of the following treatise had already for several years, for other purposes, made it his ongoing business to determine the yield of an estate by means of general formulas expressed in numerical ratios. He found a striking agreement with actual experience. This prompted him to apply his results to the problem at hand, whose solution now occupies so many thinking minds.

The course of his ideas is directed toward the two main subjects:

A credit system is necessary for Mecklenburg; but in order not to build this institution on a wavering foundation that sooner or later might threaten a dangerous collapse, the collateral value of our landed estates must be determined more convincingly than has been possible by the methods used hitherto.

The educated reader will not set aside this treatise — composed with rare acuity and precise local knowledge — unsatisfied. All the more is it to be wished that the examination by knowledgeable friends of the fatherland may arouse the general attention it deserves, as a timely contribution that has every right to claim such notice.

The Editor.

## First Section. On the Introduction of the Credit System in Mecklenburg

### Section 1. The recent events of the times made a moratorium necessary for Mecklenburg.

When war or other misfortunes cause impoverishment of the country and a general scarcity of money, then, in my opinion, for conditions such as those prevailing in Mecklenburg, the necessity of a moratorium is grounded in the following causes:

1\. In the earlier interference of the government in the interest rate, and the legal stipulation that capitalists may not charge more than 5 percent interest.

This is not the place to examine whether this law is just and sound in terms of political economy: suffice it to say that its nearly universal adoption in all countries shows that it was considered very necessary; and even if the capitalists did not give their consent to this, their voice was not powerful enough to resist it, and the limitation of the interest rate received the force of general law.

The injustice that may lie in this restriction against the capitalist is almost extinguished by long habit, and by the view inherited from generation to generation that money cannot be put to use through lending at more than 5 percent. For only at the first introduction of this law could it bring misfortune upon individual families, from whom a part of their previous income was torn away. All subsequent generations — since this law remained in force — were not deceived about the state of their wealth or about the degree of expenditure that their income allowed them.

This law, now that it has gained firmness and permanence, has exercised a great influence on the employment of capital, on the purchase price of estates, and on nearly all the relations of civil life.

Estates thereby first acquired a fixed nominal value, and became capable of being encumbered with debt. So long as the interest rate could still, by some accident, rise to double or triple, every capital lent on an estate was insecure, because the nominal value of the estate could be nearly destroyed by the unlimited rise of the interest rate.

For the same reason, every division of inheritance among the children of an estate owner had to become highly unequal. The eldest son, who received the estate, dared take on only insignificant debts against a thing of such fluctuating value; he therefore had to be richly provided for, while his siblings were left poorly endowed.

After the fixing of the interest rate, this relationship changed. The owner of a debt-free estate now calculated the capital value of his estate on the basis that the interest rate could never rise above 5 percent. And he could now impose obligations on his son who inherited the estate, making possible an equal and just division of wealth among all his children.

Now suppose the estate, hitherto owned free of debt by the father, is received by the son through the division with his siblings encumbered to two-thirds of its value; the siblings, in order to obtain cash, sell their claims on the estate to other capitalists who have no interest whatsoever in the welfare of the estate owner. Now suppose that through war or other misfortunes the value of money rises. The capitalist can now employ his money at 7 percent through establishing factories, through trade, or even through purchasing estates. Just as one cannot expect the farmer to sell his grain at average prices when grain prices are high, so too one may not demand of the capitalist that he forget his own advantage out of consideration for others and make no use of the favorable circumstances that have arisen for him. He will therefore — since the law does not permit him to demand 7 percent interest — demand the return of his capital from the estate owner. Since the reason why he demands his money back is so well-founded, and since nearly all capitalists are in the same position of being able to put their money to higher use, the calling-in of capital invested in estates will extend almost simultaneously across the entire country.

The estate owner described above may have the best will to satisfy his creditor, but his efforts to obtain a loan anywhere must be in vain, since he cannot pay the true price of money.

Convinced of the impossibility of obtaining money at 5 percent interest, he turns to the government and demands that it enforce the law — which forbids creditors from charging more than 5 percent interest. He can demand this with justice, for he can say: "That law alone is the cause of my predicament; had it not existed, my father, taking into account the possible rise of the interest rate, would not have valued the estate so highly; he would have handed it on to me less heavily encumbered, and I could then have paid 7 percent interest, for which I could easily obtain money."

But how can the government now enforce this law, when it is absolutely not within its power to increase the money supply of the country so that money cannot be employed at more than 5 percent?

There remains for it — if it does not wish to see its own laws mocked and the happiness of many families, whose entire existence was built on trust in this law, destroyed — no other than the harsh and most precarious remedy: to release debtors from the obligation to repay capital.

Thus the moratorium was already contained as a seed in that first law, and a law perhaps universally recognized as beneficial reveals itself only in its necessary consequences as pernicious.

2\. In the unnatural arrangement: "of demanding capital repayments from an estate which does not yield capital but only annual rents, and which cannot be sold piecemeal."

When a merchant's capital is called in, he need only sell a portion of his stock of goods to satisfy his creditor. He can, if he wishes, convert his goods into cash at any moment. But the wealthy landowner, whose estate is encumbered to only one-tenth, cannot even pay this tenth from the estate's income when it is called in. He will have to try to borrow money from another capitalist to pay the first. But if, despite the great security he can offer and the offer of a commission, he cannot obtain so large a capital — as was frequently the case in times of need — then he is forced to apply for a special moratorium, which, if he can demonstrate the impossibility of obtaining a loan, can hardly be refused, since it would be too detrimental to the welfare of the entire state if bankruptcy proceedings were to be opened where assets so greatly exceed liabilities.

He who promises more than he can certainly deliver, his assurance deserves no belief. An estate yields annual rents; but it is not possible to extract whole capitals from it in a single year. When an estate owner promises to pay annual rents, he can and must keep this promise; but when he commits himself to repay capital, the fulfillment of his promise depends on the chance of whether he can obtain a loan from another capitalist.

3\. In the great costs connected with the sale of an estate in bankruptcy.

If one could collect the sum of all experience of the ten years just past, the result would probably be this: that from the opening of bankruptcy proceedings to the sale of the estate, an average of two years elapsed, and that during these two years the estate yielded no income for the creditors — for various reasons — but at best bore only the administrative and other costs connected with the bankruptcy.

Upon the sale of every feudal estate, relief dues, consent fees, enfeoffment fees, and stamp duties must be paid. These amount together to 3.5 percent of the purchase price, which for an estate sold in bankruptcy is increased by another half percent, thus amounting to 4 percent altogether. If one adds to this the contract fees and other unavoidable expenses, these costs will amount to at least 5 percent of the purchase price.

It is not unusual in Mecklenburg for prospective buyers to negotiate with one another before the auction, and by one paying substantial sums to keep the others from bidding, the estate is sold at auction far below its true value. The buyer may then pay perhaps as much as the estate is worth; but a considerable portion of the purchase sum does not reach the creditors or sellers of the estate.

Even if some estates have been sold where no such collusion took place, in other cases the sum paid to the prospective buyers was all the more considerable, and amounted to more than a tenth of the purchase price. But precisely this inequality undermines the credit of estates, whose purchase price no longer stands in any proportion to their value but is left to chance.

If one wishes to restore the credit of estates, which can only happen by giving the capitalists complete security for their lent money, then this collusion must be prevented by serious and strictly enforced laws. The least that should be required is that the buyer of the estate must pay the entire sum he gave to the other prospective buyers once more to the creditors, as sellers of the estate.

From the three causes cited, the transfer of an estate to another owner is thus connected with about 20 percent in costs, and when an estate of 5,000 Thaler income is sold for 100,000 Thaler, the creditors receive only about 80,000 Thaler of their claims.

In times of scarce money, however, the capital loss of the creditors is far greater still, because the law that limits the interest rate loses all force upon the sale of the estate, since the buyer calculates and pays the value of the estate not according to the artificial, enforced interest rate, but according to the true price of money.

Suppose an estate encumbered to 100,000 Thaler with an income of 5,000 Thaler is sold in bankruptcy at a time when money can be employed at 7 percent; the capital loss of the creditors will be as follows:

An income of 5,000 Thaler, calculated at 7 percent interest, has a capital value of 71,445 Thaler, for which I will assume only the round sum of 71,000 Thaler, and the buyer will pay this price for the estate including all costs. We have estimated the costs above at 5 percent in relief dues, etc., and 5 percent in buy-off payments; this makes a deduction of 7,000 Thaler from 71,000 Thaler, and the creditors receive from the purchase money only 64,000 Thaler. But the creditors have gone without the estate's revenues for two years and received no interest, whereby their claims have grown to 110,000 Thaler. They thus lose 46,000 Thaler on the sale of an estate which, at the legal interest rate of 5 percent, had the full value of all debts secured against it.

In this manner, enormous sums have been lost in Mecklenburg, and a great number of prosperous families have been plunged into the bitterest poverty — and this would undoubtedly have occurred on an even greater scale had there been no moratorium.

It is clear that in such a case the interests of the older creditors are in direct conflict with those of the younger ones. The former wish to be relieved of the burdensome constraint and to employ their money at the actual market price; but the latter would then lose their entire capital, from which they had hitherto drawn their income and sustenance.

It is therefore the highest interest of the latter that the estate, so long as interest is still being paid, should not fall into bankruptcy — which can only be achieved through the introduction of the moratorium. They will therefore, if they know their true interest, apply for it, and the government will not refuse them this, despite the opposing interest of the older creditors; for the younger creditors were induced to lend so heavily on this estate only by the law limiting the interest rate, and this law can only be maintained in force through the moratorium.

### Section 2. Harmful Consequences of the Moratorium.

We have seen in the preceding section that the moratorium is necessary to prevent greater misfortune; but it does not follow that it works beneficially; it remains rather an evil, and indeed a very great evil.

As soon as it is introduced, cash disappears; trust and confidence vanish; the most honorable man can no longer keep his word because his own word is not kept; the wealthy, talented young man is lost to the state because he cannot obtain the money necessary for his business in exchange for all his securities, and wastes away in the unnatural idleness forced upon him.

The disadvantages of the moratorium are felt too vividly at this moment and are too fresh in memory for it to be necessary to say more about it here.

It is by no means a misfortune that the capitalist, in times of need, cannot receive the full amount in cash for his securities; for if he receives only 80 Thaler in cash for 100 Thaler in securities, he can buy just as much with these 80 Thaler as, in the flourishing times when he lent out his money, he could buy with 100 Thaler, and it is not desirable for the state that one class be enriched by the distress of the country and rejoice in it. But it is a very great misfortune that these securities cannot be converted into cash at any price, and that the holder of them is, as it were, robbed of his property and condemned to idleness and uselessness.

The cause of the unsaleability of such an obligation lies, however, in the fact that as a rule there is complete uncertainty about its security. Since it is not judicially registered, one knows neither how many other claims take precedence over it, nor the value of the estate pledged as security from any credible appraisal.

### Section 3. Means by which all these evils must be prevented in the future.

The means of entirely avoiding the moratorium in the future, or at least of making it rarer and less harmful, arise for the most part from the removal of the causes that hitherto made the moratorium necessary. They would, in my opinion, be reducible to these four subjects:

1\. The introduction of irredeemable annuity payments instead of the previous capital claims — at least for the capital standing at first mortgage.

2\. The judicial registration of annuities.

3\. A credible judicial appraisal of the estate serving as security for the annuity.

4\. Reduction of costs upon the sale of an estate.

### Section 4. Introduction of Irredeemable Annuities.

Let us now go through individually the advantages that the introduction of annuity payment instead of capital payment offers:

1\. It thereby entirely eliminates the entanglement that arises from the difference between the actual and the legal interest rate, and which always results in the moratorium.

2\. Since the estate owner promises only what he can deliver, the harmful deception to which the capitalist was subject through the useless promise of repayment ceases, and no government will protect the estate owner who does not pay the annuity punctually.

3\. It thereby greatly reduces the fluctuation of estate prices, which is so exceedingly harmful to the welfare of the whole.

In the year 1805, estates were bought at 3 to 4 percent yield on capital, or the purchase price was approximately thirty times the estate's income. An estate with 5,000 Thaler in income was thus purchased for 150,000 Thaler.

After the invasion of the French, the income of this estate fell — through lower grain prices and higher taxes — to 4,000 Thaler, and the actual interest rate rose to 6 to 7 percent. One Thaler of income had a capital value of only 15 Thaler, and the purchase price of this estate could now be only 4,000 times 15 equals 60,000 Thaler.

In many cases the fluctuation was even more considerable, as many estates were sold for less than a third of their former purchase price.

Now even if the buyer of that estate had possessed personal wealth of 75,000 Thaler, his debts now exceeded the market value of the estate. Bankruptcy could not be avoided as soon as he lost the benefit of the moratorium through failure to pay interest punctually. He then lost his entire considerable fortune, and the capitalists suffered in addition a considerable part of their claims.

This case could and did often occur without any fault of the estate owner, solely through the unfortunate fluctuation of the nominal price of estates.

Let us now see how the nominal price of this estate would have changed if in 1805 an irredeemable debt of 50,000 Thaler at 5 percent — that is, an annuity of 2,500 Thaler — had been charged against the estate.

a. Purchase price in 1805:
The buyer assumed the irredeemable debt of 50,000 Thaler. The interest on this debt amounted to 2,500 Thaler; from the 5,000 Thaler income, 2,500 Thaler therefore remained, and these had, at the then-prevailing interest rate, a capital value of 2,500 times 30 equals 75,000 Thaler. He would thus have paid for the estate 125,000 Thaler. Now if, as we have assumed, he possessed 75,000 Thaler of his own wealth, he retained no other debts than the irredeemable annuity.

b. Purchase price after the invasion:
The irredeemable debt amounts to 50,000 Thaler. The income of the estate has fallen from 5,000 to 4,000 Thaler; the payment of the annuity takes 2,500 Thaler from this; of the estate income there remain 1,500 Thaler, and these have, at the interest rate of 6.5 percent, a capital value of 1,500 times 15 equals 22,500 Thaler. The estate would be valued at 72,500 Thaler. The estate would thus have lost in value compared to the 1805 price: 52,500 Thaler. If no irredeemable debt had been charged against the estate, the loss in value would be 90,000 Thaler.

Through the irredeemable debt, therefore, a capital of 37,500 Thaler would have been saved for the state; the estate owner, instead of losing his entire fortune, would have retained an income of 1,500 Thaler; and no capitalist would have lost the slightest amount on this estate.

It is clear enough from this that if such an arrangement had been made before the invasion, a countless amount of capital would have been saved and most of the families now impoverished would have kept their prosperity. And to whom, then, would such an arrangement have been disadvantageous?

4\. Through annuity payment, the prosperity of both capitalists and estate owners becomes less changeable, and the rapid, unmerited reversal of fortune, which almost always leads only to extravagance, is thereby checked.

The capitalist who in 1790 had lent a capital of 20,000 Thaler on an estate of 2,000 Thaler income, and received 1,000 Thaler in interest for it, could live respectably and on an equal footing with the estate owner. Ten years later he could obtain only 3.5 percent for his money, if he wished to place it with unquestioned security, and his income was reduced to 700 Thaler. What made his situation still worse was that grain prices — which here generally stand in inverse proportion to the interest rate — and with them most necessities of life, had risen to nearly double the price. With his interest income he could no longer buy half the accustomed necessities of life.

The revenues of the estate owner had simultaneously, partly through high grain prices and partly through the reduced interest payments, more than doubled.

It lies in human nature that an income earned without one's own merit and without effort tempts one to extravagance. What the city dweller laboriously and of necessity saved was doubly squandered in the country.

When conditions then changed, the interest rate rose, grain prices fell, and luxury migrated to the cities.

The enormous sums that Mecklenburg received from abroad during the period of high grain prices have in this way all vanished again and have left no trace of real wealth and inner strength behind.

When the income of an estate is equally divided between the estate owner and the capitalist at an average ratio, these revenues fall almost entirely into the hands of the owner in favorable times, but in unfavorable times pass entirely to the capitalists.

The annuity payment cannot, to be sure, keep the relationship between capitalist and landowner entirely unchanged; but it does prevent one party from being entirely deprived of his income while the other receives an undeserved increase.

5\. The fear of capital call-ins keeps the estate owner in constant anxiety, prevents him from applying the money saved from the estate's yield to the improvement of the estate, and induces him — when actual capital call-ins occur, if no moratorium is in effect — to satisfy his creditors by reducing operating capital and inventory.

The introduction of annuity payment would entirely eliminate this. It may seem as though this advantage benefits only the estate owners; but the state and the capitalist are in truth just as much interested. Nothing diminishes the income of the entire country so much as when many estates are poorly managed for lack of operating capital and thereby cease to yield a net return. The resulting loss ultimately always falls on the capitalists who have lent their money on this estate, and it would be a very beneficial and just provision if estate owners who no longer possess the requisite operating capital were immediately relieved of the management of the estate.

The following reflection, since it sheds some light on the difference between capital and income, may find a place here.

In the year 1805, before the invasion of the French, the sale price of a Hufe was on average about 20,000 Thaler. The whole of Mecklenburg contains, including the domains and city estates, approximately 8,000 Hufen. The capital value of the land of Mecklenburg thus amounted at that time to 160 million Thaler.

In the years 1814 and 1815, when the country was entirely freed from the yoke of the French and, with respect to free navigation, the constitution, and external relations, was entirely restored to its former situation, the Hufe was paid for on average at about 8,000 Thaler.

The capital value of the land of Mecklenburg thus amounted in 1815 to 64 million Thaler.

This country has therefore suffered, through the invasion of the French — apart from the contributions, requisitions, and quartering costs — a loss of 96 million Thaler. — A pity that this sum is not suitable for reclamation in Paris!

Since population and prosperity always stand in proportion to the real capital of the nation, and since it is impossible that an entire people can live on half of what it hitherto needed for its existence, one would think that a country which has lost three-fifths of its capital value must be depopulated and lie partially waste.

But one finds here no deserted villages, no wasteland, no decline in population, and no bands of robbers — which always appear when the land can no longer feed its inhabitants.

The question must force itself upon everyone here: how two such contradictory phenomena can coexist.

This contradiction disappears when one considers that only income, and not the arbitrary designation of how much such an income is worth, determines the prosperity of a people. If formerly an income of 3.5 Thaler had a capital value of 100 Thaler, and one can now obtain only 50 Thaler for 3.5 Thaler of income, then according to ordinary concepts the possessor of such an income has lost half his fortune; but he can still procure just as many goods and enjoyments with it, and is therefore in reality no poorer.

He who possesses a debt-free property may be entirely indifferent as to how highly his income is valued as capital. But he who has incurred debts against his income, and has promised to satisfy the creditor not through annuities but through capital repayment, must necessarily lose more or less of his fortune: instead of the 1,000 Thaler which at the time of the loan were equivalent to an income of 35 Thaler, he must now give up 70 Thaler of income in order to purchase 1,000 Thaler for himself.

The inevitable consequence of this is an enormous scarcity of money.

### Section 5. Judicial Registration of Annuities and Reduction of Costs in the Sale of an Estate.

A state in which the mere change of the interest rate robs either the capitalist or the landowner of the greater part of his income and transfers it to the other, thereby tempting one class to extravagance and plunging the other into despondency and despair, cannot possibly attain lasting prosperity. We have seen that the introduction of annuity payment confines this evil within such limits that it ceases to be dangerous. We now turn to the investigation of how the second chief evil in the Mecklenburg credit system — the unsaleability of obligations during the moratorium — can likewise be remedied through a suitable arrangement of annuity payment.

An annuity whose continued regular payment is not beyond all doubt is equivalent to the uncertain-looking capital subject to an eternal moratorium, and can never be saleable.

In the introduction of annuity payment, it is therefore an indispensable and sacred obligation that the annuity be covered by an ample mortgage, whose income even under the most unfavorable circumstances still significantly exceeds the annuity charged against it. But it is not enough that this security actually exist; it must be so self-evident that the entire public is convinced of the impossibility that such an annuity could ever be lost. Under this condition, but also only under this condition, will the annuity, despite its unfamiliarity, soon acquire a capital value and a market rate.

The opinion of the public — whether well-founded or not — must here be respected and taken into account with the utmost strictness.

It is, however, possible and even probable that annuities registered against different estates will obtain different market rates, which would greatly impede the circulation of the securities; also, an annuity charged against a single estate would not be saleable abroad.

Both of these defects are remedied if all estate owners mutually guarantee the annuities charged against their estates, and thus the entire country becomes a common security for the annuity.

Thus a credit association, similar to the one that has long existed in the Prussian states, is the means of thoroughly healing the evils by which Mecklenburg's prosperity is constantly undermined.

An annuity certificate issued by the credit association — or a so-called mortgage bond — now has, as the nature of the matter shows and the Pomeranian mortgage bonds demonstrate, a market value even in the most distressed times; and since such a mortgage bond is just as useful for buying and trading as cash, money is made entirely dispensable to a certain degree through these mortgage bonds.

Anyone who now wishes to buy an estate in Mecklenburg, or to lease one, or to start a significant business, must prepare himself years in advance by accumulating and hoarding cash. The mortgage bonds would make these money reserves unnecessary and mobile, and I am convinced that a sudden and universal introduction of the credit system — provided it had already won the confidence of the people — would quickly transform the present scarcity of money into an abundance of money, and that one would find the present money stock in Mecklenburg too large for internal commerce.

These are the bright sides of the credit system; we must now also examine its dark side more closely.

This is the emergence of a new, hitherto entirely unknown expense — namely, the ongoing costs that the administration of the credit system requires.

Everything has its price, and the benefit of mortgage bonds can also be purchased too dearly.

If, as in Pomerania, a quarter percent is paid toward administrative costs, and from this a portion is still saved and applied to enlarging the fund of the association, then this expense is bearable and does not diminish the advantages that the institution provides.

But if the administrative costs rise to half a percent of the borrowed capital, then, for all my partiality for the credit system, I consider the advantages thereby gained to be purchased too dearly. Nor can it be a matter of indifference to the state, and it must have a detrimental effect on the prosperity of the entire people, if at least one-twentieth of all income from land, which hitherto served to stimulate industry, is used in an entirely unproductive manner.

While I readily concede that from my standpoint I cannot fully survey the difficulties and complications connected with the administration of so multifaceted a business, I also venture no decisive judgment on this matter; but I may be permitted to submit my views on the simplification of the administration to experienced men of affairs for examination.

The experience of all ages has proven that any property belonging to a commune is not so well managed and does not yield so much return as when this property is in the hands of private individuals.

It would therefore be desirable that the credit association require no significant fund belonging to the entire association in common — which can only be formed by diminishing private wealth.

But what, then, does the directorate of the credit association, or the Landschaft, which enters here as intermediary between creditor and debtor, need a significant fund of its own for? Perhaps to pay overdue interest? For this, only a small amount of ready cash would be needed, for the interest must be collected with inexorable strictness, since any leniency toward the individual spells ruin for the whole.

Or should this fund serve to cover the loss that might arise if, through incorrect appraisal of an estate, the estate did not have the value of the mortgage bonds issued against it? This case should properly never arise at all if the association wishes to maintain its credit, and if it unfortunately did occur once, would it not be even better to cover this loss through a common contribution than to accumulate a fund for it in advance?

It follows from the foregoing that I presuppose the credit association accepts no call-ins and does not concern itself with capital repayments. Where this is the case, a very large fund is indeed needed. But I ask: what can a credit association that promises capital repayment achieve? All the advantages connected with annuity payment are lost to it. When the interest rate rises, its capital is called in. However large its fund may be, it must be exhausted, and when this has happened, there remains for it no other remedy than the moratorium. The public, deceived in its expectations, to whom the given promise cannot be kept, must now become distrustful of these securities, and since the mortgage bonds — precisely because of the promise of capital repayment — could have no other market rate in prosperous times than their face value, this rate must first form itself in the unfortunate times and amid the complete unfamiliarity of the people with buying annuities; so these mortgage bonds will be convertible into cash only with difficulty and at low prices.

Also, the personnel of a directorate that deals with such enormous financial transactions must necessarily be so numerous, and consist of highly regarded, well-paid men, that thereby the administrative costs are driven to excess.

Is it then really unconditionally necessary that, as in Prussia, the interest be sent from the debtor to the Landschaft, and from the Landschaft to the mortgage bond holders? Why cannot the interest, as hitherto, be paid directly by the debtor to the creditor? In the essential nature of the credit association, as it seems to me, this necessity does not exist at all.

The most well-founded concern with direct interest payment is this: that estate owners will be negligent in sending the interest, and thereby themselves destroy the reliability that alone can give the association credit and value.

This concern can only be removed if the estate owners in this association are so convinced of the necessity of prompt interest payment that they voluntarily impose upon themselves the strictest obligations and even penalties sufficient to make every neglect highly disadvantageous.

A law stipulating that anyone who has not paid the interest on time must pay it double, and that one part of this penalty goes to the Landschaft and the other part to the creditor not satisfied on time, would thoroughly cure the evil insofar as it arises from negligence.

Even an excessive indebtedness of the estate owner cannot and must not hinder the prompt payment of the Landschaft interest, if the collateral value of the estate is correctly determined; for since the interest on the mortgage bonds does not take away the entire revenue of the estate but only a part of it, the estate's yield will suffice to cover this interest even in a bad harvest year, even if the interest on the other debts that the estate owner may have cannot be paid in such a year. But so that the estate owner is not tempted to reverse this order of priority, the non-payment of the Landschaft interest must be of greater disadvantage to him.

Agriculture is, however, subject to calamities that destroy not merely the net yield but even the gross product of the soil — for example, fire, hailstorm, etc. In such a case the estate owner is not in a position to pay the interest until the compensation payments have been received. The directorate would then have to advance the interest, and the estate owner would later reimburse the default interest and the costs of procuring the money.

I must note here that the sum of all calamities that can befall an estate nearly equals the capital value of the estate, and that therefore an estate that bears the risk of all these calamities itself has almost no collateral value. It is therefore a necessary condition that an estate can be admitted to the credit association only if it has secured its lasting capital value by joining the building and furniture fire insurance fund and the hail damage compensation association.

For still greater security of the creditor, the directorate would have to be obligated, upon the mere notification that he has not received his interest, to pay it to him; for which purpose a small amount of ready cash would always have to be available.

When debtor and creditor live close together but far from the seat of the directorate or the Landschaft, direct interest payment is much more convenient for both.

The distant or foreign creditor must, when interest payment is handled through the Landschaft, have an agent in the district town who collects the interest and forwards it to him. This seems to me a great disadvantage for the creditors and an obstacle to the circulation of mortgage bonds abroad. With direct interest payment, the debtor would be obligated to send the interest to his creditor by post in the nearest town or district town, whereby the creditor would be relieved of every trouble except paying the postage.

The mortgage bonds must then, however, not be made out to the bearer but must be issued in the name of the borrower, and can only pass to another owner through cession. In my view, this cannot hinder the saleability of the mortgage bonds, but it does protect the creditor so that the accidental loss of his paper does not also mean the loss of his capital.

Through the longer connection between creditor and debtor, a certain attachment has often arisen — at least acquaintance — and both would be reluctant to conduct their business with other persons entirely unknown to them. The debtor can also oblige his creditor by paying the interest at another place or to a third person — something with which the Landschaft naturally cannot concern itself at all.

If direct interest payment is retained, the directorate actually has no regular business at all. It would only need to be always ready to intervene actively and restore order when a disturbance arises in the workings of the mechanism.

That with this arrangement the administrative costs must shrink to negligibility is evident.

The initial admission to the credit association necessarily involves costs, since the valuation of the estate, the establishment of a mortgage register, and the issuance of the mortgage bonds cannot occur without expense; but every estate owner will gladly bear this outlay, since it occurs only once and he is richly compensated for it in the increased value of his estate.

### Section 6. Possibility of Putting a Credit System into Practice for Mecklenburg.

The question now arises how, without a fund and given the complete unfamiliarity of Mecklenburg's capitalists with possessing or buying irredeemable claims, the introduction of such a credit system might be possible.

The spirit of the public, and the aversion to loans that are not repaid brought about by the moratorium, do indeed allow great difficulties to be expected, and make a rapid spread of the credit association impossible.

If people in Mecklenburg were accustomed to stating and calculating a man's wealth in terms of income rather than capital, as is customary in so many other countries, everything would be much easier.

But for now, among very many uninformed people, the horror at a kind of loan where one would never see one's money again will be loudly expressed. This aversion must, however, gradually diminish when they see that these securities have a definite market value and that one can obtain cash for them at any time — and it will be especially favorable for the general mood when one sees that 100 Thaler of these securities are worth more than 100 Thaler in cash.

Everything is therefore gained once the mortgage bonds simply have a market rate.

Now, how a beginning can be made and mortgage bonds created, even if for the time being no capitalist wishes to lend his money for such a loan, I conceive of in the following way:

Two estate owners, one of whom owns estate A, the other estate B, entirely free of debt, are the first to join the association. Both now encumber their estates in such a way that the owner of estate A declares he has received a loan of 25,000 Thaler from the owner of estate B, while the latter declares in turn that he owes the former 25,000 Thaler through a loan received. Instead of the customary obligations, the owner of A receives 25,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds secured on estate B; the owner of B receives in return 25,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds registered on estate A. Thus 50,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds are created.

The securities of two acknowledged wealthy men will perhaps find a buyer even within the country itself; but if this is not the case, these mortgage bonds must be sold at a place where the value of irredeemable annuities is properly appreciated — for example, in Berlin.

The cash obtained for these 50,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds is now used to pay off the debts of four estate owners, each of whom owes no more than 12,000 Thaler.

Mortgage bonds are now registered against the estates of these four gentlemen — and not merely up to the amount of the capital paid off, but up to the full collateral value of their estates. By collateral value I understand: that portion of the estate's value which remains even under the most unfavorable circumstances, and which, as being beyond loss, can be encumbered with mortgage bonds.

Suppose the collateral value of each of these four estates is likewise 25,000 Thaler; then another 100,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds can be created, and the sum of all issued mortgage bonds now amounts to 150,000 Thaler.

This sum seems sufficient for the purpose of establishing a market rate for the mortgage bonds. For two reasons, however, this rate must form itself within one or a few years; for, firstly, the sale of the 50,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds has already given them a market rate abroad, and secondly, the off-putting quality that the idea of an irredeemable debt initially has with the public must gradually be lost through closer acquaintance with the whole institution, through the attention devoted to this subject, and through the clearer views that result from it.

Perhaps the mortgage bonds must at first be sold at a price that does not equal their true value; then the moment has come when the Landschaft must not shrink from making some sacrifices — which must be borne collectively by all estates already in the association and those joining it soon.

Once the mortgage bonds have obtained a market rate, every estate owner who owes no more than the collateral value of his estate can enter into free negotiation with his creditors about how many mortgage bonds he will give them for their claims. If he does not succeed in this negotiation, the Landschaft can sell the mortgage bonds to be issued on this estate and thereby satisfy the creditors.

Thus the credit association can spread only slowly; but its expansion will increase each year in geometric progression.

It still requires a special examination whether a rapid and universal introduction of the credit system — even if it were possible — would be desirable.

One may well assume that the greater half of estate owners have more debts than the collateral value of their estates amounts to. Nevertheless, this class of estate owners can consist of very wealthy and perfectly solvent men, and their general ruin would be equally ruinous for the state and for the capitalists.

Suppose one gave the mortgage bonds such a design that, besides the greater security, they also had other advantages over ordinary capital claims that were evident to the entire public; then the desire of the capitalists to possess mortgage bonds would move them to call in their capital invested in estates after the moratorium is lifted, and thus a great number of estate owners would be thrown into the greatest difficulties by the introduction of the credit system.

In this regard it seems most advantageous for the whole that public opinion on the superiority of mortgage bonds over ordinary capital claims should remain divided for a while longer, and that the demand for mortgage bonds should increase only to the extent that more estate owners are in a position to join the association.

The estate owners whose indebtedness exceeding the collateral value of their estates does not yet permit them to join the association can in some cases reach that point within a few years: one by paying off debts with the surplus of his income; another by collecting capital owed to him and using it to pay off his own debts; a third, finally, by persuading one or another of his creditors through a friendly arrangement to have his claims registered after the mortgage bonds.

Thus, under the proposed arrangement, only estates that are not encumbered beyond the collateral value can for the time being participate in the credit association, and this will be of great benefit in another respect for establishing the credit of this association. The public will, to be sure, initially transfer to those who join the credit association the preconceived notion that anyone who establishes a mortgage register and has his estate appraised is in need of money and in difficult circumstances; but when the public sees that only wealthy and very prosperous estate owners can join the association, its opinion will soon take a turn very favorable to the association.

Only later, when credit in general is more restored, and when the capitalists, instructed by longer experience, have become convinced that capital registered after the mortgage bonds still has full security, will it be advantageous and possible for estate owners whose estates are encumbered beyond the collateral value to join the association.

For now, only a core needs to be formed, to which the others can gradually attach themselves; but this must happen soon, before the probably returning better times make the estate owner careless, the capitalist credulous, and weaken the interest in the association.

There is yet another reason that can deter the capitalist from using his money to purchase mortgage bonds, and which must be addressed if one seriously wants to introduce the credit system.

This is the fear of the tax that has in recent times been levied on interest income. Since very few capital sums are now judicially registered, and since the debtor does not dare to disclose his creditors, the capitalist can, if he wishes, more or less entirely evade this tax; the publicity of the mortgage bonds would make this impossible.

A tax that rests far more on the conscientiousness of the taxpayer than on his actual income is in itself pernicious.

The mortgage bonds, through the ease with which taxes can be levied on them and through their irredeemability, offer just as good an object for taxation as land itself. The fear that this might sooner or later be exploited can also deter the capitalist who conscientiously pays the current taxes on interest from purchasing mortgage bonds.

A solemn guarantee "that the interest on mortgage bonds shall never and under no pretext be burdened with a tax for the ordinary needs of the state" seems to me therefore absolutely necessary.

Only in war and under hostile occupation of the country would this guarantee have to admit an exception; for since the enemy can increase his demands to the point of exhausting the resources of all subjects, these demands cannot possibly be met by only a part of the subjects.

One would therefore provisionally exempt the interest on mortgage bonds from the tax, and later extend this exemption also to the interest on other capital, partly so as not to give the mortgage bonds too great an advantage over other capital claims, and chiefly in order to entirely eliminate a tax that acts so detrimentally on morality.

The deficit that would thereby arise in the state's revenue would have to be covered by a successive increase of the Hufe tax, and since the yield of this tax has probably always been very small, an increase of the Hufe tax from 4.5 to 5 Thaler would likely suffice for this purpose.

To anyone who finds the complete exemption of one class of subjects and the consequently heavier burden on another class unjust, one may point out that, with the free circulation of capital — that is, after the moratorium is lifted — the taxes on interest are ultimately always paid by the one who needs the capital, and that the state, in order to establish the lasting prosperity of the country, must not take into account a minor burden on individual subjects.

Under the proposed arrangement, the mortgage bond of 100 Thaler paying 5 Thaler interest will have a market value one-quarter higher than the mortgage bond of 100 Thaler paying only 4 percent interest. It may seem at first glance a matter of indifference whether one introduces mortgage bonds at 4 or 5 percent.

But closer examination reveals that mortgage bonds bearing 5 percent interest enormously facilitate the introduction of the credit system, as the following example may show.

Suppose the mortgage bond of 100 Thaler bearing 4 percent interest has a market value of 90 Thaler in cash: then the mortgage bond of the same face value but bearing 5 percent interest must have a value of 112.5 Thaler in cash.

Now if an estate owner whose estate is encumbered exactly up to its collateral value of 20,000 Thaler wishes to join the credit association, the Landschaft can sell the 20,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds at 4 percent to be issued on his estate only for 18,000 Thaler; and he must therefore still possess 2,000 Thaler in cash to pay off his debts and be admitted to the association. But if the mortgage bonds are issued at 5 percent, the 20,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds can be sold for 22,500 Thaler, and the estate owner retains, after paying off all his debts, a surplus of 2,500 Thaler.

After these discussions I now turn to the investigation of how the collateral value of estates is to be determined.

## Second Section. On Determining the Collateral Value of Estates.

### Section 7. Faulty Appraisal Method Based on Grain Quantities.

To find the value of an estate through an appraisal, the method used until the most recent times was to deduct from the total grain yield of the field the seed two- to threefold, or else half of the harvest — whereby all grain and cash expenditures were supposed to be covered — to calculate the remaining grain at its cash value, to add to this the income from livestock, and to regard the resulting sum as the net yield of the estate.

The absurdity of such an appraisal is too great for it to be worth the trouble to demonstrate its invalidity in detail. It will suffice here to cite the simple fact — which emerges from the grain accounts of every estate — that only the best estates can bring half of their grain production to market, and that the other half of the harvest must be used for seed, for feed for the draft animals, for household needs, for threshing wages and allowances for workers paid in grain, and for several other expenses, and is thus consumed on the estate itself. On estates with poor soil, however, consumption approaches the amount of the entire harvest — and these estates can bring only a quarter or an eighth of their production to market.

In such an appraisal, therefore, at least the entire cash expenditure required for the operation of the farm is passed over in silence and not deducted at all.

To bring the enormous cash yield that can be calculated in this way back down to some moderation, the grain prices were set so low that, had they actually prevailed, estates with middling soil could not have been cultivated at all; and if this was still not enough, the grain yield was assumed far below the actual figure.

One thus sought to make good one lie by another; but since for both there was no measure at all, the yield to be calculated was entirely independent of the value of the estate, and lay entirely in the discretion of the appraisers.

If some estates were nevertheless correctly appraised by this method, it was because the knowledgeable and impartial appraisers determined the value of the estate by a general overview, and then adjusted the very pliable appraisal until it produced the sum they wanted.

In more recent times, appraisers have been directed to itemize in the appraisal the grain and cash expenditures, to deduct these from the gross yield, and thereby to determine the net yield of the estate. Through this, a great step toward improving the appraisal system has been taken.

### Section 8. Does a Precisely Specified Appraisal Lead to a Correct Estimate of Collateral Value?

In such a yield appraisal, the correctness of the result stands in exact proportion to the correctness of all the individual estimates; whereas under the old form, the grain prices and grain yield had to be set incorrectly if the result of the appraisal — the net yield — was to come out right. The appraisers are, however, here too subject to the fetters of public opinion.

The public, accustomed to the old form, simply does not want to see the true average prices in the appraisal, but unnaturally low grain prices. The sellers of the estate — especially the creditors of an estate to be sold in bankruptcy — have, since the estate can be sold at two-thirds of the appraised value, a well-founded interest in wishing that the appraisal be higher than the value of the estate.

To satisfy both demands, the appraiser must set the grain prices low, but on the other hand set all expenditures at unnaturally low figures.

I have seen an appraisal that emerged from the free conviction of educated men, that stated the grain prices according to a thirty-year average and the expenditures according to the truth — and this appraisal was bitterly criticized because the prices were set too high and the calculated yield was too low!

But can an appraisal deserve credence when the truth is not tolerated, and can the business of appraisal produce good appraisers when the search for truth is not the highest purpose?

### Section 9. Closer Examination of the Customary Appraisal Methods.

We must now cast a glance at the form of the now-established yield appraisal and see whether it can meet the demands that may be made of it.

In determining the grain yield, the seed has been taken as the unit, by saying: "the field yields the seed 4, 5, or 6 times over." In matters that are not susceptible of mathematically precise determination but only admit of an estimate, one is all too inclined to take only whole numbers and leave out the fractions. With so large a unit, however, the omission of fractions has a very great effect on the correctness of the result.

The grain price is supposed to be assumed as it has been on average over the past 30 years; but the thirty-year average price for each type of grain has not been officially published. The appraiser lacks the data for determining this question, and so the assumption of grain prices is left entirely to the discretion of the appraisers.

The condition of the buildings, the lack of timber, the distance from the point of sale, and several other circumstances that have just as great an influence on the value of the estate as the land itself receive little attention in this appraisal. The form of the appraisal does permit such consideration; but there is a lack of regulations specifying how these various circumstances alter the capital value of the estate.

The daily wage, the number of draft animals to be kept, the feed they require, and the other costs involved in maintaining the working forces are assumed arbitrarily in this appraisal, without stating the reasons for these assumptions, and without demonstrating that these costs stand in a correct proportion to the quantity of labor that the cultivation of the field requires.

These assumptions are as a rule very flawed, and cannot be otherwise when they are not based on the specific calculation of the labor that the field requires according to its size and its yield.

An appraisal, as can be delivered under these circumstances, will always have to acknowledge the voice of the public as its judge, but thereby entirely misses its actual purpose.

### Section 10. Conditions That Would Have to Be Established in the Valuation of an Estate. — Are They Attainable? — Are They Necessary?

As easy as it is to see the defects of this appraisal, it is difficult to replace it with a better and more appropriate one. Science itself has a great many questions whose answer is necessary for the complete exposition of appraisal principles — questions that have been either not at all or only very imperfectly resolved.

In what proportion must, on a given soil and with the customary Mecklenburg field-rotation system, the acreage bearing grain stand to the acreage lying fallow, if the estate is to remain in precisely the same state of fertility as it now is? And how does this ratio change when the supply of hay or the physical quality of the soil changes?

An estate which, with middling soil and small hay production, takes three sowings from six shifts will deteriorate from year to year, and resembles a capital lent on life annuity which, through the large revenues it yields, eventually ceases to be capital. An estate with large hay production and three sowings from seven shifts increases in value annually, and can be regarded as a capital from which only a part of the interest is drawn while the other part is added to the capital. If one were to take the revenues as the measure of capital value here, one would regard a variable quantity as constant and obtain a most erroneous result.

What is the actual use-value of a load of hay for a given locality? And how does this value change when the ratio of hay production to arable area changes, or when the soil and its productive capacity are different?

How much less is an acre 400 rods from the farmstead worth than an acre near the farmstead, when both are of equal productive capacity?

These and a host of other questions must be resolved if a perfect appraisal is to be possible. But if the sum of all knowledge is not sufficient to answer these questions in a way that is useful for practical life, then every effort to devise an appraisal method and instructions for appraisers that would remove all difficulties will also be in vain.

### Section 11. Further Elaboration of These Questions.

In a taxation of Mecklenburg estates to be undertaken now, the appraisers would be put in no small difficulty by the introduction of marling and by the spread of fine-wool sheep farming.

When they find, on marled soil that formerly bore no barley, lodging wheat at the boundary of the estate, should they now judge this soil by its present production and place it in the same class as originally wheat-bearing soil, or should they take no account at all of the fertility produced by the marl? Both would be wrong, but how is a determination to be made here? In any case, estates already marled would be appraised higher than those not yet marled. But since the latter, if they have marl, will follow suit in a few years, the advantage of the former will soon be eliminated again. An appraisal, however, that regarded the present difference between both types of estates as permanent would after a short time be quite incorrect. Estates that have no marl at all would necessarily have to be appraised much lower. But the search for marl is far too difficult and laborious a business for the complete absence of marl to be taken as established by the appraiser.

The wealth given to the soil by marl is, however, by no means to be regarded as inseparable from the soil and as always continuing. It is highly probable, to be sure, that through a conservative management one can increase the humus content of the field so that, when the power of the marl is exhausted, the fertility of the field nevertheless does not decline; but it is even more certain that through heavier grain cultivation one can take from the field everything it had gained in fertility through the marl. Whether Mecklenburg's farmers will in general be more inclined to invest the capital they spend on improvements in the field and be content with moderate returns, or whether they will wish to lend this capital to the field only on life annuity — on this will depend whether the capital value of estates in Mecklenburg will receive a lasting increase through marling or not.

Only time to come can instruct us on this, and it would at present be very hazardous to determine the collateral value of marled estates according to their currently elevated yield — whose continuance depends entirely on the will of the owner.

In 20 or 30 years, the estates now marled will, with respect to their yield, again be in a persistent state less subject to rapid change and the owner's caprice; and only then will it be fully possible to survey how marling has affected the capital value of the various estates. It will then also become apparent of its own accord which estates have no marl.

The fine-wool sheep farms have in recent years given an outstanding return, and the estates on which these sheep farms are found have thereby delivered a much greater net yield than the other estates. Should the capital value of these estates also be assumed to be higher in proportion to their higher net yield?

Sheep farming is not tied to a location; the owner can move his flock from one estate to another. The flock itself can be destroyed by disease, and the loss is not easily made good because fine-wool sheep are still so rare. So long as not every farmer can acquire a fine-wool flock, the higher yield of such a flock is to be regarded not as ground rent but as the interest on the capital invested in the flock.

The difficulty of acquiring a fine-wool flock diminishes with every year, and probably within not very many years one will find fine-wool sheep, or at least sheep of middling quality, on nearly all estates in Mecklenburg.

It is possible that the price of fine wool will then no longer be as high as it is now. The returns from fine-wool sheep farming will then also be lower, but certainly remain considerably higher than the former returns from coarse-wool sheep farming. This surplus is then to be regarded as ground rent and raises the permanent value of estates; but the magnitude of this future increase in value cannot yet be determined or expressed in numbers.

There is, however, yet another no less important reason that must hold us back from a new appraisal — namely, the distrust of the public.

Even supposing that, as if by a miracle, we obtained appraisals that fulfilled all demands, even the strictest — how is the public to be convinced that these appraisals are truly so correct?

The public, far from being a competent judge, will at first regard the correct and incorrect appraisals with equal distrust; opinion can form itself only gradually, and will pass over to belief only when one sees that the price of estates sold or leased always stands in proportion to the appraisals furnished for these estates. The formation of this judgment presupposes the existence of the credit association, which however cannot be introduced unless it has already won the voice of the public.

The capitalist will be easier to satisfy, for he needs only to judge whether the entire mass of pledged estates provides a secure mortgage, and the individual inaccuracies do not concern him; far harder to satisfy will be the individual estate owner who must answer for every mistake committed with his entire fortune. When in private life one already rightly hesitates to pledge even a part of one's wealth as surety for another, it would be unforgivable recklessness to assume a guarantee that puts one's entire fortune and entire civil existence at risk, unless one has the undoubted certainty that there is no danger in it.

But how is the estate owner to convince himself of the correctness of the appraisals in general?

Should the estate owner living near Demmin travel to Boizenburg and review the appraisals of the estates appraised there? This is not possible. And so the judgment of the individual estate owners will form itself almost as slowly as the judgment of the larger public, and only after a long examination will the more cautious ones join the association.

### Section 12. Conclusion.

From the reasons presented so far, it follows in my opinion that the determination of the collateral value of estates through a new appraisal cannot provide a basis for the credit association; and that, if the collateral value cannot be established in one way or another, one must rather abandon the entire idea without making a probably unsuccessful attempt that would shake the country's credit still further.

### Section 13. The Benefit of a Credit Association Has Already Been Confirmed by the Pomeranian System, Which However Can Serve as a Model for Mecklenburg Only with Limitations.

The credit association in Pomerania has brilliantly passed the great test to which it was subjected by the invasion of the French, and the public now rewards this so severely tested and so firmly proven association with boundless confidence. It is a perhaps unique phenomenon that a mortgage bond of 100 Thaler bearing only 4 percent interest is paid for with 104 Thaler in cash, while the private individual can obtain money at nearly 5 percent interest with full security.

This fact demonstrates more than any words can — the need and the high usefulness of such an association.

The accomplished success of the Pomeranian association can easily lead to the opinion that one need only imitate its arrangements in every detail to achieve the same success in Mecklenburg. But let no one deceive himself in this: the same arrangements can produce the same effects only when the circumstances are also the same; and these are, in Pomerania and Mecklenburg, very substantially different from one another in the following two points:

First, in Pomerania the King contributed a very significant fund to the initial establishment of the association, whereby the first mortgage bonds could be created without the participation of the capitalists. The Landschaft, or directorate, thus entered as a wealthy intermediary between creditor and debtor. The guarantee that the estate owner had to provide was without danger for him, because the Landschaft was wealthy enough to bear any loss that arose through incorrect appraisal or through other accidents.

Second, the appraisal of estates in Pomerania had never been conducted so arbitrarily, nor had the appraisal been so misused. The credit association therefore found confidence with the public, instead of having everywhere to struggle against distrust — as it will in Mecklenburg. The estate appraisals at the first introduction of the credit system in Pomerania were undeniably far from perfect; for example, an estate in Pomerania that had been bought for 118,000 Thaler was immediately afterwards appraised by the Landschaft at 180,000 Thaler, and 120,000 Thaler in mortgage bonds were issued on it; but the great independent wealth of the Landschaft and the confidence of the public rendered individual errors harmless.

In Mecklenburg, however, a single case of this kind would shake the entire edifice; therefore the appraisals must be more accurate, and arbitrariness must be entirely eliminated, if the credit association is to endure here.

### Section 14. How Could Adherence to the Appraisal Regulations Prescribed in the Mecklenburg Territorial Compact Provide the Closest Possible Approximation to the Correct Result? — A. For Determining the Value of Arable Land.

The appraisal of all agricultural estates in Mecklenburg, undertaken approximately 50 years ago, is an invaluable gift for a credit association now to be established, if the present value of the estates can be derived from this appraisal with some accuracy. If the collateral value of the estates is determined from the data provided by this older appraisal, then all arbitrariness is excluded, and the distrust of the public — which would accompany the new appraisals for years, out of fear that personal interest and partiality had played a part — cannot arise here at all.

Moreover, this appraisal has certain particular advantages over all later forms of appraisal, for:

1\. All parts of an estate were appraised individually; every small meadow, every piece of arable land that did not correspond in its character to the surrounding fields, was appraised separately. How much more accurately the value of the whole emerges from the appraisal of the individual parts than from an appraisal in which the most diverse items are lumped together is evident.

2\. By reckoning 75 square rods of the best soil or 300 square rods of the worst soil as equivalent to one bushel, the appraiser obtained a scale of 225 gradations. He could now express the finest distinctions of the soil in whole numbers, and since he was not hampered and confused by having to draft a formal appraisal, he could devote his entire attention to this simple rating.

3\. The appraisal itself was carried out, under the supervision of two directors, by six appraisers, of whom two always worked together and formed three divisions or "shifts." According to the regulations, each shift had to appraise the field independently, and without prior consultation with the others, record their findings in the protocol. Through this wise arrangement, the appraisers checked one another; the ignorance or negligence of one appraiser was bound to reveal itself through his divergent assessments. Since, for this reason alone, agreement with the others was already agreeable and desirable for each individual appraiser, and since this agreement was possible only when all of them approached the truth as closely as possible, the sense of honor had to become for every appraiser a driving force to pursue his business with the greatest accuracy and care.

The appraisal was carried out in order to determine the proportion in which the estates should contribute to the state taxes.

The taxes at that time were so low — approximately 1.5 Thaler per Hufe — that the desire to evade them could hardly have been strong enough to exercise a determining influence on the appraisers. Moreover, this desire was directly counteracted by the interest in giving one's estate greater prestige and a larger credit value through a substantial Hufe assessment.

One can therefore well assume that the appraisers, free from external influences, generally expressed their true convictions, and since the prescribed form in no way restricted the appraisers in the free exercise of their knowledge, one might have expected an outstanding result from the convergence of such favorable circumstances.

This expectation has, however, been disappointed insofar as the Hufe assessment is supposed to serve as a measure of the value of an estate. Experience teaches that at all times the Hufe of good soil has been paid for at a much higher price than the Hufe of sandy soil.

When estate prices were at their lowest in the years 1811 to 1813, estate G was paid at 12,000 Thaler per Hufe, while estate R was paid at only 3,500 Thaler per Hufe. Public opinion thus recognized in the value of two Hufen a ratio of 12 to 3.5, or of 3.5 to 1.

than a hufe of sandy soil.

When in the years 1811-13 the estate prices were at their lowest, estate G was paid for at 12,000 Thalers per hufe, while estate R was paid only 3,500 Thalers per hufe. Public opinion thus recognized in the value of two hufes the ratio of 12 to 3.5, or of 3.5 to 1.

Estate G, however, does not consist entirely of soil of the first quality, and estate R does not consist of mere sand but still has a considerable portion of good soil. There must therefore be an even much greater difference in the value of the hufes than the purchase price of these estates indicates. If there were an estate that, unmixed with other land, contained only such soil as is assessed at 300 square rods per scheffel, it would turn out that such an estate has no purchase price at all, and a hufe of this soil is entirely without value.

The assessment has therefore missed its actual purpose of determining the value of the estates to the highest degree -- and one is thereby all too easily led to regard it as completely useless.

However, before passing this judgment, one must carefully distinguish whether the fault lies with the principles of the assessment regulations, or with the irregular and arbitrary assumptions of the assessors.

When one inspects an estate with the assessment register in hand, one will, according to my observation, always find that the quality of the arable land stands in a certain relationship to the number of square rods that have been reckoned for this land per scheffel; indeed, after making this comparison for a few parcels of land, one will be able to form, from the assessment register alone, an almost accurate picture of the arable land of the entire estate. Thus the fault does not lie with the assessors.

If the soil of all estates had been chemically analyzed, and one had then classified the land according to its varying clay content, the net yield of the soil would likewise not stand in direct proportion to the clay content. The conclusion that land with 40 percent clay has four times the value of land with 10 percent clay could be just as false as when one assigns the land assessed at 75 square rods four times the value of soil assessed at 300 square rods; but one would not reject this classification as useless on that account, but would instead attempt to determine in what ratio the net yield increases or decreases with the clay content.

The older assessment provides a scale in which the transition from the best to the worst soil is designated by 225 grades. In this assessment, however, not only the clay content has been taken into account, but the humus content of the soil, the distance of the arable land from the farmstead, and a multitude of other essential circumstances are also considered in this grade determination.

It therefore seems well worth a serious investigation whether it is not possible to ascertain what property of the arable land is actually designated by these grades, and what net yield of the soil is associated with each such grade.

Since the assessment regulations provide no information on this matter, this task can only be solved through manifold comparison and observation of how the yield of the arable land relates to the grade by which it is designated in the assessment protocol.

For my own instruction, I attempted the draft of a table in which I set down the net yield of arable land assessed at 75, 80, 90, 100 square rods and so forth, based on the experiences and observations I had made regarding the value of these various types of soil. The table revealed an almost entirely regular order between the increasing number of square rods per scheffel and the decreasing net yield of the soil. This drew my attention, and when I then compared the calculations I possessed concerning the ratio of gross to net yield, I found between the two a surprising agreement. I found, namely, that when I took the number of square rods per scheffel as the measure of the gross yield, I obtained for this gross yield almost exactly the net yield that I had set down in the table for soil of this grade, based on general observation. According to this, then, the gross yield of the soil could be ascertained from the assessment register: if, for example, one had found from experience that land assessed at 100 square rods yields 6 scheffels, then land assessed at 150 square rods, having one and a half times as large an area, would give the same gross yield, but per equal area only 100/150 times 6, that is, 4 scheffels of yield.

This proposition can at first be only a hypothesis; however, upon manifold and prolonged testing, I have found it so frequently confirmed that I can no longer regard this as a play of chance.

It is highly probable that in drafting the assessment regulations themselves, one proceeded from the principle of taking the gross yield as the norm of value, and thereby assumed that arable land of lesser quality was so much easier to cultivate and required so much less seed than the better soil, that the production of the same product on both types of soil would still require an equal quantity of labor and costs; and that only from this incorrect assumption did the assessment miss its purpose so greatly.

Accepting this proposition as true, it requires only the presentation of the relationship between gross and net yield in order to restore the hufe-standard to its original purpose: to be the measure of estate value.

The presentation of this relationship is, however, connected with very great difficulties. The propositions on which this calculation must be founded may rest only on the basis of pure experience, if they are not to deliver nothing but skewed results. The costs of the working forces must be precisely determined; the quantity of labor that is performed by them, the labor that a field of given size requires with increasing or falling grain yield, must be known; then the general cultivation costs that cannot be apportioned to the individual branches of the economy, such as administrative costs, building costs, and so forth, must be precisely calculated; and finally this calculation, applied to the specific presentation of the net yield of each individual branch of the economy, must be verified through agreement with the actual total yield, before it can be used to establish general propositions.

The data necessary for such a calculation were for the most part available to me through five years of accounts kept precisely and in detail for another scientific purpose; but the magnitude of the work and the uncertainty whether, despite all striving for truth, it could command sufficient attention to become useful in the introduction of the credit system, long held me back, until the gradually growing conviction that such a presentation could be useful not merely for a momentary purpose, but would perhaps have a lasting benefit, finally moved me to carry it out and develop it.

The communication of the experiences and the reasons on which this presentation is based, and from which it has emerged, would require a separate work, and I can therefore here communicate only the final results that this investigation has yielded.

Now, in order to learn what grain yield is in general associated with each assessed grade of the arable land, it is necessary to compile and compare actual average yields with the assessed grade of the soil that produced that yield.

Among the data before me, the older grain yield of the estate -- which serves as the basis for the above calculations -- seems to me the most serviceable, because in my view the conditions of the estate at that time, in the points that matter here, did not deviate from the general and ordinary conditions of most estates.

The ten-year average yield of this estate was, on 60 square rods in Rostock scheffels: wheat 7.25, rye 5.75 scheffels.

The arable land where the wheat was grown contains, according to the assessment, on average 80.5 square rods per scheffel. The rye land is assessed on average at 108.25 square rods.

In the next following seven years, the average yield of this estate was: wheat 8 scheffels, rye 7.75 Rostock scheffels on 60 square rods.

The higher yield of the last seven years is a consequence of marling and other improvements; it is therefore to be regarded as a result of cultivation, and cannot be taken into account in the determination of the pledge value of estates until such cultivation has become general and lasting across the entire land. That older yield, however, belongs to the soil in its original condition and can serve as a basis for our purpose.

Soil assessed at 108.25 square rods yielded 5.75 scheffels of rye on 60 square rods. Since, according to our assumption, the assessed number of square rods is the measure of the gross yield, and indeed in such a way that one assessed scheffel, however many square rods it may contain, always gives the same gross grain yield: from this single empirical proposition, the grain yield of arable land of every assessed grade can be found. One scheffel of land assessed at 100 square rods gives, for example, just as much yield as one scheffel of land at 108.25 square rods. The yield of the latter is, on 60 square rods, 5.75 scheffels of rye; this makes on 108.25 square rods 10.375 scheffels; thus the yield of each assessed scheffel would be 10.375 scheffels of rye, and land assessed at 100 square rods would give a yield on 60 square rods of 10.375 times 60/100, equal to 6.225 scheffels of rye. This can also be found by a simple calculation: by multiplying the number of square rods of the land for which the yield is known by the scheffel number of the yield, and then dividing by the assessed number of square rods of the land for which the yield is sought. For example, 108.25 times 5.75 likewise gives 6.225 scheffels.

Wheat on soil assessed at 80.5 square rods gave 7.25 scheffels yield; land assessed at 75 square rods will therefore give 80.5 times 7.25 divided by 75, equal to 7.78 scheffels on 60 square rods.

1. That arable land assessed at 75 square rods in general yields 7.5 scheffels of wheat on 60 square rods.

2. That land assessed at 100 square rods yields 6 scheffels of rye on 60 square rods.

The monetary yield from 100,000 square rods of wheat soil at 7.5 scheffels on 60 square rods is stated above as 1,501 Thalers.

The monetary yield of rye soil at 7.5 scheffels is 1,211 Thalers.

Thus according to this, rye soil with 8.5 scheffels rye yield equals wheat soil with 7.5 scheffels wheat yield. According to the law that the yield stands in inverse ratio to the assessed number of square rods, land assessed at 75 square rods likewise has a yield of 8.5 scheffels of rye; for 100 times 6.5 divided by 75 equals 8.67 scheffels.

It follows from this that in this calculation we may no longer separate the wheat soil from the rye soil, and that the value assigned to the wheat soil according to its assessed grade can be calculated by the same formula as for rye soil. It seems to me that one can in general assume that the wheat soil encompasses the land that is assessed from 75 to 83 square rods. Rye soil of the first class, assessed at 83 square rods, probably still delivers from an equal area as large a harvest of straw and mass of grain as land assessed at 75 square rods; but the advantage of the latter consists in the fact that it bears a nobler crop which, at equal mass, has a value one-third greater. The capacity of the soil to produce this nobler crop with certainty and of the best quality is highest in land assessed at 75 square rods, diminishes from there by degrees down to land at 83 square rods, and when the latter still bears wheat, it delivers so much less mass than rye that it becomes more profitable to sow the land with the latter crop. Only in this greater capacity to produce wheat with certainty does the reason lie why one can attribute a progressive increase in value to land down to 75 square rods; for most other crops, land assessed at 80 square rods is more suitable.

Marl completely changes the stated boundary between wheat and rye soil, which, however, cannot be taken into account here.

One has also placed loam soil in the class of the best soil and assessed it at 75 to 80 square rods. Loam does bear wheat, but the harvests are so meager and the cultivation so difficult that its value is probably to be set below the value of medium soil, and only with exceptionally high manuring power does loam deserve the rank assigned to it. Since the assessment registers do not at all indicate which land consists of loam or not, the separation of this from the best soil is not possible. In the valuation of the soil on the basis of the older assessment, inequalities can arise from this; but I very much doubt that these inequalities can become so significant as to pose any danger for the pledge value to be determined; for: firstly, loam is almost never found in whole stretches, but almost always only on hilltops, and the valleys lying between them usually consist of such excellent soil that they can well carry along a portion of poor soil; secondly, the assessors took the strength and bearing capacity of the soil into account and frequently assessed land that by its clay content belongs to the wheat soil at a lower grade, while the individual and scattered loam hills were usually not distinguished at all, but assessed at the same grade as the adjoining land.

Section 17.

Necessary considerations that must often be left out of account here.

It will strike most people as remarkable that here the net yield already vanishes at a harvest of 4 scheffels of rye on 60 square rods, since according to the usual assumptions there are very many estates that yield only 4 bushels and yet have always had a rental and purchase value. I must here therefore once more repeat that the usual statement of the number of grains grown encompasses only the actual clean grain brought to the granary, and that a yield of 4 grains in this sense already presupposes a production of approximately 5 grains. To avoid the many explanations, I would have preferred to follow the customary usage here and state the grain yield in this sense; but since one person deducts much tailings and does not bring them into the account while another deducts little, since on one estate much unthreshed or half-threshed grain is fed while on another perhaps none at all: there is no precision possible in any of this, and statements of this kind are incompatible with the accuracy and correctness of a calculation.

But even apart from this, one is in general all too inclined to attribute a value even to the worst soil, and since this point is of such great importance for the establishment of the credit association, it seems necessary to me to say something more on this matter.

One can consider the value of poor sandy soil from a twofold perspective, depending on whether an estate has only such sandy land, or whether alongside the sand it also contains good arable land.

When in earlier times one broke up a sandy tract cleared of forest and, on account of the great distance, furnished it with buildings, thus establishing a separate estate: the capital invested in it could perhaps yield generous interest and thereby, after the passage of several years, repay the capital itself, as the land initially gave abundant harvests thanks to the humus accumulated over centuries. But after this humus had been extracted from the land, and the soil reemerged in its original infertility, it ceased to pay the costs of cultivation.

It was not on account of the soil, but on account of the humus that the soil contained, that the buildings had been erected. After this was consumed, the buildings had fulfilled their purpose; one should have torn them down at this point, just as one tears down a glassworks when it has consumed all the wood around it.

But to the misfortune of later generations, they have for the most part remained standing. Had they remained in the possession of one family, they could not long have escaped their natural destiny; but estates of this kind pass from hand to hand, and as each new owner brings fresh energy, they rescue their existence and continue to feed on the national income.

For estates of somewhat better soil that a hundred years ago yielded a genuine rent, circumstances have changed so unfavorably over time that their net yield too becomes doubtful. In those days wood had no value; the maintenance of buildings cost nothing but labor. Now the material constitutes the main component of the value of buildings. If such an estate has been stripped of its own timber by one of its owners, the purchase of wood alone can destroy the otherwise existing net yield of the estate; but if the estate itself has the necessary wood, then the sale of the wood used in the farm would yield as great a return as the entire estate now does; and the arable land has then, through the risen price of wood, lost its value.

Formerly the taxes were so small that they scarcely came into consideration; now they take away a large portion of the estate's income.

The demand for firewood and construction timber is determined by the number of laborer families and the size of the buildings; and both stand in proportion to the size of the harvest, or the gross yield.

The taxes are levied according to the hufes, and the hufe-standard of an estate is determined by its gross yield.

Increases in taxes and timber prices therefore worsen the expenses of the estate in proportion to its gross yield. The increase in grain prices that has occurred over time affects only that portion of the harvest that can be brought to market and sold, which on estates of this kind amounts to only about a quarter of the total production. Grain prices would therefore have had to rise in a much stronger proportion than timber prices and taxes in order to keep such estates unharmed. Since this has not happened, their value sinks in proportion to the better estates, which can bring half the harvest to market, ever further downward, and the soil that formerly yielded a rent no longer pays the costs of its cultivation under present circumstances.

In the case of estates that consist partly of good arable land and partly of sand, one must carefully distinguish the value that the sandy soil has for the manager of the estate -- whether he be owner or tenant -- from the purchase or pledge value of this soil, and not infer the latter from the former. The manager must regard every soil whose yield exceeds the labor costs to be applied to it as usable, and must cultivate it. The surplus of the yield over the labor costs he can in a certain sense regard as net yield, and estimate the value of the soil accordingly. But from this surplus the administrative costs, building costs, and taxes to the state must be paid, and the sum that then remains establishes a purchase value; but if nothing remains, then the soil has neither purchase nor pledge value, however useful it may be to the manager.

Whether the sandy soil in this connection with good arable land has a greater value than when it stands alone depends partly on local circumstances and principally on the intelligence of the farm manager. Since this soil can still be plowed and harrowed in wet weather when the good arable land may not be cultivated, proportionally less draft livestock need be kept on its account, and the cultivation costs are not to be charged as highly to this soil. But this is only the case when the sand lies together in a larger area and is managed in a purposeful field system by itself. If it lies piecemeal intermixed with the good arable land, its value is as a rule negative; for: firstly, the sandy soil lying near the farmstead cannot compensate, through its yield, for the disadvantage that it separates the good land from the farmstead and increases its distance; secondly, the management of such an estate becomes a very dangerous pitfall for the farmer. It is an almost universal tendency, especially of younger farmers, to devote all their energy to this soil in order to bring it to the same level of cultivation as the rest, and however often this attempt has already been made in vain, almost everyone begins it anew. By applying manure to a soil that, through its straw yield and through the pasture during the fallow years, cannot in the entire rotation reproduce as much manure as it received, the quantity of manure produced decreases annually, and with it the harvests also decline. In this manner estates that had a large portion of good soil have often sunk entirely to a zero yield.

In most circumstances it will undoubtedly be most expedient to sow this soil with pines.

A sandy soil covered with large pines surpasses the best wheat soil in value; and the question now arises how the pledge value and the ground rent of this land are thereby altered.

The value of an area sown with pines increases continuously because one does not take away the annual product of the soil -- the growth of wood -- but allows it to accumulate, and the great harvest that then becomes possible after a long time is the compensation for the sacrifice of the annual harvests.

If one wishes to buy a capital that can only be realized after 150 years, one will be able to pay for it a sum which, with interest and compound interest over 150 years, yields a capital as large as the one purchased. Calculated in this way, the value of a young pine plantation will turn out to be very small, and if one then deducts from this value the not insignificant costs of the initial sowing -- not insignificant on account of the frequent failures -- then at present timber prices a negative value of the soil to be planted will often result. But the price of wood will undoubtedly rise in the future to the point where this loss is at least covered; for this is the natural price of wood. However, one can hardly count on such a rise in timber prices that it would give poor arable land a significant ground rent, because in Mecklenburg we possess so much poor soil that cannot otherwise be used that no good soil need be employed for wood production.

Only on the seacoast, where the ease of marketing can generate a demand that can no longer be satisfied by the product of poor soil alone, can a ground rent thereby arise.

For individual estates it will now, in order to escape the excessive increase in the price of wood caused by hauling it from afar and by the whims of the seller, be of decisive importance to grow one's own timber supply on the estate itself; and this will have to be done even when there is no poor soil on the estate at all.

In this case, then, an estate that has just enough poor soil for the production of its timber needs would have almost as great a value as an estate consisting entirely of good soil.

If it was formerly a very correct and profitable speculation to sell the wood and convert the forest land into arable land, the present situation, where wood is approaching its natural price, calls upon every estate owner in his own interest to secure his estate's timber supply for the future.

If one wished now, in establishing the credit association, to include the increased value that the soil receives from the timber standing on it as pledge value, this would presuppose a constant supervision by the directorate to prevent the timber stock from being diminished. This supervision would, on the one hand, unpleasantly and detrimentally restrict the freedom of estate owners, and on the other hand become very expensive through the increase in the directorate's business. It seems to me far more appropriate to the dignity of the credit association and the solidity of its foundation to take only the ground rent as the measure of valuation, and to determine this for the soil covered with timber according to what it would yield as arable land or meadow.

The capital value still embedded in the wood beyond this ground rent would then remain, untouched by the pledge value, open for private credit, and indeed for a mortgage of variable value, the ordinary capital loan subject to notice is entirely suitable. As soon as the creditor sees that the value of the mortgage is diminishing, he calls in his capital, and thereby to some extent replaces the security that the unchangeability of the mortgage does not afford him.

It is understood that the wood on the estate, although not included in the pledge value, must still serve as collateral for the mortgage bonds, and that a capitalist cannot acquire a preferential right to it through a private loan. But the capitalist will, when he is convinced that the estate already has that pledge value even without taking the value of the surplus timber into account, be able to lend upon it without hesitation.

The period of a war is the most suitable time for observing the pledge value of estates, because the monetary yield of the estates then approaches the minimum through large levies, through low grain prices, or through both causes simultaneously. The decline in net yield then, however, for good and poor estates, does not stand in proportion to the former average yield. The hufe of poor soil then loses from its usual yield nearly as much as the hufe of good soil, and when the latter has fallen to half its average yield, the former is already at zero yield. This consideration alone must make the admission of poor estates into the credit association very questionable.

It scarcely needs the remark that when, according to that table, land assessed at 155.5 square rods no longer has any value, this cannot be accurate under all possible circumstances. If the assessors rated the land so low not because of its sandy character, but because of its low manuring power; or if the assessment took place in very dry weather and the land thereby appeared somewhat worse than it actually was: then such a soil, especially if it has a not too permeable subsoil and contains some marl hills, can be raised to a considerable level of cultivation. Only the most general and most ordinary circumstances could be taken into account in that table, and if it is correct for these, it would have achieved everything that was to be achieved.

If on one side the exclusion of sandy soil may provoke criticism, it can on the other side be criticized with more justification that here a maximum of yield is assumed that does not exist in nature, at least not so closely, and that can actually be exceeded through enhanced cultivation.

I have stated in Section 6 the reasons why I believe that the fertility of the arable land increased through marling is not yet to be regarded as permanent, and can have no influence yet on the determination of the pledge value. The same reasons have guided me here as well. For an ordinary state of cultivation, however, I believe the yield assumed here of 7.5 scheffels of wheat on the best soil will agree very closely with reality.

There will undoubtedly be some estates that, through the intelligence of their owners, will not only rise to the higher state of cultivation but will also maintain themselves in it, and the owners of these estates will very likely not find it advantageous to join the credit association, because the yield determined according to the old state of the estate falls too far below the actual yield of the estate.

The strength of the soil at which the land yields more than 7.5 scheffels of wheat on 60 square rods does not agree well with the Mecklenburg rotation system; at least, if lodged grain is not to be continuously grown, an unusual degree of attention to cultivation, strength of sowing, and above all to the correct distribution of manure is required. Even if the present owner of the estate succeeds in overcoming these difficulties through exceptional attention to the cultivation of the land, or through modifications in the farming system: with his death this management, because it contradicts the prevailing local practice, all too easily collapses again.

The new owner or tenant finds the higher strength of the soil merely disadvantageous, seeks to remove it through additional crops, and there can be no doubt that he will thereby succeed.

That elevated state of cultivation is therefore only momentary, as long as it is not the prevailing practice; it can give the owner of the estate a greater personal credit, but it is by nothing secured for the duration upon which alone the pledge value may be founded.

On the other hand, the rotation system does not oppose the improvement by which the soil is raised from the lower grade to the yield of land of a higher grade. Under favorable circumstances, land assessed at 100 square rods can perhaps be brought to the yield of first-class arable land.

If one wished to accept this higher yield as permanent -- which at present could be demonstrated by longer experience for very few estates only -- and if one wished to raise the valuation principles so high that the current yield of the improved estates would be correctly stated: then the valuations for all other estates of ordinary cultivation would thereby become too high. The disadvantage that can arise from some estates being valued too low stands in no proportion to the magnitude of the disadvantage that would spring from valuations that are too high. The former can be inconvenient for individual estate owners; the latter would dissolve the entire association.

May the greater value added to an estate through exceptional cultivation, just like the value embedded in surplus timber, serve as security for the capital claims to be registered after the mortgage bonds. Given their variability, they can provide adequate security for an irredeemable permanent debt -- no; but they can for a capital subject to notice.

B. The Meadows.

The yield and value of the meadows will not emerge from the older assessment with the accuracy possible for the arable land. When one views the meadows not at the time of the hay harvest, but when they are bare, they offer far fewer indicators from which one can infer their yield than does the arable land. The older assessment, like every new valuation, will for this reason bear traces of deficiency and inaccuracy.

Much of what now appears to us as a striking error of the assessors may also often be merely the consequence of the changed condition of the meadows; for a slight change in the moisture state of the meadows can cause a great change in yield. In no case, however, may one regard the great discrepancy that exists between the current actual hay yield of the estates and the number of loads stated in the cadastre as proof of a faulty assessment. Where the hay yield is now significantly greater, as a rule large stretches of marsh pasture or low-lying pasture have been converted into meadows; in the reverse case, the former meadows have been transformed into arable land, pasture, or marsh woodland. The sum of the assessed scheffels can therefore still stand in fairly good proportion to the current yield of this area, even though the type of use has completely changed.

The perspectives from which the value of the hay must be determined are less clear and allow less precision than for arable land. The value of a scheffel of rye is equally great whether it was grown on this or that estate. The feed value of the hay, as well as the value of the manure, changes with the ratio in which the hay yield stands to the arable area, and with the quality of the arable land by which the manure is to be utilized. The principles according to which the feed and manure value of the hay are to be determined still lie entirely in darkness. It would therefore now still be futile to seek to find and present a standard of valuation for all different circumstances.

For a given locality, where the hay yield stands in a medium ratio to the arable area, a calculation yielded the entirely net yield value for one farm-load of hay at 2 Thalers 31 shillings N.Z.W. The value of the manure resulting from the hay is included herein; the harvesting costs of the hay, as well as all general cultivation costs falling on the share of the meadows -- such as taxes, building costs, administrative costs, and so forth -- are deducted, so that the stated yield value could serve as the norm of the purchase or pledge value for this locality.

Since it is impossible to determine the different value of hay for every individual estate, our effort here can only be directed toward finding the average value as it would emerge from the mean of all estates.

Since the standpoint from which the above valuation was made will correspond fairly well with the average ratio, I must, in the absence of further data, take this as the basis here.

According to the assessment regulations, the valuation of the meadows was carried out in such a way that for the best meadow lands one reckoned one farmer's load of hay from 100 square rods, for less good meadows more square rods, and for the worst ones 300 square rods per farmer's load. Each farmer's load of hay, without regard to the size of the area from which it was gathered, was reckoned equal to two assessed scheffels of arable land.

Here again, therefore, the gross yield is taken as the measure of value. The harvesting costs for one load of hay become ever greater the larger the area from which it is gathered, and in order to determine the net yield of meadows of various quality, one must first ascertain how, for an equal mass of hay, the harvesting costs increase with increasing area.

According to observations in general, I assume that one laborer mows in a day 200 square rods of the best, or 300 square rods of the worst meadows, and that the work of haymaking stands in the same ratio.

200 square rods of the best meadows are assessed at 2 loads of hay; 300 square rods of the worst at 1 load.

The labor of mowing and haymaking would therefore cost, on the worst meadows, twice as much per load as on the best meadows.

Assuming that for meadows of medium quality the harvesting costs increase regularly with the area, reaching double for meadows of 300 square rods: the following ratio would result:

For 1 load of hay on 100 square rods, the harvesting costs amount to x times 1.
For 1 load on 125 square rods, x times 1.125.
On 150, x times 1.25.
On 175, x times 1.375.
On 200, x times 1.5.
On 225, x times 1.625.
On 250, x times 1.75.
On 275, x times 1.875.
On 300, x times 2.

Expressed generally, the harvesting costs for one load of hay on 100 plus y square rods equal x times (1 plus y/200). This makes, for example, for 130 square rods: x times (1 plus 30/200) equals x times 1.15; for y equals 1, or for meadows of 101 square rods, the costs amount to x times (1 plus 1/200). With each square rod by which the area increases, they therefore increase by x/200, from which the table can easily be filled in for any number.

The harvesting costs for one farm-load of hay amount on the estate taken as the reference point to 1 Thaler 4.75 shillings. Of this, the costs of hauling in, which as a rule do not stand in proportion to the size of the meadows but solely to the quantity of hay, amount to 18.5 shillings; the costs of mowing and haymaking, which change with the area, are therefore 1 Thaler 4.75 shillings minus 18.5 shillings equals 30 shillings.

The share of administrative costs and other general cultivation costs that increases with the quantity of labor and stands in proportion to it amounts to 24 percent of the labor costs; this makes for 30 shillings a further 7.2 shillings, together thus 37.2 shillings.

The meadows of this estate are assessed on average at 133 square rods per farmer's load of hay.

Reckoning one farmer's load equal to two-thirds of a farm-load, on this estate for one farmer's load of hay:

1. The harvesting costs amount to 37.5 shillings times two-thirds equals 28 shillings.

2. The net yield value is 2 Thalers 31 shillings times two-thirds equals 1 Thaler 47 shillings.

3. The yield value and the costs of mowing and haymaking together: 2 Thalers 27 shillings.

If the costs of mowing and haymaking on meadows assessed at 133 square rods amount to 28 shillings per load, this makes for meadows assessed at 100 square rods approximately 24.5 shillings.

100,000 square rods of the best meadows, assessed at 100 square rods, give 1,000 loads at 2 Thalers 3 shillings net yield equals 2,062.5 Thalers.

100,000 square rods of the best arable land, assessed at 75 square rods, give a net yield of 1,501 Thalers.

The value of these meadows therefore stands to the value of this arable land in the ratio of 137 to 100.

100,000 square rods of meadows assessed at 300 square rods give 333.5 loads at 1 Thaler 27 shillings equals 521 Thalers, which comes approximately to the value of arable land with 5.25 scheffels yield on 60 square rods.

This ratio seems to me to be in agreement, at least not in contradiction, with general opinion as expressed in lease arrangements; which would be of great significance for the correctness of this calculation, which has sprung from entirely different sources.

The quality of the hay appears to stand in no ratio to the quantity that the meadows yield; for there are low-yielding meadows that deliver fine, nutritious hay; high-yielding meadows that give coarse, hard hay; and in other meadows the reverse is true.

For this reason, no account has here been taken of the quality of the hay, but hay of medium quality has always been assumed.

The improvement that many meadows have received in more recent times through irrigation could, since the location of the meadow and the quantity of water used for irrigation probably will not change, be regarded as permanent and could establish a higher pledge value, if the knowledge required for irrigation were so general among farmers that the change of the farmer could no longer endanger the improvement once begun. At the present moment, it does not yet seem to me to be the case.

It must necessarily be noticed that the instructions prescribed to the assessors that in the best meadow lands the yield from 100 square rods should be reckoned at no more than 1 load of hay, although we undoubtedly have meadows that in two cuttings yield 2 farmer's loads of hay from 100 square rods. This, combined with the circumstance that the instructions make no mention at all of two-cutting meadows and do not at all separate them from one-cutting meadows, makes it highly probable that only the first cutting was taken as the measure of yield, that the aftermath was regarded as standing in exact proportion to the first cutting, and that the value of the hay was then set so high that the value of the aftermath was already included in it.

In this sense the above calculation, which determined the net value of one farm-load of hay at 2 Thalers 31 shillings, was also drafted, in that the value of the aftermath pasture is combined with the hay value.

The use of the aftermath is, however, not equal on meadows of different quality. On meadows of lesser quality the grass grown after the first cutting can only be utilized by grazing with cattle; on the better meadows the grass reaches such a height as to deliver another hay harvest.

The meadows undoubtedly require, just as well as the arable land, an annual replacement for the harvests they have delivered, if they are to maintain themselves at the same level of fertility. The one-cutting meadows receive this replacement in part through the manure dropped during grazing.

If one now converts such a meadow into a two-cutting one, the greater hay yield initially resulting therefrom is not to be regarded as annual yield, but rather it arises through diminution of the basic capital, and after several years one will obtain in two cuttings, thus with double the labor, only as much hay as formerly in one cutting. This case is far more frequent than is commonly believed.

There are, however, also meadows that receive through natural or artificial irrigation with sediment-bearing water so much replacement that they can annually yield two hay harvests without being exhausted. Even though the aftermath is far from having the value of the first cutting -- the hay being usually of lesser quality, the work of haymaking usually greater on account of the often unfavorable weather, and it becoming especially costly and disadvantageous because it easily causes a neglect in the sowing of the winter crop -- these meadows still have, simply by providing a greater mass of manure annually for the arable land, a great advantage over the one-cutting meadows.

For meadows of this kind a special valuation would have to take place, if the assessment registers contained indications by which they could be recognized. According to my observation, however, one cannot conclude with certainty from the grade at which the meadows are assessed whether they are one-cutting or two-cutting. A calculation based on this grade determination would then for many estates state the value of the meadows much higher than it is in reality. Since this is far more detrimental for our purpose than if in individual cases the value is not fully reached by the valuation, we may here also take no further account of the individually excellent meadows.

The meadows in Mecklenburg have received yet another very significant value that is independent of their actual use; in that they have become, as it were, the standard by which one recognizes the fertility and value of the arable land with which they are connected. The meadows have acquired this influence because the arable land of estates with much or with little hay was managed in almost the same way, and nearly the same number of crops was taken from the land on both. If now the estate with little hay maintained itself at the same level of cultivation under this field system, then the other estate, through the annual addition of manure resulting from the hay, had to continually increase in yield and fertility, and in this way a difference in the value of the arable land arose that was not at all founded in the physical character of the soil.

The decline or increase in the fertility of the arable land depends solely on whether the exhausting forces -- grain crops -- are greater or less than the restorative forces -- fallow and manuring. This ratio is, however, not tied to the hay yield, and since it is probable that science will in the future exert a greater influence on practical farming, the previous effect of the hay will not be permanent either, and one may attribute to the hay only the value that proceeds from its feed and manure value, but not also reckon in that value which it has acquired only through the monotony of farming practices.

Since we here have to consider the present state of the estates, however, the effect of the hay that it has already exerted, and the advantage that the arable land with much hay now has, must certainly be precisely determined. This determination would now be extremely difficult and perhaps impossible. Fortunately, however, the hay had already exerted its influence on the fertility of the arable land by the time the assessment was made, and since the assessors determined the assessed grade mainly according to the strength of the land: the task has already been solved by the assessment itself.

Section 19.

C. Pasture.

And specifically:

a. Pasture that was never covered with wood.

About fifty years ago, when the assessment took place, a large part of the estates still lay in the three-field system, in which a portion of the upland soil capable of cultivation and a portion of the meadow ground was used as permanent pasture.

The assessment regulations prescribe that 100 square rods of the best pasture, or 500 square rods of the worst pasture, be reckoned equal to one scheffel of arable land.

Since the general introduction of the rotation system, the pastures on the uplands -- unless they were of too poor a quality -- have almost all been broken up and converted to arable land; the pastures in the lowlands are now used as meadows, or for marsh timber, and only rarely still as pasture.

In valuing the pastures, the assessors could direct their attention only to the strength of the grass growth. But the grass growth is to be regarded as the product of the physical quality of the soil and the manuring power of the soil, and can serve as a determinant of the quality of the soil just as well as the growth of grain. One can therefore conclude with tolerable certainty from the grass growth of the old pastures the grain yield that these now give as arable land, provided that the soil was not unusually favorable or unfavorable for grass growth due to a low or high situation.

One would accordingly be able to regard pasture land assessed at 100 square rods as equal in value to an equal area of arable land assessed at 75 square rods, were it not for the following essential differences that make a modification necessary:

1. The old pasture land already has a lesser value than the arable land because it usually occupies the part of the estate most remote from the farmstead.

2. One usually took as pasture the part of the upland soil that lay low and was heavily intersected with meadows and ditches; or the part of the estate that presented obstacles to cultivation through many stones; and finally, the less fertile hills, when they still had some grass growth, were also used as pasture.

For these reasons one will, if one does not wish to expose oneself to the danger of too high a valuation, have to retain the principle contained in the assessment regulations themselves, according to which the pasture has only three-quarters of the value of arable land of equal strength.

Namely, 100 square rods of the best pasture are assumed equal in value to 75 square rods of the best arable land. If one now maintains this ratio, the result is:

that 1 scheffel of pasture assessed at 120 square rods is equal in value to 1 scheffel of arable land assessed at 90 square rods.

1 scheffel of pasture at 140 square rods equals 1 scheffel of land at 105 square rods.
At 160, equals 120.
At 180, equals 135.
At 200, equals 150.
At 207, equals 155.25.

Through this, pasture assessed at more than 207 square rods would be excluded. This seems to me absolutely necessary; for pasture of such poor quality cannot as a rule be profitably converted into arable land, and the small use it gives as pasture is completely consumed by the taxes, administrative costs, and so forth.

The low-lying pasture must, in my opinion, be treated just like the upland pasture, even though it has been converted not into arable land but into meadows; at least I would know of no valuation that would be more generally accurate and that would so well avoid the danger of too high a valuation in individual cases.

There are individual excellent pastures whose soil consists of a mixture of arable and meadow earth, and that through grazing alone can give a higher use than the best arable land. But pastures of this kind are too rare to be taken into account in a general valuation.

When one compares the assessment registers with the present state of the estates, one finds that individual lowlands that were assessed as moor pasture at 300 to 400 square rods per scheffel have, through the ditches drawn later, been drained and are now converted into good meadows. Since the assessment registers contain no indications by which they can be recognized, they cannot be taken into account here either. But these lowlands -- which are moreover seldom significant -- as well as all objects whose present uses cannot be captured by the form of our valuation and therefore remain excluded, regain their influence and the consideration due to them by giving the mortgage bonds a greater security than they would otherwise have.

Section 20.

b. Pasture covered with wood.

In the instructions it says:

"So shall also all forests, marshes, and thickets, without any distinction, be included in the farm valuation, provided that by the assessors, only the grass growth and the pasture to be utilized therein shall be considered, and by no means the quality of the soil or other uses. Therefore forests and thickets, according as they give more or less pasture, shall be classified up to 500 square rods per scheffel. More than 500 square rods, however, shall not be reckoned per scheffel even in the densest plantations, young timber enclosures, pole-timber marshes, and other thickets, even though at the time of the assessment they could give no pasture at all."

One therefore regarded timber at that time not merely as a thing without value, but as an object that hindered the free use of the land.

Since for our purpose the rent that the soil can yield according to its natural quality must be determined, and it is immaterial whether one seeks to obtain this rent through timber growth or through grain cultivation: here the assessment entirely fails us, and a revision or new valuation becomes unavoidable.

With the new valuation of a part of the estates, however, all the disadvantages recur that were mentioned above in connection with the new valuation of entire estates. The public will first want to examine the correctness of the new valuations and convince itself that no arbitrariness has taken place, and through this a long, perhaps interminable period of time would be lost. It therefore seems to me absolutely necessary to establish a maximum that cannot be exceeded by the valuation.

However different the soil on individual estates may be, one nevertheless finds, when surveying an entire region, that the soil has general characteristic features by which it is distinguished from the soil of other regions. One will, for example, not easily find in a generally sandy region a single estate consisting entirely of loam soil; in a flat region, no single hilly field.

One can here conclude not with certainty -- for otherwise no new valuation would be needed -- but with some probability that the soil covered with timber at the time of the assessment is proportional to the arable land of the same estate, and on this I base the following proposals for determining the maximum:

1. The upland soil covered with timber, or the soil assessed as hard wood-pasture, stands in value to an equal area of arable land of the same estate as 3 to 4.

2. The lowlands assessed as marsh pasture have, for an equal area, half the value of the lowlands assessed as meadows.

The value of the arable land is obtained by dividing the total area of the arable land by the sum of the scheffel count at which it is assessed, and calculating for the grade thus obtained the yield given in the table. The value of one scheffel multiplied by the total scheffel count of the arable land gives the total yield of the arable land; this yield divided by the area of the arable land gives the yield per square rod. In this calculation, however, the arable land assessed at over 155.5 square rods must not be excluded.

The value of one square rod of meadows is obtained through a similar calculation.

In most cases the present yield of the former forest land -- regardless of whether it is now used as arable land, meadow, or still for timber growth -- will reach and exceed the maximum proposed here. In this case no actual valuation is needed, but merely a general overview on the part of the assessors. If, however, the present yield does not reach the maximum, then to facilitate the valuation it might perhaps be expedient to take this as the basis, and to leave the assessors only the determination of whether the yield is one-tenth, one-fifth, and so forth less than the assumed maximum indicates, in which case the assessors, if they proceed in the spirit of this proposal, would in doubtful cases always state the certain, not the possible but uncertain, value.

Section 21.

D. Distance of the Estate from the Market.

The distance from the market determines the costs of grain transportation, and thereby the value that the grain has at the estate itself. My five years of accounts yield as an average that the costs of transportation for one scheffel of rye per mile amounted to 1.25 shillings.

The average price of rye was 46.75 shillings; at a distance of 4 miles the value of rye at the estate itself would accordingly be 46.75 minus 4 times 1.25 equals 42.75 shillings; at 8 miles distance 46.75 minus 8 times 1.25 equals 37.75 shillings; at 12 miles distance 46.75 minus 15 equals 33.75 shillings.

The price of grain determines not only the income but also the costs of labor, since these are for the most part paid in grain. One will therefore, with some deviations, be able to assume that the value of the grain itself also determines the net yield.

If we had for all of Mecklenburg only a single commercial town to which all grain had to be brought, it would be easy to state the value of every estate according to its distance from this town. But since we have several commercial towns and the price of grain is not everywhere equal, such a determination would become very complicated. It becomes, however, entirely impossible because we possess no authentic lists of the average prices in each town.

The towns of Boizenburg, Domitz, Rostock, and Wismar generally give the highest prices. In Furstenberg, Anklam, and Demmin the grain prices are usually 10 to 12 percent lower than in the first-named towns.

On an estate lying very close to these latter towns, the grain has, although the transportation costs are quite insignificant, no higher value than on estates lying 4 to 5 miles from the principal commercial towns. The estates lying near Demmin usually haul their grain to Rostock anyway, because the higher prices outweigh the transportation costs.

If one wished now, because the valuation for the location of every individual estate is too difficult, to leave the distance from the market entirely out of account and to apply the same valuation to all estates in the entire country, then one would have to assume the value of the grain on estates at the seacoast to be just as low as it is on the estates that must drag their grain 8 to 10 miles over bottomless roads, because otherwise the valuation for the distant estates would be too high, which must be avoided in all cases.

In order to alleviate this inequality to some extent, it seems to me most expedient to divide all the estates in Mecklenburg into two classes with respect to their position for marketing. In the first class one would have to count the estates that are not more than 5.5 miles from Boizenburg, Domitz, Rostock, or Wismar, and place all other estates in the second class; for the latter, apply the same valuation principles as for the estates of the first class, but at the end deduct 10 percent from the net yield that the valuation gives.

This would have the disadvantage connected with every class division, that two estates lying close together at the dividing line would nonetheless have to be placed in different classes; but the inequality arising from this is very much less than it would be if one placed estates of one and of ten miles distance in a single class.

That the regions more distant from the commercial towns grow other products whose transport is less costly in proportion to their value does indeed lessen the disadvantage of their location, but does not remove it. The cultivation of such products is the province of these regions only because the nearer regions can utilize their land more highly through grain cultivation.

Branches of farming that are universally profitable can indeed by chance first be cultivated in the more distant regions; but they constitute no lasting advantage, since they can be cultivated with the same benefit also in the nearer regions, and will therefore soon pass to these. This will be the case, for example, with Spanish sheep farming.

The advantage that the southern part of Mecklenburg has because the butter market is nearer to it can come little into consideration, since an estate that sends 100 wagons of grain to town has scarcely six small loads of butter to sell.

Section 22.

E. Peasant Lands.

The peasant estates, since they are for the most part still in communal tenure and the arable land of each peasant lies separated in narrow strips, cannot give the yield that this land would give as a demesne field. If one wishes to include the peasant estates in the association, this can, in my judgment, not be done without danger except by giving them only half the pledge value of the demesne fields.

The conversion of a peasant field into a demesne field is made so expensive through the complete cession of a part of the field to the peasants and through the construction of entirely new buildings that the value thereby added to the estate is, as a rule, consumed again by the costs of the conversion.

The reduced and completely disbanded peasant holdings can add no pledge value at all to the estate to which they belong, and the land they possess would be entirely excluded from the valuation; for the small hereditary rent they pay, and can only pay, is scarcely sufficient to compensate the taxes and other burdens borne by the estate.

The parsonage land taken from the estate as hereditary lease can give the estate no greater pledge value, since the hereditary rent already takes away the entire net yield and frequently even more. It therefore does not come into consideration in the valuation. However, where the determination of the monetary value of the hereditary rent presents no difficulty, this could be deducted from the yield of the entire estate.

Section 23.

F. Use of the Waters.

In the assessment, 120 Thalers of fishing rent was reckoned equal to one hufe of land.

To bring this into agreement with present circumstances, one would have to seek to determine, through the collection of a quantity of data, in what proportion the fishing rent has risen since that time.

From the average rent that would result for a water-hufe in this way, 80 Thalers for taxes and war costs would then be deducted; and the remainder then remaining would be regarded as net yield.

Section 26.

Overview of the objects whose value and yield are not contained in the valuation, and which one would have to add if one wished to determine the purchase value of an estate according to this valuation.

a. Objects that are not included in the valuation because their present value is not to be regarded as permanent:

1. The higher value given to an estate through an extraordinary, not yet prevailing cultivation, and through marling.

2. The yield from the timber, insofar as the annual net income -- through own consumption, growth, or sale -- exceeds the ground rent, or the yield that the forest land could give as arable land or meadow.

3. The additional value that an estate has through new buildings, compared to another estate of the same kind whose buildings are of medium age and in a maintained state of repair.

b. Objects whose entire value is not contained in the valuation because it cannot be ascertained from the assessment register:

1. Outstanding meadows, insofar as their value is greater than the valuation states for meadows of the first class.

2. Excellent pastures, insofar as through permanent grazing they give a higher use than the valuation -- which presupposes a conversion to arable land or meadows -- states.

3. The lowlands that first acquired a value after the assessment through drainage.

On the other hand, the valuation is built on the assumption that the buildings of the estate are of medium age and in a maintained state of repair, and that the firewood necessary for the farm is available on the estate itself, for which reason only the purchase price of wood, but no hauling costs, have been charged for the wood of the farm.

For estates where these assumptions do not apply, the diminished value they thereby receive must be deducted from the yield that the valuation gives, and this must happen not only in the determination of the purchase value but also in the calculation of the pledge value.

The hufe-standard, by designating the size of the gross yield, provides an excellent measure for the size of the buildings and the demand for firewood.

The costs of erecting new buildings naturally depend on the building style itself and on the timber prices of the region, and cannot be the same everywhere.

Let us assume that at medium timber prices the erection of entirely new, well-built buildings costs 5,000 Thalers per hufe, and let us set the duration of the buildings on average at 150 years: then the annual depreciation or diminution in value amounts to 5,000/150 equals 33.3 Thalers per hufe.

If we now divide the buildings by their age into three classes: the value of the buildings is:

First class, from 10 to 50 years, average 25 years: 5,000 minus 25 times 33.3 equals 5,000 minus 833 equals 4,167 Thalers.

Second class, from 50 to 100 years, average 75 years: 5,000 minus 75 times 33.3 equals 5,000 minus 2,500 equals 2,500 Thalers.

Third class, from 100 to 150 years, average 125 years: 5,000 minus 125 times 33.3 equals 5,000 minus 4,167 equals 833 Thalers.

The value of the buildings further depends on the condition in which they have been maintained, and whether they require large repair costs to put them in a maintained state.

Then it must furthermore be taken into account whether the buildings on the estate are sufficient for the ordinary state of cultivation, or whether a capital must still be spent on the construction of the missing buildings. If, however, the buildings were formerly large enough and can only no longer hold the grain because the harvests have increased through marling and so forth, this cannot be counted as a deficiency, since the increased yield has also not been counted in the valuation.

Now, in order to prevent the danger that could arise from the application of our valuation to estates with poor buildings, I would propose that on all estates whose buildings belong by age to the third class, or are not in a maintained state, or are not sufficient for the size of the fields, 1,000 Thalers of mortgage bonds per hufe fewer be granted than the valuation would otherwise have dictated. To allow subdivisions here seems to me on one hand dangerous, since all building costs are usually estimated disproportionately low, and on the other hand also unnecessary, since if the restoration of the buildings to a maintained state is possible at small cost, this can be done by every estate owner before he enters the association.

The complete lack of firewood lowers, in my opinion, the pledge value of an estate, and with it the sum of the mortgage bonds to be granted, also by 1,000 Thalers per hufe.

Here, however, subdivisions can be permitted without danger, and for an estate that has, for example, three-tenths of its firewood needs on its own land, only 7/10 times 1,000 equals 300 Thalers per hufe would be deducted from the pledge value. It is understood that this deduction is to be made only for that portion of the hufe-standard to which a value has been attributed in the calculation.

We have in the calculation of the yield from land and soil carefully excluded all objects whose value was not permanent, and it would be highly desirable if we could also proceed here according to this principle, and did not need to take into consideration two such changeable objects as firewood and buildings.

In the calculation of the net yield of one hufe, these buildings have been regarded as connected with the land, and no interest has been deducted for the capital value of the buildings. The stated net yield therefore still consists of two different parts: namely, the interest on the value of the buildings, and the actual land rent. For soil of lower grades, the yield barely covers the interest on the buildings, and the land rent then vanishes entirely. This soil therefore has a pledge value only when it is furnished with good buildings; but if the buildings are lacking or are in an unusable condition, then the soil itself can offer no security for the pledge value granted to the estate.

The consideration of the condition of the buildings can then be dispensed with when one takes only the pure land rent as the measure of the pledge value. But then the soil assessed at over 130 square rods is excluded, and the pledge value of the better soil is also so greatly reduced that the introduction of the credit system would then perhaps not be possible at all -- at least it would be without benefit for most estate owners.

It therefore seems more expedient to allow a deduction when the buildings are unsatisfactory than to exclude the buildings from the pledge value generally even when they are in good condition.

The experience of recent times has shown that in the sale of some estates only the buildings were paid for, while the land was thrown in for nothing. For the present, therefore, the consideration of the condition of the buildings, and a deduction from the pledge value for the defective state of the same, will have to take place; but it is possible and even probable that after 30 years the real value and yield of the soil will have increased through enhanced cultivation to the point that the soil itself, even without buildings, has the value that our valuation now assigns to it with the buildings. In this long period it could be adequately observed whether dropping the consideration of defective buildings and lacking firewood still involves any danger or not, and this would then have to be decided by a majority vote of the estate owners. But since no unilateral changes may later be made without the consent of the capitalists, it would be necessary for the knighthood to reserve to itself the decision on this point.

It is furthermore to be noted that the capitalist would not actually be endangered even if these considerations were dropped already now; for against an estate with poor buildings there is another with new buildings, against an estate without wood another that has it in abundance, and since for these no higher pledge value is calculated, the capitalist would find in these estates the collateral that the others cannot give him. All the more, however, will every individual estate owner have to watch over this matter.

In the case of estates that have new buildings but lack firewood, or that have poor buildings but possess enough construction timber for the buildings to be rebuilt from it, the deficiency of the one object is compensated by the greater value of the other, and in these cases no deduction need take place.

Section 27.

Determination of the average yield of an estate in relation to its pledge value.

Now that the foregoing investigations allow the net yield of an estate to be determined, the question arises: in what ratio does the average yield stand to the pledge value of the estate, or for how many Thalers of yield may a mortgage bond of 100 Thalers at 5 percent interest be granted?

Since the introduction of annuity payments or irredeemable debts works so beneficially for the whole, one must on one hand wish that the pledge value be extended to near the purchase value. On the other hand, however, the pledge value shall be unshakable and beyond loss. It must therefore be so low that, first, the burdens of a war do not bring the purchase price of the estates down to the pledge value; second, whatever restrictions foreign states may place on the grain trade, the interest on the pledge value can still be borne by the estates; and finally, third, that given the great inequality that can arise in the estate's income through crop failure, the interest on the mortgage bonds can still be paid in every year.

If one wishes to combine both considerations as much as possible, one will, in my opinion, have to take as the basis for a mortgage bond of 100 Thalers at 5 percent interest a monetary yield of 8 Thalers, and accordingly grant an estate a pledge value of 100 Thalers for every 8 Thalers of yield that it gives according to our valuation.

Since our valuation excludes and does not encompass various objects of use, but these objects nonetheless serve as collateral for the mortgage bonds, one can safely assume that the pledge value determined in this way is, in general -- but not in every individual case -- backed by an income of 9 percent as collateral.

For Estate S, our valuation yields a return of 1,778 Thalers. The pledge value of this estate is therefore 1,778/8 times 100 equals 22,225 Thalers. This estate was sold at the time of the lowest estate prices for 32,000 Thalers.

The buildings of the estate were of medium age but not well maintained. A considerable capital was therefore needed to put them in a maintained state, and only after this capital has been spent does the estate have the pledge value of 22,225 Thalers. For the condition of the estate as it was at the time of purchase, however, 1,000 Thalers per hufe would be deducted; this makes for 2,267 minus 74 equals 2,193 scheffels: 3,655 Thalers, and the pledge value then remains 18,670 Thalers.

Estate N. According to the valuation the yield is 5,656 Thalers. This gives a pledge value of 5,656/8 times 100 equals 70,700 Thalers.

The buildings were not in a maintained state; the abundance of timber will, however, approximately offset this deficiency.

The estate was sold in the year 1812 for 110,000 Thalers in gold, or approximately 103,000 Thalers N.Z.W.

Estate L. The yield according to our valuation is 1,604 Thalers. This gives a pledge value of 1,604/8 times 100 equals 20,050 Thalers.

The buildings were, however, not in a maintained state and not complete for the estate.

Therefore 1,000 Thalers per hufe is deducted, making for 2 hufes 270 scheffels: 2,450 Thalers.

The estate had approximately half of its firewood needs; for the missing half, 500 Thalers per hufe is deducted, making 1,225 Thalers.

Total deducted: 3,675 Thalers.

Pledge value remaining: 16,375 Thalers.

This estate was purchased in the year 1809 for 32,000 Thalers; at the time of the lowest estate prices, however, it was resold for 24,000 Thalers.

For these three estates, therefore, the pledge value determined according to our valuation amounts to almost exactly two-thirds of the lowest purchase price, which will correspond approximately to half of the current purchase price.

If one assumes that at that time purchases were made at 6 percent, then between the purchase price and the pledge value -- which in general will be covered by 9 percent yield -- there is a precise agreement.

I have applied these valuation principles to six further estates that I know well, and find that for two of these estates, whose state of cultivation has changed greatly, the actual yield is considerably higher than what the valuation gives; for the others, however, the two agree so precisely that I would know of no other value to state for them than the one that follows from the valuation.

It is undoubtedly at this moment a pressing need for Mecklenburg that valuation principles be presented that:

1) do not cause great costs in their application;

2) exclude all arbitrariness; and

3) never state the pledge value so high that it is not fully secured by the value of the estate.

Since for the circle of my observations the principles here presented fulfill these requirements, I felt an inner obligation to submit my views to public examination and criticism.

For this examination a twofold standpoint would be necessary: first, in order to test the correctness of the theory itself, it would have to be applied to estates that are, by general opinion, correctly assessed, that have not been significantly altered in cultivation, and that on the whole do not significantly deviate from the most ordinary conditions; second, in order to ascertain whether the application of this theory in no case states the pledge value of an estate too high, one would have to choose estates that one considers to be too highly assessed, that have very poor soil, large stretches of low-lying pasture, poor buildings, and so forth -- and in general choose those estates that, through many disadvantageous circumstances relative to their hufe-standard, have the least value.

Since the lease price is less fluctuating and stands more in proportion to the real value of the estate than the purchase price, one will do best to take as the basis the average lease sum that the estate gives or can give, but to deduct from it not only the usual annual deductions but also all the expenditures that fall upon the owner over a series of years, such as war costs, depreciation of buildings, and so forth.
