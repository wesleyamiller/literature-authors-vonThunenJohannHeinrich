# Views on the Establishment of an Agricultural Institute in Mecklenburg

*Original: Ansichten über die Errichtung eines landwirthschaftlichen Instituts in Mecklenburg (1831)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

In order to pass judgment on whether the establishment of an agricultural institute in Mecklenburg is advisable or not, one must first pose the two following questions:

1) Whether the academies and institutes already existing in Germany are not sufficient for the number of those who seek instruction; and

2) Whether we may hope to achieve the purpose more completely in Mecklenburg than is done there?

In the academies and institutes at Möglin, Hohenheim, Schleissheim, and Idstein, Germany already possesses four educational institutions that are staffed with excellent teachers and in which all the auxiliary sciences of agriculture are taught. Lectures on agriculture are also given at most German universities, and in particular this science is taught at Jena and Göttingen by scholars who are well versed in practical farming, and its application is demonstrated on excursions to neighboring estates.

The aforementioned institutes are not overcrowded, and any Mecklenburger can gain admission there without difficulty. The establishment of an institute in one's own fatherland would indeed spare the Mecklenburger the costs of a journey; but the subsidies that the institute would require are always greater than this saving, and for the state no increase in national income would result from it.

There is thus no reason yet that could call for the establishment of an institute among us, and we must therefore turn to the second question posed above:

"Whether we may hope to achieve the purpose more completely than is done at the other institutes?"

If we look at the effectiveness of the existing institutes, we find the striking phenomenon that these institutes are attended by scarcely 100 students, while the number of students at all German universities rises to 16,000.

Now the number of farmers, even excluding those who manage only small estates, is greater than the number of all civil servants, physicians, theologians, and so forth taken together — and if nevertheless such a disproportion exists in the number of students (which was not balanced even at the time when the two most famous agriculturalists of Germany, Thaer and Schwerz, stood at the head of two institutes), then the verdict of the public seems to lie herein: that the scientific study of agriculture has little value for the farmer.

If one considers, on the other hand, how undeniably useful the natural sciences and mathematics are to the farmer, how only knowledge of these sciences can make clear to him the reasons for his practical procedures, and if one has had the opportunity to observe how eager the striving of so many young men is to learn agriculture scientifically, then one is compelled to suspect other causes for that disproportion.

Agriculture cannot be learned in a purely theoretical manner. The practice of it requires knowledge that can only be acquired through observation and exercise. These practical skills are, however, indispensable in the operation of agriculture, and the lack of them can never be replaced by the greatest scientific education. For those who are not in possession of these skills — which can either not be acquired at all, or only very imperfectly, at the existing institutes — the scientific instruction loses, precisely because those skills are the foundation of the entire edifice, the greater part of its value; and the late Thaer himself repeatedly declared that only those who already possessed practical knowledge of agriculture could derive real benefit from his lectures.

The learning of practical agriculture, however, presents great difficulties for the young educated man. Only few farmers occupy themselves with the instruction of young people, and the conditions under which this occurs are often so off-putting for distinguished and wealthy young men that they often give up the acquisition of practical knowledge entirely, and in the correct presentiment that without this knowledge attendance at the agricultural institutes is for the most part useless to them, they do not attend these either.

When they later take over their estates, they must acquire the practical knowledge through their own experience and mistakes at great cost; or they fall into a dependence on their inspectors, which necessarily sours their inclination toward agriculture and gives their activity another, often unproductive direction.

The state seems to me to be above all interested in winning such young men for agriculture — men whose sphere of influence is so great and who, when educated both practically and scientifically for agriculture, can exert so beneficial an influence on the cultivation of the country — and in facilitating for them the means of obtaining this education.

The sons of small farmers do not lack practical knowledge; but apart from the difficulty of meeting the expenses that attendance at an institute entails, they mostly lack the school knowledge without which the scientific lectures of the teachers cannot be understood. For this class of farmers, in my view, better school instruction must first precede before scientific instruction in agriculture can be of use to them.

For the increase of their knowledge, and thus also of their prosperity, the establishment of model farms seems to me to be the most effective means at the present moment. But as easy as it is to recognize the deficiencies of our peasant and smallholder farms, it is nevertheless difficult to set up an ideal for them. The appropriate organization of such farms is far less scientifically developed than that of larger estates. I therefore fear that every model farm of this kind arising from mere theory will entirely miss its purpose, and it is my conviction that the model for it must be taken from other countries — where small farms have been conducted in an exemplary manner for centuries, and where through them the land has reached a high level of cultivation, wealth, and population — and that only those modifications required by differences in climate, soil, and commercial conditions may be introduced.

Accordingly, the attendance of an agricultural institute is useful only for educated young men, and since it cannot be changed that the stay at the institutes is connected with considerable expense — it is possible only for well-to-do, educated young men.

For such young men, however, the difficulty in obtaining a complete agricultural education lies not in the lack of educational institutions, but, as stated above, in the lack of opportunity to learn the practice of agriculture — and the poorly attended state of the existing agricultural institutes accordingly seems to be caused not so much by the opinion that scientific knowledge is superfluous for the farmer, but rather by the difficulty of acquiring practical knowledge.

From this point of view, the question of whether it is advisable to establish an agricultural institute in Mecklenburg is reduced to the question:

"Is it possible to give an institute such an arrangement that the practice can be learned simultaneously with the theory of agriculture?"

Since the practice of agriculture can be learned almost only through direct participation in the management of the farm, and since this participation becomes ever more difficult the greater the number of learners on one and the same estate, the solution of the problem lies in overcoming this difficulty.

The following proposals, which I submit for examination to the esteemed committee, are therefore primarily directed toward removing these difficulties, and are to be regarded as an attempt at how instruction in the theory of agriculture can be combined with instruction in practice.

## Plan for the Organization of an Agricultural Institute

The students must have reached the age of 18 when they wish to be admitted to the institute. The course of study lasts two years. Every student is obligated to keep a work journal in which he daily records every task undertaken on the estate and the number of people and draft animals employed in these tasks.

This compels him, as long as the field work lasts, to visit the places where work is being done at least twice a day, and thus to observe the course of the farm's operations. He then keeps another journal in which he describes the agricultural operations he has seen that day, and daily writes down the observations he has made, as well as everything he has learned in the practical management of the farm.

As soon as a field task — for example, hoeing the seed furrows, mowing wheat, hauling manure, and so forth — is completed, the student compiles from the work journal how many men, women, and horses have been occupied with these tasks, compares the total with the size of the area hoed or mowed, the number of cartloads hauled, and so forth, and in this way arrives at the knowledge so indispensable to the farmer, yet so difficult to obtain: what quantity of work one may expect from the laboring forces.

A great deficiency of the agricultural institutes is that the learning of manual tasks — sowing, hoeing, and so forth — is so greatly hindered or even made impossible there. For the students cannot be permitted to practice sowing, hoeing, and so forth at their own discretion in the field: the institute's farm, which is also supposed to be a model in the cultivation of the fields, would — apart from the great pecuniary loss — otherwise only present a picture of disorder.

In my opinion, however, this deficiency can be remedied without too great an expenditure by giving each student who desires it a plot of land of about 100 square rods, on which he performs all tasks, from hoeing and sowing to cleaning the grain, under the guidance of an expert.

If the nature of the soil permitted giving the students plots lying next to one another, of equal soil quality and equal fertility, this would at the same time become a most instructive experimental field, where the influence of a more or less good cultivation on the growth of the crop would first be shown to the eye, and ultimately, at the threshing of the crop, would be expressed in numbers.

In the second year of their stay at the institute, the students would then have to draw up a plan each day — just as if they already had the direction of the farm — for the work to be undertaken on the following day, with precise specification of how many people and horses are to be employed at each task, and submit this in writing to the director. The director then tells each student, with explanation of the reasons, what is deficient or inexpedient in his plan. This must necessarily lead the student to reflect on the course of the farm's operations, and since such a correction of his views is repeated daily throughout an entire summer, such a preliminary exercise for his future profession — farm management — must advance him further than several years of managing on his own. In these drafts, the degree of knowledge and judgment of the individual students would at the same time be clearly expressed, and from this would arise the spirit of competition that spurs greater activity and effort and ensures success — whereas the lack of that competitive spirit so often causes private instruction to remain fruitless.

That the director of the institute must at the same time be the director of the farm, that he and not the inspector must order the work, follows already from this. That in these arrangements, which are to be a subject of reflection and instruction for the students, a perfect consistency must prevail, and without this would only serve as an object of ridicule for the students, likewise follows from this.

Since, however, a perfect management of the farm claims almost the entire undivided energy and attention of a man, the director must not be burdened with holding regular lectures during the summer. The instruction he gives in summer is therefore limited to the following:

1) Each morning the director assembles the students around him, communicates his arrangements to them, tells them what to pay particular attention to in the tasks occurring that day, and explains the reasons why the work must be done in this way and not otherwise. Since with the progress of the season different tasks constantly arise, this discursive instruction, though fragmentary, will in the course of the year touch upon almost all subjects of agriculture.

2) In the incidental conversations during the day, the director answers the questions directed to him by the students and gives clarification on what has remained doubtful or unclear to individual students.

3) In the evening he receives from the older students the drafts of the arrangements for the following day.

Besides the director, the following must also be appointed:

1) A teacher of the natural sciences;
2) A teacher of mathematics;
3) A teacher of national economy and public finance;
4) An accountant.

Were it possible to obtain teachers of the natural sciences and of mathematics who at the same time possessed knowledge of agriculture, it might perhaps be advisable that the instruction of these teachers, like that of the director, should in summer be merely fragmentary and always directed to the subjects occurring in the practical farm operations, so as to concentrate the entire attention of the students on one point.

In any case, however, the students must not be too much confined to the room during the summer, and they must therefore not hear more than two lectures daily.

But just as the summer should be devoted primarily to practice, so the winter should be devoted particularly to the study of the sciences — which is also, for the educated practical farmer in later years, the course of activity assigned to him by nature itself.

In my view, lectures must be given on the following subjects:

1) Arable farming and animal husbandry, or agricultural science in its full scope;
2) Forestry;
3) Natural history;
4) Botany;
5) Physics;
6) Chemistry;
7) Pure and applied geometry;
8) Literal calculation and algebra;
9) Statics and mechanics;
10) Rural architecture;
11) National economy;
12) Public finance.

Concerning the usefulness of these sciences for the farmer, as well as the purpose and direction of the lectures, I permit myself the following remarks.

**Forestry.** In order to satisfy the entire wood requirements of an estate, 10 percent, and with poor forest management 15 to 20 percent, of the entire estate area must be devoted to timber cultivation. The net yield of the land depends in forestry, almost even more than in arable farming, on the appropriate arrangement and management, and with faulty practice soon sinks to zero or below zero.

Forestry knowledge has not yet been very widely disseminated in Mecklenburg, and the management of private woodlands here is generally still very deficient. With the growing population and the gradual disappearance of the primeval forests, however, the price of wood will probably rise in the future, and thereby timber cultivation will gain an increasing importance.

All of this calls for promoting the spread of forestry knowledge and combining instruction in forestry with that in agriculture.

Perhaps it will be considered appropriate to combine a forestry institute with the agricultural one, since agriculture and forestry require almost the same auxiliary sciences, and the teachers appointed for these would then suffice for both institutes. In that case, only a separate teacher for forestry would need to be appointed, who would then give a special course for those devoted to agriculture. Should this not take place, however, it would be highly desirable for the director to possess sufficient forestry knowledge to be able to give, with the aid of a good textbook, a condensed overview of this science, with particular regard to what can immediately be applied in Mecklenburg.

*When the interest on the value of the timber stock amounts to more than the value of the annual timber growth, the yield of the land itself is negative, and this case will not infrequently occur in the high forests subjected to selective cutting.*

**Natural history.** In the teaching of natural history, one would have to limit oneself in entomology to knowledge of the insects harmful to agriculture and forestry, and in mineralogy to the types of stones and minerals occurring in Mecklenburg.

**Botany.** In botany, the instruction would first and principally have to be directed to the plants cultivated for agricultural purposes, then to the plants growing wild in Mecklenburg, but must not enter the vast field of studying foreign plants.

**Physics and chemistry.** These sciences must not only be taught with constant reference to their application in agriculture and in the technical trades usefully combined with farming, but must also be practically applied wherever an opportunity presents itself. For example, marl, pond and meadow muck, the field to which these substances are applied, the water used for irrigating the meadows and for watering the livestock, and every soil on which the condition of the crops presents unusual phenomena, would have to be chemically analyzed. I consider the repeated, careful, and precise examination of meadow muck to be of particular importance; for on the one hand we recognize its effectiveness — which, as it seems to me, depends on the presence of as yet unexplored substances — almost only from its results; and on the other hand, with continued experiments it may perhaps be possible to discover some method of treatment, or to find some not too costly agent, by which the meadow muck could be brought into fermentation and transformed into actual manure. Through such a discovery an immeasurable treasure of fertilizer would be gained, and agricultural production could be raised to an incomparably higher level.

**Geometry.** The students' exercises in surveying, under the supervision of the teacher, can at the same time serve another economic purpose if they are directed toward determining the size of the area occupied by each individual crop. In any case, however, the size of the area devoted to each product in the institute's farm must be determined and established not, as in private farms, by the quantity of seed sown or by fluctuating estimates, but by precise measurements — because otherwise the students cannot arrive at any definite knowledge of the yield of the field.

**Literal calculation and algebra.** In teaching these subjects, the instructor should constantly endeavor to demonstrate their application in the practical operation of agriculture. The cases where these calculations are useful, indeed necessary, for the practical farmer to proceed correctly are frequent. But since this is seldom discussed in agricultural writings, and still less is there a book in which applications of these calculations have been compiled, the director would have to seize every opportunity to provide the mathematics teacher with material for such applications.

**Statics and mechanics.** In teaching these subjects, particular attention must be paid to the tools and machines in use in agriculture and in the associated technical trades, and the suitability of each must be tested according to mathematical principles. Nothing has promoted the prosperity and thereby indirectly the civilization of peoples more than the invention of useful machines, by which the same human effort is rewarded with a greater product. Especially important and successful have been the inventions in machinery in the factories, and it is impossible to foresee where the discoveries, now emerging at an accelerating rate, will lead. Far less has machinery accomplished in agriculture, and while the nature of the subject does not permit as extensive an application of it as in factories, there is no doubt that great advances can still be made here as well. There are many who feel drawn by inclination and natural talent to improvements and inventions in machinery, and when these are supported by knowledge of mathematical principles, they undoubtedly arrive all the sooner at new discoveries. But even he who does not feel such a calling must, as a farmer, know the construction, operation, and use of the machines in current use; which is why I consider instruction in mechanics to be an essential requirement of the agricultural school.

**Rural architecture.** The value of the buildings on estates with superior soil amounts to at least one-third to one-quarter of the total value of the estate, and on estates with poor soil often exceeds half the estate's value. The capital invested in buildings moves toward its destruction more slowly or more rapidly, depending on whether the maintenance of the buildings is attended to with more or less expert knowledge and attentiveness. From this alone it is apparent how useful a knowledge of architecture is for estate owners, but this becomes even more evident in the construction of new buildings, where the lack of knowledge frequently causes a doubling of costs and often results in an impractical arrangement, as well as a lack of durability of the structure. Since so much in architecture is grounded in mathematical principles, the teacher of mathematics will have to undertake the instruction in this subject; and even if he should not be able to teach the practical part of architecture, he will nonetheless, with the aid of a good textbook, be able to give the main rules and to draw up cost estimates, and thus be of use to his students — although the instruction of a practical master builder would always be far more desirable.

**National economy,** or the science of the nature and conditions of national prosperity.

Since it is unusual to regard this science as one that is useful or even necessary for the farmer, the proposal to include it in agricultural instruction requires a further development of the reasons.

The task that the farmer has to accomplish is this: to draw the sustainably highest net yield from the land. This net yield is indeed connected with the gross yield or the size of the harvest, but by no means in direct proportion. The harvest can be increased:

1) Through increased cultivation of the soil; improvement of the soil mixture; enrichment of the field by the application of marl and muck; deepening of the topsoil where there is a sufficient supply of manure; use of other harvesting methods, for example the use of the sickle instead of the scythe, and so forth;

2) Through the introduction of another farming system, for example by now using the hitherto unproductive fallow for the cultivation of useful plants, or by introducing stall-feeding instead of pasturing the livestock, and so forth.

Every increase in the harvest achieved in this way is, however, purchased by an expenditure of capital and labor. Whether these improvements increase or diminish the net yield depends solely on whether the increase that the harvest thereby receives exceeds or falls short of the increased expenditure of capital and labor in value. This in turn depends on the ratio in which wages and the rate of interest stand to the price of the products. Since this ratio differs so greatly in different countries, and even in one and the same country at different times, it follows that in every country — consistency of farming being presupposed — the character of agriculture must express itself in a distinctive way, and that in the course of time, with the advances of civil society — which directly affect that ratio — a gradual but constant change in the organization of farming must take place. It is further explained from this why the transplantation of a farming system from one country to another has so often — as the recent period shows in so many examples — had an unfortunate result. The farmer who does not recognize the influence and importance of that ratio can — even when he is properly equipped with knowledge of the natural sciences — strictly speaking, farm correctly only in the country where he learned farming. As soon as he leaves that place, he is, like the rudderless ship, given over to chance.

But it is not only in improvements and the choice of the farming system that the level of prices exerts such an influence; rather, in every farming operation — if carried out consistently — the degree of perfection with which the work, such as hoeing, harrowing, threshing the grain, gathering the potatoes, and so forth, must be performed depends on it. The price of agricultural products is therefore — when the rate of interest and wages remain unchanged — the actual regulator of the entire economic organization.

Now the price of grain and other agricultural products is by no means arbitrary or accidental, but is itself bound by definite laws — and the knowledge of these laws, as well as of those that regulate the level of the rate of interest and of wages, is taught by national economy.

From those laws, the farmer cannot, to be sure, determine the future price of products a priori and express it in figures, since for this, in addition to the knowledge of those laws, a precise overview of the production and consumption of all peoples standing in commercial connection with one another would be required — an overview that lies beyond the reach of a single person. The farmer will therefore always have to base his calculations on the average prices derived from experience; but the farmer versed in the principles of national economy will be able to judge what influence individual changes in civil society will have on the change in average prices.

Every new discovery in manufacturing, every new tax imposed in one's own country or in foreign states, every expansion of factories in one's own country, as well as every facilitation or impediment of communication with other peoples, brings about a change in the ratio between the prices of the various products, and makes the restriction of one and the expansion of another production advantageous.

The farmer must therefore survey the relationship in which he stands to the whole of civil society, in order to be able to arrange his farming in a timely manner — that is, in a way that is at the same time advantageous for himself.

To be sure, the farmer can, even without knowing those laws, in many cases infer from the change in the average price of the various products, with some probability, the future price, and arrange his farming accordingly — as this is also in fact the regulator of the customary farming. But a rise or fall in prices produces in the average price derived from a long period, say the last 30 years, so slight a change in the first years that it is scarcely noticeable and cannot prompt a change in farming. A farm regulated according to this principle must therefore always lag behind the timely farming by about a decade.

In other cases, however, the earlier average price cannot serve as the norm for the future price at all. Thus there are individual products, for example fine wool, whose price can remain in steady decline for half a century, because their use value, and with it the market price, stood far above the production cost, and the increase in supply proceeds only slowly.

There are, on the other hand, other products, for example oak bark and cowhides, whose price continues to rise with the increase in population.

Here, therefore, a calculation based on the earlier average price must always lead astray.

But this principle becomes wholly inadequate when the question arises whether the cultivation of a plant not previously produced in the country will be profitable in the future, since here the average price is not given by experience.

Now if on the one hand the principle of regulating the farm according to the earlier average prices of products is far from always sufficient, it is on the other hand wholly inadmissible to make changes in the farm based on the market price of products of one year or a few years; for in the fluctuating market price there is absolutely no guarantee of the continuation of that price.

The farmer is thus continually referred back to his own reflection, and this will undoubtedly lead to a more important result the more familiar he is with the laws that decide the subject of his reflection. The farmer must be able to recognize, from the position of his fatherland relative to other states, which products this country can durably supply to the world market at the lowest prices — in which it can therefore withstand the competition with all other countries — and only on the production of such products may permanent arrangements of the farm be based.

But if this difficult task is to be even somewhat adequately solved, a serious and deep study of national economy is necessary.

The study of national economy will further lead the farmer to the conviction that, transplanted to a country foreign to him, he must not begin with the improvement of the farming customary there before he has acquired a precise knowledge of the average prices of products, the costs of the various types of labor, the amount of taxes, and all mercantile and political conditions. Only then, when he is in possession of this knowledge, will he subject the existing farming to a free critique, and if he finds it deficient, undertake improvements with success; but if he finds it suited to the conditions of the country — that is, consistent — which occurs far more often than is commonly believed — he will save himself the futile costs of a supposed improvement.

It is almost impossible that the farmer who does not recognize the inner connection between the level of wages, the rate of interest, the land rent, and the prices of products with the form of farming can successfully introduce a new farming system in any country unknown to him.

But since up to now almost only the natural-scientific side of agriculture has been developed, while the national-economic side has scarcely been touched,* there lies in the resulting deficient state of agricultural science, in my conviction, a principal reason why the attempts to introduce new farming systems have so often failed, even when undertaken by thinking farmers who were educated both practically and theoretically.

*Thaer did earn the merit of having treated several economic subjects with his characteristic clarity and insight, and particularly in his guide to agricultural business science, of having shown their connection with agriculture; but his system of agriculture itself still entirely lacks the national-economic foundation. Up to now, only Professor Schulz in Jena, in his work "On the Economic Foundation of Industrial Science," has articulated the necessity of grounding agriculture in national economy.*

When one day national economy has taken its rightful place alongside the natural sciences as a fundamental science of agriculture, and the textbooks of agriculture are so imbued with the principles of national economy that they recommend no farming system, no agricultural operation, no improvement without indicating how the price of products, labor costs, land rent, and so forth affect it — how a change in these factors reduces the advantage of the proposed measure to zero or below zero, and expressing this, based on data drawn from experience, in figures — then the study of scientific agriculture will no longer lead to failures but to certain successes, and the farmer educated through this study will be given a great advantage over the merely practical farmer.

For the reasons developed here, the teaching of national economy must by no means be absent from a newly established agricultural institute, and the less familiar the young men are with this science, the less instruction they can obtain from agricultural writings, the more thorough and comprehensive must the instruction in it be.

**Public finance.** These sciences stand in close connection with national economy and are to be regarded as an application of it to the life of the state. They are dispensable for the farmer as such; but they are indispensable for the future representative of the country — and many of the young men who attend the institute will have this vocation. But even apart from this purpose, the study of these sciences serves the farmer, as it does every educated man, to broaden his horizon, to lift his gaze from the narrow circle of his own occupation to the general — to the life of the state — and to displace the judgments about government measures that are so often mistaken due to ignorance, and to put correct judgments in their place.

**Veterinary science.** In this so difficult, itself still so little developed science, half-knowledge — which is all that can be attained at an agricultural school — seems to me even more harmful than complete ignorance, and so I consider the teaching of this science to be superfluous.

Since the course of study lasts two years, but the arrangement must be made so that new students can enter at Easter each year, a division of the students into two classes is necessary. To one class then belong the younger, newly entered students, to the other those who have already been at the institute for a year.

The order in which the lectures are to be given is as follows:

**A. For the younger students:**

1) In summer: Botany; Pure and applied geometry;

2) In winter: Arable farming; Physics; Literal calculation and algebra; National economy.

**B. For the older students:**

1) In summer: Natural history; Statics and mechanics;

2) In winter: Animal husbandry; Forestry; Chemistry; Rural architecture; Public finance.

In this way, neither teachers nor students are overburdened with work, which must in any case be avoided. If both still have time remaining, they can fill it usefully by giving and receiving private instruction at their own discretion.

In this plan, the drawback arises that the teacher of national economy is entirely unoccupied in summer. Perhaps, however, an arrangement could be made whereby an academic teacher would give lectures at the university in summer and at the institute in winter. But perhaps cameralists who are training for future positions as officials will also take part in the school, and for these the teacher of national economy could then lecture on the remaining branches of political science, such as natural law, constitutional law, politics, and so forth, in summer.

**The farm of the estate on which the institute is situated** shall be a model farm — that is, it shall be able to withstand comparison with the best Mecklenburg farms in the appropriateness of its arrangement, in the quality of its field cultivation, in the orderliness with which it is operated, and so forth; but it must not seek to set up farming forms and methods from other countries as models. It shall not teach what is daring and uncertain, but only what has been proven by experience, what can be carried out without risk of failure — and therefore, in the first years, the farms of the most excellent Mecklenburg farmers must themselves serve as its model. Its purpose is not to expand science, but to teach and communicate what is already known.*

*On the other hand, the application of the natural sciences to agriculture would directly lead to new discoveries. It would be most desirable to obtain a teacher for these sciences who applied them to agriculture with the same spirit as Doctor Sprengel in Göttingen.*

Equally, the conducting of experiments must not be the purpose of this farm; these would only scatter the time and attention of the teachers and divert them from their main purpose: instruction. However, individual outstanding students who feel the need within themselves to question nature about as yet unexplored subjects could be permitted to conduct experiments.

**A particular consideration deserves the accounting system.**

The purpose of the agricultural enterprise is to draw the highest sustainable net yield from an estate. The net yield as a whole is already apparent at the end of the accounting year from the total of the money paid out and the comparison of the value of the inventory and stores at the end and the beginning of the year, and thus requires no actual accounting.

But since the estate's yield arises from the cultivation of manifold crops and the keeping of several types of livestock, individual branches of the farm can produce losses while others yield a profit, without this being visible in that overview of the net yield as a whole.

A perfect farm, in which not only some but all branches of cultivation must yield the highest net return, and in which no loss-producing branch of cultivation may be found, therefore presupposes a completely precise and definite knowledge of the net yield of the individual branches of the farm. For this, however, it is necessary that the costs of the laboring forces and the quantity of labor expended on each branch of cultivation be determined — and this can only be achieved through very precise, detailed bookkeeping.

For the rational farmer who, at the beginning of his practical career, does not wish to imitate the customary farming but wishes to manage his farm according to his own judgment, this knowledge is so indispensable that, in the absence of it, he inevitably heads toward his ruin, even if he possesses all other practical and scientific knowledge of agriculture; for with all the consistency of reasoning and calculation, an account kept with incorrect figures will always give false results.

And precisely this most indispensable knowledge cannot, given the current state of the science and of instruction, be acquired by the aspiring farmer either at all or only very imperfectly. Many farmers already make a secret of the grain yield of their fields; nearly all make a secret of the monetary yield of their estate. But even if this were not the case, the practical farmer cannot as a rule be in possession of that knowledge about the cultivation costs of each individual branch of agriculture, because this can and should only emerge from accounting — and so the young man who learns farming from a practical farmer remains almost entirely in the dark on this point.

From agricultural writings, the aspiring farmer can also acquire such instruction only imperfectly. In the books one does find a great many calculations of this kind; but for the most part these are the product of a superficial overview drawn from memory, and they have so little reliability that their authors frequently do not believe in them themselves — which is shown by the fact that they do not bring their farming into agreement with the results of their calculations.

Another portion of the calculations is indeed drawn from actual accounting by trustworthy men; but these calculations are then usually communicated incompletely and give only results in which the factual and the judgment of the authors are already so mixed that the two can no longer be separated. But since the principles according to which the calculation is to be formed from the data given by the bookkeeping have not yet been scientifically established, and are themselves still a subject of discussion, quite different results can be drawn from the same data depending on the different views of the authors — and such a calculation is basically only the pronouncement of an individual opinion that must always be subjected to scrutiny anew.

Only when the facts on which such calculations are built are communicated in full; when the conclusions and inferences that the author has drawn from them are so clearly presented and so decidedly separated from the facts that it becomes possible for the reader to judge the fallacy or truth of those conclusions — only then do such calculations have value for the worker of science, and only then can the student draw from them true instruction that guides him on his way and does not lead him astray. Works that provide calculations of this kind are, unfortunately, very rare.* The institute could become truly beneficial to the public if this deficiency in science and instruction were remedied through it.

*I know of only one such work in the agricultural literature. It is: "Farming Experiences" by Count von Podewils, 1801. The value of this work was not recognized by the author's contemporaries, and now it is almost entirely forgotten.*

To this end I permit myself the following additional proposals:

A dedicated accountant who possesses agricultural knowledge is appointed. He keeps the work journal, the grain accounts, the monetary accounts, keeps books on the use of hay and straw, and on everything that is necessary to calculate the yield and costs of the individual branches of the farm.

At the end of each quarter, these accounts are compiled by the various categories, and at the close of the accounting year the quarterly extracts are combined into a general overview.

From this, the costs that a day-laborer family, a team of horses, the household, and so forth have incurred are calculated; these costs, compared with the total number of working days performed, then show how much a day-laborer's working day, a team of horses, and so forth cost in the various periods.

From this, the costs of the work expended on the various cultivated crops and on the various branches of the farm — whose extent is known from the work journal — can then be calculated and expressed in money.

From the gross yield of each individual branch of cultivation, the costs expended on it — labor, grain, interest on invested capital, and so forth — being deducted, gives the surplus for that branch of cultivation.

Then the general cultivation costs — that is, those costs that cannot be allocated to any single branch, such as administrative costs, building maintenance costs, state taxes, and so forth — must be specially calculated.

The sum of the general cultivation costs, deducted from the sum of all surpluses that the individual branches of cultivation have yielded, gives the net yield of the entire estate.

The net yield as a whole is, according to the method first described, already known; and the comparison of the sums found for the estate's yield by the two different methods shows, through their greater or lesser agreement, the degree of accuracy that the calculation extended to the individual branches of cultivation has achieved. But one may always expect only an approximation, never a perfect agreement.

Besides the bookkeeping, the accountant also has the task of auditing the work journals kept by the students.

The director is spared the bookkeeping itself, but he must review the accounts so carefully that he can certify their correctness.

Furthermore, every student has the right at any time to inspect the books at the accountant's office and to satisfy himself of their correctness; or, if he finds deficiencies in them, to expose these publicly.

After the closing of all accounts for a year, the director communicates the results of them in detail in his lectures.

Then the accounts are printed and made available to the public.

Thereby the public is put in a position to judge the director's management of the farm, and so the director is subjected to a very salutary oversight.

But if the director possesses the confidence of the public, the publication of the accounts can be of very great benefit to the public itself. The practical farmers can thereby acquire the precise knowledge of the net yield of individual branches of cultivation that they can only rarely obtain for themselves, because of the great difficulty connected with a precise and comprehensive bookkeeping. From this knowledge follows directly the avoidance of all unprofitable branches of cultivation, and thus an increase in the estate's yield.

The institute's farm, which at its initial organization must take the better farms of the country as its model, can in this way in the course of time itself become the model for other farms, and so exert a most beneficial influence on practical agriculture in a wide circle — at least as far as the commercial and climatic conditions are similar.

**Correct appraisal principles for the valuation of landed estates** are of great importance for the private individual in the purchase or leasing of an estate, for governments in the leasing of domains, and especially in the imposition of new taxes. All appraisal principles adopted as the norm in the various states up to now, however, bear the stamp of great deficiency — and this is mainly because they are not based on a secure foundation drawn from experience. But how is one to establish correct appraisal principles as long as one cannot yet state, for a single estate, what share the individual components of the estate — arable land, meadows, pastures, woodland, and so forth — have in the total yield? For the properly managed estate, however, the average of the net yield over a series of years is the measure of its value itself. If now the accounts are kept so that from them, for the fields of different quality, for the meadows, the pastures, and so forth, the gross yield, the costs connected with their cultivation, and thus the net yield of the individual components of the estate can be derived: then in the average result of the accounts of several years lies the appraisal of the individual parts of the estate itself — and from this estate, as from a fixed point, one can proceed by analogy to other estates and thus arrive at general principles.

The accounts of the institute's estate could in this way, after the passage of several years, serve as the foundation for the development of appraisal principles, and thus address a keenly felt need.

Since the correct assessment of the value of an estate in purchasing or leasing it can be decisive for the farmer's fortunes throughout his entire life, the science of estate valuation would have to form a principal part of the instruction given by the director, and every student would have to, before leaving the institute, draw up a detailed appraisal of the value of the institute's estate and submit it to the director for correction and evaluation.

The officials employed in the administrative authorities need agricultural knowledge, since their business so frequently involves economic matters. This knowledge is not now required of them upon their appointment to office, and they must acquire it through their own performance of their duties.

But one day, sooner or later, the granting of office will be tied to the possession of the necessary knowledge — and if this natural demand has not yet been made, it is no doubt because the difficulty of acquiring this knowledge has been taken into consideration.

The agricultural institute would remove this difficulty, and so it could attain a new, great sphere of influence.

Tellow, December 1830.

J. H. von Thünen.
