# Wool Market and Wool Depot

*Original: Wollmarkt und Wollniederlage (1834)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

Which city, Rostock or Güstrow, is best suited for such a central wool market?

Rostock, as a significant trading city, offers the foreign wool merchant great facilitation in procuring funds; for all wool exported by sea, the shipping costs are reduced; the completion of the paved road from Rostock to Neu-Brandenburg will greatly ease the transport of wool from the Strelitz region; if Mecklenburg joins the Prussian-German Customs Union, a large portion of the wool producers in New Pomerania will probably bring their wool to Rostock; with a significant influx of visitors, these will more readily find accommodation in Rostock, and the rent for lodging will not be driven up as high as in Güstrow; it is to be expected that a significant independent wool trading house and a sorting establishment will form sooner in Rostock than in Güstrow; for those who have to pay capital sums or interest at the Midsummer term, it would be convenient and cost-saving if the wool market coincided not only in time but also in location with the payment term.

Güstrow, on the other hand, lies much more in the center of the country; a circle drawn around Güstrow with a radius of eight miles encompasses a far greater portion of Mecklenburg than a circle drawn around Rostock with the same radius; the transport costs of wool to the market are thus significantly reduced, and the journeys of the sellers to the market are shortened; the wool warehouse and its interior arrangements are, according to the testimony of all visitors, as perfect as at almost any other marketplace, and moreover the management of the wool warehouse has so willingly accommodated all reasonable wishes of the sellers that these would only reluctantly leave this market, and only once they are convinced that they will find equal convenience and equal obligingness at another location; the construction of the warehouse and its interior fittings have cost a considerable amount of capital, which, if the wool market were discontinued, would be largely lost for the city of Güstrow and thus for the state; moreover, the city of Güstrow, for the patriotic spirit with which it made the initial expenditures at a time when success was uncertain and the pecuniary advantage was still very doubtful, has a claim to the gratitude of the country.

In general, it appears to follow from this that in the city of Rostock, as a central market, wool prices would be somewhat higher than in Güstrow, but that wool producers would save on transport and travel costs if the central market were in Güstrow. Whether, however, this or that consideration is predominant, whether on the whole the sum of arguments speaks more for Güstrow or Rostock, seems so doubtful to the reviewer that he has not yet been able to arrive at a definite opinion.

The struggle arising from the competition between the two cities will one day decide this, and the city that is most favorably situated for the wool market will carry the victory.

But this struggle is unfortunately very harmful to the interest of the state. If the struggle continues for a long time, and our wool markets sink as a consequence of fragmentation and private sales to such insignificance that the great foreign wool merchants no longer consider it worth the trouble to visit our markets, then the local wool price can sink so far below the world price that at least the larger wool producers will find it advantageous to send their wool directly to Berlin or Hamburg and to abandon our markets entirely. The ultimate consequence of that competition would therefore be that both cities would lose the source of income that a wool market offers, and that the domestic wool trade would remain withheld from the native larger merchants.

A swift resolution of this struggle would therefore be highly desirable.

But what can the patriotic association contribute to a more rapid resolution?

First, by taking into account all relevant circumstances, through attentive and serious deliberation in all districts, and by hearing the opinion of discerning wool merchants — whose voice carries considerable weight here — to bring the question to a decision: "Which city is most suited to be the central wool market?"

If this question is correctly decided — which one should expect from a society that counts 376 domestic members — then the outcome of the struggle generated by the competition between the two cities is recognized in advance, and the evils that the continuation of the struggle inflicts upon the country can be averted. For then it is the duty of every patriot to deliver his wool only to the city that has been recognized as the most suitable for the central market — and every other market ceases to exist as soon as no more goods are delivered to it.

Whoever acts in such a way that what he does benefits the state as the embodiment of the whole, thereby not only fulfills his duty to the state but also promotes, at least for the future, his own lasting interest — and very often patriotism requires not so much a personal sacrifice as merely a clear, correct insight.
