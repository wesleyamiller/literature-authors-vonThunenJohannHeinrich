# Opinion on Improving the Agriculture of Towns

*Original: Erachten über die Verbesserung des Ackerbaues der Städte (1831)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

LX. Opinion on Improving the Agriculture of Towns.

(By Herr Dr. von Thünen of Tellow.)

(Conclusion from No. LVII, in the 6th issue.)

**Fourth Section.**
**Utilization of farmland located more than half a mile from the town.**

Before we attempt to answer the question of how the distant farmland can be utilized most profitably, we must first determine how high the net yield of this land is under its current mode of use.

The distant farmland is far behind the land situated close to the town in terms of manure content, and if the average yield of the entire field is 7 grains of winter grain, I believe the yield of this remote land can be estimated at no more than 6 grains.

If the average areal distance for the entire field amounts to a quarter of a mile, then for the more distant section of farmland alone, it will amount to approximately three-quarters of a mile.

How do these conditions affect the production costs of grain? My calculations on this matter yield the following:

1) That for farmland of the stated fertility, the reduction in grain yield by 2 caused by inadequate cultivation compared to the estates results in an increase in production costs of 8 shillings and 3 pfennigs per bushel of rye;

2) That when the average areal distance on this section of the town's farmland is three-quarters of a mile, but on the estates only a quarter of a mile, this produces a difference in production costs of 6 shillings and 3 pfennigs per bushel of rye.

Both causes together thus produce an increase in production costs of 8s. 3pf. + 6s. 3pf. = 14s. 6pf.

For the reasons developed in the first section, the price of rye in the town is 12 shillings higher than its production costs in the countryside. If one subtracts these 12 shillings, the result is that every bushel of rye grown on this section of farmland costs the town residents 1 shilling and 3 pfennigs more than the price at which they could purchase it.

Of 6,000 square rods of this farmland, the grain harvest under the three-field system amounts to:

a. 2,000 square rods sown with rye yield, at a return of 6 Berlin bushels per 100 square rods, 120 Berlin bushels, equal to 168 Rostock bushels of rye;

b. 2,000 square rods sown with oats yield, at a return of 9 Berlin bushels, 180 Berlin bushels, equal to 252 level Rostock bushels of oats.

If the production of one Rostock bushel of rye costs the citizen 1 shilling and 3 pfennigs more, and a bushel of oats 6 pfennigs and 3 farthings more, than the price of these grain types in the town, then this amounts to:

for 168 bushels of rye at 1s. 3pf. = 4 Reichsthalers 24 shillings,
and for 252 bushels of oats at 6pf. 3f. = 3 Reichsthalers 6 shillings.
Total: 7 Reichsthalers 30 shillings.

According to my calculation, these 6,000 square rods of farmland without the addition of meadows and pasture would yield a land rent of 5 Reichsthalers 8 shillings in the hands of a farmer.

If the farmer were to cultivate this land under such aggravating circumstances that the production costs of grain increased by 7 Reichsthalers 30 shillings, then not only would the land rent be consumed, but the cultivation of this land would require an additional annual subsidy of 7 Reichsthalers 30 shillings minus 5 Reichsthalers 8 shillings = 2 Reichsthalers 30 shillings.

The same situation applies, however, to the farming citizen, and the cultivation of this land not only yields him no rent but costs him 2 Reichsthalers 30 shillings more than the harvest he obtains from it is worth. The farming citizen who owns 6,000 square rods of this land and cultivates it himself would therefore increase his annual income by 2 Reichsthalers 30 shillings if he gave this land away for nothing.

When a theoretical investigation arrives at results that stand in such stark contrast with what happens in reality and what has existed for centuries, one has every reason to distrust the investigation and to subject it to renewed examination.

Such an examination I have now undertaken repeatedly, and its result is as follows:

1) In the calculation itself, in the arrangement of the computation, and in the inferences drawn therein, I have been unable to discover any error.

2) The data for this calculation are taken from a five-year farm account kept by me, and are thus based on experience, and I myself at least cannot doubt their credibility and usefulness -- even though the nature of the subject does not permit mathematical precision here.

3) That the town's farmland gives a grain yield one-sixth lower due to inadequate cultivation than if this land belonged to an estate is merely an assumption, and this may be erroneous. But from everything I have learned from credible and well-informed town residents, from everything that visual inspection has shown me, I must regard the assumed difference as the smallest that occurs on average.

4) Various factors mentioned in the first section that affect the production costs of grain could not be included in the calculation, and perhaps the reason why the result of the calculation agrees so poorly with what happens in reality lies in this omission.

These factors work partly toward lowering and partly toward raising production costs, and it is not clearly apparent in which direction their combined effect inclines. The principal factor that produces a reduction in production costs -- the keeping of horses for the operation of trades -- does not come into consideration for this distant section of farmland, because these horses already find full employment on the nearer section of farmland during the time when they are not occupied with trade work. I must therefore believe that if it were possible to include these factors in the calculation, the result would show an increase, but not a decrease, in the calculated production costs for the distant section of farmland.

When the landowners in the towns lease land for potato cultivation, they receive, as I am told, 4 pfennigs and 3 farthings per square rod, without undertaking any cultivation of the land. The income from 100 square rods then amounts to 8 Reichsthalers 16 shillings; if one deducts from this 2 Reichsthalers for the manure consumption of the potatoes, there remains a net income of 6 Reichsthalers 16 shillings per 100 square rods, which amounts to 380 Reichsthalers for a Last of farmland.

When a Hufe of farmland is leased on the town field, a meadow is usually added. Since hay has a higher value in the towns than on the estates, the rent that remains for the farmland itself becomes even more insignificant in proportion to what is paid in rent for estate farmland.

The grain grown near the towns does not cost much more to produce than on the estates, owing to the lower price of manure and the shorter distance to the farmland.

If one now considers that the towns, even under their current farming system, produce goods that are far more profitable than on the estates, and that a portion of the farmland devoted to grain cultivation yields not much less than the same estate farmland, then to explain the fact that the town's farmland -- at least in some cases -- bears only half the rent of equal estate farmland, the assumption is insufficient that individual parcels of the town field yield no net return at all; rather, one is compelled to assume that some parts of the town's farmland do not cover the labor costs and require a subsidy -- because otherwise the net return of the whole could not sink so low.

Indisputably, however, the most distant part of the town's farmland is the least profitable, and thus one must seek in its cultivation the main reason why the net return of the whole is so insignificant.

This result emerges, even if one abstracts from all the views and investigations developed earlier, already from a superficial development of known facts, and stands independently on its own. Accordingly, the above calculation -- according to which the cultivation of the distant farmland requires a subsidy of 2 Reichsthalers 30 shillings per Last, and the production costs of a bushel of rye amount to 1 shilling and 3 pfennigs more than its price -- is far more confirmed than refuted by the phenomena that reality presents.

Through the urban farming system, it has thus come about that the town residents, in the midst of a fertile land with flourishing agriculture and low grain prices, nonetheless eat expensive bread.

But certainly what exists in reality -- however inconsistent it may now be -- did not arise without reason. I take the liberty here of sharing a conjecture about the origin of the present urban farming system, for which, however, I can cite no historical data.

It seems to me that the present farming system of the towns, that especially the extensive grain cultivation of the same, dates from the original founding of the towns, from the time of the Middle Ages, when any neighboring knight could declare war on the town at will and cut off or at least impede the supply of provisions. Under such circumstances it was consistent, indeed necessary, to grow the greater part of the needed grain oneself and to store it within the town walls (for at that time the barns were probably located in the town itself).

What in our day still moves the various states to restrict free trade -- "the preservation of independence" -- had to move the individual towns in those days to extensive grain cultivation. These circumstances have now been completely changed for several centuries; no one threatens the security of the towns any longer, no one impedes the supply of provisions any longer; the outdated institutions have become impractical and harmful to the prosperity and flourishing of the towns; but the urban constitutions are so favorable to the state of inertia that the institutions continue to endure long after the causes that gave rise to them have disappeared for centuries!

According to the proposals made in the third section, this cut-off, distant farmland should be used either for establishing dairies or for growing timber, and the question now is which of the two modes of use is the more advantageous under given circumstances.

If dairies are established here, the farmland that previously yielded no rent at all is immediately brought to the same net return that estate farmland of equal quality provides -- that is, the rent from this farmland is thereby made equal to the land rent.

If this farmland is devoted to timber cultivation, the rent that the land thereby yields exceeds the rent of the forest land of those estates that have previously supplied timber to the town by the entire amount of savings in transport costs.

If, for example, the most distant forest from which timber must still be delivered to the town to satisfy demand were 1.5 miles away, and if in this forest the area that annually supplies 100 cords of beech wood yielded a rent of 250 Reichsthalers, then an equal area in the town forest would yield as rent, first the same income, thus 250 Reichsthalers, and in addition the difference in transport costs. Suppose the transport costs from the 1.5-mile distant forest were 5 Reichsthalers, but from the town forest only 2 shillings and 6 pfennigs per cord: then the difference in transport costs per cord amounts to 2 Reichsthalers 3 shillings, and for 100 cords, 225 Reichsthalers. The same forest area that yields a rent of 250 Reichsthalers on the estate then yields on the town field a rent of 250 + 225 = 475 Reichsthalers, thus nearly double.

But as forest cultivation on the town field expands, the timber still lacking for the town's needs no longer has to be fetched from that distant forest but can already be supplied by the closer-lying forests. The price of timber in the town then decreases, and the rent from the town's forest land declines. If finally the timber cultivation on the town field is expanded so far that the town's entire demand is thereby satisfied, then the forest land on the town field and that on the estates bear an equal rent.

It follows from this that, as long as the town's timber demand is not yet fully met by its own production, timber cultivation on the distant farmland is more advantageous for the town than the establishment of dairies.

The following two cases may, however, constitute exceptions to this:

1) When the distant part of the town field consists of farmland of excellent physical quality, and the neighboring estates possess poor sandy soil, or such lowlands as are only suitable for timber production, in great extent.

2) When a town possesses very extensive and poor pastures that become partly dispensable through the introduction of convertible husbandry on the nearer farmland, and can now be devoted to timber production.

Both cases, however, certainly occur only rarely to such a degree that the distant farmland becomes entirely dispensable for timber production.

Whether the distant farmland is devoted to timber cultivation or to the establishment of dairies, the new mode of use always requires unity of plan and of ownership, and the farmland now distributed among many owners can therefore no longer remain in their hands.

To achieve this unity of ownership, the town treasury would have to take the land on hereditary lease; an outright purchase is not advisable as long as the purchase price of urban landed property is still so high in proportion to the rent paid for it.

However certain it is that the cultivation of the remote farmland gnaws at the prosperity of the towns, there is little prospect that the owners of the same will be convinced of its worthlessness to them. It is rather probable that the town treasury will not be able to come into possession of this land unless it gives the proprietors an annual hereditary lease per 100 square rods equal to the average rent paid for the total town farmland per 100 square rods.

But since the new mode of use brings this land to a net return that is at least equal to the average rent, this does not constitute an insurmountable obstacle.

**Approximate overview of the area that must be devoted to timber production in order to satisfy the timber demand of a town of a given population.**

Let the number of inhabitants of a town be 3,000; let the timber demand amount to a quarter of a cord of beech wood or three-eighths of a cord of birch wood per person: then the demand of the whole town is 750 cords of beech wood, or 1,125 cords of birch wood.

According to Cotta's auxiliary tables, a beech forest on the best soil at 100-year rotation yields 11,400 Saxon cubic feet per Saxon Morgen. This amounts to 4,104 Mecklenburg cubic feet per 100 Mecklenburg square rods. If one reckons the yield of all thinnings at two-thirds of the timber mass that the final harvest provides, then 2,736 cubic feet are added. The total timber yield from 100 square rods in 100 years is thus 4,104 + 2,736 = 6,840 cubic feet; this amounts annually to 68.4 cubic feet per 100 square rods. If one reckons the cord at 198 cubic feet of space equal to 144 cubic feet of timber mass, then 210 square rods annually produce one cord of beech wood.

Birch trees yield, according to Cotta, at 80 years of age, 9,217 Saxon cubic feet per Morgen, or 3,318 Mecklenburg cubic feet per 100 square rods. If one adds two-thirds of this yield for thinnings, the total timber yield in 80 years is 3,318 + 2,212 = 5,530 cubic feet.

If the birches are used as 20-year coppice wood, one can reckon on average one-quarter of this timber mass per rotation; this yields in 20 years 1,382 cubic feet, and for one year thus 69 cubic feet per 100 square rods. For one cord of 144 cubic feet of timber mass, 209 square rods are thus required.

For new timber plantations, it will be more practical for the towns, for several reasons -- especially because of the earlier utilization -- to grow coppice wood rather than high forest, and thus to establish a birch forest instead of a beech forest.

If 209 square rods annually produce one cord of birch wood, then for the production of 1,125 cords, 209 x 1,125 = 235,125 square rods are needed.

Thus a town of 3,000 souls, with excellent forest soil and proper silvicultural management of the forest, would need a forest area of 235,000 square rods to produce its demand for firewood on its own field.

For each inhabitant of the town, 78 square rods of forest area are thus required. If one compares on the estates that produce their own timber demand the forested area with the number of people living on the estate, one will find that where the day laborers suffer no shortage and do not steal wood from neighboring fields, at least 100 square rods of forest area are needed per person.

In these calculations it is assumed that approximately half of the total demand for fuel is met by peat.*

*) The question of whether it might not be advantageous to cultivate faster-growing tree species instead of birches -- for example, Canadian poplars or northern alders -- cannot be discussed here, but is worthy of thorough examination by foresters.

On estates that possess much peat of excellent quality, peat can certainly be used as a substitute for timber in a larger proportion, and such estates naturally manage with a proportionally smaller forest area.

But the use of peat beyond that proportion is already in the countryside, because of the uncertainty of keeping the peat dry and because of its lesser suitability for several uses, associated with many difficulties and inconveniences, and therefore seems to me not to be practicable in the towns.

The forest area of 78 square rods per person calculated for the towns can therefore be regarded as the minimum, which is sufficient only with excellent forest soil and proper management of the forest.

This, however, covers only the demand for firewood, not for construction and utility timber. It is, to be sure, less necessary that the town also produce the required construction and utility timber itself -- because for these the transport costs constitute a smaller share of the value than for firewood -- but where the field of the town is large enough for the purpose, the land will certainly be usefully employed for this end.

The area required to satisfy the town's timber demand is therefore so considerable that the distant, cut-off farmland -- unless the town already possesses very extensive forests and the two above-mentioned exceptional circumstances occur -- must be entirely devoted to timber production.

I may be permitted here to express a wish whose fulfillment -- at least in the future -- will be greatly facilitated by the execution of the proposals made here. This wish is:

that the town treasuries may provide the poorer class of town residents, from whom timber theft is to be feared, with the needed fuel at a price so low that stealing wood is no longer worth the trouble.

Universal, loud, and urgent -- at least in some regions -- are the complaints of the estate owners who live near the small towns, that they can no longer protect their woodlands from devastation by timber theft.

The evil has, in some places, already reached such a degree that the free use of property ceases; but with the growing population in the towns and the diminution of forests on the town fields, the evil will continue to increase in the future, and -- if no remedy is found -- it will reach the point where all timber in the vicinity of the towns is eradicated, and the land, where the soil is suitable for no other production than that of timber, must be left lying waste.

But however great the pecuniary loss arising from this may be, it is still only insignificant compared to the evil that timber theft causes through the demoralization of the people.

Once someone has violated the property of another, where is the step at which he stops; where can one still trust him? Honesty lies in principle, not in the object to which it is directed, and admits of no division: it must either extend to all objects, or it does not exist at all.

What can school and religious instruction accomplish if theft, this nursery of vice, persists, if the child, following the commands of its parents -- who necessarily hold authority for it -- daily violates the commandments of religion, and thus grows accustomed to regarding teaching and action as irreconcilable with each other?

One must begin with the eradication of timber theft, and then let the better schools follow, but not proceed in the reverse order if one wishes to educate better people. Nor can one hope for the removal of this evil through better instruction itself when the workers are in a situation where they cannot subsist without timber theft.

Now, however, as a rule, neither in the countryside nor in the town is the earnings of a day laborer high enough for him to buy the timber he needs, and if his necessary requirement of fuel is not provided to him free of charge or for a small price, then timber theft is a condition of his existence.

The simplest and at the same time most natural means of remedying this evil would be:

to raise the day's wages high enough that the workers could buy their timber needs, and then to punish every case of timber theft severely.

Given the present great supply of manual laborers, however, it is not even within the power of governments, much less of individual communes or private individuals, to raise the day's wages, and so in the present time no remedy is to be expected by this path.

Since the workers will take from the forests the timber they need even if it is not given to them voluntarily, no overall saving of timber results from withholding the needed fuel from the workers. On the contrary, the damage caused by timber theft can often far exceed the value of the stolen timber, because the gap created in a young stand by an irregular cut yields no return at all until the timber is harvested -- that is, for a very long series of years.

If one therefore gave the day laborers the timber they need free of charge, the national income would not only not be diminished but would actually be increased.

There is thus no doubt that this would be appropriate to the welfare of the whole, and the only question is: who should provide the timber?

Indisputably, the one for whom the day laborer works, because he derives the benefit from the low -- below the natural price of labor -- day's wages.

Suppose the value of the fuel that a worker in the countryside needs annually amounts to 6 Reichsthalers. If he receives this free of charge, it amounts, distributed over the sum of his working days during a year, to approximately 1 pfennig per day. If the day's wages in money are 8 pfennigs, then the labor of a day laborer costs the farmer, inclusive of the fuel, 9 pfennigs per day, and it would lead to the same result if the worker received 9 pfennigs instead of 8 pfennigs daily, but then purchased his own fuel.

Suppose now that another farmer, who likewise pays 8 pfennigs in daily wages, gives his workers no fuel: they must then steal the timber they need, and if none is available on the estate itself, they take it from the forests of neighboring estates.

Who then enjoys the fruits of this theft? Not the worker, for he has only obtained what was indispensable to his subsistence; he can sell nothing of the stolen goods, procure no enjoyment from them; at the end of the year he has saved nothing, merely sustained life. But it cannot give him pleasure, after completing hard daily labor, to deny himself sleep and subject himself to a new exertion on an unlawful, perilous path -- only necessity can move him to it.

But for the master of these workers, a man's day labor costs only 8 pfennigs, while it costs his neighbor, who provides his people with fuel, 9 pfennigs.

If there were no timber nearby to steal, the master of these workers would inevitably have to buy and deliver the necessary fuel for his people. These costs are now saved for the master through the timber theft of his people.

When once the surplus of workers has ceased and the demand for workers has come into equilibrium with the supply, then the workers will flee those estates where they suffer want of fuel, and the masters of the same will be compelled to give their people the full supply. Then there will be no need, in this regard, for the exercise of a moral duty, nor for the intervention of legislation; competition alone will find and establish what is right.

But as long as the present condition persists, and the workers, merely to find any position at all, must accept every condition -- so long does the high moral obligation rest upon the master of these workers not to let his people suffer want in any essential necessity of life.

It is not only the violation of property brought about by want; it is above all the moral degradation of the people for which he will one day have to answer.

If the evil of timber theft is to be remedied through legislation, then the law must not direct its main attention to actions that appear as necessary consequences of a deeper-lying cause; it must rather seize the source of the evil itself.

The obligation to provide timber to day laborers free of charge or at a low price is less clearly apparent in the towns than on the estates, because one cannot demonstrate there, as one can here, who enjoys the advantage of the day's wages reduced by timber theft. But the obligation always rests upon the town, considered as a whole, that through it the property of others not be violated, and that through it vice not be promoted. Nor is there any doubt that the low day's wages benefit the town as a whole.

If, according to the proposals made above, the timber demand of the small towns is produced entirely or for the greater part on their own field, then it becomes possible for the town treasuries to provide timber at a low price to the poorer class of town residents.

Perhaps one will declare these proposals impracticable precisely because of the prevailing timber theft, saying it is impossible to protect the young timber stands from devastation.

But if the gaze does not merely rest on the present but turns to a somewhat more distant future, this objection collapses of itself. For if timber theft continues, and through it all timber on the town field and on the neighboring estates is eventually destroyed, the impoverishment of the small towns will advance at a rapid pace, and one will then have to resolve to procure timber for the poorer residents of the towns -- but now, of course, at double the cost from a distance.

To prevent such a misfortune, one must already now establish timber plantations, and to save these from devastation, one must already now procure timber at a low price for the poorer class of town residents.

Thus what humanity commands and what rightly understood interest demands coincide.

**Fifth Section.**
**On the goal of urban agriculture.**

If one views the town from a higher standpoint as a whole, as a unity, then in considering new arrangements it does not matter whether this or that individual, whether this or that class of citizens gains thereby, but only whether the income of the town as a whole -- that is, the sum of the incomes of all town residents together -- is thereby increased.

In this regard, it appears to be of equal value for the town whether a change in the farming system raises the rent of the landowners by 6,000 Reichsthalers, or whether through this change the prices of several necessities of life fall so far that all consumers of the town together save 6,000 Reichsthalers annually in their expenditures.

But this is of equal value only for the moment, not for the future flourishing of the town. For every reduction in the price of necessities of life has the effect that the trade-practicing citizens can bring the products of their trades to market more cheaply; the lowered price of manufactured products is immediately followed by greater sales, and the increased sales bring about the expansion of existing and the establishment of new enterprises; at the same time, the resulting reduction in costs can enable the town's enterprises to withstand foreign competition -- and thus from that change there proceeds a progressive growth of the town in population, income, and wealth.

The reduction in the price of necessities of life is therefore a far higher goal for the towns than the increase of farm rent.

The proposals made so far for improving urban agriculture have always been directed only at increasing the farm rent, and the question now arises whether our investigation has not thereby taken an entirely wrong direction.

To this end we must now examine how the proposed expansion of the production of milk, potatoes, and firewood affects their price in the town.

**Milk.** Through the execution of the proposals made, the number of dairy cows will probably not be increased; but the cows, which will then always have abundant pasture in summer and in winter receive abundant hay instead of the previous straw feed, will give almost double the amount of milk. The supply of milk must therefore be much stronger than before, and the town will produce a far greater portion of its needs itself and can dispense with the supply of milk from the more distant estates that previously brought milk to the town but could only be induced to do so by a high price, as the delivery from the nearest estates now fully satisfies the demand. The price of milk must therefore fall below the former level.

**Potatoes.** If the town does not grow its own supply of potatoes, the price of them in the town must be high enough that the neighboring estates can supply them. But if the production of potatoes on the town field is expanded to the point where the town's entire demand is satisfied, then the nearby estates can only be discouraged from further delivery if the price falls so far that the costs of production and transport are no longer covered. It follows already from this that the expansion of potato cultivation on the town field is accompanied by a decline in the price of potatoes.

**Firewood.** It has already been shown above that if the production costs of a cord of beech wood are 4 Reichsthalers, and the transport costs per cord per mile amount to 2 Reichsthalers, the price of a cord will depend on the distance from which the timber is fetched.

Suppose a part of a town's timber demand must be brought from a distance of 2 miles: then the price of a cord in the town is 8 Reichsthalers.

If later the entire timber demand is produced on the town field itself, the price sinks from 8 to 4 and 7 shillings, thus to nearly half the former price.

For a town whose timber demand is 750 cords, the expenditure for firewood at the price of 8 Reichsthalers per cord amounts to 6,000 Reichsthalers.

Low prices of timber can make the establishment of factories advantageous, especially those that consume much fuel. But since factory establishments as a rule require large capital outlays, factory entrepreneurs will only settle in a town when they have the prospect that the low timber prices are lasting.

A guarantee for the continuation of the low price is available to the towns, however, only when they produce their demand on their own field; and thus the towns are urged to expand timber cultivation on their own fields already through the concern for the preservation and security of their factories and trades, even if for the moment this is not yet commanded by pecuniary interest.

Perhaps the prosperity of the small towns is threatened by nothing more than by the gradual disappearance of forests from their vicinity -- inasmuch as factories and trades that formerly flourished here, with the resulting successive increase in the price of timber, can ultimately no longer withstand the competition of those in other towns and countries, and must close.

It is shown here, then, that the landowner, in striving solely for the increase of rent from his farmland, thereby -- even if unconsciously to himself -- at the same time promotes the welfare of the town; and although not the increase of rent but the reduction in the price of necessities of life is the higher goal for the towns, we may nevertheless, in our proposals for improving urban agriculture, direct our attention solely to the increase of rent -- because thereby both purposes are achieved at once.

But if every expansion of the stated productions leads to a reduction in the price of the products thereby produced, the question presses itself forward:

How can an increase in farm rent result from the expansion of these productions?

This question is resolved by the fact that a small profit, when it is multiplied, can far exceed a larger profit.

In the enlargement of factories, such a phenomenon frequently occurs. Suppose an already long-established factory is so greatly enlarged that it now delivers ten times the amount of goods as before: the increased output will not find buyers unless the manufactured goods are sold at a lower price than before; the profit of the entrepreneur on each individual product then decreases, but his profit as a whole can nonetheless rise very considerably.

How this applies in agriculture may be illustrated by the following example.

Assume:

1) The town's demand for table potatoes is 36,000 bushels, and the production of this quantity requires an area of 36,000 square rods.

2) When one-third of the town's demand is produced on the town field, the price of a bushel of potatoes is 8 pfennigs and 3 farthings; if the cultivation of potatoes rises to two-thirds of the demand, the price falls to 7 pfennigs and 6 farthings; and when the town's entire need is satisfied by potato cultivation on its own field, the price of potatoes falls to 7 pfennigs per bushel.

The net return from potato cultivation exceeds that from barley cultivation, per 100 square rods:

by 5 Reichsthalers when the bushel of potatoes is worth 8 pfennigs and 3 farthings,
by 3 Reichsthalers and 6 shillings when it is 7 pfennigs and 6 farthings,
by 3 Reichsthalers when it is 7 pfennigs.

The profit that potato cultivation brings to the landowners collectively amounts then to:

a. when 12,000 square rods are planted with potatoes: 600 Reichsthalers,
b. when 24,000 square rods: 900 Reichsthalers,
c. when 36,000 square rods: 1,080 Reichsthalers.

Although here, through the expansion of potato cultivation from 12,000 to 36,000 square rods, the profit that the cultivation of potatoes yields compared to barley falls per 100 square rods from 5 Reichsthalers to 3 Reichsthalers, the profit as a whole nonetheless rises from 600 Reichsthalers to 1,080 Reichsthalers, thus by 480 Reichsthalers.

Greater, however, than the profit of the landowners is, according to this example, the profit of the consumers; for the saving of 1.5 pfennigs per bushel of potatoes applied to the total demand of 36,000 bushels amounts to a considerable sum.

Thus in agriculture too, the expansion of production of necessities of life on the town's own field simultaneously increases the farm rent and reduces the price of provisions, and thereby improves the welfare and prospects for the flourishing of the town -- showing that these objectives, which might at first seem contradictory, are in fact mutually reinforcing, and that in pursuing one, the other is attained as well.
