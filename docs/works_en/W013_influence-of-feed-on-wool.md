# What Influence Do the Feeds That Sheep Receive Have on the Quality of Wool?

*Original: "Welchen Einfluss haben die Nahrungsmittel welche die Schafe erhalten auf die Güte der Wolle" (1819)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

As a contribution toward answering this question, I share the following observation:

In the year 1812, four purebred Merino sheep were sold at an auction at Gahlenbeck in the Strelitz region, from the estate of the late Colonel von Reigerstein, namely:

1) Duenna — 2 years old,

2) Risa, lamb of Duenna,

3) Astorga — 1 year old,

4) Princessa — 1 year old.

The stud registers, whose reliability could not be doubted in the least, showed that these sheep descended, in the first and second generation, from the flock that had come from Spain approximately ten years earlier.

Of these sheep, I received No. 3 and No. 4; the first two — Duenna and Risa — were acquired by my brother-in-law, Herr Schröder of Vorwerk near Demmin.

At that time, we could — as could several others who saw the sheep — perceive no difference in the quality of the wool of these sheep; however, we were of the opinion that if any superiority were to be found, it would belong to Duenna.

In the summer of 1817, that is five years later, Herr Schröder sent his purebred breeding flock, which had by then increased to five ewes, here in order to have them bred with a local ram.

I now had the opportunity to compare the wool of Duenna again with that of Astorga and Princessa, and found to my astonishment a considerable difference.

The wool of Duenna had lost its softness and silky appearance; the fineness had deteriorated particularly on the hind parts of the sheep down to the croup; individual wool fibers had separated from the rest of the fleece, protruded above it, and appeared to be in the process of transitioning into hair.

At first I could not discover the reason for this striking change; but finally it became clear to me that, since no other significant difference existed in the management of the sheep — which had been separated for five years — other than with respect to the pasture, this alone must be the cause.

At Vorwerk, the sheep had enjoyed at least as abundant nourishment on the very extensive pasture area as at Tellow, and they were at least as well maintained there in summer as here; but with regard to the plants that the pastures of the two estates produced, there was a considerable difference.

At Vorwerk, the sheep pasture consisted partly of heath and partly of very sandy outlying fields, which had in part never been broken up, and produced only silver hair-grass (*Aira canescens*) and other poor grasses.

At Tellow, there is no permanent pasture; rather, the land is used alternately for grain cultivation and for grazing according to the rules of the enclosure system. The terrain is hilly and is not as productive in grass yield as in grain yield; pasture is therefore only seldom abundantly available, but the land bears for the most part, alongside white clover, the finer grasses of better soil.

When I asked my old, experienced shepherd whether he noticed any difference between our purebred sheep and Duenna, he said: the wool of Duenna had deteriorated so much that he had believed it was not the same sheep at all that he had formerly seen under this name, until he had convinced himself through the ear-mark, which was well known to him, as well as through a closer examination of the entire build of the sheep.

When I then communicated to him my supposition that this might stem from the different pasture, he agreed entirely with this opinion and, in confirmation, related the following experience from his youth:

"He had served as a hand under a shepherd who had leased the sheep operation at Dobbin and Bäbelin. On the main estate of Dobbin, the sheep had clover pasture, but on the secondary estate of Bäbelin the pasture had consisted mostly of heath and goatsbeard and other coarse grasses. The arrangement there had been that the ewes and yearlings stayed at Dobbin, and that the young stock, once it reached the age of one and a half to two years, was removed from Dobbin and brought to graze at Bäbelin. Now, it had struck everyone at the time that the same animals which had borne fine wool — fine by the standards of that era — at Dobbin, had yielded, the following year, after grazing for a year on the Bäbelin pasture, a wool so very much worse — which felt almost like hemp tow — that the shepherd, in order not to suffer a loss at sale, had not dared to bale it separately, but had to pack it in among the Dobbin wool. They had made this same experience every year, as long as he had been there."

Since the truthfulness of the old man is entirely beyond doubt, the experience cited may well deserve some attention.

The opinion that the nature of the pasture has an influence on the quality of wool is certainly not new; but it has become doubtful and uncertain again through the assertion of some writers "that feed affects only the quantity of wool, and that the quality and fineness depend solely on the breed."

In the Lüneburg Heath and in nearly all regions where sheep pasture consists mostly of heath, the sheep bear an extremely coarse, hairy wool.

It is unlikely that, when the land was first settled, people had this coarse breed of sheep brought in from elsewhere; rather, it is far more probable that the first sheep brought there were of the same breed that existed in the surrounding regions, and that the altered feed gradually brought about a change in the nature and characteristics of the sheep, until it had finally developed into a constant breed that was fully adapted to the soil, the climate, and the nature of the feed.

Under these circumstances, it would seem to be a futile endeavor for estates that have a considerable proportion of poor pasture to seek a high degree of refinement in spite of the perpetual resistance of nature.

If, however, heath and goatsbeard have such a detrimental effect on wool when used as feed for sheep, then it is also probable that the better grasses and herbs are not all equally favorable for wool production, and it would become a subject of particular interest to seek out the very best grass and clover species for this purpose.

Thus, I recall having heard from a well-known wool merchant that the Oschatz district in Saxony produces the finest wool, and that the surrounding regions cannot achieve this fineness even when they introduce sheep from the aforementioned district.

In order to make use of the judgment of a recognized wool expert, I asked Herr Schröder to send me the fleece of Duenna after shearing; and after I had received it, I laid it alongside the fleeces of Astorga and Princessa before Herr Aaron Hirsch for evaluation and appraisal.

Herr Aaron Hirsch criticized in the fleece of Duenna the great unevenness and harshness of the wool; but he thought the harshness stemmed mainly from the sandy pasture and would diminish somewhat if the wool were beaten vigorously.

Without taking the current high prices as a standard, he then valued the wool:

of Duenna per stone of 23 pounds at 18 Thaler,
of Princessa at 26 Thaler,
of Astorga at 30 Thaler.

The fleece of Duenna weighed 2¾ pounds.
Those of Astorga and Princessa together weighed 5½ pounds.

When sorting, I took from this:
as first-grade — and as second- and third-grade —

from Duenna: 1 pound — 1¾ pounds,

from Astorga and Princessa: 4¾ pounds — ¾ pound.

The appearance of Duenna's fleece surprised me at first greatly, as the wool on the underside seemed to be of entirely equal quality with that of Astorga and Princessa. But after the fleece was unrolled and the upper side of the wool came into view, the difference showed itself again just as strongly as in the previous summer on the sheep itself.

The closer examination of the fleece revealed: that the wool was particularly poor on the hind parts of the sheep — that on the front parts it was indeed not of the quality of the wool of the local sheep, but could still be classed as first-grade — and that the base of the wool fibers was almost uniformly soft and fine, but the tip of the fiber was everywhere thicker and harder, especially on the hips and thighs of the sheep.

It thus appears that in the deterioration of wool in itself, those qualities which are hardest to achieve through breeding are the first to be lost again: for already in the initial breeding improvement, the wool becomes fine at the shoulder blade of the sheep much sooner, much later at the hips and thighs, and only through very prolonged breeding can the uniformity of the wool fiber from base to tip be achieved.

Herr Schröder has, since the summer of 1818, separated his breeding flock from the rest of the flock and gives the former only clover pasture.

If my supposition — that the deterioration of the wool was caused by the influence of the pasture — is correct, then now, with better pasture, the wool of Duenna must improve from year to year and, after six years, have regained its original quality.

If this sheep lives that long, the question posed can be satisfactorily resolved through this.

J. H. von Thünen.

Postscript by the editor.

These observations are of the greatest importance for the owners of improved sheep operations and deserve the most attentive study through sustained and continued experiments. Most probably one will then find everything confirmed that the above observations contain. I solicited the opinion of Herr Professor Flörke on this subject, who communicated to me the following account, which well deserves a place here:

"The experiences presented here may well have been made elsewhere too. I once visited the director of the powder factory near Berlin, Herr Fibau, and when he showed me the powder magazines, I saw some sheep of common breed grazing among them. To my question why he did not keep a few Merino sheep, he answered: 'That will not do.'"
