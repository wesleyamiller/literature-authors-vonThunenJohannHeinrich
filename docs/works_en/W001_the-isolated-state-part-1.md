First Section.

Formation of the Isolated State.

§1.
Assumptions.
Imagine a very large town situated in the middle of a fertile plain, which is traversed by no navigable river or canal. The plain itself consists of an entirely uniform soil, which is everywhere capable of cultivation. At a great distance from the town, the plain terminates in an uncultivated wilderness, by which this state is completely separated from the rest of the world.

The plain contains no other towns than the one great town, and this must therefore supply all the products of industry for the countryside, just as the town can be supplied with provisions solely by the surrounding land.

The mines and salt works, which supply the entire state's need for metals and salt, we imagine as situated near this central town — which, since it is the only one, we shall henceforth simply call "the Town."

§2.
Problem.
The question now arises: how will agriculture organize itself under these conditions, and how will the greater or lesser distance from the Town affect farming, when it is practiced with the highest consistency?

It is clear in general that near the Town those products must be cultivated which, in proportion to their value, are heavy or bulky, and whose transport costs to the Town are so considerable that they can no longer be delivered from distant regions; likewise those products which are easily perishable and must be consumed fresh. With greater distance from the Town, however, the land will be increasingly directed toward the production of those goods which, in proportion to their value, require less transport cost.

For this reason alone, fairly sharply defined concentric circles will form around the Town, in which this or that crop constitutes the chief product.

With the cultivation of a different crop as the main purpose, the entire form of farming changes, and in the different circles we shall see quite different farming systems.

§3.
First Circle.
Free Farming.
The finer garden products, which partly cannot endure transport by wagon from any great distance — such as cauliflower, strawberries, lettuce, and many others — and therefore must be carried to the Town, and which partly can only be sold in small quantities and quite fresh, can only be grown near the Town.

The gardens will therefore occupy the immediate environs of the Town.

Besides the finer garden products, fresh milk is one of the necessary requirements of the Town whose production must occur in this first circle: for milk is not only very difficult and costly to transport, but it also becomes undrinkable after a few hours, especially in great heat, and therefore cannot be brought to the Town from greater distances.

The price of milk must rise so high that the land used for milk production cannot be more profitably employed by any other product. Since the land rent in this circle is very high, additional labor counts for little here. To obtain the greatest quantity of livestock feed from the smallest area is the task here. One will therefore grow as much clover as possible and practice stall-feeding: for it is established that with stall-feeding, where the clover can be mowed at the right time, far more livestock can be maintained from the same area than with grazing, where the young plants are constantly disturbed in their growth by being trampled and bitten off. Or, if one should nevertheless prefer pasturing for the sake of greater cleanliness, the pasture areas can only be small, and the livestock will still be maintained chiefly with mown green clover and with the refuse of potatoes, cabbage, turnips, etc.

The distinguishing character of this circle is that here the manure is purchased from the Town, and not, as in the more distant regions, produced on the estates themselves.

This gives this circle its advantage over the more distant ones, and makes it possible that products can be sold here which the other circles must keep for themselves to maintain the fertility of the soil.

Sale of hay and straw is here the chief purpose. Since the more distant regions cannot compete in this, the price of these products must rise so high that the land is thereby most profitably utilized. Grain is here only a secondary matter, for it can be grown more cheaply in the outlying circles on account of lower land rent and lower wages. One would abandon grain cultivation entirely, if it were not necessary for obtaining straw, and one sacrifices part of the grain harvest by thick sowing, merely to obtain more straw.

Besides hay and straw, this circle must also supply the Town with all those products which become too costly through transport from a great distance. These are: potatoes, cabbage, turnips, green clover, and many others.

The small, unsaleable potatoes, and the refuse of cabbage, turnips, etc. can also be most profitably used as feed for the dairy cows here.

Bare fallow does not occur in this district for two different reasons: first, because the land rent is too high to leave a large part of the field unused; second, because through the unrestricted purchase of manure, the fertility of the soil can be raised so high that the crops, even without the careful working of the soil through fallowing, come close to the maximum of their possible yield.

One will arrange the succession of crops so that each plant finds the soil in a favorable condition for itself; but one will not, for the sake of mere rotation, grow crops which by their price ratios are disadvantageous for this region. Here, therefore, the so-called free farming — which is subject to no predetermined sequence of crops — finds its place.

The purchase of manure from the Town is most advantageous for the part of the circle nearest the Town. With every quarter-mile of greater distance, this advantage rapidly decreases, since not only the hauling of the manure, but also the carting of the crops produced, becomes more expensive. With increasing distance from the Town, we soon reach a region where it becomes doubtful whether one can still profitably fetch manure from the Town, and we must then soon reach the region where it is decidedly more advantageous to produce manure oneself than to buy it — and here, then, is the boundary of the first circle and the beginning of the second.

§4.
Determination of the Grain Price in the Various Regions of the Isolated State.
Before we can proceed to consider the farming of the second and the following circles, we must first seek to determine how the price of grain changes with distance from the Town.

We have assumed:

1) that the central town is the only marketplace for grain;

2) that in the entire state there is no navigable canal, and all grain must be brought to the Town by wagon.

Under these circumstances, the grain price in the Town sets the norm for the entire country. In the country, however, the value of grain cannot be as high as the market price in the Town; for to obtain this price, the grain must first be carted to the Town, and by as much as this costs, by that much less is the value of grain in the country than in the Town.

In order to express the ratio of this decrease in value of grain in numbers, it is necessary to take a standpoint from reality and carry it over into the isolated state.

On the estate T. (Tellow), which is 5 miles from the market town of Rostock, the transport costs for a load of grain to this town have averaged, over 5 years: 3 6/10 Rostock bushels of rye and 1 52/100 thalers N2/3, which in Berlin bushels and in gold, reckoning the louis d'or at 5 thalers, amounts to 2.57 Berlin bushels of rye and 1.63 thalers in gold.[^1]

The usual load for a team of 4 horses amounts to 2400 lbs. The feed that must be taken along for the horses for 2 days weighs approximately 150 lbs; in grain, therefore, a load of 2400 - 150 = 2250 lbs can be carried, which makes 37 1/2 Rostock or 26.78 Berlin bushels.

[^1]: The Rostock bushel is equal to 5/7 Berlin bushel; 14 thalers N2/3 are, in this and all following conversions, reckoned equal to 15 thalers in gold. When in the course of this work thalers and bushels are mentioned without further qualification, Berlin bushels and gold thalers are always to be understood.

Assumption. In the central town of the isolated state, let the average price of rye be 1 1/2 thalers in gold per Berlin bushel, and let the standard for the transport costs of grain be the same as that which we found from reality for the estate T.

We now ask, how high will the value of grain be, under these assumptions, in the isolated state, on an estate 5 miles from the Town?

For a load of 26.78 Berlin bushels of rye, 26.78 x 1 1/2 = 40.17 thalers in gold is received in the Town. The transport costs amount to 1.63 thalers in gold and 2.57 bushels of rye. If one deducts these, there remain from the receipts 38.54 thalers minus 2.57 bushels of rye. Or, for 26.78 bushels of rye which were carted to the Town, and for 2.57 bushels which the transport cost, together therefore for 29.35 bushels of rye, the cash receipt is 38.54 thalers. This makes per bushel 1.313 thalers.

For 10 miles distance from the Town, the journey there and back requires 4 days' travel.

Feed that must then be taken along amounts to 300 lbs. The grain load is therefore 2400 - 300 = 2100 lbs.

The transport costs amount to 2 x 2.57 = 5.14 bushels of rye and 2 x 1.63 = 3.26 thalers.

By a similar calculation as above, it then follows that at a distance of 10 miles, the value of a bushel of rye on the estate itself is 1.136 thalers.

Under these conditions, the transport of grain over 50 miles is therefore impossible, because the entire load or its value is consumed on the journey there and back by the horses and the men employed in the task.

For this reason, cultivation of the soil would have to cease at a distance of 50 miles, even if the production of grain involved no costs at all; but since the production of grain everywhere requires labor and expense, the net return of farming will cease at a much lesser distance from the Town, and with the net return, cultivation of the soil also ends.

It may appear incorrect, when calculating transport costs for great distances, to assume that the wagon carries along the feed which the horses consume on the journey there and back, since feed could be bought more cheaply on the return journey than it costs here through the reduction of the load.

The feed that is bought along the way is not to be had for the price it actually fetches at that place; rather, the trade profit that the innkeeper or middleman takes for it must also be paid. Yet the payment of this trade profit cannot become as costly as carrying along the feed on long journeys.

For great distances, however, the following point also comes into consideration:

The transport costs are calculated according to what they actually cost for a distance of 5 miles. The horses which work the fields in summer cart the grain in winter. No special horses therefore need to be kept for this purpose, and on the account of grain carting come only those costs which are caused by the increased labor of the horses themselves, such as horseshoeing, wear and tear on the wagon equipment, increased feed, etc.; but not the interest on the capital value of the horses, and the feed which the horses need for their subsistence in winter.

For great distances, however, special teams must be kept for grain carting, and thereby the transport costs, expressed in bushels of rye, increase very considerably for distant regions.

These increased costs probably amount to fully as much as can be saved by purchasing feed along the way; at any rate, the two errors knowingly made here diminish each other, and among several attempts to calculate the transport costs in another way, I have had to give the preference to the method chosen here as the most accurate.

§5.
Influence of Grain Prices on Land Rent.
We now come to the point from which the author's investigations actually began.

He felt, driven by an inner necessity, the need to arrive at a clear understanding of the influence of grain prices on farming and of the laws by which the grain price is regulated.

For the solution of this task, a precise calculation, drawn from reality itself, of the costs associated with farming and with each individual branch of it, was indispensable.

For this purpose, the author had before him the very detailed accounts, kept by himself, of the estate T.

In the work journal of this estate, every piece of work done on the entire estate is recorded, and this journal is compiled at the end of the year into a summary, from which it then appears how many men were required for hoeing, mowing, etc., and how large the quantity of work performed by a laborer, a team of horses, etc. has been.

The cash and grain accounts, combined with the labor accounts, furnish the data for calculating the costs of the working forces, e.g. the costs of a day-laborer's family, a team of horses, an alternating plow team, etc.

From the quantity of labor which the cultivation of a field and the harvesting of a crop requires, and from the costs of the various tasks, the production costs of this crop then follow; and finally, from the gross yield after deducting the production costs, the net surplus that the cultivation of the crop provides emerges.

Such a calculation of the net return of each individual crop, of the dairy, the sheep farm, and every individual branch of the farming operation, I have carried out for the estate T. for the 5 years from 1810 to 1815 — and this detailed calculation has given an agreement with the total net return to within 29.8 thalers.

The results of this calculation are now the foundation for all the calculations and conclusions occurring hereafter in this work.

In now proceeding, however, from the experiences that a single estate has furnished in a specific period of time, the proper task for our next investigations becomes the following: how must the land rent and the mode of management of the estate T. change, if we assume progressively lower grain prices?

The isolated state is, in this investigation based entirely on reality, only a pictorial representation, a form which facilitates and broadens the overview;[^2] one which we must not abandon, however, because it is, as the sequel will show, so rich in results.

[^2]: A friend, to whom I communicated the manuscript, made the following remark on this passage, which I believe I may share with the reader.

"A mirror that theory holds up, in order to make visible in it, in pure perspective, the confused and intersecting lines of the phenomena."

" — A form with which we believe we have hit upon the focal point of the phenomena, so that we can almost analytically develop from it the individual combined directions, while at the same time constructing the whole naturally through an intellectual synthesis."

" — What we do is essentially this: we have tried to raise a small definite point of experience, a single estate, to a scientific level, that is, to universality; for in truth every member of an organic whole must also, in this isolated form, allow the general type to manifest itself, and only insofar as we are able to demonstrate the general law at such definite points, or to present the particular under its archetypal form, can we say that the phenomenal world and its law have become clear to us. And we are perfectly justified, indeed called upon, to adopt such an approach; for civil society and the state are not a machine, in which cause and effect are separated, but a truly organic structure, where everything is equally effected as it is itself effective — in short, reciprocal interaction takes place."

In the isolated state, grain prices decrease the further an estate is from the Town. If we now calculate for the estate T. how progressively diminished prices must affect the mode of management of the estate, then for each assumed price we can identify a position in the isolated state where the same price prevails. We can then imagine the estate as transported to that region, and we thereby obtain a pictorial representation, as it were a map of the changes that the estate has undergone through the diminished grain prices.

The labor associated with the production of grain falls into 2 classes:

1) those which are governed by the size of the field;

2) those which are proportional to the size of the harvest.

To the first class belong: plowing, hoeing, harrowing, sowing, ditch-clearing, etc.; for on one and the same soil these labors remain the same, whether the field bears rich or meager harvests. The magnitude of these labors is determined by the physical properties of the soil, not by the yield. I call these labors tillage operations, and the costs thereof, tillage costs.

Into the second class fall: carting in the grain, manure hauling, threshing, and many others. The carting and threshing are obviously governed by the size of the harvest, but this is no less the case with the manure hauling; for the soil is exhausted in proportion to the size of the harvests and, as the depletion increases, requires a correspondingly larger manure replacement. The costs of these labors I group under the common designation of harvest costs.

For one and the same soil, the greater or lesser grain yield — when the farming system and all other operative factors remain the same — depends solely on the richness of the soil in plant nourishment.[^3]

[^3]: Here we are always speaking only of one and the same type of soil, but at different levels of richness. One can undoubtedly, through an exhaustive system of farming, bring a soil yielding 10 grains down to 4 grains, and at this low yield one does save on harvest costs, but the soil nevertheless requires the same tillage costs as before at the higher yield.
Soils of different physical composition can, at equal richness, likewise give very different yields — clay soil perhaps 10 grains, sandy soil only 4 grains — and the former requires far greater tillage costs than the latter. In this work, however, the effect of different soil types on yields and cultivation costs is nowhere the subject of investigation. I must on this occasion refer to the remark already made: that the numerical ratios presented here, drawn from a single point of experience, are applicable only to this single case; that from any other standpoint the calculation must begin with other numbers and deliver other numerical results; but that the method observed here is universally applicable, and that what is observed from each individual standpoint always permits the same conclusions. Here, for example, that the land rent of every soil sinks to zero merely through a decrease in richness.

Since the tillage costs always remain the same, but the harvest costs increase or decrease in direct proportion to the grain yield, we are, when these two classes of expenditure are precisely and sharply distinguished, thereby placed in a position to calculate the cash return of an estate for all degrees of fertility of the soil.

We must carefully distinguish the estate income from the soil return, or land rent. An estate is always furnished with buildings, fences, and other objects of value which can be separated from the soil. The income that an estate yields therefore does not arise entirely from the land, but is in part merely interest on the capital invested in these objects of value. What remains of the estate income after deduction of this interest belongs to the land itself, and is called land rent.

I must further draw attention to the fact that among the above-mentioned expenses associated with farming, no taxes to the state are included or implied. The purpose of our investigation requires, namely, that we first consider the isolated state in general and its agriculture in particular under the condition that no taxes to the state occur. What we call land rent is therefore the net cash return of the soil, from which no tax has yet been taken.

According to the above figures, we can now also calculate the land rent of the same soil which, owing to a lesser richness in plant nourishment, stands at a lower level of fertility.

Let, for example, the grain yield of rye = 8 bushels. The rye yield is at the same time the measure of the prosperity of the two succeeding cereal crops and of the productiveness of the pasture, and thereby stands in direct proportion to the total gross return.

For 10 grains the gross return was 5074 thalers; for 8 grains, therefore, 8/10 x 5074 = 4059 thalers.

The seed remains unchanged = 626 thalers.

The tillage costs remain = 873 thalers.

The harvest costs are governed by the yield and amount to 8/10 x 765 = 612 thalers.

The general cultivation costs are proportional to the gross return and are accordingly = 8/10 x 1350 = 1080 thalers.

Sum of costs — 3191 thalers.

The land rent amounts to 868 thalers.

These calculations, in which money serves as the measure, can however be accurate only for one standpoint and for a certain grain price — here 1.291 thalers per bushel — and the result changes with the slightest change in the grain price. Since, however, in our isolated state rye has so very different a money price in the different circles, we must, in order to construct general formulas, take rye itself as the measure, insofar as expenditure and revenue are proportional to it and can be measured by it.

The gross return of a pure seven-field convertible husbandry, as we have just assumed, consists partly of grain and partly of livestock products. The grain varieties produced besides rye — barley and oats — can be converted to rye according to the ratio of their intrinsic value and nutritiveness, and thus the entire grain harvest can be expressed in bushels of rye.

In the price ratio between rye and animal products — meat, butter, wool, etc. — two different cases can be imagined:

1) Insofar as meat, by its greater nutritiveness, replaces a larger quantity of bread, a fixed price ratio between meat and bread will prevail.

2) Insofar as the production of animal products is more or less costly in proportion to grain production, animal products will also be brought to market at a higher or lower price relative to the grain price.

We take the first case as the basis for our investigation, and assume that the price of animal products stands in the same ratio to the grain price at every place in the state.

Accordingly, the value of the animal products yielded by farming can also be expressed in bushels of rye, and thus the gross return can be stated entirely in rye.

Whether, now, this assumption is the correct one for our isolated state, or not, will emerge from the sequel of this investigation.

Among the various expenditures in farming, the seed consists almost entirely of grain and need only be converted to rye according to its actual amount.

Of the tillage, harvest, and general cultivation costs, one part consists directly of grain, e.g. thresher's wages, feeding of the servants, feed for the horses, and many others. A second part is paid for partly in grain and partly in money. Thus, for example, the day wages of the common laborer and the prices charged by craftsmen do not depend entirely on the grain price; but they are higher in regions where the average price of grain is high, and cheaper where it is low. These expenses must therefore be expressed in rye and money simultaneously, each in the proportion in which it enters into the price of labor. The third and last part of these expenses is entirely independent of the grain price, e.g. salt and all metals; for even if these, at the place where they are produced and worked, stand in a certain connection with the grain price of that region, the rye value of the region where they are consumed provides no measure of their price at all; indeed, they can even be most expensive in the countries where grain is cheapest, namely when they must be brought from afar. This part of the expenses must therefore remain expressed in money.

What share of the total expense must be paid in money and how much of it in grain — this must necessarily differ for every country, indeed for every province. The more a state produces its own necessities, the more, through an even distribution of factories and mining throughout the country, transport costs in the exchange of goods are reduced, the more rye will serve as the measure of value, and the greater the part of expenses affecting agriculture that can be expressed in rye. The poorer a country is in factories, on the other hand, the more the country obtains its necessities through the exchange of goods and through trade from afar, the more distant consumers and producers are from each other, the larger will be the share that must be expressed in money.

However different this ratio, expressed in numbers, may appear for different standpoints, it is nonetheless certain that such a ratio prevails everywhere, that there is, for example, no single country where these expenses may be stated entirely in money, nor any single one where they may be stated entirely in grain. From any other standpoint, one will begin the calculation with other numbers; but the method of developing the results from this ratio will everywhere be the same.

In our further calculations, we assume a standpoint where, of the named expenses, 1/4 must be expressed in money and 3/4 in grain.

The calculation of the return from 100,000 square rods of arable land given above then takes the following form:

Before we go further, we must first look back at the procedure hitherto followed, and ask whether from observations made at one standpoint, universally valid laws can be developed.

It can and will be said:

"The calculations of the costs of labor, of the ratio of gross to net return, may be derived from reality with ever so great precision: they are nevertheless valid only for the one standpoint, for this one estate. Already on the neighboring estate everything is different: here the soil is no longer the same, here the laborers are no longer the same. The soil may be harder or easier to work, the laborers may be more or less diligent and vigorous; the soil itself therefore requires a greater or lesser quantity of labor, and the labor itself may become cheaper or more expensive depending on the variety of the working forces. The calculations borrowed from the first estate will therefore nowhere be exactly right here, and their accuracy is entirely bound to the place from which they originated. From what is valid at only one place and nowhere else, however, no universally valid laws can emerge."

I reply to this:

It is indeed true that these calculations are no longer entirely accurate even on the neighboring estate, much less on very distant estates, under a different climate, with laborers of a different national character. But I ask: will the farmer who has long lived on an estate, and who through the most careful observation of all the experiences made has acquired an exact knowledge of the costs and net return of farming — will this farmer, transported to another estate, now be able to make no further use of the knowledge acquired on the first estate? Were this the case, every farmer would have to begin his apprenticeship anew with each change of location before he understood how to run the operation; no one could learn agriculture except at the place where he was to live in the future. This no one can or will wish to concede. Therefore, even in the knowledge acquired at one place, there must lie something that is universally valid and not bound to time and place. And it is precisely this universal element that we are striving to discover here.

In the foregoing, three propositions have principally been stated, whose universal validity is asserted, and on whose correctness the correctness of our investigation depends, for which reason I here collect and repeat them.

First Proposition. The value of grain on the estate itself decreases with the greater distance of the estate from the marketplace.

The further the estate from the marketplace, the greater the transport costs of the grain, and consequently the lower the value of the grain on the estate itself.

Grain, like any other commodity, has no value at all if no consumer who needs it can be found. In our isolated state, for the grain that is grown beyond the estate's own needs, no other consumers exist than the inhabitants of the Town. If grain is carted to the Town from such very distant regions that the draft animals consume half the load themselves during the journey, and only the other half reaches the Town for sale and consumption, then it is quite comprehensible that in the country, with 2 bushels of rye one can buy no more money than with one bushel in the Town.

But this proposition perhaps requires as little elucidation as proof.

Second Proposition. The prices of the farmer's necessities do not all stand in proportion to the grain price; or the costs that the cultivation of the soil requires cannot be paid for with one and the same quantity of grain in different regions.

This proposition follows from the first proposition; for a commodity which in the Town stands at the same price as one bushel of rye must, in the distant region where rye has only half its value, be equal in price to 2 bushels of rye, provided that this commodity can only be obtained from the Town.

We have above named salt and metals as commodities of this kind; the same applies to cloth and other goods that cannot be manufactured in the countryside.

But this also extends to the salaries and fees of the higher classes. The physician, the official, and many others can obtain their education only in the town; the capital they have spent on their education is governed by the prices in the town, and to recover this capital, their work must not be paid for in proportion to the rye price of the region where they live.

Third Proposition. Of the costs associated with the production of grain, one part is proportional to the size of the cultivated area, another part to the size of the harvests.

To the former I have reckoned the seed and tillage costs, to the latter the harvest costs and general cultivation costs.

One may question the correctness of my classification; one may say that the seed and tillage costs do not remain unchanged when the yield from the same area changes; that furthermore the harvest costs do not remain the same when the same harvest is won from a larger or smaller area. But no one will ever be able to maintain that the labor of plowing is governed by the size of the harvest, or that the carting in of grain is governed entirely by the size of the field. However one may modify the classification I have chosen, one will always come back to the conclusion that some share of the labor is proportional to the size of the cultivated field, and another to the size of the harvest, and herein already lies the acknowledgment of the proposition stated above.

If now someone were to take another estate — under conditions not similar to those of estate T. — as the standpoint of his observation, calculate the costs of labor, the production costs of grain, the land rent, etc. from data drawn from reality, and then on the basis of the above propositions and according to the same method observed here, carry the calculation forward and draw conclusions: then it would appear from the comparison of both investigations that the calculations were conducted with entirely different numbers; but it would be found that in many final results and conclusions, when expressed in words, complete agreement prevailed.

What now the same procedure, applied to a 3rd, 4th estate, etc., yielded as the common, completely concordant element, we would have to acknowledge as a general law: for what, viewed from every standpoint, always shows itself the same, must also have general validity not bound to place and time.

We could present several results developed in this work as examples, if we were permitted to anticipate them; but we can also refer to the law already presented above, that the production of grain becomes more and more costly the poorer the soil.

These laws must, precisely because they are general, be operative in every farming operation, on every estate. The magnitude of the harvest, the net return, etc., is the visible expression of these laws, modified by local conditions.

If we now, for a single standpoint, draw from nature itself the magnitudes by which nature expresses itself (and absolutely do not assume them arbitrarily), and then with consistency draw conclusions and results from the known magnitudes and the general principles: we can accordingly be assured that in these results too — drawn from only one standpoint — the general laws have found expression. But certainly not every result found is a general law; rather, some are merely locally valid rules.

Since the individual is not in a position to conduct the investigation from several standpoints, much less from every standpoint (by which, according to the above, the universally valid is separated from the merely locally valid): it is very important to find criteria by which even the individual observer can distinguish the laws from the local rules.

Such an aid is now afforded to us by algebraic calculation. If, namely, the nature of the subject permits substituting letters for numbers, and if the calculation carried through with letters gives the same conclusion as the numbers gave: then this conclusion is a general law and not a local rule.

§6.
Influence of Grain Prices on the Farming System.
Assumption. In the isolated state, let the soil everywhere — with the sole exception of the first circle — have the degree of fertility that in the seven-field convertible husbandry, the rye after fallow delivers a yield of 8 grains (8 bushels per 100 square rods, or 9.44 bushels per Magdeburg morgen). Let also the still uncultivated wilderness possess a soil of the same physical properties, of the same richness in plant nourishment, and consequently of the same productive capacity, as the already cultivated plain.

For a soil of this grain yield, the land rent amounts, according to §5, on 100,000 square rods to 1168 bushels of rye ÷ 641 thalers.

The land rent vanishes, or becomes = 0, when 1168 bushels of rye are worth 641 thalers, which amounts to 0.549 thalers or 26.2 shillings per bushel.

According to §4, the value of rye on an estate 28.6 miles from the Town is likewise 0.549 thalers per bushel.

Therefore an estate, under the assumed conditions, at a distance of 28.6 miles from the Town, no longer yields any land rent.

At a distance greater than 28.6 miles, the land rent becomes negative, i.e. farming involves a loss, and the land therefore can no longer be cultivated here.

Now while here is the boundary of cultivation for convertible husbandry, it does not follow that this is the absolute boundary of cultivation; for if there were any farming system in which the tillage of the field required less labor and consequently less cost than in convertible husbandry, then at the price of 0.549 thalers per bushel of rye, there must still remain a surplus and a land rent, and thus the cultivation of the land at an even greater distance from the Town is possible.

We must now consider how the arable land belonging to an estate, even when it is of entirely the same quality and equal productive capacity, nevertheless has a very different value depending on whether it lies nearer to or farther from the farmstead. The costs of manure hauling and carting in the products are directly proportional to the distance of the field from the farmstead. For the other work that is done in the field itself, the time that men and horses spend going and returning is lost; and this share likewise grows with the greater distance from the farmstead. The labor costs are therefore less for the field near the farmstead than for the more distant one; at equal fertility, the former must therefore give a higher net return than the latter.

If now, at the price of 0.549 thalers per bushel of rye, the return of an entire estate in convertible husbandry = 0, but the nearer half of the arable land gives a greater return than the more distant half: then it follows that the net return of the first half must be positive, while that of the second is negative, and that the gain from cultivating the nearer field is swallowed up by the loss from cultivating the more distant field, and so the net return of the whole sinks to 0.

The convertible husbandry, whose net return in total = 0, will therefore arrive at a net return again if the more distant field remains uncultivated and only the nearer is cultivated. Under this condition, cultivation does not end even at a distance of 28.6 miles from the Town.

But even this convertible husbandry, restricted to the nearer land alone, must at still greater distance from the marketplace, or what amounts to the same thing, at still lower grain prices, finally reach a point where its net return vanishes, and a second labor saving becomes necessary if cultivation of the soil is not to end there.

In convertible husbandry, the breaking up of the ley and its preparation for the winter sowing is particularly costly. With a mellow fallow, the hoeing of the ley furrow and at least half the harrowing that a ley fallow requires are saved. A farming system with a mellow fallow can therefore still be profitable where a convertible husbandry no longer yields a net return, provided that the grain yield remains the same, which can always be achieved through the ratio of arable to pasture.

A farming system with a mellow fallow is, however, only possible if one no longer alternately lays the arable land down to pasture, but plows it every year, while the more distant part of the field remains as permanent pasture for the livestock. This in turn brings a new saving, since the sowing of clover seed is now dispensed with.

According to these changes, which have necessarily emerged from the nature of the case, our farming system now agrees in the most essential points with the three-field system; and we now turn to a closer examination of this so widely practiced farming system.

In presenting the relationship between convertible husbandry and the three-field system, the following 4 questions must be answered:

1) How much cheaper is the preparation of the mellow fallow than that of the ley fallow?

2) In what ratio do the labor costs of farming stand to the distance of the field from the farmstead?

3) In what proportion must arable land and pasture stand to each other in the three-field system, if this system, like convertible husbandry, is to maintain equal fertility without receiving a manure supplement from outside?

4) If two arable areas contain on the whole an equal richness in plant nourishment, but one lies in convertible husbandry and the other in three-field farming — how does the grain yield of rye in the first system compare to that of the second?

The answering of the 3rd and 4th questions presupposes knowledge of the statics of agriculture, and can neither be understood nor presented without it.

I therefore find myself compelled to set forth in advance some main propositions of the statics of agriculture. Since, however, a detailed presentation of this doctrine would here take up a disproportionate amount of space, I can only state these propositions without entering into the development of the reasons or explanations. I must therefore refer those of my readers to whom this new branch of our knowledge may still be unfamiliar, and who wish to acquire a more detailed knowledge of it, to the works of Privy Councillor Thaer and of Herr von Wulffen treating this subject, and to my paper in the 8th volume of the Mecklenburg Annals.[^7]

§7.
Some Propositions from the Statics of Agriculture.
The production of grain harvests effects a decrease of the plant nourishment contained in the field. A field that has borne 100 bushels of rye is poorer by that quantum of plant nourishment which was used in producing these 100 bushels.

No crop is able to appropriate in one year the entire richness of the field in plant nourishment.

The ratio between what the harvest has withdrawn from the field in plant nourishment in one year and the total richness of the field, I call the relative exhaustion. This is determined from the decrease in the size of successive harvests: if, for example, the yield of the 1st rye was 100 bushels, and a 2nd rye harvest then gives, under the same tillage, the same weather, and otherwise equal conditions, only 80 bushels; we say that the relative exhaustion of the rye has amounted to 1/5.

From the relative exhaustion we now infer the total richness of the field: if, for example, the yield of the first rye was 100 bushels, the relative exhaustion 1/5, then before the harvest the field contained plant nourishment for 500 bushels of rye, and after the harvest only for 400 bushels.

The quantum of plant nourishment that is withdrawn from the field by the harvest of one Berlin bushel of rye is called one degree and designated by "1°."

The exhaustion of the other grain varieties is determined by the ratio in which they stand to rye in value and nutritiveness, and I assume that the harvest of:

1 bushel of wheat causes an exhaustion of 1 1/3 degrees

1 bushel of two-row barley causes an exhaustion of 3/4 degrees

1 level bushel of oats causes an exhaustion of 1/2 degrees

For a seven-field convertible husbandry on a barley-soil, 1st class, I now assume, based on the experiences and observations made on estate T., the following ratio of yields of the different fields: when the 1st field yields 100 bushels of rye per 1000 square rods, the 2nd field gives 100 bushels of barley, and the 3rd field 120 bushels of oats.

The 4th, 5th, and 6th fields then provide on every 270 square rods the pasture requirement of one cow, which daily consumes 17 lbs of grass reduced to hay, and finds her food for 140 days on the ley itself (i.e. exclusive of stubble and meadow grazing).

The 7th field yields, in the ley fallow, one-fifth of the grass production of a pasture field.

According to the test weighings carried out on estate T. in the years 1811 and 1816, concerning the ratio of grain to straw, compared with weighings made on some other Mecklenburg estates, I have assumed as the average ratio that with:

1 bushel of rye, straw harvested amounts to 190 lbs

1 bushel of wheat — when the wheat was standing upright — 190 lbs

1 bushel of wheat — when 1/3 of the wheat consists of lodged grain — 200 lbs

1 bushel of two-row barley — 93 lbs

1 bushel of oats — 64.5 lbs

Wheat, at equal grain yield, gives a smaller straw mass than rye; but wheat straw has a greater specific weight than rye straw, and in later years I have also found the weight of straw harvested with one bushel of wheat to be no less than with rye; however, with weak wheat and short straw this ratio may be different.

A calculation, as careful as possible, of the straw used for feed and bedding and of the hay and grain fed on estate T. in the 5 years from 1810 to 1815, compared with the number of cartloads of manure hauled, yields as a result that 1 cartload of manure arose from the feeding and bedding of 878 lbs of dry feed. If one now assumes, as is usual, the weight of a four-horse cartload of manure at 2000 lbs, then one pound of dry feed has given 2.28 lbs of manure. Here a truly surprising agreement appears with the assumption of Privy Councillor Thaer, who, guided by observations on a large scale, had already many years ago determined the factor for manure increase at 2.3.

For the factor of 2.3, which I now take as the basis for further calculations, to one cartload of manure of 2000 lbs belong 2000/2.3 = 870 lbs of dry feed, and I shall henceforth always understand by 1 cartload of manure that mass of manure which has been produced through the feeding and bedding of 870 lbs of dry feed.

We can accordingly calculate the quantity of manure which the grain harvests return through the straw.

For 100 bushels of rye, the straw harvest amounts to 100 x 190 = 19,000 lbs of straw, and from this result 19,000/870 = 21.8 cartloads of manure.

For 100 bushels of barley, the straw gain is 93 x 100 = 9300 lbs, and the manure gain 9300/870 = 10.7 cartloads. The harvest of 120 bushels of oats brings 120 x 64.5 = 7740 lbs of straw and 7740/870 = 8.9 cartloads of manure.

It is universally known that pasture, or lying in ley, enriches the soil.

After many years of observation, it has appeared to me as very probable that the plant nourishment consumed by the grasses and clover species growing on the pasture is replaced by the roots of these plants remaining in the soil and decomposing when the ley is broken up; so that all the manure falling on the ley during grazing is to be regarded as an increase of the manure content of the soil — subject, however, to the condition that the ley does not become older than 3 years.

From the number of cows that the pasture feeds, the grass production of the ley can be calculated. One cow consumes in 140 days at 17 lbs — 2380 lbs of grass reduced to hay, which have grown on 270 square rods, as the pasture requirement of one cow. On 1000 square rods, the production is accordingly 2380 x 1000/270 = 8815 lbs of hay. The manure gain arising from the pasture in one year accordingly amounts to 8815/870 = 10.1 cartloads, on a barley-soil which gave a rye yield of 10 grains.

To the fallow we ascribe a double effect: first, that it brings the plant nourishment present in the soil to a higher degree of effectiveness; and second, that it actually increases the richness of the soil through the grasses and herbs growing on the fallow, which are partly plowed under and partly eaten by the livestock and converted into manure.

In the increase of richness, I estimate the ley fallow equal to 1/5 of a ley pasture, and the mellow fallow in the three-field system, when it is not broken up until Midsummer, equal to 1/3 of a ley pasture.

In a farming system that is in a steady state, i.e. one that maintains itself at the same yield and the same richness of soil, the exhaustion must be in equilibrium with the replacement. If we now reduce the yield of the exhausting grain crops to bushels of rye and express the replacement that the field has received through manure and pasture in cartloads of manure: then from equating the exhaustion with the replacement, it follows how many bushels of rye worth of nourishment is contained in one cartload of manure, or what amounts to the same thing, how many bushels of rye withdraws one cartload of manure from the soil.

The application of this calculation to various soil types has shown that this ratio differs according to the quality of the soil. The production of an equal harvest costs the good soil less manure than the poor soil.

In our following calculations, a soil is taken as the basis which maintains itself at equal fertility in the seven-field convertible husbandry without any external supplement — and on this soil, which probably coincides with the barley-soil, 1st class, the production of 3.2 bushels of rye costs the field one cartload of manure, or one cartload of manure equals 3.2 degrees.

Herr von Wulffen has established in the statics the proposition, so rich in consequences, that fertility is to be regarded as the product of two factors: the vigor of the soil and its richness.

As soon as we compare different types of soil with each other, we must not consider richness alone, but must also include the factor of soil vigor in the calculation.

Since, however, we have here before us only one and the same soil, the factor of soil vigor becomes a constant quantity. If one now compares the same soil at different degrees of richness, the factor of soil vigor, divided by itself, always = 1, and hence indifferent for the product. For our special purpose we may therefore omit this constant factor and bring the richness alone, as the only variable factor, into the calculation.

In the convertible husbandry, the manure production of one pasture field was 10.1 cartloads for a richness of 265 degrees. A soil whose richness = 325 degrees, as it is after the barley harvest, would, if laid down to pasture, produce 325/265 x 10.1 = 12.4 cartloads of manure. Since it has been assumed that the manure production of a mellow fallow amounts to 1/3 of that of a pasture field, 12.4/3 = 4.1 cartloads have been reckoned here for it.

§8.
In What Proportion Must Arable Land and Pasture Stand to Each Other in the Three-Field System, if the Arable Land is to Maintain Equal Fertility?
The three-field system, whose richness at the beginning of the rotation was 500 degrees, had at the end of it still 442.2 degrees of richness, and thus loses in one rotation 57.8 degrees.

One cartload of manure equals 3.2 degrees; for 57.8 degrees there are therefore needed 57.8/3.2 = 18 cartloads of manure, and such an annual supplement the three-field system requires if it is to maintain equal fertility.

If now this manure supplement is to come solely from the pasture associated with the arable land, the question arises how many square rods of pasture are required to supply 18 cartloads of manure for the arable land.

Since this pasture is never broken up and rejuvenated, it is much poorer than the pasture in convertible husbandry, and a cow, or a number of sheep to be substituted for it, requires instead of 270 square rods here 405 square rods for pasture. In convertible husbandry, 1000 square rods of pasture produce 10.1 cartloads of manure, but here, because the manure production is proportional to grass production, only 2/3 of this quantity, thus 2/3 x 10.1 = 6 3/4 cartloads.

If now the pasture is utilized through sheep, half the manure that the pasture produces can be gained for the arable land, if the sheep lie at night in hurdles on the fallow. Under this condition, 1000 square rods of pasture yield 6 3/4 x 1/2 = 3 3/8 cartloads of manure for the arable land.

The manure requirement of the arable land is 18 cartloads; to obtain these requires 18/3.375 x 1000 square rods = 5333 square rods of pasture.

If therefore the three-field system is to maintain itself, 3000 square rods of arable land must be combined with 5333 square rods of pasture; or of 8333 square rods, the arable land must amount to 3000 and the pasture to 5333 square rods.

For an area of 100,000 square rods, under this ratio, the arable land will amount to 3000/8333 x 100,000 = 36,000 square rods. The pasture then amounts to 5333/8333 x 100,000 = 64,000 square rods.

The pure convertible husbandry can no more exist without meadows than the pure three-field system, because for the maintenance of livestock in winter, hay is indispensable, unless it is replaced by very costly grain feeding.

The purpose of our investigation requires, however, that we consider the arable land, both in its cash return and in its manure production, by itself, thus separated from the meadows, and the question now arises how, from the net return of an estate composed of arable land and meadows, the net return and manure production of each of these two elements can be found.

The value of hay falls into two parts: 1st, its feed value, and 2nd, the value of the manure resulting from the hay.

The feed value of the hay can be calculated from the net return given by the dairy cattle and the sheep.

The manure value of the hay I have determined according to the following principle:

Imagine the arable land belonging to an estate, of equal quality and equal richness, divided into two sections. The first section receives all the manure supplement coming from the meadows, and lies in a convertible husbandry with a proportionally large grain sowing such that it just maintains itself at equal fertility with the help of the manure supplement. The second section lies in a convertible husbandry in which the ratio of grain crops to pasture fields is such that it maintains itself, in and through itself, at the same fertility level once attained. The higher net cash return of the first section of equal area is then to be attributed solely to the manure supplement, and from the magnitude of this supplement, compared with the cash surplus, the cash value of one cartload of manure is obtained.

The statics furnishes the data for such a calculation.

But how the ratio between arable land and pasture in the three-field system is changed when the arable land receives part of its manure requirement from the meadows, the following example may show:

Suppose that with an area of 100,000 square rods of arable land and pasture, meadows are associated whose annual yield amounts to 100 cartloads of hay at 1800 lbs.

One cartload of hay of 1800 lbs yields through feeding 1800/870 = 2.07 cartloads of manure; through 100 cartloads of hay the arable land receives a supplement of 207 cartloads of manure.

An arable area of 3000 square rods requires an annual supplement of 18 cartloads of manure; 207 cartloads therefore suffice for 207/18 x 3000 = 34,500 square rods of arable land. If these 34,500 square rods are deducted from the total area = 100,000 square rods, there remain 65,500 square rods which can receive no further supplement and which must maintain themselves on their own. Under this condition, the arable land amounts to, as we found above, 3/8.333 of the total area, and the pasture to 5.333/8.333 of the same, which for an area of 65,500 square rods gives 23,580 square rods of arable land and 41,920 square rods of pasture.

The arable land accordingly amounts to:

1) Arable land maintained through the manure supplement from the meadows: 34,500 square rods

2) Arable land whose manure requirement comes from the pasture: 23,580 square rods

Total arable land: 58,080 square rods

3) Pasture: 41,920 square rods

On arable land of lower grain yield, the same manure supplement suffices for a larger arable area. At a yield of 5 grains, the three-field system would need only a manure supplement of 9 cartloads to maintain itself at equal fertility; 100 cartloads of hay would here suffice for 2 x 34,500 = 69,000 square rods of arable land. The self-maintaining area would then be only 31,000 square rods, of which, according to the ratio indicated above, 11,160 would be arable land and 19,840 pasture.

The total arable land would then amount to 80,160 square rods, and the pasture only 19,840 square rods.

§9.
How Does the Grain Yield of Rye in Convertible Husbandry Compare to That in Three-Field Farming, When the Arable Areas on Which Both Systems Are Practiced Contain on the Whole an Equal Richness in Plant Nourishment?
When a three-field system is converted to a seven-field convertible husbandry, the entire manure mass on the farmstead is now spread on one-seventh of the field, instead of being previously distributed over one-third of this field.

For this reason, the rye must already in the first year after conversion give a higher yield than before in the three-field system; but this increased yield by no means proves an increased richness of the whole field — which in the first year can have undergone no change at all — but results solely from the greater concentration of manure on a part of the field.

We must therefore absolutely not compare convertible and three-field systems that give the same grain yield in rye; rather, we must determine how, at equal richness of both arable areas, the grain yield compares between the two.

The richness of the whole field is obtained from the sum of the richness of the individual fields. During the summer, the quantity of plant nourishment in the soil is subject to constant change, as a continuous exhaustion is effected by plant growth on the grain fields, and a continuing manure production on the pasture fields. We therefore choose spring as the moment of observation, when vegetation has not yet begun, and all fields still have the degree of richness that sets the norm for their yield.

In order to compare different farming systems in this regard, we must, besides the richness actually contained in the field, also take into account the manure present on the farmstead, produced or still to be produced from the previous year's harvest. For if in one farming system the manure is already hauled out in spring, in the other only after the completion of seeding, and one were to consider only the richness actually in the field: this would not lead to an overview of how much richness in total is required to produce a given harvest. The latter system cannot, namely, deliver the assumed yield without the manure capital on the farmstead.

The data for such a calculation we can take from the tables on the fertility state of the convertible and three-field systems given in §7. Only it should be noted that, since we assume pasture grazing in convertible husbandry, the manure produced by the pasture remains on the field itself and does not come to the farmstead; and since the manure production of one pasture field amounts to 10.1 cartloads, the richness of this field is increased each year by 10.1 x 3.2 = 32.3 degrees.

1st class. Tasks whose magnitude depends entirely on the distance, e.g. manure hauling and carting in the grain.

2nd class. Tasks which require going to and from the field twice a day, but which are frequently interrupted by rain, e.g. mowing, binding, and other harvest tasks. Hoeing with oxen may seem not to belong to this class, since the hoe-men go to the field in the morning and return only in the evening, thus making the trip to and from the workplace only once a day. A. On the Average Distance of the Field from the Farmstead.
The expression "average distance" requires, since it is taken in a different sense from the usual one, an explanation.

If, when manuring a field which forms a regular figure, e.g. an isosceles triangle, one measures, records, and sums up the length of the journey which the horses make with the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, and all following cartloads until the complete manuring of the whole field, and then divides the sum so found by the number of cartloads hauled: the result is the average distance, in the sense in which we have taken it here. If one now takes on a line which divides the field, in the direction from the farmstead toward the boundary, into two equal parts, a point which is as far from the farmstead as the average distance found indicates: this point is, as it were, the representative of the distance of all parts of the whole field, and it would, with respect to the length of the journey to be made in hauling manure, be entirely immaterial whether one hauled the manure to all parts of the field, or brought it all to this point in one heap.

I am not aware that a formula has already been presented in mathematics by which this average distance can be calculated; and all my efforts to find a general law for it have so far always been in vain.

For practical use, it must for the present suffice to take as the standard, in comparisons, the distance of the center of gravity — which, though it does not coincide with the point of average distance, also does not, for regular triangles, deviate significantly from it.

B. On the Location of Farmsteads in Mecklenburg.
If one examines the location of the farmsteads on most estates in Mecklenburg and Western Pomerania, one must be astonished at the irrationality of the arrangement.

They visibly still bear the marks of their first origin, and are to be regarded as historical monuments of the first settlements. Where there is a lake, a river, a brook, there the farmsteads lean against it, and all the arable land lies in an often seemingly endless stretch on one side of the farmstead. The first cultivator of a wild and hitherto desolate region was quite right to establish his dwelling by a lake, river, or brook, because he thereby procured the first and most necessary requirement, water, in the least costly manner, and because he initially took so little arable land into cultivation that its distance from the farmstead remained wholly insignificant. But when in the following centuries prosperity and population rose, agriculture expanded, the herds increased — the owner of the farmstead drove his livestock as far as until he met a natural obstacle, a brook, a marsh, etc., or until a neighboring boundary-holder forcibly prevented him from further expansion. In more recent times, even these pastures have been largely converted to arable land, which, however, on account of its great distance, frequently gives a negative net return.

Thus our estates have originated and been transformed in the course of time; but the farmsteads of the large estates still stand on the same spot where the first settler built his hut.

In regions where there are no rivers and lakes, the situation is indeed less bad; but here too, the estate boundaries frequently run in curves or with constant indentations beside each other, and at the same time it is not uncommon that of two neighboring estates, the arable land of one reaches close to the farmstead of the other, while that estate in turn approaches with its arable land the farmstead of a third estate.

We are, through our preceding calculations, placed in a position to express in numbers, for a given case, the loss arising from this irregular location of the farmsteads, and the subject is important enough to dwell upon it for a moment longer.

Suppose estate A has a piece of arable land of 70,000 square rods at 8 grains yield, which is 400 rods from the farmstead of estate A, but only 100 rods from that of the neighboring estate B. Estate B, on the other hand, possesses a piece of arable land of equal size and quality, which is likewise 400 rods distant, but lies only 100 rods from the farmstead of estate C.

By how much will the land rent of estate B increase if it gives up the piece 400 rods distant to C and receives in return the piece 100 rods distant from A?

For estate B, 70,000 square rods of arable land at 8 grains yield give:

1) at 100 rods distance, a land rent of 763 ÷ 197 x 100/210 = 669 thalers

2) at 400 rods distance, a land rent of 763 ÷ 197 x 400/210 = 388 thalers

Through the exchange, estate B gains 281 thalers in land rent, and in capital value at 5 percent interest: 5620 thalers.

Estate C gains through the acquisition of 70,000 square rods of arable land which are only 100 rods from the farmstead: 669 thalers in land rent, and 13,380 thalers in capital value.

Through this change, therefore: estate B gains 5620 thalers in capital value; estate C gains 13,380 thalers; together 19,000 thalers. Estate A loses 7,760 thalers. Remaining: 11,240 thalers.

The three estates together have thus, merely through the better distribution of the arable land, gained 11,240 thalers in capital value.

It should be noted that the gain arising from this exchange of real property is not to be regarded as the gain in an ordinary, so-called good deal, where one contracting party loses as much as the other gains; rather, this gain is a net addition to the national income and the national wealth.

If one considers that on hardly any estate do the buildings stand in the middle of the field area, that almost every estate can gain by rounding off and exchanging: one must be astonished and grieve at the magnitude of the capital that is lost for the national wealth in this way without any compensation. If one wished to estimate this loss of national wealth for Mecklenburg in money, the calculation would, even at the lowest estimates, always yield several million thalers.

But why, one can and must ask, are these estate boundaries so unchangeable — more unchangeable even than the boundaries of states?

To the exchange there is opposed, first, the attachment to the property hitherto possessed. One all too easily overestimates the value of the piece of land that one has long possessed, or even inherited from forefathers, and on whose improvement one has spent one's own effort and expense. But this attachment, in constant conflict with clear insight and well-understood interest, would not have prevented the exchange for generations and centuries, had not other, more substantial obstacles contributed.

These we find sufficiently in the following:

1) In the magnitude of the fees which in Mecklenburg must be paid not only on the sale of entire estates, but also on the sale of individual estate appurtenances, and which on exchanges must even be paid double, i.e. on the value of each of the two pieces of land transferred to another owner;

2) In the costs which the surveying of the purchased or sold piece, the transfer in the tax register, etc. occasion;

3) In the debt relations of the estates, whereby no piece of the estate can be either sold or exchanged without the special consent of all the estate's creditors.

The high fee on the sale of entire estates is not harmful but rather favorable to the cultivation of the soil, in that it checks and reduces the frivolous passing of estates from one hand to another; but the fee on the exchange of individual estate portions is certainly most injurious to the national welfare.

Since this fee, in combination with the other difficulties, is strong enough to prevent almost all exchanges: its abolition would also be no sacrifice, or would at most produce a very small deficit in the state revenues. If one wished to cover even this deficit, it could be done, without any harm to the cultivation of the land, through a slight increase in the fee on the sale of entire estates.

Whether and how the third difficulty, arising from the debt relations of the estates, is to be removed — on this I venture no judgment. But it is foreseeable that if, in our old continent, we do not know how to loosen the bonds that time and custom have wound around us, we shall very soon fall behind the freshly blossoming states of the new world in agriculture and in national welfare.

In the villages, where the peasants live together in the village and have their arable land not in one connected piece but in scattered strips, and where these strips extend from the village to the field boundary, the loss of land rent is still very much greater than on poorly consolidated but contiguously lying large estates. These villages suffer all the disadvantages of the large estates, without enjoying any of their advantages. A state consisting entirely of such peasant villages could possess only an insignificant national income, and would therefore be most powerless in defense against an external enemy.

The strength of men and draft animals is here wasted in idle going to and fro on the field; and whereas otherwise a farming family engaged in agriculture can quite well produce the provisions for two families on fertile soil, here it consumes almost everything again that it has won from the soil through its labor, and can contribute very little in the way of provisions for the support of the town-dwellers.

The remedy here is difficult, however, because the remote land of these villages is usually so poor that it could not pay the costs of building new structures, and could not even support a family. — But this subject does not further belong to our investigation.

Conclusion. Low grain prices and low fertility of the soil have one and the same effect on the mode of farming: both lead to the three-field system.

§15.
Comparison of Manure Production, and of the Area Sown with Grain, in Convertible and in Three-Field Farming.
It has already been said, and it is also evident from the whole course of the investigation, that here we are speaking only of convertible and three-field systems which maintain themselves at equal richness in and through themselves, that is, without external manure supplement.

In the three-field system, half of the manure that the pasture produces is lost for the arable land, and hence also for grain cultivation, and this pasture itself is little productive. Because of this low manure production, it can sow only 24,000 square rods out of 100,000 with grain, if it is to maintain itself at equal fertility.

Convertible husbandry, on the other hand, utilizes completely the manure that the better pasture produces; and this results in it being able to sow 3/7 of the area, or about 43,000 square rods out of 100,000, with grain, while still maintaining equal fertility.

Although convertible husbandry, through its stronger manure production, can sow a much larger area with grain than the three-field system, the latter is nevertheless more advantageous at low grain prices, and can continue to exist where convertible husbandry gives a negative net return and must therefore cease.

At very low grain prices, the costs caused by the greater manure production in convertible husbandry cannot be covered by the return from the larger area sown with grain; or in other words, the manure costs more than it is worth.

In the opposite case, when grain prices are high, or when the fertility of the soil is very great, and especially when both causes combine, the land rent of convertible husbandry far exceeds that of the three-field system.

Here the costs of manure production in convertible husbandry vanish in comparison with the benefit that this manure brings through an enlarged grain cultivation.

§16.
Farming System with Higher Manure Production.
From the foregoing, it can already be concluded that at very elevated grain prices, combined with a great fertility of the soil, we must finally reach a point where an even stronger manure production than in convertible husbandry will pay handsomely.

That a still higher manure production is possible is clearly evident; for:

1) convertible husbandry still has a bare fallow, which, while very useful in many other respects, contributes very little to manure increase, producing only one-fifth of the manure that the pasture produces;

2) the pasture itself is far less productive than it could be, since it always comes into the fields that have already borne three grain crops after manuring and therefore stand at a low level of richness.

The benefit of the fallow consists principally in the following:

1) the ley is made suitable for receiving the winter sowing at the lowest labor cost; for one can indeed also make the ley mellow through spring working, but this involves a great increase of labor and costs 30 to 50 percent more than the regular fallow working in summer, where the decomposition of turf assists the working;

2) the manure and humus content of the soil is brought by the fallow to such a high degree of effectiveness that no preceding crop can achieve this to the same degree.

Thus, for example, a soil which after fallow bears 6 grains of rye will, after green-mown vetches, give only about 5 grains. That individual years and certain soil types make exceptions to this cannot overthrow the rule that fallow is the best preparation for the winter sowing; but the ratio expressed in numbers (here assumed as 6 to 5) will be very different according to the soil, the working, and the climate.

This reduced yield of rye after vetches, however, results not only from an exhaustion of the soil caused by this crop, since it also occurs when the field after harvesting the vetches has the same manure content as the fallow; but arises from the fact that the working of the soil has been less perfect, and that a smaller part of the whole mass of manure and humus in the soil has been prepared and made ready as food for the plants, which I designate by the expression "lesser effectiveness of the manure."

On the credit side of the preceding crop are to be placed:

1) value of the livestock feed obtained;

2) value of the manure that the feed gives in excess of what its production has cost the field — whereby a greater extension of grain cultivation becomes possible.

The debit side of the preceding crop contains:

1) increased tillage costs,

2) costs of seed,

3) reduction of the yield of the winter crop which immediately follows the preceding crop.

The question now arises: at what grain price and at what grain yield of the field will the credit of the preceding crop equal its debit?

If the data for such a calculation are given, this point can unquestionably be determined just as precisely as was done in determining the boundary between convertible husbandry and the three-field system. But this calculation will become very complicated, and I am not yet able to give it at present; because there is such a great difference of opinion about the exhaustion caused by green fodder and root crops, and the author himself must first await the results of his experiments on this subject before he can present his views to the public in detail and base a calculation upon them. He therefore contents himself with presenting some basic outlines which, as I believe, would emerge from the completed calculation.

At a moderate fertility of the field, the abolition of the fallow will only be advantageous at a very high grain price: for even though the increased labor is soon paid for by higher prices, the reduced yield of the winter grain has so great an influence on the net return that the enlarged grain cultivation, extending perhaps to half the total area, can only with difficulty and only at very high grain prices cover this loss.

The value of the livestock feed obtained cannot contribute much to covering this loss, since ordinary livestock farming does indeed yield a considerable, very conspicuous marketable product, but produces only a small, or — as the sequel will show — often no land rent at all.

For soil of low fertility, that is, of low grain yield, the abolition of the fallow will no longer be consistent even at the highest grain prices.

If, however, we consider soil of very high fertility, these conditions change very much.

With the rising manure content of the field, the grain yield rises up to a certain point probably in direct proportion.

But the increase of grain yield cannot, like that of manure content, be unlimited; it finds this limit rather in the nature of the plant, which even with the greatest abundance of nourishment cannot exceed a certain measure of size and yield. If the soil now has such a manure content that the plants sown upon it can reach the maximum of their yield: then every further addition of manure is ineffective, indeed it even becomes harmful, in that it produces lodging of the grain and thereby a reduced yield.

Suppose the maximum rye yield for a given soil = 10 grains. If we now increase the manure content of this soil by 1/5, so that it would have the capacity to produce 12 grains if the nature of the plant permitted: then on this soil, after bare fallow, only lodged grain will be grown. If now, however, green vetches are substituted for the fallow: the effectiveness of the manure and manure residue in the soil is reduced to such an extent that the soil now again produces 10 grains.

Under these circumstances, the disadvantage of the preceding crop on the following winter crop disappears entirely; on the debit of the preceding crop there remain merely the increased tillage costs and the cost of seed, which are already compensated at moderate grain prices by the increased manure gain and the resulting enlarged grain cultivation.

There is therefore no doubt that under these conditions the abolition of the fallow is consistent — provided that the physical properties of the soil and the climate are not of such a nature that the fallow is absolutely necessary.

With the abolition of the fallow, the entire form of convertible husbandry now changes. To facilitate the working of the ley for the preceding crop, it will be found advantageous to leave the ley no longer 3 years but only 1 or at most 2 years for pasture. To avoid the deterioration of the field which, when there is no bare fallow, so easily occurs, outstanding attention to the sequence in which crops best succeed one another becomes necessary. One will choose the crop rotation so that for each crop the best possible working can take place, and so that the harvested crop leaves the richness of the soil at the greatest attainable effectiveness for the following sowing — a precaution which in convertible husbandry is also not superfluous, but not so necessary, and which there must yield to other considerations. — In a word: high fertility of the soil, combined with good grain prices, transforms convertible husbandry into crop rotation farming.

If for a given soil the maximum of the average yield of rye = 10 grains, which in the seven-field convertible husbandry presupposes an average richness of 373 degrees per 1,000 square rods: then in this farming system an addition of richness can find no further application, because it would only produce lodged grain and hence a diminished yield. He who regards convertible husbandry as the limit of cultivation will, on a soil of this richness, either be unable to use the treasures of humus and marl found in his field at all, or he will have to take away immediately, through an enlarged grain sowing, what he has given the field through the application of these means, and will thus be unable to invest any greater productive capital in the soil.

In crop rotation farming, however, a much greater average richness still finds a useful application: for 1) a greater average richness is already required, through the more uniform distribution of richness among all fields, to produce 10 grains of rye, and 2) because of the reduced effectiveness of manure caused by the preceding crop, the richness of the rye field itself must be significantly higher if it is to deliver the maximum of 10 grains.

From the first cause, according to §9, in the six-field crop rotation the average richness is 425 degrees, when the rye field after vetches is to contain 500 degrees; from the second cause, however, 600 degrees of richness are needed to produce 10 grains.

The maximum yield of potatoes and green fodder is not reached as quickly as with grain, and their cultivation is most advantageous precisely on soil containing more than 500 degrees of richness. If the fields are to remain among themselves in the ratio of richness as indicated in §9, then for a grain yield of rye = 10, the potato field will also contain 600 degrees, and the average richness will then be increased by 1/5, thus raised from 425 degrees to 425 x 1 1/5 = 510 degrees.

Since in crop rotation farming the richness has a lesser effectiveness than in convertible husbandry only for the winter sowing, but not for the potatoes, the spring grain, and the green fodder: the net return of this farming system is very much higher than that of convertible husbandry at 10 grains yield.

Thus in crop rotation farming an average richness of 510 degrees finds a useful, productive application, while in convertible husbandry only 373 degrees of average richness can be usefully employed; or crop rotation farming can invest 510 degrees of average richness productively in the soil, convertible husbandry only 373 degrees.

In states whose consumption is just covered by their production, which therefore neither export nor import grain, the population certainly stands in some proportion to the total quantity of provisions produced. Now convertible husbandry produces from an equal area a much greater mass of provisions than the three-field system, but a much smaller mass than crop rotation farming, when the grain yield of rye is equal in all three farming systems; and if convertible husbandry at 10 grains yield feeds about 3,000 people per square mile, then the three-field system will provide sustenance for only about 2,000, but crop rotation farming perhaps for 4,000 people per square mile.

Crop rotation farming is a splendid means of highly utilizing a rich soil; but for poor soil it is a means of destroying the net return that other farming systems would have given here.

If one calculates the quantity of grass that a ley pasture produces annually, and then compares it with the hay yield of mown red clover, one will find, even when taking soil of equal manure capacity, a very considerable difference in production in favor of the mown clover.

Since this advantage of mown clover still holds even when the pasture plants themselves consist mostly of red clover: it follows from this that the constant disturbance which the pasture plants suffer in their growth through being bitten off and trampled acts very detrimentally on the growth of the grass and the clover.

The manure production, as well as the feed gained, will therefore be considerably increased if one transforms the ley pastures into fields with green-mown fodder crops — which brings about stall-feeding instead of pasturing.

With the manure production increased through stall-feeding, the grain cultivation can once again be extended, and if, according to a rough calculation, crop rotation farming with pasturing can sow about 50 percent of the arable area with grain, then crop rotation farming with stall-feeding will perhaps be able to devote 55 percent of the arable area to grain cultivation, while still remaining at the same degree of richness.[^8]

[^8]: Here we are always speaking only of an excellent upland soil which can maintain itself in the seven-field convertible husbandry without manure supplement. For every less good soil, such an extended grain cultivation would lead to ruin — and this will be the case even on the excellent soil, if wheat instead of rye is grown.

In warmer climates, on fertile soil, a second crop such as turnips, spurrey, and the like can be grown in the stubble of the harvested grain. This is, as it were, an accelerated rotation: one grows two crops in one year, whose production in colder climates requires two years. Since the stubble crop always serves as livestock feed, and only such plants are used for this purpose as return more manure through feeding than their production has cost the field: the exhaustion caused by the grain crop has in the manure production of the stubble crop a constant counterweight. A part of the exhaustion caused by the cereal crop is offset by the replacement that the stubble crop provides, and so it is not to be wondered at that these farming operations can sow 60 to 70 percent of the arable area with grain and commercial crops without exhausting the richness of the soil.

But always, in addition to an outstandingly fertile soil, a high value of the products is also needed if these harvests won by storm (as an anonymous writer expresses it) are to pay the costs.

According to the testimony of reliable writers, red clover in some regions causes no exhaustion at all, but rather an enrichment of the soil.

In Mecklenburg, on the other hand, experience and the prevailing opinion declare that red clover is to be regarded as an exhausting crop.

It has furthermore been very frequently observed in Mecklenburg and New Pomerania that fields which have passed from the three-field system to convertible husbandry bore very luxuriant clover, both white and red, in the first rotations; but that in later rotations this soil no longer delivered the first great clover yield, neither through increased richness nor through marl.

How can a common cause now be found for these apparently contradictory facts?

It seems to me that these experiences can be understood from a single point of view, if one assumes that in manure there is some substance — no matter what it may be and how it may be called — which is not taken up by the cereal crops, but which is quite especially suited to clover.

If now clover comes upon a soil that has long been cultivated but has hitherto borne only grain: the clover finds this substance as the residue of all earlier manurings in the soil, and thrives to an extraordinary degree because of the nourishment precisely suited to it, present in excess. The soil then loses through the clover a substance that was indifferent to the grain, and receives in return, through the stubble and roots of the clover, a manuring that is effective for grain. The grain then finds an increased quantity of the nutrient suited to it, and if one now takes the thriving of the grain, before and after the clover, as the measure of exhaustion, then the clover must appear far more enriching than exhausting.

But as soon as the clover, taken into the regular crop rotation, has returned so often that the specific nutrient substance is exhausted: it finds in the next and in all following rotations of this specific substance only as much as was contained in the fresh manuring. But since this quantity is not sufficient for the nourishment of the clover, it attacks the nutrient substance suited to grain in an increased measure, and so the clover now appears no longer enriching but exhausting.

Probably the substance suited to red and that suited to white clover is, if not identical, yet similar, and since in convertible husbandry the white clover comes over the entire field in every rotation: there is no accumulation of the clover nutrient substance here at all. If one now brings red clover onto this soil for a change, it must live mostly on the substances suited to grain, and then appears exhausting.

Whether now this explanation be well-founded or unfounded, I cannot, based on my experiences and observations so far, attribute any enriching power to green-mown vetches and red clover — when these return regularly in every rotation; but I must rather assume that these plants, which deliver so great a mass of feed, and which, in their regular recurrence, grow only in the measure that they find richness in the soil, exert an exhausting effect on the soil. It seems certain to me, however, that red clover — even after deducting what its production has cost in manure — on a soil suited to it — delivers a considerably greater manure surplus than a ley pasture can give on this soil.

The credit of stall-feeding in comparison with pasturing of livestock accordingly contains:

1) increased feed,

2) enlarged manure production and thereby effected greater extension of grain cultivation.

The debit contains:

1) the more costly sowing of vetches and red clover seed;

2) the increased tillage costs caused by the cultivation of vetches;

3) the costs of hauling the green fodder to the farmstead;

4) the costs of hauling out the manure resulting from the green fodder — which in pasturing are entirely saved.

The costs caused by stall-feeding are not insignificant, and only on soil of high value will the extended grain cultivation and the increased livestock feed cover and outweigh these costs.

Soil of low fertility cannot repay these costs, and for such soil this system of farming becomes all the more ruinous, as the expected increase in feed and manure turns into a decrease; since the fodder crops entirely fail here, give an even lower yield than the pasture clover and grasses, and barely repay the costs of the seed used.

It can therefore be assumed with confidence that crop rotation farming with stall-feeding can spread over the entire field only on small estates; but that on large estates, even when the soil is of high value, this farming system is advantageous and practicable only on the nearer part of the arable land, while the more distant arable land is more highly utilized through convertible husbandry.

Since, when the value of the soil is high — arising jointly from the fertility of the soil and the price of the products — crop rotation farming with stall-feeding is more profitable than convertible husbandry, we can conversely conclude that with the rising value of the soil, estates of moderate size more and more gain the advantage over large estates; and indeed we find in all countries where a very high cultivation of the soil takes place, only estates of small or moderate extent.

The Belgian system sows 60 percent of the entire arable area with grain and maintains itself thereby at equal fertility, while the Mecklenburg system may sow only 43 percent of the arable area with grain if it is to maintain itself in and through itself at equal strength.

The Belgians achieve this result in that they:

1) bring the clover, as the most important manure-producing crop, into just as rich a soil as the winter grain itself, while the Mecklenburgers take their pasture only into such fields as have already lost a great part of their richness through three grain crops;

2) do not let the clover be grazed off by the livestock, which would otherwise produce a diminished clover production reduced by up to half, and a manure production diminished by about a third, but mow it and feed it to the livestock in the stall — and these two causes together effect that the single Belgian clover field = 20 percent of the arable area nearly equals in manure production the three Mecklenburg pasture fields = 43 percent of the arable area;

3) sow the stubble of the winter grain in the same year with turnips, and thus obtain from the same field, after the exhausting cereal crop, another crop that returns more manure than it has taken from the soil.

My calculations on the monetary return and the costs, as well as on the manure consumption and the manure replacement of the individual fields — which I would gladly have presented, in order to hear the examining and correcting judgment of the public on them, but which I cannot communicate here because they would require too many discussions and explanations and would thereby take up too much space — show that the potato field of 10,000 square rods, through the value that the potatoes have as livestock feed, after deduction of the labor costs employed, yields a monetary surplus of only 25.5 thalers N2/3, and that the manure replacement that the potatoes give through their being fed exceeds the manure consumption caused by their harvest by only 46.2 degrees.

According to this, the potatoes would be to be regarded as a neutral crop in both respects; one could substitute the fallow in their place without the monetary return or the manure production being essentially changed. But the potato cultivation saves the greater part of the fallow working, which is so costly in convertible husbandry, in that after potatoes one needs to plough only once for rye, whereas in fallow working one must plough four times — and thereby the potato cultivation becomes of great importance for the net return of the Belgian system.

The cultivation of fodder crops in Belgium gives, just as little as elsewhere, a significant net return; but the cultivation of clover and turnips becomes important and necessary through the manure production, which alone makes an extended grain cultivation possible, and the cultivation of potatoes through the saving of fallow working.

Remarks and Explanations.
What moved the author to the comparison between the Belgian and the Mecklenburg system was the closer study of Schwerz's excellent work on Belgian agriculture. He found in this work such a quantity of valuable data, he found the information chosen with such care and circumspection, and in them such an inner coherence, that he believed he was undertaking, through the compilation and comparison of the same with his own experiences, a work that would be most instructive for himself — and this expectation has not deceived him.

When the author undertook this comparison, it was not his intention to incorporate it into this work, which for the greater part was already written down for the first time over 6 years ago; but after its completion he found in the results such a close connection with the propositions already developed in this work, that he believed he might communicate the results themselves to the public here — although he very well recognizes the deficiency of this comparison, for which the unity of standpoint is lacking, and therefore can and will present this work only as an attempt.

Where the calculations came to points that are not cited in Schwerz's work, the gap had to be filled by the ratios found for T. — this was in part unavoidable in determining the harvest costs, but especially in determining the general cultivation costs.

Where, for the continuation of the calculation, assumptions about the exhaustion caused by root crops and green fodder, as well as about the quantity and value of the replacement they provide, were unavoidable, the author has adopted those propositions which, according to his experience and the sum of his observations, appear to him as the most correct; but he is far from considering these propositions already settled, and looks forward with desire to the time when his views will be corrected through decisive experiments and through experience on a large scale.

The great deviation found between the market prices cited by Schwerz for fodder potatoes, clover, straw, and other crops destined for livestock feed, and the feed value that I attribute to these crops, makes an explanation necessary here.

In the market prices of these crops are contained:

a. the feed value,

b. the manure value,

c. the transport costs of these crops from the place of their production to the market.

The most careful examination and comparative calculation has convinced me that in Belgium too the net return from livestock, and thus also the feed value of these crops, is only small, and that the greater part of the high market price that these crops have in Belgium arises from the high value that manure has in this country.

My calculations yield for 100,000 square rods of arable land in the Belgian system a rental value of 3,797.2 thalers N2/3.

The actual rent of the arable land for which this calculation is designed amounts, according to Herr Dieroxen's statement in the second part, page 398, of Schwerz's work, to 54 florins per bunder, which for 100,000 square rods of arable land makes 3,706 thalers N2/3.

Between my calculation and the actual paid rent there is therefore a difference of 91.2 thalers, or of about 2 1/2 percent.

The grain prices are assumed in my calculation as Herr Dieroxen gives them in his notes, according to which the Berlin bushel of rye comes to 1 thaler 12 shillings N2/3. In the comparison of the Belgian with the Mecklenburg system, the same grain prices necessarily had to be taken as the basis for both farming systems, and the bushel of rye has therefore been reckoned here for the Mecklenburg system likewise at 1 thaler 12 shillings N2/3. This price agrees almost, but not quite exactly, with the price assumed in the remainder of this work. For this reason, and also because in the distribution of the general cultivation costs and in some of the static assumptions small changes have been made, the land rent found here for convertible husbandry cannot fully agree with the land rent calculated earlier for this farming system.

Furthermore, the calculation of the Belgian system cannot, because it has not proceeded from one and the same standpoint as our earlier investigations, serve to demonstrate the place that the Belgian system could occupy in our isolated state. The comparison presented here must therefore be regarded as an interpolated, self-contained treatise.

§18.
Mention of Some Other Considerations in the Choice of a Farming System.

In the foregoing we have investigated how the two forces: grain price and richness of the soil determine the farming system to be chosen. These forces are indeed the most important but by no means the only ones that affect the choice of a farming system. To investigate the influence of these two named forces, we had to tear them out of the conflict in which they stand in reality with the remaining forces, to free them as it were, so that what each — under given circumstances — can accomplish by itself alone might become visible. For this purpose we have assumed all remaining forces as constant, unchanging quantities, and now these two forces, as the only variable ones, were also the only ones that came into consideration in our investigation.

Under other conditions or from other points of view, however, one or several of the forces we regarded as constant quantities may appear or be conceived as variable; and then the influence that the increase or decrease of these quantities exerts on the farming system becomes the subject of a new inquiry.

The new investigations arising from such altered suppositions do not, to be sure, essentially belong to the purpose of this work; but I believe I must cite some of the most important considerations of this kind, in order as far as possible to prevent misunderstandings.

A. Farming systems with increasing richness of the soil.

It is customary, in comparing two farming systems, to cite it as an advantage of one or the other that through it the soil increases in richness and yield from rotation to rotation.

Now, however, it is not an essential attribute of one or another farming system that it enriches or exhausts the soil. One can exhaust the soil just as well through convertible and crop rotation farming as through three-field farming. A six-field crop rotation with 4 grain sowings is, like the seven-field convertible husbandry with 4 cereal crops, an exhausting system; on the other hand, the seven-field crop rotation with 3 and the six-field convertible husbandry with 2 grain sowings are enriching systems. It lies not in the crop rotation, not in the farming system, whether a farm is an enriching or exhausting one; but solely in the ratio between the manure-producing and the exhausting crops — for which ratio I shall henceforth, for brevity, use the word "sowing ratio."

If now one sets two estates with two different farming systems against each other, and adopts for the one an enriching, for the other an exhausting sowing ratio, and if one now wishes from the final outcome — regardless of whether this proceeds from a correct calculation or from actual experience — to show which farming system deserves the preference: then this investigation answers only the question whether the soil enriched through the sparing farming system has in the end a higher value than the poorer soil which has remained in its previous condition — a question about whose answer in itself no doubt at all can exist.

In such a juxtaposition, that farming system to which one assigns the most enriching sowing ratio must always carry off the victory.

If, then, the comparison of two farming systems is to lead not to confusion of concepts but to clear insight, the following points of view must be sharply distinguished:

1) If the purpose of the farming system is to maintain the soil in a steady state with respect to its richness, which farming system then delivers the highest monetary return?

2) Under what conditions is it advantageous to increase the richness of the soil at the expense of the monetary return, and to what degree can the richness of the soil be increased with advantage?

3) If the purpose of the farming system is directed not toward the highest monetary return but toward the enrichment of the soil, through which farming system is the increase of richness then achieved at the least cost?

The solution of the first, but not of the second and third problem, is the subject of this work: we have indeed placed soils of different levels of richness side by side and compared them; but we have always regarded and had to regard the soil as being in a steady state. The second and third problems, almost more important than the first, await their solution rather from the future advances of the statics of agriculture.

B. Ratio of hay yield from meadows to the size of the arable land.

If an estate in convertible or three-field farming has no meadows connected with it, and the livestock is now maintained in winter on straw alone: then the livestock loses so much weight in winter that it must use the greater part of the grass consumed on the pasture for its recovery and the restoration of its body condition, and can devote only a small part of it to the production of milk or wool. Under these circumstances, however, the gross return from the livestock is so small that the costs of livestock-keeping are barely covered by it, that consequently not only the straw fed, but even the pasture itself yields no return at all.

Under such conditions it becomes necessary to come to the aid of the livestock in winter with grain feed — be it that one gives the grain pure, or that one does not let the straw be threshed clean — in order to maintain it in such a condition that at least the utilization of the pasture is not entirely lost.

The draft animals must, as is evident to everyone, always be maintained in a condition to perform the required work. If hay is lacking, this must obviously be replaced by grain feeding.

But if we compare the production costs of clover hay and potatoes with those of grain, we find that grain is a much more expensive feed than clover hay and potatoes.

In the calculations on the Belgian farming system we found that the production:

of 1 bushel of oats required in labor costs 13.4 shillings,
of 1 bushel of potatoes 3.3 shillings,
of 1 hundredweight of clover hay 4.3 shillings.

According to other observations and calculations — which cannot, however, be communicated here — I further assume that one bushel of oats, including the straw harvested with it, has for the livestock, and in part also for the draft animals — for which, however, the entire quantity of grain cannot be replaced by hay — an equal feed value with 117 lbs. of clover hay, or with 2 1/3 bushels of potatoes.

The production:

of 117 lbs. of hay costs in labor [FORMULA] x 4.3 = 5 1/3 shillings,
of 2 1/3 bushels of potatoes 2 1/3 x 3.3 = 7.7 shillings,
of 1 bushel of oats 13.4 shillings.

The costs of oat feeding are accordingly to those of potato feeding as 100 to 58, and to those of clover hay feeding as 100 to 40.

Or, if one hitherto fed 100 thalers worth of oats to the livestock, one saves through the substitution of potatoes 42 thalers, and through that of clover hay 60 thalers.

It follows from this that in such three-field and convertible farms where hay is either entirely lacking or not present in sufficient quantity, one must resort not to grain feeding but to the cultivation of fodder crops. Since now these fodder crops can be produced as cheaply in no other farming system as in crop rotation farming: it follows further from this that these estates must put such a portion of their arable area as is sufficient to deliver the necessary winter feed of hay, potatoes, etc. into crop rotation farming, even if the grain price has not reached the height and the soil not the degree of fertility at which this farming system would be appropriate for the entire arable area.

But only on rich soil is the production of fodder crops cheap; on poor soil the clover fails entirely, and the potatoes give so small a yield that their production easily costs double what we have calculated for it here.

We are thereby led to a new interesting question.

Will it be appropriate, namely, when meadows are lacking, on arable land of medium or low richness, to bring a part of the arable land to a high manure capacity and introduce crop rotation farming on it, when the enrichment of this part of the arable area can only take place at the expense of the other larger part?

I venture no definite judgment on this; but I believe that closer investigation would answer this question in the affirmative.

The poorer, however, the arable land is on the whole, and the worse the physical quality of the soil, the greater the difficulties in cultivating fodder crops — and this explains why in regions where such soil predominates, meadows have so high a value that their possession is almost the condition under which one can practice agriculture at all.

For our isolated state we have assumed that connected with the arable land there is such an area of meadow as delivers the hay necessary for convertible and three-field husbandry, and that the manure resulting from the meadow hay benefits not the entire arable area but only a part of the arable land lying in a separate rotation. We have then paid no further attention to this part, but directed our investigation solely to the larger division of the arable area — which must maintain itself in and through itself, and to which the necessary meadow hay is delivered against payment of the feed value and against return of the manure resulting from it.

We could equally well have assumed — and perhaps the matter would have become even clearer thereby — that no meadows at all were present, that the arable area of each estate lay in two divisions, of which the smaller was devoted to the procurement of the necessary winter feed and utilized through crop rotation farming, while the larger division in its mode of management followed the laws arising from the change in grain prices and soil richness.

C. Stall-feeding.

Experience teaches that a cow fed abundantly and with strong feed pays for the feed consumed far more highly than a cow maintained frugally.

In stall-feeding, the cows as a rule receive not merely abundant summer feeding but also strong winter feeding.

If one now places the return of a cow fed uniformly and abundantly in summer and winter alongside the return of a pasture cow that is well fed in summer but frugally in winter: then not only in the gross return but also in the net return a very great difference appears in favor of stall-feeding.

Now, however, the frugal winter feeding is by no means necessarily connected with pasture farming; there is rather no reason at all why it could not be given just as abundantly as in stall-feeding.

In comparing stall-feeding with pasture farming, the following two points of view must therefore be precisely distinguished:

1) What share of the higher return of the stall-fed cow is due to the stronger and more uniform feeding throughout the whole year?

2) If the pasture cow is just as abundantly and uniformly nourished as the stall-fed cow, what advantages then still remain to stall-feeding?

The uniformly abundant maintenance of the livestock throughout the whole year is of the greatest importance. In summer stall-feeding, this uniformity is, if only green fodder is present in sufficient quantity, easy to achieve. In pasture farming, however, this is attended with greater difficulties: for in the months of May and June the growth of grass is so vigorous that the livestock cannot consume it all, but lets a part of it shoot into stalks, while in the months of July and August the grass growth diminishes, and the livestock then as a rule suffers want if it is dependent on the ley pastures alone.

To remedy this evil, one would need to be able to provide from time to time in the months of July and August fresh pasture on mown meadows and on the clover stubble; or one would need to haul some green fodder to the pasture as assistance.

If now in this way the uniformity in the nourishment of the livestock is secured, and the pasture cows receive the same winter feed that the stall-fed cows get: then no further reason is to be seen why the pasture cows should not produce from an equal quantity of feed just as much milk and butter as the stall-fed cows.

I have therefore in §16 too, where stall-feeding is discussed, assumed no higher utilization of feed by stall-fed cows than by pasture cows, but have credited stall-feeding only with the essential advantages inseparable from it, and charged it only with its inseparable disadvantages.

But whether the manure obtained in the stall has a higher or lower value than that dropped on the pasture, to which the benefit of the livestock's lying is also added, is so doubtful and so little settled until now that we cannot enter into it at all.

The fundamental condition under which stall-feeding is possible at all is that the soil be rich enough to bear mown clover instead of pasture clover and grasses.

If now this fundamental condition is fulfilled, the essential advantage of stall-feeding consists in the fact that the clover is now mown instead of grazed off, whereby a considerably larger, nearly double quantity of feed, and a greater manure production, i.e. a greater surplus of replacement over exhaustion, is obtained from the same area and the same richness of soil.

On the other hand, however, stall-feeding is essentially and inseparably connected with labors and costs which do not occur in pasture farming, such as hauling in the green fodder, hauling out the manure produced on the stall in summer, and much else.

Whether now stall-feeding or pasture farming is more advantageous depends entirely on whether the value of the additional feed and manure gained through stall-feeding is greater or less than the amount of the costs caused by stall-feeding.

This, however, is again dependent on the greater or lesser price that feed and manure have, and so we see here too that the price of agricultural products, alongside the richness of the soil, ultimately decides whether, when, and where stall-feeding has the advantage over pasture farming.

D. Modifications of the various farming systems.

Our investigations have shown that through the transition from low to high grain prices, as well as through the step-by-step increase of richness in the soil, three different farming systems, namely three-field, convertible, and crop rotation farming, become necessary.

The characteristic features of these farming systems, in the respect in which we here consider them, are:

a. For the three-field system:
1) a part of the field lies constantly as pasture,
2) a third of the arable land is bare fallow annually,
3) all manure is applied to the bare fallow.

b. For convertible husbandry:
1) the entire arable area is alternately used for grain cultivation and for pasture,
2) in every rotation a bare ley fallow occurs,
3) all manure is applied to the fallow,
4) the grain sowings are taken one after another without interruption, and the pasture comes after the grain sowings into the fields that contain the least richness.

c. For crop rotation farming:
1) all arable land bears crops and no bare fallow takes place,
2) the manuring is applied to fodder crops, and these come into those fields that contain the highest richness,
3) grain sowings and fodder crops alternate with each other.

These farming systems are, however, capable of very many modifications, in that one of the characteristic properties of the one system can be sacrificed and in its place a property of the other system adopted. Mixed systems thereby arise, which stand midway between the pure forms, and form the transition from one to the other form.

Since the mixed farming systems in innumerable gradations can approach now more, now less the character of the pure farming systems, it is impossible to enumerate them all, and still less possible to take them all into account in theory. It will here be sufficient to include in the scale of pure forms some of the principal modifications they can undergo.

1) Pure three-field farming.

2) Three-field farming which breaks up its pasture from time to time, about every 9 years, takes a couple of grain sowings from it without manuring, and then lays it down again as pasture.

This farm applies the costs of ley working — which are perhaps not covered by the grain harvests — in order to obtain through the harvested straw a manure supplement for the actual arable land, and in order to rejuvenate the pasture.

3) Convertible farming systems which in a rotation have, besides the ley fallow, also a mellow fallow, and then leave the land for pasture longer than three years. Such a system is the 12-field convertible husbandry with the following crop rotation: 1) Ley fallow, 2) Winter grain, 3) Spring grain, 4) Mellow fallow, 5) Winter grain, 6) Spring grain, 7) Spring grain, 8) to 12) Pasture. This system still bears the traces of the transition from three-field farming, in that it retains the mellow fallow and leaves the land as pasture for so many years in succession. It diminishes the costs of ley working by restricting it to the 12th part of the field, and bears in return the disadvantage that its 4 and 5-year-old pasture produces little grass and manure.

4) Pure convertible husbandry, which keeps no mellow fallow but only ley fallow.

5) Convertible husbandry which, besides the fallow, also manures a part of the after-field or of the fore-field. This system remains in its outward form entirely similar to pure convertible husbandry; but it already has the essential property, that the pasture no longer comes into poor but — at least in part — into rich soil, in common with crop rotation farming, and is therefore to be regarded as a transition to the same.

6) Pure crop rotation farming.

The modifications cited already arise even when the entire arable area from the farmstead to the boundary is of uniform manure capacity. If now, however, the distant soil is, as is usually the case in reality, poorer than the rest of the arable land: then new modifications are thereby established.

The greater costs caused by the cultivation of the distant soil alone already produce the tendency to separate the remote arable land in its mode of management from the rest of the arable land. If inequality of richness now combines with this, this separation is decidedly appropriate. In convertible husbandry this produces a so-called inner field and an outer field. The two then differ in their mode of management in that in the inner field the ratio between the grain-bearing fields and the pasture fields is larger, but in the outer field smaller, than it would be if the entire area lay in one rotation; that thus the former is devoted in the larger proportion to grain cultivation, the latter in the preponderant proportion to pasture.

We have seen in §14 that in our isolated state, three-field farming can already be practiced at the price of 0.470 thalers per bushel of rye, and that only at a price higher than 0.665 thalers per bushel does convertible husbandry give a greater net return than three-field farming. If now there were no other than the pure farming forms, the soil at prices lying between 0.470 thalers and 0.665 thalers could be utilized only through three-field farming, whereas here a stronger manure production than pure three-field farming provides would already be advantageous, if only it could be effected with lower costs than in pure convertible husbandry — which is accomplished by the mixed farming systems.

We have further seen in §16 that in pure convertible husbandry only an average richness of 373 degrees per 1,000 square rods can be utilized, while crop rotation farming usefully employs an average richness of 510 degrees. If now, with rising richness, convertible husbandry were to pass suddenly and at once into crop rotation farming: then a system would be introduced for which the soil is not yet rich enough, and through which therefore the net monetary return would be diminished. Convertible husbandry with manured after-field can utilize a higher average richness than 373 degrees very well, without becoming more costly in its organization than pure convertible husbandry — and it thereby becomes a useful stepping-stone between pure convertible husbandry and crop rotation farming.

If we now imagine, instead of the steady state, a gentle and gradual but lasting rise in grain prices and soil richness — as is also usually the case in reality — we would behold in a single farm in the course of time all the forms that we have here considered as isolated and standing side by side.

When, namely, the two forces — grain price and soil richness — have risen so far that a somewhat more costly farming system than three-field farming would pay, but not yet high enough to make pure convertible husbandry advantageous, then a mixed system, composed of both forms, will be introduced. Since now this mixed system can in innumerable modifications approach now more the one, now more the other form: for every level of grain price and soil richness a farming form exactly corresponding to that level will be able to be found. — Consistency of management presupposed — the gentle rise of both forces will always be accompanied by a gentle change in the farming form, until this finally passes into pure convertible husbandry.

But even here, if the two named forces continue to grow, only a momentary tarrying, not a resting and persisting, will take place.

The farm whose manure capacity has reached the point where the fallow can bear no stronger manuring will, with still further rising richness, apply the surplus manure to the manuring of the after-field, i.e. of the third grain field in which the clover is sown. The clover, which formerly came into the poorest soil, now receives a rich soil, which after the completed pasture years requires either no manuring at all or only weak manuring in the fallow. Thereby the part of the after-field that can be manured is enlarged to an increasing extent from rotation to rotation, until this application of manure too has reached its goal. The further increase of richness then brings about the abolition of the fallow, and with it convertible husbandry at the same time disappears, and crop rotation farming takes its place.

In mountainous regions only the valleys serve for tillage and the mountains are used solely for pasture. Here now, if the mountains absolutely do not permit cultivation, a spread of convertible husbandry over the entire estate is impossible. Thus, with rising grain prices and rising richness of the soil, the transition from three-field farming to crop rotation farming cannot, as on level ground, take place by way of convertible husbandry.

If now the plain is so small in proportion to the mountain pastures and the meadows that the richness of the soil, despite the exhausting three-field farming, increases, then the question arises: how and at what degree of richness must this farming system pass over to crop rotation farming?

My calculations do not extend to this special case, and I therefore cannot decide on it theoretically. Practice, however, has long since settled this question to the effect that under such conditions a part of the fallow, or the entire fallow, is sown with potatoes, clover, peas, flax, etc. A sown fallow, however, ceases to be fallow, and three-field farming under these circumstances loses its most essential characteristic features. Rather it agrees with crop rotation farming on the main point, the abolition of the fallow and the utilization of the entire arable land; but it lacks all the advantages that arise from a correct alternation of crops. It therefore admits of no doubt that under such circumstances crop rotation farming is more advantageous than three-field farming with sown fallow; and indeed, since through our teacher of scientific agriculture, through Thaer, crop rotation farming became known among us, and became a subject of reflection for all educated farmers, a multitude of such three-field farms in the mountainous parts of Silesia, Moravia, and Saxony have passed to crop rotation farming.

In our investigations we have indeed had before us soils of different levels of richness, but always only soil of one and the same physical quality. In reality, on the other hand, we find on almost every estate soil of different quality. The purpose of this work by no means permits us to go further into this; but it must be evident how complicated the task of choosing the farming system becomes when diversity in the richness of the soil, diversity in the quality of the soil, together with the unequal distance of the soil from the farmstead, occur together on one and the same estate; it must be evident that however perfect the theory of agriculture may one day stand, nevertheless the business of the farmer, if he does not wish to be a blind imitator but wishes always to be conscious of the reasons according to which he acts, can never become mechanical, but will always require a serious and profound study of his standpoint and of the conditions of civil society.

Now that the investigations have been carried forward to this point, we can return to the isolated state, namely to the determination of the concentric circles forming around the Town.

§19.
Second Circle. Forest Management.

The plain of the isolated state must supply the Town not only with provisions but also deliver its need for firewood, building timber, useful timber, charcoal, etc.

The question now arises, in which region of the isolated state the production of wood will take place.

If we take the price that wood has in the Town as given, e.g. 16 thalers for a cord of beech firewood of 224 cubic feet, and reckon the transport costs of a cord per mile at 2 thalers, then it would follow from this that from a greater distance than 8 miles no firewood at all could be brought to the Town, even if the production of the wood cost nothing and the land were to bear no land rent at all.

From this it would follow that the distant regions would be excluded from the production of wood for the purpose of sale to the Town, and that the production of wood would have to take place in the vicinity of the Town.

If, on the other hand, we take only the price of grain as known (at 1 1/2 thalers per bushel of rye) and now ask, how high will the price of wood in the Town be under the given conditions, the problem thereby becomes very much more difficult.

Wood and grain have no common measure of their use-value: one cannot be replaced by the other.

"Why," someone might say, "should a cord of wood not be able to fetch 40 thalers, even though a bushel of rye fetches only 1 1/2 thalers? But if this is possible, then your conclusions that wood must be produced in the vicinity of the Town are entirely invalid; it can rather be delivered from a great distance. The objection you make, that such a price ratio is found nowhere, can decide nothing: for almost everywhere remnants of the old primeval forests are still present, and where these are no longer found, the market is more or less supplied with wood from the primeval forests of other regions. But the production of the primeval forests has cost man no labor, care, or capital investment, and they therefore have, at the place where they are found, scarcely a higher exchange value than water, however high the use-value may be. In the isolated state, however, where only the final outcome — not bound to any measure of time — is the subject of investigation, all primeval forests must be regarded as having long since disappeared, and all woodlands as having been produced by human labor. You must therefore demonstrate an inner connection between grain prices and wood prices, if your conclusions are to have validity."

We must concede the cogency of this objection, and now attempt whether we can satisfy the demand made.

Let the price of a cord of wood in the Town be therefore unknown, or equal to y thalers.

Let us now imagine a beech forest of 100,000 square rods divided into 100 compartments, of which one is felled annually: then with a regular management we shall have one compartment with one-year-old, one compartment with two-year-old, and so on up to hundred-year-old trees.

The yield of the felled compartment shall be 500 cords.

The intermediate yields, arising from the removal of trees standing too closely together from the compartments with younger wood, may likewise amount to 500 cords.

Total yield: 1,000 cords.

The costs connected with the management of this forest, such as administration or supervision costs, seeding or planting of the cleared compartment, replanting of dead trees, etc., after deducting the utilization provided by the mast and the hunt, we shall estimate at 500 thalers annually.

Just as in agriculture we have regarded not the entire net return of an estate but only that part of it remaining after deduction of the interest on the capital invested in buildings and other objects of value as land rent: so too in forest management we must regard not the entire return but only the part remaining after deduction of the interest on the capital invested in the timber stock as land rent, or as the return of the land itself as such.

Agriculture cannot be carried on without the investment of a capital tied up in buildings, etc.; the practice of forest management presupposes that trees from one-year-old to hundred- or more-year-old age are present.

One could fell the entire timber stock of all 100 compartments — assuming a sufficiently large market — at once, sell it, and put the money realized out at interest; and only insofar as the annual net return from the wood exceeded the amount of interest obtainable in this way, could one attribute a value to the land itself.

Suppose now that the timber stock of all 100 compartments = 15,000 cords; then, at the interest rate of 5 percent, the interest on the capital invested in the timber stock would equal the value of 750 cords of wood. If these are deducted from the annual yield of the forest = 1,000 cords, then the utilization of the land itself = 250 cords.

On these 250 cords now fall all the expenditures connected with forest management: for if someone had felled the entire timber stock and converted it to a monetary capital, all these expenditures would no longer affect him — and only in order to obtain the excess return of 250 cords are the costs connected with forest management still further applied.

If now the annual expenditures = 500 thalers, then the production costs for a cord, on the stump itself — thus without felling and splitting wages — amount to 2 thalers.

In the production costs — in the sense in which I take this expression — no land rent is contained: for only from the surplus of the actual price over the production costs does the land rent first emerge.

If now the felling and splitting of the wood costs half a thaler per cord: then the cord will cost at the spot itself 2 1/2 thalers.

This price, however, like every other price expressed in money, is valid only for one standpoint, and changes with the change of grain prices. But the solution of our problem requires figures that are valid for every standpoint in the isolated state.

We must here therefore, just as was done in the calculations on agriculture, express 1/4 of the expenditure in money and 3/4 of it in rye.

Of the production costs of a cord = 2 1/2 thalers, 1/4 x 2 1/2 = 0.62 thalers therefore remain expressed in money, and in grain 3/4 x 2 1/2 = 1.88 thalers must be stated. If now the calculation according to which the cord costs 2 1/2 thalers is designed for a standpoint where the bushel of rye is worth 1.291 thalers, then 1.88 thalers are equal in value to [FORMULA] = 1.46 bushels of rye; and thus the production costs of a cord of wood, generally expressed, amount to 1.46 bushels of rye + 0.62 thalers.

Now we can, according to §4, calculate the price of rye for every standpoint in the isolated state: the bushel of rye is worth, namely, in the region x miles from the Town [FORMULA] thalers. If rye is reckoned at this price, then 1.46 bushels of rye + 0.62 thalers = [FORMULA] thalers; or the production costs in the region x miles from the Town amount for 1 cord to [FORMULA] thalers.

The question further arises, how high the transport costs of a cord come to when it is delivered from a region x miles distant to the Town.

With products, however, which lack this common measure, a change in the demand for one or the other product can bring about a great change in the price ratio.

If the cord of firewood formerly stood at equal price with 14 bushels of rye, it would, after this change, retain only the price of about 12 bushels of rye.

Inventions and improvements in production bring about a similar effect as diminished consumption.

After this interruption we now return to the consideration of forest cultivation.

In our calculations we have assumed the annual wood yield at 1,000 cords, and the timber stock of all compartments together at 15,000 cords. According to this, the increment is to the stock as 1 to 15; or the annual wood increment amounts to 1/15 of the timber stock.

Experience has, however, abundantly taught that in purchasing an estate it is most dangerous to appraise the forest connected with the estate according to the quantity of the timber stock, and then to buy according to this appraisal. Some buyers have thereby suffered great losses, some have even lost their entire fortune. For it appeared later that the wood did not bear full interest, i.e. that the annual wood yield amounted not to 1/20, but often only to 1/30, or even only to 1/40 of the timber stock, and that therefore the capital spent on the purchase of the forest brought only 3 1/3 or even only 2 1/2 percent interest.

We also possess appraisals of forests in which the annual increment is assumed by foresters themselves at only 1/40 of the timber stock.

If we now assume that what experience teaches is grounded in the nature of the tree itself, that by virtue of this nature of the trees the forests cannot increase by more than 1/40 of their stock annually, and if we then develop the consequences lying herein: we arrive at very remarkable results.

1) The soil covered with timber yields not merely no land rent, but the return of the soil is actually negative, since the interest on the capital invested in the timber stock already amounts to double the annual return.

2) Every forest owner who knows his own interest must fell all the timber at once and sell it, since through the capital to be realized from the timber sale he draws double the interest, and receives the land of the forest into the bargain, which he can likewise sell. If the market is too limited to sell all the timber at once, the owner must not reseed the annually felled compartment — and so he will, more slowly but no less certainly, bring about the destruction of the forest.

3) Such a gradual destruction of the forests must raise the price of wood; but this is the peculiarity of this case, that the highest wood prices cannot make forest cultivation profitable, and cannot protect the forests from further destruction: for with the increased wood prices the capital invested in the timber stock also grows, and the interest on it always amounts to twice as much as the income from the forest. High wood prices therefore only make the destruction of the forests still more profitable and invite it all the more. Only the fall of the interest rate to below 2 1/2 percent can set a limit to the destruction of the forests. But if the fall of the interest rate does not occur and if such an indispensable material as firewood is not to disappear entirely from the earth: then the governments must take from all private persons the free disposition over their forests and compel the owners by force to draw from their property only half the benefit they could have. But after this violation of the right of property, forest cultivation will be practiced with the greatest negligence, and so this measure too can afford help only for a short time.

If we now consider, on the other hand, the growth of a young tree, say that of a young fir, we find that the two-year-old fir perhaps surpasses the one-year-old in mass tenfold, that the three-year-old fir again amounts to about seven times the two-year-old, and so on; that thus the annual increment not merely amounts to a fraction of the mass the tree already had, but surpasses that mass many times over. In the following years of the tree's life, the absolute increase in mass rises from year to year, but the relative increase, i.e. the annual increment in proportion to the mass of the tree, must nevertheless decrease, because the mass with which the increment is compared grows ever larger. If now, say, in the fifth year the annual increment equals the mass the tree already had, then in the sixth year the increment will amount to about 9/10, in the seventh year perhaps 81/100, and so on.

With this step-by-step decrease of the relative increment, we must undeniably finally reach a point where the annual increment amounts to 1/20 of the mass of the tree.

If we now imagine, instead of the individual tree, an entire timber compartment, or a section, in which nothing but trees of equal age stand: then for this entire area too a point in time must arrive where the wood increment amounts to exactly 1/20 of the entire timber stock on this area.

If now the compartment is felled precisely at this point in time, and if one then compares the wood yield with the sum of the timber stock of all the compartments that are planted with trees from one-year-old to harvestable age, it will be found that the annual yield amounts to more than 1/20 of the timber stock: for since the increment in the harvestable compartment still amounts to 1/20, but in all compartments with younger trees is significantly larger, the increment on average, i.e. for all compartments together, must also be greater than 1/20.

If then on the one hand it is fully established that the nature of the trees makes an even stronger relative increment than 1/20 possible, and on the other hand the experience that in some forests the increment amounts to only 1/40 is incontestable: then it follows from this that the management of such forests must be most incorrect and faulty.

In forests where 100- and 200-year-old trees stand together with trees of 10 and 20 years of age and are intermixed, in which trees are present that no longer grow at all but occupy a large space and suppress the young timber, where consequently even the absolute increment is very small and this must be compared with a very large timber stock; there the relative increment can easily sink to 1/40 and even lower.

Such a forest cultivation, or rather lack of cultivation, can only be justified where the wood cannot be sold, and the land itself has so small a value that the costs of grubbing out the tree trunks and converting the forest ground into arable land cannot be covered.

In the earlier centuries this may have been the case for a great part of Germany. Conditions have since changed very much; but this change of conditions has not everywhere brought about a change in the treatment of forests, and we find even in our day many forests that are treated in the traditional but now most inconsistent manner.

But even where the correct insight already prevails, the forests can only gradually be torn from their natural state: for just as the lifetime of trees far exceeds that of man, so too several human lifetimes are needed to spread the correct forest cultivation over an entire forest area.

With correct forest cultivation, only trees of equal age may stand together, and these must be felled as soon as the relative increment has sunk to 5 percent. In the case of high forests, the trees will then not be allowed to grow out fully; the rotation period will have to be much shorter than the lifetime of the trees extends; and it is open to question whether the rotation of the beech forest, which we have here assumed at 100 years, should not be still shorter according to these principles.

The consideration that the wood of more fully grown trees has a higher value as fuel and is paid for more dearly than the wood of young trees can indeed extend the rotation beyond the point where the relative increment amounts to 5 percent; but only by a few years: for this increase in value of the wood as fuel cannot long grow in the preponderant, or even equal proportion with the production costs rising through loss of interest.

Quite differently does the matter stand with building timber. This must have a certain thickness if it is to be usable at all, and the trees must not be felled before they have reached this thickness. The rotation will therefore have to be much longer than in the case of firewood production. The production costs of building timber are thereby very considerably increased; but since it cannot be dispensed with: an equal mass, e.g. a cubic foot, must also be paid for more highly the thicker the timber is, and indeed the price must rise to such a height and to such a degree that thereby the production costs of building timber of every degree of thickness are exactly compensated.

Building timber must therefore, at equal weight, have a higher price than firewood, and the transport costs in proportion to the value amount in the former to less than in the latter.

For this reason, within the circle devoted to forest cultivation in the isolated state, the production of building timber must take place in the part of this circle most distant from the Town.

The waste from building timber, which used as firewood could not bear the transport costs to the Town, can, by being converted through charring into a material of lower specific weight, still be brought to the Town with advantage; and so the outer edge of the wood circle will supply the Town not only with building timber but also with charcoal.

At the inner edge of the wood circle, lying nearest to the Town, it will perhaps be advantageous to cultivate fast-growing trees, whose wood as fuel admittedly has not so high a value as beech wood, but which from the same area deliver a greater annual yield of wood; while the more distant region can bring to the Town only firewood of the highest value.

Thus within the circle devoted to forest cultivation, several divisions or concentric rings would themselves arise, in which the cultivation is directed toward raising different kinds of trees.

This circle must supply the Town and the circle of free farming with wood; but not the circles lying further back, or more distant from the Town. These namely produce their own supply of wood, but can deliver nothing to the Town, and are in this respect indifferent for the Town; for which reason wood cultivation will not be further mentioned in the consideration of the remaining circles.

§20.
Retrospect on the First Circle, with Particular Reference to the Cultivation of Potatoes.

The investigations in the preceding sections have shown that the production of firewood must take place near the Town, and that forest cultivation in proportion to agriculture yields an ever higher land rent, the nearer to the Town it is practiced.

We have, however, already assumed earlier that the circle of free farming will occupy the immediate environs of the Town. We have indeed supported this assumption with reasons; but the reasons themselves are not developed deeply enough to be able to prove the assertion set forth, and we must therefore take up this subject for investigation once more.

Free farming and forest management contend as it were for the place where they are to be practiced: both lay claim to the immediate surroundings of the Town. Since, however, they cannot be practiced below and beside each other, the question arises which of the two farming methods will carry off the victory and displace the other.

Now, consistently, in every region that farming system must be practiced through which the soil is most highly utilized, and the above question is thus reduced to the question: "which farming system gives the highest land rent in the immediate environs of the Town?"

We must therefore investigate whether in the vicinity of the Town the cultivation of another crop yields a still higher land rent than forest management gives; and we turn in this respect to the consideration of the cultivation of the potato.

Price of potatoes in the Town.

Between potatoes and rye there exists a common measure, namely that of their nutritive capacity, and when — as is here presupposed — no special preference for the one or the other crop exists: the price of both will stand precisely in the ratio of their nutritive capacity.

Now the chemical analyses and the experiences in distilling almost all agree that three heaped bushels of potatoes are equal to one bushel of rye in flour content as well as in yield of spirits; and we accordingly assume the price of one bushel of potatoes in the Town itself to be 1/3 of the rye price, thus 1/2 thaler per bushel.

In the following calculations on the yield of potatoes and the costs connected with their cultivation, the investigations on the Belgian farming system communicated in §17 serve as the basis.

We have there assumed that, at equal richness of the soil, on the same area where 1 bushel of rye grows, 9 bushels of potatoes grow, and we have there found that the production of 5.7 bushels of potatoes costs no more labor than that of 1 bushel of rye.

A crop which, in proportion to rye, from the same area delivers three times as much nutritive substance, and which rewards the labor of man with double the quantity of nutritive substance, is in truth so remarkable, and its general diffusion is so well suited to bring about a complete revolution in the conduct of agriculture, that we necessarily had to devote a place in this work to the consideration of this crop, even if we were not called to it by the determination of the boundaries of the first circle of our isolated state.

We have already earlier, in the assumption that the plain of the isolated state has the degree of richness that the soil everywhere bears 8 grains of rye after bare fallow, excepted the circle of free farming from this, and assigned to it, because of the purchase of manure from the Town, a much higher richness. In the following calculations I now assume for this circle the same soil richness that we ascertained in §17 for the Belgian farming system.

When the harvested potatoes are fed to livestock, they return through being fed fully as much manure as their production has cost the soil. The matter stands quite differently, however, when the potatoes are not fed but sold.

Just as in grain cultivation not all arable land can be sown with grain, but a part of the field must bear crops that return more manure than they have taken from the soil, so that the exhaustion caused by the grain may be replaced, so too in the cultivation of potatoes for the purpose of sale not the entire arable area can be sown with potatoes.

If one now wishes to calculate how much a given area, e.g. of 100,000 square rods, can deliver annually in potatoes, and if one wishes to compare the yield of nutritive substance that this area gives through the cultivation of potatoes with that which the same area would bring through the cultivation of grain: then it must first be ascertained what fraction of the whole area can bear potatoes, if the soil is to maintain itself in and through itself at equal richness.

In grain cultivation, straw is always harvested together with the grain, and this straw already replaces a part of the exhaustion; but the replacement that the straw provides is not sufficient to cover the whole exhaustion. In a seven-field convertible husbandry with the crop rotation: 1) Fallow, 2) Rye, 3) Barley, 4) Oats, 5) Pasture, 6) Pasture, 7) Pasture, we find as many pasture fields as grain fields; and if on good soil this farming system maintains itself at equal strength, it follows that one grain field must be combined with one pasture field if the exhaustion caused by the grain sowing, after deduction of the replacement from the straw harvested with it, is to be made good; or the exhaustion of one grain field is as great as the manure production of one pasture field and the replacement from the straw together.

The potatoes return no straw, and their exhaustion must therefore be entirely replaced through the cultivation of manure-producing crops.

If we now, in order to arrive at an easier overview, take one pasture field as the unit, we can ask: how many pasture fields will have to be combined with one potato field if the exhaustion of the potatoes is to be covered by the manure production of the pasture?

Now, the absolute exhaustion of potatoes is the greater, the richer the soil on which they grow, or the greater the yield; the manure production of the pasture is likewise greater on rich, smaller on poor soil. To cover the exhaustion of one potato field of given richness, more pasture fields are needed if the pasture comes on poor soil, a smaller number if it comes on rich soil.

My calculations on this yield the following:

a. If the potato field has the same richness as the barley field, but the pasture fields have equal richness with the pasture fields in convertible husbandry: then 2 3/4 (more exactly 2.76) pasture fields are needed to replace the exhaustion caused by the potatoes.

b. If the potato field and the pasture fields contain equal richness: then one potato field must be combined with 1 5/6 pasture fields.

c. If the potatoes are produced on very rich soil, where clover cultivation with stall-feeding takes place, and where clover and potatoes come into soil of equal richness: then 1 1/2 (more exactly 1.46) clover fields replace the exhaustion of one potato field.

If we now wish to compare the yield of nutritive substance that potato cultivation furnishes in proportion to grain cultivation, we find in the case considered under a: 1) that 3 grain fields of 1,000 square rods each, on soil which in convertible husbandry delivers 10 grains, give a yield of 235 bushels reduced to rye; 2) that one potato field of the richness of the barley field, on the other hand, produces 720 bushels of potatoes = 240 bushels reduced to rye. The 3 grain fields must be combined with 3 pasture fields, the potato field with 2 3/4 pasture fields, to cover the exhaustion. To the production of 235 bushels of rye therefore 6 fields belong, and to the production of 720 bushels of potatoes = 240 bushels of rye 3 3/4 fields belong.

In grain cultivation one field of 1,000 square rods therefore produces, in nutritive mass reduced to rye, 39 bushels; in potato cultivation, however, one field delivers 64 bushels reduced to rye. The ratio of yield between grain and potatoes is therefore as 39 to 64, or as 100 to 164.

The ratio which at first superficial view results, according to which potatoes from the same area deliver three times as much nutritive substance as rye, thus undergoes a great reduction upon closer examination; nevertheless the superiority of the potatoes still remains most significant.

According to Adam Smith, the price of all goods can be resolved into the three elements: wages, profit of capital, and land rent. We have been led by our investigations to decompose the price of agricultural products into the three components: production costs, transport costs, and land rent; and although production and transport costs can undeniably be resolved again into wages and profit of capital, we have not yet been called by the course of our investigation to make this separation.

The substance whose price determination is here in question can, however, be called neither a good nor a product, and in vain shall we ask: how much wages, profit of capital, and land rent its production has cost; or how great the production and transport costs are, and how much the land rent falling on its production amounts to. This substance, whose production is involuntary, whose quantity can be neither increased nor decreased by an increase or decrease of demand, and which the possessor must remove even if it involves the greatest costs, which consequently has a negative value for him — such a substance is indeed of so peculiar a kind that its price can be determined by none of the laws mentioned above, and the question of how its price is to be ascertained thereby acquires a special interest.

In the price determination of street manure, very different interests are therefore at play. The town-dwellers must be rid of the manure, even if they receive nothing for it, or even have to pay for its removal; the farmers living near the Town can pay a high price, the more distant farmers only a low price. Which of these different interests will gain the upper hand and determine the price?

We must here distinguish two cases:

1) when the street manure is present in such great quantity that it cannot be entirely used up on all estates up to 4 3/4 miles from the Town;

2) when the quantity of street manure is not so great that it can satisfy the manure demand of all estates up to 4 3/4 miles away.

In the first case, after the entire region up to 4 3/4 miles from the Town is supplied with manure, a part will still remain over which must be removed at the expense of the Town. If now under these circumstances the Town wished to charge the farmers who fetch the manure for it, e.g. take 0.83 thalers per wagonload: then all farmers living more than 4 miles from the Town would thereby give up fetching manure, the remaining portion would be enlarged, and the costs to be applied to its removal would be considerably increased. The Town will therefore, if it does not wish to act against its own interest, have to let the distant farmer have the manure for nothing. But will the Town then be able to charge the nearby farmer for the refuse or street manure, when the more distant farmer receives it for nothing? Will the seller of a commodity be able to determine its price according to the benefit it brings the buyer, and sell it to one cheaply, to another dearly? This does not seem achievable without arbitrary compulsory measures; and so we must assume that under the given circumstances the street refuse will receive no price anywhere, but will be available for nothing.

In the second case, when the street manure is not present in sufficient quantity to satisfy the demand of the entire region that can usefully employ it, the nearer and more distant farmers will compete with each other. If, for example, the manure were initially available for nothing: then it would be partly hauled to distant regions, and the nearer regions, for which it has so high a value, would not receive their supply. To secure this supply, the inhabitants of the nearer region would be compelled to pay for the manure a price sufficient to make fetching it to distant regions unprofitable. Suppose the quantity of street manure were sufficient for the demand of a circle of 4 miles around the Town, then they would have to pay 0.83 thalers per wagonload: for if they wished to pay less, e.g. only 1/2 thaler per wagonload, the region lying beyond this circle could still buy and fetch the manure with advantage, and the nearer region would then not receive its supply.

The land rent which our calculation gives for the soil lying in the immediate environs of the Town appears so enormously high that we are prompted to ask: whether in reality any example of so high a land rent occurs anywhere.

Now, we should not be surprised if in reality no such example could be found: for firstly, our calculations are based on a soil which not only contains the highest usefully applicable richness but is also of excellent physical quality, and such a soil may occur in contiguous larger areas only seldom; secondly, in reality there is no considerable, much less a very large town that does not lie on a navigable river; through the river, however, the circle that supplies the Town with potatoes is very much enlarged, and this has, as we shall soon see, the consequence that the price of potatoes per bushel sinks below 1/3 of the rye price.

Upon closer inquiry, however, we find not merely examples of an equal, but of a still higher land rent.

In the first decades of this century, near Hamburg the cattle pastures lying in the immediate environs of the Town yielded a rent of one mark per square rod, which amounts to about 37 thalers in gold for 100 square rods.

According to Sinclair (Grundgesetze des Ackerbaues, page 558), one acre of garden land near London yields:

in poor rates, tithes, and other levies 8 pounds sterling;
together therefore 18 pounds sterling;
this makes for 100 square rods approximately 58 thalers.

Now the rent is not yet pure land rent, but from the rent must be deducted the interest on the capital invested in glass windows, enclosures, etc. to find the actual land rent; but this interest can be very considerable, and the pure yield of the soil still exceeds that which we have found for the isolated state.

However high the purchase price of this soil near the great town must rise through its high yield, this is yet only the prelude to an incomparably higher increase of the ground value in the town itself. He who outside the gates of the town wishes to build a new house and buy a building site for it will need to pay no more for it than the value that this site had for the production of garden crops. After the building of the house, the land rent that this place formerly gave is transformed into ground rent; but the amount of both is at this point still entirely equal. Further into the town, however, this ground rent rises ever higher, until at last in the center of the town, or at the main marketplace, the mere site where a house can stand is paid for at more than 100 thalers per square rod.

If we investigate more closely the causes why the ground rent of houses rises ever more toward the center of the town, we find them in the saving of labor, the greater convenience, and the reduction of time lost in the conduct of business; we find, therefore, that ground rent and land rent are regulated by one and the same principle.

We must here now observe that even though we have calculated the land rent that potato cultivation yields, the land rent that the soil actually gives in this circle cannot thereby yet be determined: for firstly, the nature of plants does not permit them to be grown every year on the same spot without alternation with other crops; and secondly, a multitude of other crops must be produced in this circle, which partly yield a higher, partly a lower land rent than the potatoes.

The potatoes can therefore on every estate occupy only a part of the field, and the land rent of the entire field results only from the net return of all crops occurring in a rotation. This calculation can, however, only be furnished by a farmer who himself lives near a large town and takes the data for it from his own farming. Such an investigation would be very difficult but also most instructive, and it would bring to light and illuminate many obscurities in the theory of agriculture.

In every case, however, the potatoes will occupy a large part of the arable land in the circle of free farming, and from the knowledge of the land rent that potato cultivation yields we can sufficiently infer the actual land rent to be able to decide the question which place free farming and forest management will occupy in the isolated state.

If we now take labor itself as the standard, the result follows that on rich soil the same labor through potato cultivation produces double the nutritive mass for men as through grain cultivation; but that on poorer soil, the labor applied to potato cultivation produces no greater product than the labor devoted to grain cultivation.

If now on the one hand, on soil bearing only 8 grains, the production costs of potatoes are so considerably increased; if on the other hand we consider that on soil of this richness no clover cultivation with stall-feeding can take place, that then 2 3/4 pasture fields are required to replace the exhaustion of the potato field, that consequently only a small part of the arable area may be sown with potatoes: then we can convince ourselves even without a more exact calculation that a soil of this richness lying 4 miles from the Town cannot, through the cultivation of potatoes for the purpose of sale, be raised to a land rent of 2,458 thalers, and that consequently forest cultivation cannot be pushed back by such a farming system.

The common measure that exists between rye and potatoes through the ratio of nutritive capacity therefore determines the maximum of the price of potatoes at a very great demand; at a smaller demand, however, the price of potatoes is regulated not by this ratio of nutritive capacity, but by the costs of bringing them to market.

Now the Town of the isolated state is of such a size that its demand for potatoes will not be fully satisfied by the circle of free farming; the price of potatoes must therefore rise to the maximum, and our above assumption that potatoes in the Town itself will be worth 1/3 of the rye price is thereby justified.

It deserves to be noted that potatoes, although in proportion to grain they deliver from the same area so great a quantity of nutritive substance, are nevertheless so little suited to supplying a very large town with provisions without the help of grain.

In farming system A we found that the land rent of potato cultivation on very rich soil already vanishes at 9.3 miles distance from the Town, while grain cultivation on soil of far lesser richness yields a land rent up to 31.5 miles from the Town. If now potatoes were the only vegetable food, the cultivation of the soil would already have to end at 9.3 miles from the Town, the isolated state would therefore have a small extent, and the Town itself would have to contain a very much smaller population.

Potatoes still offer material for many questions and investigations. Thus one could, for example, raise the questions:

1) what effect does the spread of potato cultivation, when the potatoes are used as food for men, have on the grain price;

2) what influence does the introduction of potato cultivation, when the potatoes are used as livestock feed, have on the price of livestock products and on the magnitude of the land rent that livestock farming yields?

For such an investigation and for the solution of the questions raised, we are not yet entitled here, since we lack the necessary premises. Only the following remark may still be in its place here.

Potatoes can, as we have seen, in the isolated state be delivered to a small town at half the price they have in the large town. In reality, the location of towns on rivers diminishes but does not abolish this difference. Now as the potatoes become more and more a staple food and restrict the consumption of grain, the difference in the wages paid in both towns must also grow ever greater. For even if the real wage, i.e. the sum of necessities that the worker can procure for his wages, is entirely equal in both towns: yet this wage expressed in money must, according to the difference in the price of the primary necessities of life, turn out very differently.

Now factory and manufactured goods can be manufactured most cheaply at the place where wages are lowest, if all other circumstances are equal: and so in the greater use of potatoes for human nourishment there lies a tendency against the accumulation of people in very large towns.

Third Circle. Crop Rotation Farming.

The third circle, immediately adjoining the forest, is occupied by crop rotation farming. Here the land has a sufficiently high value — resulting from the proximity to the Town and the accordingly still considerable grain prices — to make the abolition of the fallow and the introduction of improved crop rotations advantageous.

§22.
Fourth Circle. Convertible Husbandry.

Beyond the region of crop rotation farming, where grain prices have fallen to the point where the costs of the more intensive cultivation can no longer be repaid, convertible husbandry takes over. This is the most extensive of the circles, since it can be profitably practiced over a wide range of grain prices.

§23.
Fifth Circle. Three-Field Farming.

At still greater distances from the Town, where grain prices have fallen so low that even convertible husbandry can no longer cover its costs, the three-field system — with its lower labor requirements — still yields a positive net return.

§24.
By What Law is the Price of Grain Determined?

In the isolated state, the price of grain in the Town must be high enough that the most distant estate — whose product is still needed to feed the Town — can cover its production and transport costs. The grain price is thus determined by the production costs of that grain which is produced under the least favorable conditions, but which is still needed for consumption.

This is a law of the greatest importance. The price of grain is not set by the average costs of production, but by the costs at the margin of cultivation. All estates nearer to the Town, which produce at lower cost, receive the same price and thereby earn a surplus — the land rent.

[Extended discussion of grain price determination, marginal cost theory, and its implications]

§25.
Origin of Land Rent.

Land rent arises from the advantage of location or superior soil quality. The estate near the Town has lower transport costs; the estate on fertile soil has lower production costs. In both cases, the owner receives the Town market price and the surplus over his own costs constitutes the land rent.

Land rent is therefore not a cause of the high price of grain, but a consequence of it. If grain had no value, there would be no land rent. Land rent is the result of the fact that soil of different quality or location must be cultivated to meet demand, and that all grain sells at the price determined by the marginal producer.

[Extended discussion of the origin and nature of land rent, its relationship to grain prices, and comparison with Ricardo's theory]

§26.
Sixth Circle. Livestock Farming.

Beyond the boundary of the three-field system, at the most distant fringes of the cultivated plain, the land can no longer produce grain for the Town market at a profit. But livestock products — butter, cheese, and live animals — can still be sent to Town, for their value relative to their weight is much higher than that of grain, and their transport costs are proportionally much lower.

In this outermost circle, therefore, livestock farming predominates. The land is used almost exclusively for pasture, and only enough grain is grown for local consumption. Butter is produced and sent to the Town; live cattle are driven to market. This circle extends outward until even livestock farming can no longer cover its costs, at which point the cultivated plain ends and the uncultivated wilderness begins.

§33.
On the Restriction of Free Trade.

If the government of the isolated state restricts the importation of certain goods — for example, if it prohibits the import of grain from beyond a certain distance, or imposes tariffs on manufactured goods — what are the consequences?

Restrictions on trade always reduce the total wealth of the nation. The grain price in the Town may be artificially raised, benefiting some farmers but harming consumers. The cultivated area may be artificially expanded into regions where the soil should properly be left uncultivated, or artificially contracted.

The grain price is determined not by one cause alone but by two: the cost at which the most distant producer can deliver grain, and the ability of consumers to pay this price. When these two forces are in equilibrium, the state is in a steady state. Trade restrictions disturb this equilibrium.

Third Section.

Effect of Taxes on Agriculture.

§34.
Taxes Proportional to the Scale of Operations.

A. In Relation to the Isolated State.

If a tax is levied on each unit of cultivated land, or on each bushel of grain produced, this increases the costs of production. The effect is the same as if the transport costs had increased: the boundary of profitable cultivation contracts toward the Town, and land that was previously at the margin of cultivation must now be abandoned.

On the estate at the boundary of cultivation, where the land rent was already zero, the new tax makes the land rent negative. The farmer must either abandon cultivation or, if he is the owner, accept a reduction in his land rent. The boundary of cultivation therefore retreats.

As the boundary of cultivation retreats, the cultivated plain shrinks. The population that can no longer be fed by the reduced agricultural area must emigrate. Buildings and invested capital on the abandoned land lose their value.

The amount of the tax that the government receives is therefore less than the total loss inflicted on the nation. Not only does the government take the tax, but in addition, the capital and labor invested in the abandoned territory are lost, and the prosperity of the entire state diminishes.

B. In Relation to Reality.

In the real world, the effects described above do not manifest themselves so sharply and immediately. The boundary of cultivation does not retreat overnight. Some farmers will try to continue for a time, accepting lower incomes. The full effects may take years or decades to work themselves out.

Nevertheless, the fundamental law holds: a tax on agriculture causes a contraction of the cultivated area and a reduction in the population that the land can support. The loss to the nation is greater than the revenue the government collects.

After a longer period, the customary farming system will adapt to the new conditions and restrict cultivation to the land that can pay the costs. But through this slow and uncertain transition, the nation loses a far greater capital than the tax itself required.

§35.
Effect of the Tax When the Consumption of Grain Remains the Same.

What has been said above is valid only for the case where the new tax reduces grain consumption. But where the people are rich enough to pay a higher price for grain, and consumption itself remains the same, the effect of the tax is quite different.

If, for example, in the isolated state the distant regions cease, as a result of the tax, to deliver grain to the Town, this immediately creates a shortage in the Town; the shortage produces higher prices; the higher price again makes it possible for the distant regions to grow grain for the Town, and so the equilibrium is restored. Since the Town's demand cannot be satisfied unless grain cultivation extends to 31.5 miles from the Town, the price of grain must also rise high enough that the most distant estate receives not only its production and transport costs, but also the newly added tax.

In this case, therefore, the consumer of grain must pay the entire tax laid upon agriculture.

According to the teachings of the physiocratic system, all taxes laid upon trades and industries ultimately fall back upon agriculture. If a craftsman, for example, must pay a trade tax of 10 thalers, he does advance these 10 thalers, but to survive he must raise the price of his wares enough to be compensated for the outlay. According to these views, it would therefore be much more expedient to lay the tax directly on agriculture, rather than to collect it from agriculture by a roundabout route.

We have now seen, however, that the tax laid upon the farmer is paid not by the farmer himself, but by the consumer of grain — when consumption remains the same.

While these two classes of citizens shift the tax laid upon them onto others, the civil servants living on salaries cannot unilaterally raise the price of their labor, and these must now pay not only the tax laid upon them, but also the increased price of all necessities. Under these circumstances, however, no more candidates will present themselves for government offices, and the state will be compelled to raise the salaries of its officials enough to compensate for both the tax itself and the increased prices of all necessities.

It therefore appears that, with the exception of capitalists living on their interest, every other class is compensated for the tax, and that the state could increase taxes to the utmost without endangering the welfare of the whole, since none of its active citizens is thereby oppressed, because each only advances the tax and does not himself pay it.

The conclusions by which we obtain this very striking result rest on the assumption that after the introduction of the tax, consumption remains the same, and we must now examine whether this assumption is correct or not.

As we have already mentioned in §33, the price of grain is not determined one-sidedly by the amount of the costs that delivering it to market causes the farmer, but simultaneously also by the ability of consumers to pay this price.

In the Town as well as in the country there is a great multitude of people whose income just barely suffices to buy the most necessary requirements. If the price of grain rises, their income or earnings do not suffice to procure it in adequate quantity. However indispensable grain may be, the poorer consumer can never give more for it than his earnings and his assets together amount to; if both are insufficient, he must make do with smaller quantities, that is, go hungry and ultimately perish.

Suppose now that in the isolated state, as a result of a tax falling directly or indirectly on agriculture, the price of grain rises: since the poorer inhabitants of the Town cannot pay this price, consumption must decrease. But since at the moment when the tax is introduced, production has not yet decreased, and therefore no real shortage of grain can exist: the reduced consumption must produce a surplus of grain, the price must fall again and indeed fall so low that even the poorer class can again procure it in adequate quantity, i.e. grain sinks back to its former average price.

At this average price, however, agriculture, now burdened with a tax, can no longer be carried on at its former extent, and all the effects of the tax cited in the previous section now appear: contraction of the cultivated plain, emigration of the inhabitants of the abandoned district and of the town-dwellers who worked for this district.

When the state is in a steady state, and all relationships are in equilibrium, the price that consumers can pay coincides exactly with the price at which the most distant producers can deliver the grain, and we therefore did not need to take into account this twofold determinant of the grain price in the first section of this work. But as soon as the previous equilibrium is disturbed by the introduction of taxes or by other interventions of state power, the two determining causes also diverge from each other.

We therefore find in reality that in all rich countries grain prices are high, and in all poor countries low.

A grain shortage, even a famine in northern Norway, produces no high grain prices either in the rest of Europe or in Norway itself, because the people are too poor to pay high prices. By contrast, a moderate grain demand in London raises the grain price throughout Europe, and from all the ports of the continent, ships then hasten with grain to this world market.

We find in our day among all European states a striving, through high tariffs or through complete import bans, to keep foreign grain from the domestic market, in order through artificially created high prices to promote domestic agriculture.

That agriculture is improved both intensively and extensively through high grain prices is entirely well-founded and also follows from all our previous investigations; but it has been overlooked that if one wishes to enforce high grain prices, one must simultaneously also make the people rich enough to pay these high prices. If this does not happen simultaneously, the increase in grain prices is only of short duration, and after some years the price sinks again until it is in equilibrium with the means of payment of the consumers. Through the artificial elevation of grain prices, one simultaneously drives away the factories and manufactures that work for export, since these now migrate to countries with low grain prices; thereby, however, the means of payment of the nation are not increased but diminished, and the final consequence of this measure must be, instead of the intended increase, a decrease in the lasting grain prices.

The effect which a tax exerts at its first introduction must be precisely distinguished from that which it produces in its final outcome, because between the two there is a great difference.

The first introduction of a tax brings impoverishment and misery to the people, because the total income reduced by the amount of the tax must still be distributed among the same number of people, and because the surplus people who can no longer be fed do not emigrate voluntarily, but must first be, as it were, drawn by lot through a struggle for existence ruinous to all, in that those who succumb in this struggle are compelled to emigrate.

But once the number of people has again come into equilibrium with the national income through emigration or through a reduction in marriages: it is by no means necessary that any member of the active classes (the landowner I count among the active classes only in his capacity as administrator of his estate, but not in his capacity as recipient of land rent) need live worse, i.e. receive fewer enjoyments for his work, than before the introduction of the tax. For it depends on the character of the people to what degree they will endure privations and exertions before deciding on emigration or a reduction in marriages. If now the national character has undergone no change through the introduction of the tax itself — which does not necessarily follow from it — then the active classes, such as craftsmen, day-laborers, tenant farmers, etc., will not have less remaining for their maintenance after payment of the tax than before.

We also find in reality that in England, so heavily burdened with taxes, all these classes certainly do not live less well than in Russia, where taxes are small.

The long-standing taxes are therefore by no means a misfortune for individuals; but the number of individuals has been reduced by the tax, and the state has with these individuals simultaneously lost the capital they possessed and the land rent they received. In that the state, through an increase of taxes, diminishes its power as well as its wealth and population, it thereby acts against itself and against its own interest, and so we find that the rightly understood advantage of the state, or of the ruler representing the state, is closely and intimately connected with that of the people.

§36.
Taxes on Trades and Manufacturing.
If a considerable tax is imposed on the craftsman or manufacturer, he is undoubtedly inclined to have this tax repaid through an increase in the price of his goods. At the higher price, however, a multitude of people must give up or restrict the consumption of these goods; the reduced consumption then produces a surplus of goods of this kind, which in turn causes the price to fall.

If the manufacturers and craftsmen cannot subsist at this price, a part of them must abandon their trade and seek another dwelling place. After this has happened, the market is more sparingly supplied, the price of the goods rises again, and must — since labor in this trade cannot be permanently paid less than in other trades — finally rise high enough that the imposed tax is thereby repaid.

As a result, an indispensable commodity for the farmer — for example, wrought iron — becomes more expensive, the cultivation costs of the soil rise, the land rent of the most distant estate sinks below zero, and the same phenomena already often cited, which a tax laid on agriculture produces, then appear.

[Extended discussion of how trade taxes ultimately affect agriculture, the difference between the isolated state and reality with respect to international trade, and the effects of competitive taxation between states]

In the isolated state, neither a single trade nor agriculture can be burdened with a tax without all other classes being affected by it. Just as no limb of the human body can be injured without the whole body suffering, so too in the isolated state neither a single trade nor agriculture can be burdened with a tax without all other classes being seized by it.

But conditions are quite different in reality, when several states come into contact with each other. When in a European state with free trade a particular trade is too heavily burdened with taxes, the tradesman cannot compensate himself through an increase in the price of his goods, because the same goods are still manufactured just as cheaply in other countries, where no such tax exists, and can now be imported at a price for which the domestic trade cannot supply them. Here, therefore, a trade can be entirely crushed by the tax imposed upon it, while the other classes remain almost uninjured.

§37.
Consumption Taxes and Poll Tax.
Consumption taxes laid on goods that do not belong to the necessary requirements of life and that can be entirely dispensed with by the poorer classes restrict the luxury of the rich and well-to-do, without hindering the expansion of soil cultivation and the useful employment of capital. They are disadvantageous only for those occupied with the production and processing of luxury goods: for the tax reduces the consumption of these goods and a part of these people thereby loses their livelihood; but this class of workers is neither so numerous nor so important for the state as the class occupied with the processing of the necessary requirements of life.

If the tax on luxury goods is laid on goods coming from abroad, then only the merchants, sailors, and carters who handle the transport of these goods lose their livelihood.

Consumption taxes laid on the indispensable necessities of the common man are far more injurious than poll taxes. For on the one hand, the collection of the consumption tax is so costly that a large part of the revenue is thereby consumed, so that far more must be taken from the subjects than the treasury needs and receives; on the other hand, this tax also strikes the truly destitute person who lives only on the charity of others; whereas the poll tax is collected only from those persons who possess earnings and a real income.

The poll tax, which is considered the most unequal of all taxes, because without regard to income and assets it takes as much from the poor as from the rich, does not, when it has been long established, exert any continuously disturbing effects on the happiness of the subjects: for it is obvious that the common worker must earn enough to support his family frugally and at the same time pay the poll tax. The worker must therefore be compensated for the tax through increased wages, and he lives no less happily than the worker in another state where no poll tax at all exists.

But quite different is the effect of the tax when it is first introduced, which can be most clearly surveyed in the isolated state.

The worker, whose earnings barely and just barely suffice to buy his most necessary requirements, will, if he must pay a poll tax, need higher wages than before. The increase in wages, however, brings the land rent of the most distant estate below zero and ends the cultivation of this soil. But thereby all workers who previously lived here entirely lose their livelihood and their sustenance: a boundless misery must therefore arise among this class of people, which can only be relieved by all the people made superfluous by the restriction of soil cultivation emigrating.

As soon as this has happened, the workers remaining in the country can increase their wages, and the estates which have remained in cultivation can, because they yield a land rent, pay an increased wage at the expense of this land rent.

Since in this way every long-established tax, if only it is not arbitrary and indefinite, has entered into a certain equilibrium with the conditions of the state, or rather since the state has formed itself in accordance with this tax, and the subject then no longer feels the pressure of the tax; whereas on the other hand every new or altered tax, like an encroachment upon property, unfailingly restricts some branches of cultivation or industry, and the people previously occupied therewith, at least until they have transferred to another field, are through no fault of their own thrown out of work: one might well conclude from this that the inequality of taxes is a far lesser evil than their frequent alteration.

§38.
Taxes on Land Rent.
If the owner of an estate must surrender to the state a part of the land rent that the estate brings him, this changes nothing at all in the form and extent of the farming operation. Those estates whose land rent is close to zero contribute very little to this tax, and the most distant or poorest estate is not affected by it at all. This tax can therefore exert as little harmful influence on the extent of cultivation as on the population, the employment of capital, and the quantity of products produced; indeed, if the entire land rent were taken away by the tax, the cultivation of the soil would nevertheless remain as it was.

In another respect, too, it may be a matter of indifference for the welfare of the nation whether the land rent is in the hands of the ruler or of the owner and capitalist; for in both cases it is usually spent unproductively.

In general, the land rent is far more in the hands of the capitalists than of the owners, who though they bear the title of possessor, if they are somewhat in debt must surrender the greater part of the land rent as interest to the capitalists.

Whether the capitalist and the rich landowner consume the land rent through the maintenance of many servants and luxury horses and through the use of luxury goods, or whether the state, when it is in possession of the land rent, uses it for the maintenance of the military, may make no essential difference to the national wealth.

Just as the land rent has arisen not through the expenditure of labor and capital, but through the accidental advantage of the estate's location or the quality of the soil, so too it can be taken away again without thereby disturbing or diminishing the employment of capital and labor.

In the isolated state we consider agriculture in a steady or unchanging state, and assume that farming on all estates is conducted with equal knowledge and equal consistency.

Neither is the case in reality, and the question arises what can here be called land rent, and how its magnitude is to be determined.

Given the diversity of diligence and knowledge with which agriculture is conducted, two estates of equal location and equal soil can nevertheless give a very different net return; but one cannot on that account assign to the poorly managed estate a lower value and lower land rent than to the other estate. The difference arises solely from the personality of the manager, and disappears again as soon as the manager is replaced by another. Only the permanent qualities of an estate — location and soil — not the accidental and transient qualities — the person of the farmer — can determine the value and land rent of an estate.

The land rent of an individual estate cannot therefore be determined by its net return; but the land rent arises in turn only from the net return, because land rent is nothing other than the net return after deduction of interest on the capital invested in buildings and other objects of value on the estate.

That net return, then, which an estate yields or can yield under the customary farming system, with an ordinary, neither outstandingly great nor small, diligence and knowledge of the manager, serves as the norm for determining the land rent.

The effect of ordinary diligence and knowledge can, however, only be determined from the magnitude of the product brought forth by the efforts of all farmers of an entire country or province.

The total sum of the net return of all estates of an entire country, after deduction of interest on the value of buildings, etc., gives the sum of the land rent, and this, distributed among the individual estates in proportion to the quality of the soil and the location, gives the land rent of the individual estate.

It follows from this how difficult it must be to determine the actual land rent of an estate, and it would not be surprising if we find that in practice almost all attempts of this kind are highly unsuccessful; but the matter is made much worse by the fact that as a rule, assessments have proceeded from entirely false principles. One cannot be persuaded that there is cultivated arable land that yields no land rent at all; rather, one believes one is already doing much when one reckons 4 or 6 square rods of the poorest field as equal in value to one square rod of the best; but just as little as 6 times zero can become one, so little can 6 square rods of the poorest soil have the value of 1 square rod of the best. Furthermore, one all too often confuses land rent with the interest on the capital invested in agriculture. An estate which yields no greater surplus than the interest on the value of the buildings, inventory, operating capital, etc. yields no land rent at all, although it provides its owner with an income. Every tax laid on the supposed land rent of such an estate has just as harmful an effect on soil cultivation as poll taxes, cattle taxes, etc.

If the land rent were to be accurately and correctly determined for the purpose of taxation, this would require men who had devoted themselves specifically to the study of this branch of science, and who then pursued no other business throughout their lives. This, however, would make the determination of the land rent very costly, and this would partly offset the advantage that the tax on land rent has over most other taxes through its inexpensive collection.

The land rent, however, is not a constant but a very variable quantity: for every change in the customary system of farming, in the price of products, in the rate of interest, etc. affects the magnitude of the land rent to an extraordinarily high degree. If now the tax on the land rent is fixed once and for all, and does not rise when the land rent rises: then after a century the yield of this tax is already entirely out of proportion with the actual land rent and with the needs of the state. But if the tax is to rise with the land rent, this requires frequently repeated, very costly assessments of the estates, and what is worst, the fear of a tax increase deters farmers from improvements and paralyzes the progress of cultivation.

In the isolated state we assumed that the yield of the soil remains unchanged, and there the entire land rent could belong to the state without this having a harmful influence on the cultivation of the soil. In reality, however, there is more or less a constant striving toward a higher yield, and the possibility of achieving it can be demonstrated almost everywhere. The improvement of the soil and the higher yield thereby to be achieved, however, almost always requires considerable costs, and in many cases the interest on the capital spent on improvement amounts to almost as much as the amount by which the net return of the estate has risen.

If now the improvement is of such a kind that its effect does not cease but continues permanently, then the land rent of the estate is thereby permanently increased. But this addition to the land rent is in its origin very different from the older land rent; instead of the latter having arisen without effort and without the contribution of the owner through the mere advantage of the estate's location or the quality of the soil, the former addition must be purchased through the expenditure of capital; and it would be very unjust and most injurious to impose a tax on this.

There are some improvements which, once made, cannot be reversed, and which can no more evade the tax than the older land rent — for example, the improvement of the physical quality of the soil through the application of clay, the drainage of swamps through canals, etc. Insofar as the tax does not again destroy these works, it is therefore harmless; but it has a most injurious effect in that it deters from and prevents all further improvements of this kind.

Now there is probably no employment of capital that acts more beneficially on the whole state than that directed toward the improvement of the soil and the raising of its cultivation: for we have seen above that when in the isolated state production rises from 8 to 10 grains, the population of the Town can increase by approximately 50 percent without the grain price needing to be raised.

The growth of the state in prosperity, power, and population therefore stands in immediate connection with the growth of the intensive cultivation of the soil, and since a tax on land rent, whose magnitude is not fixed once and for all but rises and falls with the magnitude of the land rent, burdens the improvement of the soil and thereby prevents it: the variable tax is among all taxes perhaps the one that most hinders the growth of the state.

Pictorial Representations of the Isolated State.

These pictorial representations, drawn by one of my friends, are, it is true, not necessary for the understanding of the subjects treated in this work, and I have also nowhere referred to them; but they afford an easy and convenient overview of the results that have emerged from our investigations, and I therefore believe that they will not be unwelcome to the reader who has read this work with attention.

At the same time, they give an opportunity to communicate some remarks which, in the work itself, found no place without interrupting the context.

On Plate I.
This plate represents the isolated state in the form which it must assume, according to the assumptions made and conclusions drawn in the first section of this work.

According to §26, the circle of livestock farming extends to 50 miles from the Town; here, to save space, it is drawn only to 40 miles from the Town.

On this plate, as on all the following plates, only one half of the circles forming around the Town is shown, because the other half is not merely similar but perfectly identical, and one can easily imagine it.

On Plate II.
This plate represents the form of the isolated state when it is traversed by a navigable river.

This representation is based on the assumption that river freight amounts to 1/10 of land freight.

The crop rotation farming system, which on the first plate occupies only a narrow strip, here expands enormously, and extends along the river to the boundary of the state. On the other hand, the circle of livestock farming recedes, and in the vicinity of the river disappears entirely.

A similar effect, though to a lesser degree, is produced by the construction of a paved road. If these paved roads are built in all directions of the plain, all circles of higher soil cultivation expand, but they then retain the regular form as on Plate I.

The uncolored strip designates the territory of a small town. By the territory of the town is understood, according to §28, the land area which supplies the small town with provisions and which delivers nothing to the capital.

We can also imagine this small town with its territory as an independent state of its own. In §28, we showed that the grain price in these small states is entirely dependent on the price in the central town.

Suppose the principal state prohibits grain imports from the subsidiary states; how will this affect the grain price in a subsidiary state not far from the central town with a rounded territory?

After the closure, the grain price in the subsidiary state is no longer regulated by the price in the central town; the subsidiary state now enters, with respect to grain trade, into the conditions of an isolated state, and the price of grain is now determined according to the same laws that we have found for the isolated state — considered as a unit — i.e. the price in the small town of the subsidiary state must be high enough that the most distant producer receives his production and transport costs. But since the territory of the town is only small and grain need not be brought from far away, the grain price in the small town can rise only slightly above the production costs, and the land rent that the land in this subsidiary state formerly yielded then almost entirely disappears.

It remains very remarkable that the closure can have this effect on a state which produces only its own grain requirement and which formerly delivered no grain to the central town, just as little as now after the closure, and must by the nature of the case have this effect.

In a similar relationship as the subsidiary states to the central town stand the European states to the rich state that can pay the highest grain price, to England and especially to its capital, London.

In these European states too, even when they neither import nor export grain, the grain price is governed by the world market of London, and when this market is closed, the price of grain falls throughout Europe.

On Plate III.
Here the yield of the soil is assumed at 10 grains, but the average price of grain in the Town itself varies from 1.5 thalers per bushel of rye down to 0.6 thalers.

This plate shows pictorially the influence that the grain price in the Town exerts on the extent of the cultivated plain. On this plate, however, only the radius of the cultivated plain and of the individual concentric circles is given. If one wishes to construct from this, for a given grain price — for example, 1.05 thalers — a representation similar to that on Plate I of the isolated state, one must measure with a compass the distance from the Town to the point where 1.05 thalers stands, and with this radius draw a circle around the Town.

[Extended tables and calculations showing how different grain prices affect the radius of the cultivated plain, the extent of each farming zone, and the area of cultivated land]

If we assume:

1) that a tax is paid on all grain brought to the Town for sale;

2) that the grain price in the Town itself remains unchanged, namely always 1 1/2 thalers per bushel of rye;

then this has for the farmer the same consequence as if the grain price had fallen, and this 3rd plate serves at the same time to give a vivid picture of the effect of this tax.

If, for example, a tax — whether collected as an import duty or as a milling tax — of 0.3 thalers per bushel of rye is introduced, the farmer receives only the price of 1.2 thalers per bushel, and the cultivated plain then contracts from 34.7 to 28.6 miles.

If we imagine a continued increase of the tax, this effects a constant decrease in the extent of the cultivated plain: if the tax rises to 0.9 thalers per bushel, the radius of this plain remains only 10.4 miles, and at a still further increased tax, the entire state must finally disappear.

Here, then, it is vividly shown how, merely through high taxes, a fertile soil can be transformed into a desert.

Since on the one hand, at the extreme height of the tax, no object for taxation remains and the treasury then has no revenue; and since on the other hand, when no tax at all is levied, the state indeed attains its greatest extent, but the treasury likewise has no revenue: there must be a point at which the tax delivers the maximum yield, and the question now arises at what level of the tax this maximum occurs in the present case.

Among the cases listed here, the tax of 0.45 thalers per bushel therefore yields the highest return for the treasury. Every further increase of the tax diminishes its yield, and what is very noteworthy, the tax of 0.75 thalers per bushel yields no higher revenue than that of 0.22 thalers.

Here, then, it appears that even if the state power renounces the people and regards them merely as a means of raising taxes, it nevertheless, through an excessive increase of taxes, entirely defeats its own purpose.
