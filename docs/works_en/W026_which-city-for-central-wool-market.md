As in previous years, wool samples were taken in the morning from the rams put forward for the wagers at positions A 1, 5, and 9, and in the afternoon the rams were carefully shorn under the constant supervision of a committee member.

The wool samples were handed over to Mr. Merchant Jeppe for measurement, and since he kindly undertook to carry out the factory-style washing of the fleeces, these were sent to him later.

The weighing of the washed fleeces took place on June 30 — during the Güstrow wool market — by the committee members, and with the participation of most of the competitors.

Remarks.

During the Güstrow wool market, a letter dated June 25 from Mr. Merchant Jeppe arrived, in which he reported

that although he had made a start on the washing of the fleeces entrusted to him for the competition for the medal and for

--- Page 13 ---
p. 751

Wager No. 3, he was unable despite all effort to get the wool clean with the water he could obtain in Rostock; and that due to other business he could not complete the wool washing before the following autumn.

This placed the committee in the most unpleasant predicament. A postponement of the washing until the following autumn was not feasible, because there would then be a lack of experts for the appraisal of the fleeces — and so the kind offer of Mr. Engelbrecht, "to fetch the fleeces from Rostock and have them washed in Ridsenow as quickly as possible," had to be gratefully accepted, although it was foreseeable that in such a short time the washing could not be accomplished with the customary perfection, and even less could the fleeces be fully dried.

The washed fleeces arrived in Güstrow on June 30. As was to be expected, the washing was not equally good for all fleeces, and the fleeces with dense, long wool were more or less damp.

Since a postponement of the appraisal was not possible, there was no other recourse than to request the appraisers to take into account not merely the quality of the wool itself, but also the washing and the degree of dryness, and thus

--- Page 14 ---

to determine the value of the fleeces in their given condition.

Even if the wool merchants had actually solved the extremely difficult task of correctly appraising each fleece in this threefold respect, and Wager No. 3 had thus been correctly decided, the value of the wool itself — that is, of the wool in a clean, dry state — cannot be determined from the appraisal; just as little can the actual wool yield be determined from the fleece weight.

For scientific investigations, for example concerning the relationship between fineness grade and value of the wool, between wool yield and fineness of the wool, and so forth — to which the sheep show in earlier years had already provided important contributions — the results of Wager No. 3 are entirely worthless. This must be expressly noted here, so that a future scholar working on the science of sheep breeding, who wishes to use the results of the sheep show for his investigations, knows precisely which data he may fully trust.

If, on the other hand, what has gone wrong is not identified as such, then a future scholar cannot distinguish the true from the incorrect, and for him, as for science itself, not only the incorrect fact loses its value,

p. 753

but at the same time the entire series of well-founded facts loses its worth as well.

Very important, on the other hand, is the result of this year's sheep show with regard to the question of to what weight the wool yield of a ram can rise.

Wager No. 1 and the competition for the premium have brought this question close to resolution, and the illusion to which one so frequently surrendered regarding the enormous wool weight of individual rams has thereby been destroyed forever.

The ram P, presented for the premium, was selected by Mr. Pogge from the admittedly richest-woolled flock in the country with sole regard to the greatest quantity of wool, and setting aside all consideration of wool quality. The ram is of large stature, its wool very long and coarse, and was appraised by the wool merchants at only 50 Thalers per hundredweight. Nevertheless, its one-year wool yield amounted to only 4 pounds 24.8 Loth, and from this weight a considerable amount will still be lost when the wool is factory-washed and fully dried.

Next year's sheep show will give us precise information on this.

--- Page 16 ---

One may not doubt the truthfulness of the reports according to which individual rams have yielded 8 to 10 pounds of shearing; but if the wool loses 60 percent in the factory wash, then from 10 pounds of fleece weight only 4 pounds of clean wool remain. If, on the other hand, another ram yields 6 pounds with a good wash, and this wool loses 20 percent in weight during the factory wash, then the former ram has delivered double the shearing weight, and yet both rams yield the same amount of clean wool, namely 4 pounds.

The statement of fleece weight is therefore meaningless and not worth any attention when no reduction to factory-wash weight is associated with it.

The sheep breeder who knows only the shearing weight of his sheep remains in complete ignorance regarding one of the most important factors for evaluating the worth of his flock — the actual wool yield. It therefore cannot be urgently enough recommended to inform oneself about the yield of one's flock in clean wool through trial washings. If one has apothecary weights, one need only wash 200 grains of fleece wool perfectly clean in warm, strong soap lye to determine the loss that the wool suffers in the factory wash to within 2 percent, and this effort is truly not great. Difficult, however, is it to select from even a single fleece a sample

--- Page 17 ---

that can serve as representative of the entire fleece with respect to washing; still more difficult, however, to find a sample that can be regarded as an average for the entire wool lot with respect to washing. Complete accuracy is not attainable here; but the error that can arise from this is much smaller than if one contents oneself with an estimate by mere visual inspection. In my experience, one will always find in the trial washings that an apparently small addition of grease and dirt increases the weight loss that the wool suffers in the factory wash beyond all expectation.

This seems to be grounded in the fact that dirt has a far greater specific gravity than wool, and that a volumetrically insignificant addition of those substances increases the absolute weight of the fleece far more than the eye can perceive — unless it has already been trained through many trials.

Especially important is the knowledge of the yield of sheep in clean wool when selecting those sheep from which one wishes to raise rams. If one goes by shearing weight here, one will always select the sheep that have a pitch-like grease in the wool and cannot be washed clean, and instead of a rich-woolled

--- Page 18 ---
p. 756

stock, one then breeds a dirty stock.

But also in judging the quantity of wool on the animals' bodies, the grease in the wool gives rise to many deceptions. The fleece surrounded by a layer of dirt and grease appears uniform and densely stapled; the tips of the wool hairs are not visible; the staples stick together, therefore resist the penetration of the fingers when touched, and thus everything indicates dense wool growth — while the same fleece, when freed from the layer of grease enveloping and connecting the staples, not infrequently shows itself to be loose and straggling.

J. H. von Thünen.
