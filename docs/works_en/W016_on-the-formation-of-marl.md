# On the Effect of Marl

*Original: Ueber die Wirkung des Mergels (1829)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

[A report presented by Herr von Thünen of Tellow at the autumn assembly in Teterow.]

According to my experience, marl has had little or no effect on very dry sand, on raw clay, and on fine soft soil near the farmstead; on the other hand, it has shown a very great effect on moist medium-quality soil. It has also been shown in most cases that, on the same type of soil, marl is of lesser effectiveness in three-field farming systems than in convertible husbandry systems. Experience has further taught that clay marl, loam marl, and sand marl produce nearly the same effect, and that the greater or lesser lime content of the marl has no significant influence on its effectiveness; indeed, even gravel and drift sand containing only traces of lime have shown themselves to be at least as beneficial as ordinary marl, at least for the first crops.

The experiments, conducted on several estates and by several farmers, have further shown that in the first harvests after marling, it makes no noticeable difference whether one applies half a cart, a whole cart, or four carts\* of marl per square rod.

The only question is how these various phenomena are to be explained and unified under a single principle.

Since sorrel — a plant that itself contains much acid and requires acid in the soil to thrive — completely disappears from the field after marling (provided the process has been carried out properly), and since sandy and clay soils contain less acid than moist medium-quality soil, this led me to the thought of whether the binding of acid by the lime might not be the principal cause of the effect of marl.

\* A cart being 10 to 12 cubic feet. — The author.

This at least explains why marl does not work on certain soil types, why a lesser or greater application of marl, as well as the smaller or larger lime content of the same, proves so equally effective in practice; for according to this, only as much lime needs to be applied as is required to bind the acid, and all lime contained in the marl beyond that amount is, at least in this respect, ineffective.

If this hypothesis is confirmed, one could determine in advance by a simple test whether marl will be effective on a given soil or not, since litmus paper turns red when brought into contact with soil containing acid, but retains its blue color when the soil is free of acid.\*

I communicated these views to my cousin and former pupil, Herr Schröder of Quigenow, whose field has not yet been marled.

Herr Schröder then conducted a large number of tests on his own field and on other neighboring fields concerning the acid content of the soil, which, as far as I can recall from an oral communication,\*\* yielded the following results:

The soil located near the farmstead, which was regularly manured, reddened the litmus paper only weakly; with greater distance from the farmstead, the litmus paper was reddened ever more strongly by the soil, and this reddening was especially strong on the field that had formerly been used as permanent pasture.

The field cultivated under a three-field system reddened the litmus paper far less than a similar field on which convertible husbandry was practiced.

\* For such a test, the soil must be poured into a glass, covered with water, and stirred into a paste before the litmus paper is inserted. — The author.

\*\* Herr Schröder has promised a detailed written communication of his investigations. — The author.

Herr Schröder was not content with this, however, but now investigated the behavior of various types of manure and of lime toward litmus paper that had been reddened by acids. He found that cow dung, when brought into contact with the reddened litmus paper, slightly restored the original blue color of the paper; that horse dung exercised this effect to a greater degree; and that sheep dung surpassed horse dung in this regard, almost fully restoring the blue color — while lime completely converted the reddish color back into blue.

These experiments now shed light on why marl is less effective in three-field farming systems than in convertible husbandry; why marl is of so little use on soft, frequently manured fields; and why in those three-field systems that have always maintained a large sheep flock, marl is often entirely without effect.

The view that marl enhances the fertility of the field by the lime neutralizing the acid present in the soil is certainly not new; but it seems to me that this view has not received the attention it deserves, has not been recognized in its full significance, and has had no influence whatsoever on the practice of agriculture.

Nevertheless, I by no means believe that the effect of marl is limited solely to the neutralization of acid in the soil, and although a great many phenomena observed during marling can be explained from this one property of marl alone, it is nevertheless very probable that careful observations made from another standpoint will lead to phenomena that cannot be satisfactorily explained from this property alone; in every case, however, the neutralization of acid in the soil will remain a principal factor in explaining the effect of marl.

---

The assembly received this interesting communication from Herr von Thünen on a substance to which Mecklenburg must attribute the greatest part of the elevated state of cultivation of its fields with great pleasure — although it had not yet been possible, despite several theories put forward for the purpose, to satisfactorily explain this effect.

The assembly requested Herr von Thünen to continue devoting his attention to the subject, and also to kindly encourage Herr Schröder to continue his instructive experiments and to publish their results in detail through the Annalen, since they provide indications that appear equally important for practice and science.

It had been demonstrated that chemical analysis of topsoil frequently revealed a content of vegetable acid, usually acetic acid, and that soil so afflicted was as a rule infertile.

The assembly was therefore pressed by the question: whether acetic acid is the cause or the consequence of infertility, and whether it is harmful to vegetation? — It was not considered difficult to determine the effect of acetic acid on vegetation through experiments.
