## Editor's Preface

Johann Heinrich von Thunen died on September 22, 1850; the loss that science suffered through the death of this tireless researcher is felt all the more painfully because he was not granted the opportunity to carry forward the principal work of his extensive literary activity — "The Isolated State in Relation to Agriculture and Political Economy." But his valuable manuscripts had to become common property — and joyfully, yet with reverent awe, I have taken on the task of presenting a selection from the rich scholarly estate to the public.

For the difficulty of making this selection also in the spirit of the author, I found a counterweight in several years of personal relations with him, in gratitude and reverence for my unforgettable teacher the courage, and in the counsel of his sons the indispensable support.

It is greatly to be regretted that von Thunen was not granted the opportunity to bring to completion his investigations on the relationship between wages and land rent; but fortunately the materials for this have been determined from the economy of the Tellow estate, and the application of the formulas found to concrete cases has been demonstrated.

May the "full magnitude" of the task posed in "The Isolated State," may the plan of this work and the method applied therein encourage very many disciples of science to continue, using the available materials, the investigations that von Thunen had to leave unfinished; may this wish, expressed in the introduction to the first section of the second part, become a deed promising success.

Where it appeared important and interesting, even when the investigation was a fragment, it has not been withheld. One thing or another is perhaps a bright star on the dark path of research, and in any case, correctly interpreted, a valuable hint for the method of investigation.

Some valuable calculations, for example on road construction, nutritive value of fodder crops, require editing for publication, and this part of the scholarly estate will gladly be handed over to capable hands for use.

Following the investigations on the relationship between wages and land rent are smaller essays and letters on the statics of agriculture.

To the calculations on cultivation costs and net yield of the soil, promised in the preface to the first part, an extract from the labor journal has been added, as well as an overview of the sixty years of yields from the "classic soil of Tellow" (Carl von Wulffen). Thus not only extremely rare statistical data have been provided, but also the foundations of the calculations and conclusions occurring in "The Isolated State." Before us lies clearly the laboriously won ground for the natural-law truths of this work, and we recognize with admiration the path of discovery that the master trod.

In the first part the formula for the mean distance is given. The presentation of the method by which this formula was found, and the execution of the proof, reserved for the second part of the work, may also be of interest for the mathematical sciences, and are found at the end of Section 7, along with a list of von Thunen's printed writings and essays.

A treatise on forestry will appear as the third part.

Should this or that of what has been communicated appear at first glance as belonging to an earlier time, or as being only of historical value, a more careful examination, entering into the spirit of this work, will easily lead to the recognition that the laws found, which have now become the property of science, maintain their value for all times. From this follows directly the value that we must attach to the factors of the calculation in and of themselves, as well as for the continuation of the investigations; calculations on cultivation costs, etc., made in more recent times and in other places may well yield different figures — the laws to be derived from them will remain the same. It had to appear as a duty to be all the less reserved, the more the recognition grows "that von Thunen's laws appear destined one day to become the source of deeply penetrating investigations." (L. Stein.)

We learn moreover how von Thunen understood how to maintain the higher scientific standpoint even in apparently less important questions, and to mediate between science and economic life.

When the English government, during the deliberations on the "Corn Laws," made inquiries about wheat production on the continent and about the views prevalent there, it was especially von Thunen's calculations and answers that were highlighted in the report (1828) as particularly noteworthy, and which stand in close relation to those ideas developed in the "Reflections" (Section 6.1); von Thunen characterized the Corn Bill in his modest manner of expression as at least superfluous.

Only in 1846, however, did the customs barriers drawn by the Corn Bill fall — the history of political science will also hold open an honorable column for those views of von Thunen's, which had already at that time ripened to rare clarity.

With respect to the statics of agriculture, a cautious examination of prevailing views about those laws that are supposed to govern the nature of field cultivation will answer the following questions in the negative: Has von Thunen's definition of "richness" and "humus content" in the static sense already been replaced by a more correct one, more valuable for the practice of agriculture? — Has the statics of agriculture already found its "Euclid"?

"The errors to which the modern mania of wanting to solve all practical tasks of the farmer scientifically can lead are innumerable" (Franz Schulze), and the futile endeavor to regulate agricultural science solely through the natural sciences must awaken the effort to expand, correct, and develop the doctrine of the statics of agriculture, prepared by von Thunen, Thaer, von Wulffen, von Riese, von Voght, and others — now supported by the successful efforts of representatives of the natural sciences who pursue the path of their research without prejudice and without passion. It is through this consideration that the communication of the letters (Section 5) has been occasioned, and thereby at the same time, in full agreement with the first part of this work, von Thunen's position on that question is clearly marked.

Though a Liebig may shake the entire science like an earthquake, his brilliant outbursts are nevertheless mostly only mud volcanoes (Carl von Wulffen to Johann Heinrich von Thunen) — though he may continue to portray the earnest and solid efforts of many insightful and capable men as foolish and ruinous, nevertheless the experiences in agriculture and the laws found through them are the unavoidable foundation for the further development of science, and remain the stepping stones to new research and higher knowledge.

Pitiful is the standard that materialism, on the other hand, applies to science, because it knows how to value only what may serve the momentary advantage! Younger generations take up their residence in uncultivated regions, and make the basis of economic operations those results of scientific research which in other places are already considered settled and appear only as a mirror in which one historically contemplates a bygone era.

Or — the torch of war reddens the horizon, its instruments wage a campaign of annihilation against man and the cultivation of his fields; then it looks sad for the coming peace if, in ignorance of the political-economic laws of agriculture, we do not know where the less advanced economic stages have their proper place.

But to those who devote themselves to scientific research, "however graceless, barren, and thorny the path may be that leads there," these words and this rule of life of Johann Heinrich von Thunen apply:

"There is surely no more worthy, more beneficial occupation than this: to pursue a thought into its last hiding place and to hunt down one's own errors; if we have discovered their source, we are not only freed from these errors, but also secured against similar aberrations in the future!"

Zarchlin, June 24, 1863.

H. Schumacher.

## Section 1

### Reflections
occasioned by the result of the investigations on the relationship between wages and interest rates.

In our investigation we first placed ourselves in an ideal situation and assumed that people, whose number remains constant and who consist partly of capitalists and partly of workers, live in a fertile land where all land is to be had for nothing and is of equal quality. Under these conditions no land rent can exist, and the yield of an estate must be divided between the capitalist, to whom the buildings, equipment, etc. belong, and the worker who cultivates the field. We then asked according to what law this division of the product between capitalist and worker takes place; or what the natural share of the worker in the product brought forth by his labor might be.

Our investigation has now shown that — when the maintenance of a worker's family amounts to *a* Scheffel of rye per year, and the annual labor product of this family amounts to *p* Scheffel of rye — the natural wage = sqrt(*ap*).

Here man appears as lord of creation: what he can wrest from nature through his labor — that is his property. Capital itself is a product of labor, and the compensation that the capitalist receives is only a reward for labor previously performed.

Then we investigated, secondly, according to what law wages are determined in reality.

In reality, wages are regulated by the competition of workers. Since, as experience teaches, the multiplication of workers finds its ultimate limit only in the lack of subsistence means, the magnitude of competition depends on the magnitude of the costs that it takes to raise a worker from his earliest childhood to the age where he can support himself.

In this way the education costs of the worker now enter into the calculation as a contributing factor in the determination of the relationship between interest rate and wages.

In our investigation we have adopted the following designations:

*q* is the size of the capital with which a worker's family works; wherein the product of one year's work serves as the unit and measure for the size of the capital.

*h* designates the labor product when *q* = 1, expressed in Scheffel of rye.

*p* designates the labor product when the worker works with a capital of *q* annual labors (A.L.). The size of this labor product we set at *h* times *q*^*n* Scheffel of rye.

*m* designates the size of the education costs of a worker's family, consisting of man and wife, expressed in annual labors.

*a* designates the size of the subsistence means expressed in Scheffel of rye that a worker's family, under the condition that it raises two children to marriageable age, necessarily requires.

Our investigation has yielded the following expression for wages:

Wages = (*mhq*^*n* + *a*) / (*m* + 1)

We distinguish a threefold application of capital, namely:

1) that in productive investment, e.g. in trades or in agriculture;
2) that in lending capital at interest;
3) that for the education of workers.

Our calculation shows that the use of capital in these three different applications is in equilibrium when a certain equation involving *h*, *q*, *n*, and *m* is satisfied.

*a*, *h*, *n*, and *m* are here determined quantities; *q* is a variable quantity. The value of *q* that results from solving that equation we set = *C*. Then we investigated at what value of *q* (1) the interest rate and (2) wages reach a maximum, and we found that not only the interest rate but simultaneously also wages are highest when *q* = *C*.

Since for *q* = *C* the use of capital is equally high in all types of employment and consequently no cause exists that could bring about a transition of capital to another use, since furthermore for *q* = *C* both the interest rate and wages are at a maximum and thus neither an increase nor a decrease in the size of capital (*q*) lies in the interest of either the capitalist or the worker: nowhere is there a reason that could produce a striving for change.

Here there is rest, and hence here is the steady state.

For *q* = *C*, however, the wage arising through the competition of the workers themselves is:

sqrt(*a* times *hC*^*n*) = sqrt(*ap*)

According to this law, by which wages are determined in reality, there remains to the worker from his wages, after deducting his maintenance costs, nothing but the interest on the capital spent on his education; for his labor, his exertion itself, he has nothing but life — his bare subsistence.

At this price the capitalist can always obtain workers and enjoy the fruits of their labor.

Just as the labor of the slave costs his master nothing but the feeding of the slave and the interest on the purchase price, just as the labor of a machine costs the manufacturer nothing but the maintenance costs and the interest on the production costs of the machine: so too does the labor of the free man cost the capitalist nothing but his maintenance and the interest on the capital that the worker's education has required.

Just as the labor of the machine is to be had for the replacement of its production costs, so the labor of the free man is to be had for the restitution of the costs that his education required.

This view is revolting; but reality shows only too clearly in every long-cultivated country that it is true, and one is tempted to indict fate, which, for the benefit of a small part of mankind, has bent by far the greatest part — the workers — under the dominion of capital, and made them a tool in the hands of the former — the capitalists.

Let us now, however, go back to our first investigation, where it was demonstrated:

1) that capital in itself is something dead and attains effectiveness only through the labor of man;
2) that capital itself is only the product of human labor;
3) that the rent that the worker acquires through labor directed at capital creation cannot be higher than the amount of interest that the wage laborer receives for his surplus; and
4) that the rent of the capital-creating worker is highest when wages = sqrt(*ap*).

It then seems quite incomprehensible that man should come under the dominion of his own product — capital — and be subordinated to it; and since in reality this is undeniably the case, the question forces itself involuntarily upon us: "How has the worker been able to become, from the master of capital — as its creator — the slave of capital?"

The workers have passed from the state of freedom into that of necessity.

Is this, then, the will of fate, or the fault of men themselves?

Stirred by these questions and doubts, I felt irresistibly compelled to seek the inner connection between wages in reality and wages in the state of freedom = sqrt(*ap*), and to find a point of union for both.

My attempts were long in vain. Finally I hit upon the idea of investigating what influence the size of the worker's education costs exerts upon wages and upon the rent that the worker can acquire for his surplus.

I soon found that this rent rises with the increase of education costs up to a certain point, and when I then differentiated the expression for the magnitude of the rent, it turned out that this rent is a maximum when *m* = a certain value involving *q* and *n*.

Substituting this value of *m* into the equation for wages yields, after simplification:

Wages = sqrt(*ap*)

Expressed in words, this calculation says the following:

The general increase of workers' education costs raises the reward of labor; it therefore lies in the interest of all workers to give their children a better education, and when they raise the education costs of their children to the point where their labor finds the maximum reward: the workers remain indeed still subject to the necessity imposed upon them by the law prevailing in reality — according to which the capital spent on education determines wages and capital thus dominates man — but wonderfully, the wages determined according to this law then coincide at one point with the wages of workers in the state of complete freedom — where he himself appears as the creator and master of capital.

Necessity and freedom then lead to one and the same goal.

The workers are led by their own interest, by the striving for greater physical well-being, to the attainment of freedom; but since the attainment and preservation of freedom is tied to a better education of their children, they serve, along with the striving for physical well-being, the far higher purpose: "enlightenment and intellectual cultivation of the entire human race" — or in other words: from that striving for personal well-being there emerges, unknown to themselves, a far higher good.

If the dominion of capital did not take place at all and the worker unconditionally enjoyed the higher wages that he can now enjoy only after achieving freedom, the workers, instead of applying their surplus to the better education of their children, would sink into luxury and indolence. But the development of the faculties inherent in man can only occur through work and exertion, and without them only a sinking back and an approximation to animality takes place. In order to lead men toward their higher destiny, the compulsion imposed upon them by the dominion of capital is therefore necessary, and thus necessity no longer appears as the scourge but as the educator of the human race.

Despite the dominion of capital and the low wages prevailing in this state, it is still left to the discretion of each individual, instead of the greater number, to bequeath to the world better-instructed and better-educated children.

If this is done by a single individual, no increase in wages can result from it; but if it is done by all, then all attain the higher wage that prevails in the state of freedom, when man is master of capital.

Thus the interest of the individual is tied to that of the whole. The individual suffers along when others act incorrectly, and thus it lies in his own interest to lead them to correct insight and right action. And conversely, what truly benefits the whole also benefits the individual.

Humanity appears here as a great organic whole, where every injury to an individual member is felt by the whole, and where no perfect well-being of the individual parts is possible unless the whole is well.

The individual is a fragment split off from the spirit of humanity, and as such can only partake of a partial freedom.

But the whole of humanity, educated upward to unity, can raise itself to absolute freedom.

What we now regard as a barrier drawn by necessity, restricting our freedom, would then cease to be a barrier — we would of our own insight voluntarily do what we must now be compelled by necessity to do. Or, the barriers that necessity draws are only offensive to him who strives to break through them — and the human race that had arrived at the knowledge of its higher destiny would not feel the barriers by which it is surrounded, because it would of its own free will keep itself within them.

After this model, man should also proceed when he himself appears as the restrictor of the freedom of others.

Thus in education no other compulsion should be imposed upon children than such as the child, once it has reached maturity of understanding and correct insight, will itself recognize as salutary and beneficial.

Thus legislators should impose no other compulsion upon human society than such as a man of deep and high insight would voluntarily impose upon himself — and under a perfect legislation, a man of complete insight could walk over the earth without knowing a single law, and yet violate none.

If from my childhood I had known a wise man and heard his teachings and precepts, but had at first doubted the correctness and truth of them because I did not grasp the reasons on which they rested — if then in the course of life one precept after another had become clear to me, if every advance in insight had been connected with the understanding and comprehension of one of those pronouncements, and if at last, on the advanced path of life, against the innumerable cases where clarity had come to me, only a few pronouncements had remained obscure — then I would involuntarily be pervaded by the belief that those pronouncements too, whose reasons are still veiled from me, contain truth, and that the failure to comprehend is to be ascribed only to the lack of a higher insight.

Such a faith, which is not commanded but is the free product of the mind, is closely akin to comprehension itself, and worthy of the free man.

When in the investigations of the relations of civil life, in which I formerly believed I found darkness and confusion, indeed sometimes even harshness and injustice of fate, I arrived at clarity, and in what had formerly appeared so dark to me, light, harmony, and a higher purpose for the welfare of humanity suddenly revealed themselves — then I was suddenly, as if by an electric shock, pervaded by the belief, indeed the conviction, that the necessity to which man is also subject in other and higher relations redounds to his welfare, and that the sufferings and destinies that generally befall humanity are indispensably necessary for the cultivation and higher development of the human race — and in this inspired mood I made the pronouncement:

The sufferings and pains that man bears with so much displeasure, and that make him unhappy, would be imposed and created for himself by the man who for a moment had attained to higher, supernal insight, for the duration of earthly life.

A universal lot of all humanity is:

1) that in old age man's powers decline, his effectiveness is hampered, the joys that the world of the senses afforded vanish, and instead diseases, physical ills, and pains become his portion;

2) that man, when he has completed his life's course here, loses the nurturers and providers of his childhood, his parents; that loving spouses are torn apart; and that not seldom parents must carry their children, the fruit of their life and their work, to the grave.

This lot seems terrible; but because it is universal, it can only serve higher purposes, and according to that belief arising from growing insight, we must assume that man cannot reach his higher destiny without such sufferings.

For the attainment of earthly purposes, however, these sufferings are fruitless, and the belief that the necessity of bearing these sufferings is imposed on humanity for the attainment of a higher purpose leads directly to the belief, indeed to the vision:

that man endures when the earthly shell falls from him.

What the higher, supernal destiny of man may be, we cannot know, only surmise. Just as physically the gulf between two worlds is insurmountable for man, so the human mind cannot, in recognizing the beyond, dispense with the transitional stages.

But in the laws of nature there is unity of purpose, and what we have recognized as purpose in the relations of civil society, we may well, in surmise, apply as a stepping stone to the recognition of our destiny.

Where, then, does necessity lead man in civil life?

We found freedom tied to the mastery of the most powerful of the sensory drives, and from the dominion of this drive over reason we saw necessity arise.

From the necessity that prevails over man springs a great part of the evils that oppress man in his civil relations and make him unhappy. But in these evils lies the spur to raise oneself to freedom and thus to the mastery of the passions.

The sufferings that the workings of necessity inflict upon man are for him a spur to raise himself to freedom; but they do not compel him to it. Whether the sufferings drag man down to complaints, discontent, and despair, or raise him to ennoblement and higher intellectual development — that is entirely left to the free will of man.

Man shall therefore become from and through himself; he shall be his own work.

Necessity leads man to activity and exertion, and thereby to the development of his powers. The intellectual powers that man develops, cultivates, and as it were calls forth from himself in the struggle with necessity belong to his most essential, innermost being, and are, wherever he may be placed, his inalienable property.

Development of intellectual powers thus appears as an end in itself.

When a single man raises himself to mastery over the passions, to the development of his powers and to correct insight, and thereby to right action, he can nevertheless not escape the evils that necessity imposes — only when all act rightly do the evils disappear.

The happiness of the individual is thus tied to the happiness of all, and thereby it becomes the task of life:

to develop and cultivate one's own powers in the enlightenment and happiness of others.

When man sacrifices his personal interest to the interest of humanity, through a wonderful chain of connection the increase of the welfare of the whole falls beneficently back upon him, and he needs no other moral principle than this:

Do that which, if all others acted the same way, would redound to your welfare, and willingly make the sacrifices that this principle demands if others do not follow it.

We have seen that the workers are led through the striving for physical well-being to freedom and to the mastery of passion, thus to the attainment of a great good. But nevertheless from this there emerged another, by them not sought, still higher good: "enlightenment of the entire human race."

Should not thus also, from the striving for the happiness of others, a highest good still unknown to us emerge! The bliss that falls to man's share after every noble deed seems to confirm this presentiment.

As the laws that are revealed to us about the destiny of man fill us with admiration, and we are nevertheless led to the presentiment that they are only subordinate parts of a higher law, we gaze into a mysterious darkness, but a darkness full of joyful hope, and to the perceiving mind the great veiled lawgiver becomes visible: God!

## Section 2

### Application of the Formulas Found to Concrete Cases

In Section 23 of Part 2, Division 1, it is noted:

In the previous investigation, labor product, interest rate, and wages have been expressed through algebraic formulas. The letters represent any numerical value, and the formulas expressed therein thereby rise to general validity.

For the concrete case, however, the letters take on a determined numerical value, and if the formula is correct, regularity must also show itself in the results expressed in numbers.

In our next investigation on the relationship between wages and land rent we already need the specification in numbers of *a*, *p*, *q*, *y*, and *z* for a concrete case.

These numbers, however, must not be arbitrarily assumed but must be taken from reality, because reality shall be the touchstone of their correctness.

In the absence of other data, the values of these letters for the conditions of the Tellow estate have been determined as follows.

### 1) Subsistence Costs and Income of a Day-Laborer Family at Tellow, from 1833 to 1847

On pages 215 to 276 of Division 1 of Part 2, a detailed calculation of the subsistence costs of a day-laborer family and of the income thereof was already communicated in Appendix A at that time; this communication was prompted by the great importance that the question of how much income a day-laborer requires for a decent life, protected from want, had at that time.

Accordingly, the sum of the income of a day-laborer family that does not keep a servant (Hofganger), who performs the work for the estate instead of the wife, amounts to 168 Thaler 26 Schilling N2/3, or 196 Thaler 18 Schilling Prussian Courant.

#### G. Value of Timber Stands

According to our definition of capital, all objects of value that can be separated from the land belong to capital. Accordingly, the value of the trees — the value they have if they are all felled at once — also belongs to capital.

The value so calculated must not, however, be confused with the value that the forested land attains through its yield.

The younger stands, when felled, have little or no timber value; their value consists rather in the fact that they will provide a considerable income at a later time, the present capital value of which can be calculated. From the excess of the thus calculated capital value of the woodland over the value of the present timber stand, one then obtains the capital value of the forested land, and the interest on this capital constitutes the land rent. The older the timber becomes, the more the value of the future income and the value of the present timber stand approach each other, and when both become equal, the land rent disappears.

The difference between both values is greatest for an orchard planted with young fruit-bearing trees.

In the middle of the period from 1833 to 1846, namely in the year 1840, according to an estimate:

4) the breaking up and loosening of the waste soil.

How large these costs have been, no one can say, since none of the living has seen the soil in its raw state. The capital spent on these works is inseparable from the land and has grown together with it, and the interest thereon now accrues to the land rent. We learn from this, however, that a component of the land rent consists of the interest on capital previously invested.

Among the improvements that become advantageous with advancing cultivation and increasing value of the land are:
1) the improvement of the physical condition of the soil by carting on types of earth that the soil does not possess in sufficient quantity,
2) the installation of drains for drying the soil,
3) the construction of irrigation meadows,
4) the enrichment of the soil through conservative farming, carting on marl and muck, application of mineral fertilizers, etc.

As with every classification there are objects that stand on the boundary of two classes and of which it is doubtful whether they belong to this or that class, so here too the question can be raised whether the costs of enriching the soil belong to capital, or whether their interest must be counted as part of the land rent.

In favor of the latter view speaks the following reason: the humus inherent in the soil cannot be separated from the soil, cannot be carted away and sold; it thus lacks the mobility that belongs to the concept of capital.

In favor of the first view, on the other hand, speaks the following: It is indeed correct that the humus cannot be carted away, cannot be sold, and that it becomes worthless as soon as the land is abandoned. But its quantity is by no means unchangeable; rather, the greater part of it can be extracted from the soil; only this removal requires a far longer time than with other capital objects.

### 3) The Estate Rent

The determination of the estate rent in the sense in which it is taken here presents, when the estate owner himself lives on the estate and has assumed the management of the farm, considerable difficulties.

The cash surplus cannot be the determining factor here; for it is quite possible that the income of several estates may be consumed on one estate. The only expedient seems to me to be this: to credit to the estate as income what the estate owner with his family has used and consumed partly in cash money, partly in produce raised on the estate, and to deduct from this the costs of administration to which a tenant could reasonably lay claim.

Now, in the 14-year period from 1833 to 1847, the average estate yield amounted to 3,127 Thaler 16 Schilling N2/3.

After an estimate that admittedly cannot claim accuracy, I add for this: 450 Thaler.

This gives altogether: 4,624 Thaler 20 Schilling N2/3.

4) Maintenance costs for the inspector's riding horse: 40 "

Together: 450 Thaler

But every administration, if one wants to be secured against negligence and overcharging, also needs supervision, and the costs thereof must be further deducted from the estate yield.

But how high shall these be estimated?

If the estate is leased, the tenant combines in himself the administrator and overseer, and thus this question transforms into the other:

How high shall the tenant's compensation be?

This question can scarcely be decided theoretically; practically, it could probably be decided only if one had knowledge of all leased estates in a province, of how much the tenants on average receive from the estate yield.

But even this knowledge would deliver highly different results in the different periods of time.

The tenants who leased in the first decade of this century mostly became poor. Those who leased in the period from 1826 to 1836 mostly acquired a considerable fortune and frequently bought estates. The tenants who have leased in the last decade will mostly have to take part of the rent from their own fortune.

Perhaps we approach reality for a longer period of time if we assume:
- that the tenant receives 1.5 times what the administration by an inspector and a housekeeper costs;
- and that the tenant receives, for the assumed risk, in addition to the interest, 2 percent of his capital exposed to the danger of loss.

The capital exposed to danger amounts to 12,572 Thaler N2/3 (without seed and cultivation costs: 9,775 Thaler).

The tenant, on the other hand, stakes his fortune and has no guarantee that he will not have to suffer want in old age.

### 4) The Labor Product

By "labor product" I understand, according to Section 6.3 of Part 2, Division 1, what remains after deducting all farming costs and the entrepreneurial profit of the manager from the estate yield, and what comes to be divided between the landowner, the capitalist, and the workers.

Here it must be noted that among the farming costs, as already indicated in Part 1, Section 5, taxes to the state are not included, because these are independent of the farming operation and are only to be taken into account later, when land rent, capital gain, and wages have been determined.

If we now wish to determine the labor product from the estate rent, the amount of these taxes must be added back to the determined estate rent.

## Section 3

### Investigations on the Relationship between Capital and Labor Product

#### Equation between Capital and Labor Product

Let *p* = *h* times sqrt(1 + *q*).

The interest rate is determined by the increase *dp* that the labor product receives when capital grows by *dq*. The calculations yield expressions for the rent *R*, wages *A*, and interest rate *z* in terms of *h*, *q*, *n*, and *a*.

For *h* = 80 and *n* = 1/2, a scale between capital, labor product, wages, and interest rate was developed. The scale satisfies the following requirements:

1) *p* follows directly from the equation *p* = *h* times sqrt(1 + *q*), without needing to be sought from summation of increments as in earlier drafts.
2) For *q* = 0, *p* = *h*. This follows here from the equation itself.
3) In our European conditions, at low capital investment, if full interest is to be paid, wages cannot reach *a* — or rather, full interest cannot be paid since wages cannot fall below *a*. The table yields a result consistent with this.
4) The rent grows steadily with capital.
5) The proportional share of the worker in the product decreases when capital grows, although the absolute amount of wages remains increasing.
6) The rent of the capital-creating worker reaches its maximum when wages = sqrt(*ap*).

After more than 20 years of fruitless efforts, it appears to have finally succeeded in presenting an equation and developing a scale that meets all presently known requirements.

The investigation then considers whether the subsistence means or the annual labors are the correct measure for capital. Using the Tellow data (*p* = 2.76*a*, *k* = 16.3*a*), and the condition that wages at the border equal sqrt(*ap*) = 1.66*a*, the exponent *n* is found to be approximately 0.43, and *h* approximately 0.87. The interest rate *z* comes out to about 4.78%.

Further comparisons between scales based on annual labors and those based on subsistence means as the measure of capital confirm that the rent of the capital-creating worker reaches its maximum at the values consistent with the Tellow experience.

## Section 4

### Fragments from the Investigations and the Plan of this Work

#### 1. What Are the Causes of the Origin of Land Rent? In Formulas for Its Magnitude

When one assumes, to simplify the investigation, that the capital indispensable to the worker — contained in household goods, livestock, etc. — likewise belongs to the estate owner, who receives interest thereon from the worker, then the land rent arising from the labor of a family can be presented as follows:

The labor product = *p*. The worker receives sqrt(*ap*). The capitalist receives for the capital of *q* annual labors, at the interest rate (sqrt(*ap*) - *a*) / (*qa*), the rent *R*. The land rent then = *p* - *A* - *R*. When *p* = *af*^2, land rent = *p* - *af*^2 = 0 at the border of the Isolated State.

For the Tellow estate at the border, where land rent = 0, at a rye price of 1/2 Thaler per Scheffel: wages = sqrt(*ap*) = 173 B. Sch. R., *a* = 123 B. Sch. R., *f* = 1.406, and *p* = *af*^2 = 123 x 1.976 = 243 B. Sch. R., in exact agreement with the specific determinations.

Most noteworthy is that for wages and capital use combined we have obtained the simple expression *af*^2.

In the labor product of a worker, three components are contained:
1) Wages *A* = *af*
2) Capital use *R* = *af*(*f* - 1)
3) Land rent *L* = *p* - *af*^2

*A* + *R* + *L* = *p*.

Here capital use has been traced back to labor, and just as annual labor is the measure of capital, so it also shows itself here as the measure of capital use.

The land rent that an estate yields is here proportional to the number of workers. This is the basis for the phenomenon that the wealth of the great in Russia is estimated not by the area of their estates but by the number of peasants upon them.

#### 2. Second Cause of the Origin of Land Rent

With increasing intensive cultivation on the same area, ever more workers can be employed; but with each later-employed worker his product decreases. The wage, however, equals the value of labor of the last-employed worker, which determines the wage of all other workers. The surplus that remains after paying wages and interest on the original capital constitutes a land rent that accrues to the owner of the land.

Here we encounter a second source from which land rent flows, namely the increase of intensive cultivation.

This increase of cultivation is connected with a constantly decreasing wage (in rye), but the real wage need not suffer, because the subsistence means expressed in rye decrease as one approaches the city.

#### 3. Third Cause of the Origin of Land Rent

When wages successively decrease, improvements of the soil that formerly did not pay become profitable — marling, construction of irrigation meadows, muck carting, improvement of the physical condition of the soil, deepening of the topsoil, etc. Some of these improvements are indestructible and remain fused with the soil; others require only annual maintenance; still others are consumed by plant production itself but can, with appropriate crop rotation, permanently increase the richness of the soil.

From the capital spent on improvements, only interest need be charged — if the improvements are such that they can be regarded as founded in the soil. But correctly applied capital yields, besides interest, an additional annual profit. Later, however, interest and profit can no longer be distinguished, and both accrue to the land rent, of which they form the third component.

The distinction of the three different components contained in land rent is of great importance especially when levying taxes on land rent.

#### 4. On Taxes and Free Trade

**Taxes.** Suppose the state needs, for meeting state expenditures, a revenue that, distributed over the labor product of all workers, amounts to delta Scheffel of rye per worker. This tax can be levied in various ways:

1) It is taken from the labor product without regard to whether *p* will become larger or smaller in the future.
2) The tax amounts to the *m*th part of the product and rises or falls with the size of the product.
3) The tax can be placed on the necessary subsistence means, e.g. in a salt or milling tax, whereby the necessary subsistence means are raised from *a* to *a* + delta.
4) The tax can be levied on the interest of the applied capital contained in the labor product *p*.

Which of these taxes, all yielding equal revenue to the state treasury at the moment, is least hindering to the progress of culture and national wealth?

**Taxes on Land Rent.** The first component of land rent, which arises without any effort or labor of the landowner from the mere expansion of cultivation, can be entirely taken away by the tax without the slightest disadvantage for cultivation — if only it can be recognized and captured. The second component may be taxed like any other branch of industry. The third source — the improvement of the soil — acts so beneficially on the welfare of the entire civil society that it should far sooner be promoted by premiums than hindered by taxes.

**Free Trade.** Whether customs duties on imported goods or the taxes to be substituted for them act least disadvantageously on the welfare of the nation? — In deciding this question so many other forces come into play that it is almost entirely removed from the domain of theory and must be decided separately for each individual state.

Adam Smith, in defending free trade, generally has the cosmopolitan standpoint before his eyes, but there also occur in his work passages that spring from a national standpoint — and so opponents and adherents of Adam Smith can both cite passages from his work for their views.

**The Corn Trade between Countries of Unequal Fertility.** Through a detailed analysis using two isolated states, A and Q, connected by a navigable river, von Thunen shows that free grain trade can cause state A (with less fertile soil) to lose population, capital, and land rent to state Q (with more fertile soil), even though the aggregate welfare of humanity increases. This leads to the fundamental tension: the ruler's interest in national power conflicts with the people's interest in material welfare. The resolution will come when disputes between princes and peoples are settled not by the sword but by a European court of justice, and when eternal peace is achieved — demanded alike by the peoples' striving for material welfare and by the requirements of civilization and humanity.

**Railroads.** The Isolated State with the old Mecklenburg roads extends 31.5 miles; with paved roads, 52.5 miles; with railroads, 77.9 miles. If the interest on railroad construction capital were foregone and freight halved, the cultivated plain would extend to 154 miles, occupying nearly 25 times the area. Von Thunen envisions railroads connecting St. Petersburg to the Crimea via Moscow, and Riga to Odessa through eastern Poland, transforming vast stretches into highly cultivated regions. He calculates the cost at roughly two military campaigns — a remarkably small number.

**Climate's Influence on Agriculture.** The Isolated State with railroads and foregone interest would have a diameter of 308 miles — roughly from the southern tip of Calabria to the northern tip of Jutland. Von Thunen discusses how the cultivated plain's shape would become elliptical rather than circular, how the advantages of fallow diminish southward, how livestock costs differ dramatically between north and south, and how nomadic herding is the most productive form of animal husbandry. He calls for systematic research across latitudes, requiring a well-funded man spending at least a month at each degree of latitude.

**Distribution of Cities.** Von Thunen analyzes why all factories and trades cannot simply be distributed equally across the countryside. Some need concentration near raw materials (mines), others need the large market and division of labor that only a great city provides. The association of trades — where one industry supports another — is critical: isolated factories fail because they cannot get their machinery repaired locally. Railroads, by removing the disadvantages of distance, promote the growth of large cities while the small towns would decline — except that railroads also make the surrounding countryside flourish.

**Limits of the Isolated State.** The state expands until the rent earned by capital-creating labor falls so low that it no longer compensates for the effort and self-denial required. At the border (31.5 miles from the city), the rent per annual labor amounts to only 1/63 of *a*; at this rate, approximately 33.6 years of saving and compounding at 3.65% interest are needed to accumulate enough capital for a subsistence rent in old age — roughly matching the duration of a man's productive working years.

**Population Density.** Thinner population means greater distances to towns, more costly access to craftsmen, doctors, schools, and churches. Education costs per capita triple when population density falls by two-thirds. This explains why ignorance of the people is so frequently connected with thin population. North America is an honorable exception, but its settlers brought with them a different scale of valuing knowledge and education.

**Education and Human Capital.** Von Thunen demonstrates that better-educated workers produce more and earn more. Two laws emerge: (1) the size of the profitably applicable education capital always grows as the interest rate falls, and (2) the rise of workers' subsistence costs makes investing more in education profitable. This explains why English workers are vastly superior to workers in Egypt, Persia, and India. The immaterial goods of people belong to the national wealth, because the better-educated people, equipped with the same material goods, create a far greater income.

**Man as Capital.** An inner reluctance seems to hold writers back from considering what a man costs, what capital is contained in him. But from this reluctance springs obscurity about one of the most important points of political economy. It becomes particularly destructive in war: one spares capital but not men, unhesitatingly sacrificing a hundred men in the bloom of their years to save a cannon. In those hundred men at least 20 times as much capital is lost as in the one cannon. But procuring the cannon costs the state treasury money, while men can be had again for nothing through a conscription order.

If man were valued even equally with capital, the state would have to: (1) compensate the family of every soldier killed in war for his education costs; (2) pay the crippled soldier not only for his destroyed education capital but lifelong support; (3) reimburse the healthy returning soldier for the depreciation of his strength. Wars would become immensely costly, but this would redound to the welfare of humanity. Napoleon's response to the objection that an operation would cost too many men — "That is nothing; the women produce more than I use" — reveals how even the most superior intellect was a slave of monetary interest.

**Poor Tax.** The worker who has fulfilled his life in strenuous activity deserves an independent, carefree, and effortless old age as the natural reward for unceasing exertion in the days of strength and health. The current system, where the aged worker must beg for alms or burden the poor fund, is unjust. Low wages directly call forth high poor taxes. If wages were raised sufficiently and adult children were enabled to support their aged parents, the endless calculations and the army of officials required for poor relief could be eliminated.

## Section 5

### Letters on the Statics of Agriculture

#### J. H. von Thunen to Freiherr von Voght
*Tellow, December 14, 1821*

Having been occupied for many years with a subject of our knowledge that aroused my entire attention and to which I felt increasingly drawn by the need for light and clarity, I nevertheless found until now no one to whom I could communicate my views. This joy has now come to me through the letter with which you have honored me.

That you are not in agreement with me on some points has only increased the interest that your communication has for me and has prompted me to a renewed examination of my views.

I could answer so substantial a letter only in hours of rest and freedom from business, and these have only now arrived for me, for which reason you will kindly excuse the delayed reply.

The remarks and objections that you raise against my essay stand in such close relation to von Wulffen's theory that I do not know how to answer them adequately without first setting forth the reasons that have determined me, even after the appearance of von Wulffen's work, to remain faithful to my earlier views. But since, apart from this purpose, the critique of so interesting a work has a scientific interest of its own, I take the liberty of communicating to you the remarks I have made on Wulffen's theory, in abbreviated but as complete a form as possible.

According to von Wulffen (Versuch einer Theorie, p. 7), *KR* = *F*, and therefore *K* = *F*/*R*. Since fertility can never be greater than richness, but is always smaller, *K* must therefore always be a proper fraction less than 1. Nevertheless, in this theory *K* is always calculated as a whole number, and precisely thereby a mystical veil is thrown over the meaning of *K*, making this method of calculation completely unintelligible to most farmers, but also potentially confusing even for the knowledgeable, as is shown particularly in the application of the calculation to wheat cultivation.

If we omit the proportionality number 60 from the formula (*K* + 1)*R* = 6 x 60 (page 12) — which is used here merely to facilitate calculation and whose necessity is by no means demonstrated — we obtain (*K* + 1)*R* = 6, and if *R* = 72: (*K* + 1) x 72 = 6, therefore 72*K* = -66, *K* = -11/12, which makes no sense because *K* cannot be a negative number.

But if we change the formula to ((*K*/60) + 1)*R* = 6, we obtain for *K* the same value as on page 14, namely 4; but this 4 is not a whole number, but rather the numerator of a fraction whose denominator is 60.

This, however, also changes the form of the calculation, and I would not say, as in the example given on page 23: the power of the soil after the fallow = 12, the richness = 30, therefore the fertility = 30 x 12/60 = 6; but rather: the power is = 12/60, the richness = 30, therefore the fertility = 12/60 x 30 = 6.

In the formula ((*K*/60) + 1)*R* = 6, the so-called proportionality number 60 is assumed. But one easily sees that if one substitutes a different number that can equally well be divided into 3 factors, for example 48 or 120, this must have an essential influence on the magnitude of *K*.

If *R* = 72, then for the proportionality number 60: *K* = 4; for the proportionality number 120: *K* = 9. In the first case, the natural power of the soil is raised by the fallow from 4/60 to 5/60, that is, by 1/4; in the second case from 9/120 to 10/120, that is, only by 1/9. The proportionality number therefore cannot be arbitrarily assumed, but must, just like *R*, be derived from a case given by experience.

The magnitude of *K* is therefore not found through this calculation, but merely assumed.

But apart from all these formulas and calculations, it seems clear to me that from the mere knowledge of the difference between two fallow-rye harvests one cannot yet know what the yield of the stubble rye will be.

**Distinction between Richness and Fertility.**

On a soil whose power after the fallow = 3, whose richness = 120 degrees, and whose fertility is therefore 3 x 120 = 360 degrees, 6 Scheffel of wheat extract 7.2 degrees (according to page 29 of the Theory).

If we now set the power at its true value, not at 3 but at 3/60, then the fertility becomes: 3/60 x 120 = 6 degrees. The yield of 6 Scheffel of wheat extracts 7.2 degrees. Here, therefore, more fertility has been exhausted than was developed, which, according to the definition of fertility given on page 5, cannot occur.

On one and the same soil, where *K* and *R* are thus determinate quantities, the quantity of nutritive substances that directly sustain the life of plants (Wulffen's "fertility") is greater or smaller depending on the different crop grown on this soil. The quantity of this substance is therefore not determined by *K* and *R* alone, but depends, besides these, on the greater or lesser power of the plant to attract this substance.

*KR* is, for a given soil, a determinate quantity; *F* (in von Wulffen's sense) is supposed to equal the product of these two factors, but is in fact dependent on the crop grown, and therefore not a constant quantity that could serve as a measure of the yield of all crops.

That the fertility of the soil is to be regarded as the product of several factors is a splendid idea, through which Herr von Wulffen has earned a lasting merit for agriculture.

This proposition acknowledged as correct, it remains to seek out and determine the factors in the application of this proposition.

Herr von Wulffen decomposes fertility into the two factors *R* and *K*, and I can regard this decomposition as neither sufficient nor exhaustive, for several reasons.

Here only a few reasons: Since sandy soil, according to von Wulffen, has a greater power than loamy soil, it would follow from this theory, where *KR* = *F*, that manure applied to sandy soil would within a shorter period produce the same product as the same manure applied to loam soil would deliver in a longer period. Just as the capitalist who has the choice of investing his money at either 6 or 4 percent will undoubtedly prefer the higher interest, by which he recovers his capital in 17 years instead of 25, so too the farmer who possesses both loamy and sandy soil but does not have enough manure to bring the entire area into the highest cultivation would have to direct his manure toward the sandy soil and withdraw it from the loam. In practice, however, precisely the opposite occurs, and the theory immediately finds itself in contradiction with a universally recognized correct procedure.

According to all experience, sheep-fold manure passes far more quickly into plants than cow dung. If one now gives a soil whose richness = *R* a manuring of cow dung containing 15 degrees, the richness is raised to *R* + 15, and *F* is then *K*(*R* + 15); according to this formula, the yield would be equal in both cases, which contradicts all experience.

If one wished, in order to produce agreement with reality, to assume that *K* is raised by the sheep-fold, then *K* ceases to be a property of the soil, for the property inherent in the soil must remain one and the same as long as its physical composition does not change. The altered yield arises not from a change in the soil, but from the difference in the manure applied to it.

According to von Wulffen, the power of the soil is raised by the fallow from *K* to *K* + 1. We note quite generally that the same soil at equal richness gives a different yield depending on the degree of cultivation. But whether the effect of cultivation is to be regarded as an addition to the power of the soil seems to me very doubtful. If we bring the fallow cultivation into the reckoning as an addition to the power, then we must not exclude the cultivation for spring grain from the reckoning either, for it affects the yield of the spring grain just as much as that of the winter grain, whether we plow once, twice, or three times.

If we set the power of the soil for spring grain, when it receives 3 furrows, = *K*, then to represent the correct yield in the formula, we would have to assume for 2 furrows *F* = (*K* - 1)*R*, and for one furrow *F* = (*K* - 2)*R*. But for all our cereals, cultivation is necessary, and if it ceases entirely, their yield = 0. For this case *F* = 0 as well, so *KR* must also = 0, which can only occur if we subtract *K* itself from the power of the soil for the missing cultivation: then (*K* - *K*)*R* = 0.

Through this, however, the power of the soil would become entirely dependent on cultivation and would remain nothing but an expression for the influence that cultivation exerts on fertility — which, again, cannot be right, since the law expressed by von Wulffen, "that the transition from humus to direct plant nutrition requires an unequal duration on different soil types," is confirmed in reality even when all soil types receive equal cultivation. It seems to me certain, therefore, that the influence of cultivation and the influence of the soil's power may not be regarded as of the same nature, and that their joint effect cannot be represented by an addition or subtraction.

If I have here dwelt so long on what seems deficient to me in von Wulffen's theory, without mentioning what is excellent in it, this is only because the former — for the enrichment of science — is a subject for further discussion, while the latter, as already settled, requires no further investigation. I beg you rather to regard these criticisms of Wulffen's theory as a sign of my respect for its author; for only what is significant is worth the trouble of correction.

I confess that in the detailed communication of my views on Wulffen's theory, I flattered myself with the hope that you too would consider these views worthy of your examination, and through the communication of your judgment, would strengthen or correct my opinion.

After this long introduction, I now proceed to the actual answering of your letter.

An essential difference in our opinions arises from the fact that you extend your theory to soil types whose richness has arisen not merely from manuring, but which also contain other nourishing substances, while I restrict my investigations to a soil whose entire humus content is the residue of earlier manurings, and deliberately exclude all fertilization through marling, muck, or alluvial deposits from my calculation — because it seems to me that the calculation becomes too complicated so long as the simple case has not yet been brought to complete clarity.

I had in mind a three-field system that has been carried on uniformly for centuries and has received nothing but manure as replacement for the exhausting grain crops. Whatever the soil contained of fertilizing substances at its first reclamation must now, if it was suitable for plant nutrition, have disappeared — just as in the series 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, etc., the last terms become so small that they are to be regarded as 0. Of the older humus, therefore, nothing can remain in the soil; if anything other than the residue of manurings is contained in it, this must be a substance that is annually supplied to the soil.

Very interesting to me is your remark that the soil, besides humus, also possesses all gas that is absorbed by it or arises through decomposition within it. This accepted, two cases remain to be investigated:

1) The absorption and decomposition stand in proportion to the humus content of the soil, or:

2) The magnitude of the absorption is conditioned solely by the physical properties of the soil and is independent of the humus content.

In the first case, the humus content would after all again become the sole measure, since with the increased quantity of it, an equally increased absorption would occur.

In the second case, the soil itself would cover the exhaustion for a portion of the harvest, say for *b* Scheffel, and if the total harvest amounted to *a* Scheffel, only the exhaustion for *a* - *b* Scheffel would need to be replaced through manure.

This assumption gains some probability from the fact that dead earth becomes fertile over time and that old clay walls have a fertilizing power; but I have returned from every attempt I have made to assign the soil a share of the harvest in the theory, because I could not reconcile so many experiences with it.

I am therefore very eager to learn what you have observed and discovered on this subject.

Already in the notebook that I wrote in 1806, there stands, regarding Section 2 of my essay — against which you have made such well-founded objections — the following addition, which I communicate here in its essential content nearly verbatim:

"The only well-founded objection that can be made against this proposition is: that the freshly applied manure is not in the same condition as the manure particles already longer in the field; from the former, therefore, a portion of the manure mass is used for vegetation that is either greater or less than the portion from the latter. This relationship changes as the manure in the soil grows older; for example, it may be that in the first year 1/5, in the second year 1/6, and in the third year 1/7 (i.e., of the residue) passes into the rye."

"If the manure passed into the plants in a stronger proportion than the humus, then the conclusion from the yield to the manure mass would be incorrect. Comparative experiments, in which one observed the decline of the crops on a field freshly manured and on a field manured several years ago, could soon decide this. One would thereby find that of the manure *a* parts and of the humus *b* parts pass into the plants, and so one could likewise conclude the entire mass of the humus."

"But if, as we have assumed, the crops decrease regularly in yield, it follows unconditionally that the humus has been brought by the fallow to an equal degree of solubility with the manure, and that the solubility of both is maintained in an equal degree by the fact that for the second crop the soil is plowed three times and for the third crop twice. Since experience appears to confirm these propositions, we may also accept the conclusion from yield to quantity of manure, until exact experiments show us the modifications of this law under other conditions."

This addition was no longer satisfactory to me; I omitted it because I wished to devote a separate section to this point — the most intricate in the entire doctrine of fertility — and because I wished first to make the reader familiar with the idea in general before I proceeded to the more rigorous and difficult determinations. This method is perhaps objectionable in itself, but it becomes entirely inadmissible through my communicating the first section separately. I had not considered this, and I thank you for having drawn my attention to it.

If the two objections answered here against Section 2 were occasioned only by my deficient exposition, the subject of the third objection was, by contrast, entirely overlooked by me in the theory.

"You regard manure not merely as plant nutrition, but also attribute to it an exciting power, whereby it carries the humus along into fermentation and makes it fit for direct plant nutrition."

However little I incorporated this view into the theory, yet upon reading your letter no doubt remained as to its correctness, for I immediately remembered that in practice I had long acted according to this view.

I had previously left some field pieces unmanured that were very rich and of which I feared they would produce lodged grain after the marling. But although I had to attribute to these pieces a greater richness even without manuring than to the adjoining pieces with manuring, I found that they fell significantly behind in yield in the first crop.

The question now is, however, whether the manure shows this exciting power in all circumstances or only under certain conditions. The pieces mentioned had a moist situation and had lain fallow for 2-3 years. From other observations it seems probable to me that the manure can exercise this exciting power only when the slow fermentation, by which the humus gradually becomes direct plant nutrition, has been interrupted either by the wetness of the soil or by lying fallow (assuming that all humus has arisen from residues of earlier manurings); but that in three-field farming on dry, annually cultivated soil, this fermentation process continues without interruption and therefore does not need to be kindled by fresh manure — for otherwise, in my opinion, in a three-field system that manures once every 6 years, the difference between the manured and the unmanured fallow field would have to be greater than we find it in reality.

But even if there are also circumstances and conditions in which the manure cannot accelerate the fermentation process, nevertheless in a formula that aims to represent the relationship of humus content to yield in general, and in a proposition that teaches how to find this relationship, the exciting power of manure should not be overlooked.

It becomes very difficult, however, to establish principles by which the richness of the soil can be determined from its yield; for just as the method of inference I have used is no longer adequate for this, neither can one draw conclusions from the difference between two harvests, because the fermentation accelerated by the manure cannot cease all at once, but only diminishes gradually, in a still unknown proportion, back to the natural fermentation (produced by soil and cultivation).

Now permit me the remark that the reproach made against Section 2 strikes not merely my theory but also the present state of science itself. All calculations in Wulffen's theory and in your work on rapeseed cultivation are tacitly based on the same inference used in Section 2. For first, the richness that a manuring contains, expressed in degrees, is added to the richness already in the soil, thereby assuming the homogeneity of both kinds of richness; second, 100 degrees of richness that have been added by manuring act on the yield just as 100 degrees already in the soil do. There is therefore between us only a difference in expression, not in principles.

Your remark that in marsh soil the humus stands in no proportion at all to the high yield is new to me and quite contrary to my previous opinion. Nor does this agree with Crome's chemical investigations, who in his work "The Soil and Its Relationship to Plants" (pp. 89-91) found 18 percent humus in a marsh soil from Oldenburg, 10 percent in the Oderbruch at Jaelsbruch, and 17 percent at Alt-Briesdorf. You would therefore oblige me if you would communicate your reasons for this.

You say: "The general experience that the manure in certain soil works with approximately half its power in the following year." This is not entirely clear to me, and I ask you for an explanation through an example expressed in numbers.

You assume as an empirical proposition that every winter grain cereal diminishes the fertility of the soil by much more than the richness it has lost.

Against this I must assume, on the basis of the sum of the experiences and observations before me, that if the cultivation for the spring grain following the winter grain is carried to the degree where further cultivation no longer increases the yield, then the power of the soil (to use your designation) for the spring grain is just as great as it was through the fallow for the winter grain.

Building on different experiences, our views and calculations must here necessarily diverge. Since, however, in well-conducted three-field and Koppel systems the cultivation of the soil is really carried to this degree, I can for these two farming systems assume the degree of the soil's power for spring and winter grain to be equally high. It is different in the farming systems where the winter grain is sown not in the pure fallow but in the stubble of another crop; here the same richness can no longer deliver the same yield; but I believe, for the reasons given in section VI, that the changed relationship between richness and yield cannot be represented by an addition to or subtraction from the soil's power.

In my view, the influence of cultivation and of the preceding crop are to be regarded as factors of fertility.

Suppose fertility equals the product of the factors *A*, *B*, ... *Y*, *R*: then *F* = *A* x *B* x ... x *Y* x *R*. If now on a given soil the yield of the rye after the fallow = *b* Scheffel, then *A* x *B* x ... x *Y* x *R* = *b*. Through this harvest the richness has declined from *R* to *R* - *b*. The yield = *A* x *B* x ... x *Y* x (*R* - *b*) = *x*. It is therefore the case that *A* x *B* x ... x *Y* x *R* : *A* x *B* x ... x *Y* x (*R* - *b*) = *b* : *x*, and so *R* / (*R* - *b*) = *b* / *x*.

Here all factors that have undergone no change — both the known and the still unknown — become = 1. Since the factor 1 changes nothing in the magnitude of the product, it would also be very superfluous to want to state it each time; one need rather only specify those factors that undergo a change in order to present the correct product.

This is now the reason why I, although acknowledging the effect of the other factors (p. 188 of my essay), nevertheless take richness, so long as it is the only variable factor, as the sole measure of fertility. As soon, however, as one wishes to compare the yield of different crop rotations on different soils with unequal cultivation methods, the factor corresponding to each difference must reappear in the formula.

Very keenly I share the wish that we might use the same technical terms for the same concepts. The deficiency of the expressions I chose I felt acutely myself in preparing my essay. I have used too many words as synonyms that, though they may be taken as such in common life, ought in systematic exposition to be sharply distinguished. To designate the different yield that the same richness gives, depending on whether the grain is sown in the fallow or in the stubble of another crop, by a different degree of the manure's effectiveness, is inconvenient and perhaps unsuitable. Von Wulffen's terms "richness" and "fertility" are far more apt. But I could not adopt these for the reasons given in section IV, and to avoid misunderstandings I sought to avoid the use of these words as much as possible, which often constrained me. I therefore request you to suggest technical expressions that remove my stated concerns, and I shall then very gladly adopt them. Above all, the word "fertility" would have to be defined differently than has been done by Wulffen. "Capacity" (Vermogen) seems to me more fitting than "richness"; for it is surely contrary to usage to attribute "richness" to a soil that yields only 3 grains.

From several passages in your letter, and especially from the fact that you attribute to sandy soil a lesser power than to loamy soil, I see that you understand by power of the soil something quite different from Wulffen, and I am convinced that you thereby achieve a far greater agreement with reality. Now I fear, however, that even through the sharpest definition you will not prevent the generally very inattentive reader from always confusing this concept with what Wulffen calls power and Thaer calls activity, unless you choose a different expression for it.

The excessive length of this reply you must attribute to the profoundly penetrating remarks of your letter, which I could either not answer at all or only in detail.

From the interest that your letter has inspired in me, you may gather with what eagerness I look forward to the early appearance of your work on the statics of agriculture.

Already through your work on rapeseed cultivation you have earned a great merit for agriculture. The statics of agriculture (is there no more fitting name for this new doctrine?), which has hitherto been regarded as dead knowledge, has thereby at once entered into practical life. Your calculations are seldom understood, the consequences of rapeseed cultivation that you show us are generally unwelcome; all the greater, however, is the desire to allay these aroused concerns, and the wish to refute your propositions. One need not wonder, therefore, if some shallow objections come to light; but precisely these must make thoughtful farmers feel the need to study the statics of agriculture.

From an earlier time, when you permitted Staudinger's students to instruct themselves through the inspection of your operations and your accounts, I know what a treasure of experiences is deposited in your agricultural bookkeeping. Easily centuries may pass before circumstances again combine so favorably, and I have often lamented that all this should be lost to posterity and to science.

I therefore cannot suppress the wish that it might please you to devote all your leisure to the advancement of agriculture, so neglected a science, and to communicate your many years of experiments and experiences to the world. Connected with one another by the thread of a comprehensive theory, they would be of far greater use to science than the experiments and observations of the English, in which the unity and the higher standpoint that theory provides are only too much wanting.

**Further development of the propositions contained in the letter to Freiherr von Voght.**

*Supplement to Proposition 6.* The transition of humus to extractive substance is promoted by the cultivation of the soil, and proceeds the more rapidly the more each individual particle can be brought into contact with the atmosphere. The latter, however, is dependent on the physical properties of the soil: clay soil resists this far more than sandy soil. If, according to experience, the transition of humus to extractive substance proceeds more slowly on the former soil than on the latter, this arises in part undeniably from the fact that clay soil cannot be divided into such small particles (which is also why fallow is all the more effective on this soil, as the weather favors this pulverization).

Here the soil appears as hindering, not as active, and in this respect we shall have to attribute to the soil the property of impeding the transition — a force that is in itself negative, but that can appear to us as positive when we compare its effect on soil that is less impeding (sandy soil) with the strongly impeding soil (clay soil).

This seems to be confirmed by the fact that manure, when lying unmixed with the soil on the manure-heaps, completes the cycle to extractive substance and finally to carbon even more rapidly than on the most active sandy soil. In sandy soil we find the manure, several years after manuring, still partly undecomposed as carbon; but on the manure-heap we find this transformation already after one year and in far greater proportion.

I will not deny that besides this impeding property, the soil may also act chemically on this cycle. In any case, however, the more or less rapid transition to extractive substance would then be to be regarded as the product of two forces: the chemically positively acting and the mechanically impeding or negatively acting force.

It would also be striking if sand, which otherwise behaves almost neutrally toward all substances and has no affinity for acids, should show itself so active in the formation of extractive substance.

*Supplement to Proposition 4, page 6 of the letter.* It follows further that the fertility represented by the two factors *K* x *R* can provide no measure for the magnitude of the harvests of different grain types. Here the peculiar capacity of different plants to appropriate a greater or lesser part of the prepared plant nutrition comes into consideration. Nor can one assume that all plant nutrition prepared for the direct nourishment of plants can be exhausted by a single grain harvest. On clayey soil, after the most careful fallow cultivation, small clods will remain lying on the surface, and the prepared nutritive substance contained in these clods is in any case ineffective for this grain crop; for even if the plant roots could penetrate the clods, this does not happen, since grain plants drive their roots only downward and sideways, but not upward. The quantity of prepared plant nutrition (fertility) therefore stands in no such determinate relationship to richness that we could specify the magnitude of one from the other; at least we lack all data for representing this relationship.

#### J. H. von Thunen to von Voght
*Tellow, May 20, 1822*

I hasten to answer your letter of March 13, which I received only yesterday, by return post. Kindly excuse my not having reported to you earlier the correct receipt of your manuscript.

For the time being, I can only tell you that I have read the manuscript with great interest, and that nothing seems to me so well suited to extend one's own views and to promote knowledge in general as the comparison of observations on the same subject from two different standpoints.

After reading your manuscript, it seems to me — if instead of the one-sided written communication, the oral one could take place — no longer difficult to find a point of agreement for our different views.

What you say about the power of the soil is original, new, and interesting — especially important to me is the later remark on this subject — and provides rich material for reflection. I dare not yet express my opinion on it, all the less since it has not yet firmly formed, and since it can only arise from the comprehensive comparison of whether this way or that way brings the phenomena of nature best into agreement.

In some cases it seems to me that the property of the soil which you call power relates to quality as cause to effect; but in other cases the concept of power is more complicated and cannot be demonstrated in experience as clearly as quality.

If the soil, by virtue of its peculiar power, can attract substances from the air that serve as nutrition for plants — is the magnitude of this absorption entirely bound to the physical properties of the soil, and also in its effect on vegetation independent of the material humus?

Does the soil find the substances that it can absorb and transform into plant nutrition everywhere in adequate quantity, or not?

If oxygen and nitrogen gas belong to this, the former would be the case; but if carbonic acid gas plays the main role, then even the nature of the atmosphere would have to be included in this calculation, since carbonic acid gas is surely found in greater quantity mixed with the atmospheric air near cities and in humus-rich cultivated regions than in uncultivated deserts.

The concept of power necessarily leads to these and other questions, whose answers have a high scientific interest.

Since you yourself invite me to make remarks on your manuscript, I take the liberty of expressing my opinion on two points where my view deviates from yours.

That the fine wheat soil is not so severely exhausted by a wheat harvest as the moderate rye soil by a rye harvest of equal bushel number, experience teaches. But this is not restricted merely to wheat; the fine soil is also less exhausted by an oat harvest of equal volume than the moderate soil.

It seems to me that the law that exhaustion stands in direct proportion to the nutritive mass of the harvest must remain the same for every soil without distinction, and that the different exhaustion which the same grain harvest produces on unequal soil is to be attributed to the different quality of the soil types.

You attribute to your sandy soil a lesser power than to the fine loam — this I consider in agreement with reality; but you further assume that 1 cartload of manure gives the loam 3 degrees of richness, the sandy soil 5 to 7 degrees.

Since the mild loam in one rotation produces decidedly more from 1 cartload of manure than the poor sandy soil, and since the degrees of richness that we assign to 1 cartload of manure are supposed to designate how many Scheffel of rye arise from this manure until its complete consumption, I cannot agree with this assumption.

You yourself say: "Sandy soil does not pay for the manure," and in the note to the table you attribute to 1 cartload of manure on sandy soil 5 degrees of expression of fertility. We are therefore certainly in agreement on the main point; you wished through the higher degree of expression to fit the formula to the actual experience, where sandy soil in the first year gives a higher yield from the same manuring. But must not richness be regarded as something given and determinate, and its different expression be expressed through the other factor? Will you not, in calculating a whole rotation on sandy soil, have to distinguish the degree of actual richness from the degree of its expression in each following year? Should this not lead to the conclusion that the decomposition of fertility into power and richness alone is not sufficient, that a third factor — or rather a proportionality number designating the part of the humus that attains full effectiveness — is necessary?

If this opinion should receive your agreement, our views would thereby be further reconciled.

#### J. H. von Thunen to von Voght
*Tellow, October 3, 1822*

Through the sending of your newest writing you have made me a most agreeable gift, and I feel impelled to convey to you at once, by return post, my most obliged thanks.

Your manuscript I have read repeatedly and each time found new instruction in it.

If we wish to question nature through experiments, these would have to be continued for at least 30 years in the same direction and with equal attention, in order to determine the average influence of weather, if their results were to serve as an immovable basis for theory — which would then only need to bring these results into agreement. Experiments that meet these demands we shall probably never have.

In the views of a farmer who has 30 years of experience, the overall impression of his observations is expressed. In experiments, nature speaks purely — without an interpreter; in views, the facts have received an interpretation through the observer — and so it depends entirely on the spirit of the observer whether his experiences are completely worthless for science, or whether they have an importance approaching that of experimental results.

In this regard, your manuscript is of inestimable value to me; my experiences are expanded thereby, and at the same time I obtain a second fixed point of comparison. I only wish, for myself and for science, that you would extend and elaborate this further, and thereby pass on a larger piece from the rich field of your experiences and observations to posterity.

Through reading your manuscript and your letters, some of my views have been further strengthened by finding them in agreement with yours; others have been expanded, corrected, or newly stimulated through the comparison. Your ideas afford me this advantage especially because they are original, not at all related in their origin to my ideas, and conceived from a quite different standpoint.

But if the originality of thoughts and observations is already of such great value for the contemporary, this must be the case in an even higher degree for the future researcher of the statics; he will then find in the writings of his predecessors the materials for a completed building.

In this sense it seems to me very important that the present cultivator of the statics preserve his originality, take up nothing foreign except what can be brought into perfect harmony with his observations and ideas, that he deliver with the presentation of his ideas also the history of their development and gradual modification, and thereby put the later cultivator in a position to trace back from the views of his predecessor to the facts from which he drew, and thereby to track down any possible error at its source.

It also seems to me dangerous to lightly abandon a view that has already withstood the quiet examination of several years: it has already happened to me several times that I could not immediately refute ingenious objections against an opinion and was on the point of abandoning it, but later found that it was rooted and grounded in earlier recollections and experiences.

If now, as it seems to me, the preservation of originality in ideas and thereby also in formulas and expressions is desirable for the progress of science, it is on the other hand undeniable that the difference of the writers on statics in views and language enormously complicates the study of statics, and that thereby the impact of this science on practical farming is greatly delayed. But it seems to me that the first purpose stands — at least for now — higher than the second.

Meanwhile, among the present cultivators of statics there are some differences and deviations that are as little grounded in the individuality of the writers as in their observations, and for whose reconciliation I should so gladly claim your mediation.

Thus you, Thaer, Wulffen, and I each assume a different ratio in the value or nutritiveness of different grain types relative to one another, and consequently also a different ratio of the exhaustion that their production causes. Here none of us proceeds from his own experiments, which could justify a difference. Everyone refers to chemical analyses, to the spirit content, and to the average price, and thus draws from the same source. The difference seems to me to arise from the fact that to facilitate calculation the larger ratio numbers are avoided, and the approximate ratio is then expressed by some in decimals, by others in common fractions. This now, however, enormously complicates and confuses the study of the writings on statics, and makes their comparison very difficult.

With your prestige and your connections, you would probably succeed in bringing about an agreement on this, if you would be so kind as to submit proposals to Thaer and Wulffen.

Then it seems to me still highly desirable that universally the exhaustion caused by the production of one Berlin Scheffel of rye be adopted as the unit and measure for the richness of the soil, and indeed without regard to the soil type, thus equally for wheat and rye soil.

Since I first conceived the idea of designating the exhaustion of the soil by numbers, it was indeed my opinion that the manure power of the soil was the sole measure of fertility — and perhaps I would never have come to the execution of the propositions built thereon, but would, like so many others, have been scared off by the difficulty of wanting to represent through a calculation the great number of forces acting on vegetation, if I had not started from so simple an idea. In applying my calculation, the influence of the soil had soon to confront me, and already about 10 years ago I arrived at the view of the quality of the soil. When I wrote the essay "on the quantitative effect of manure," the influence of the soil on fertility was by no means foreign to me; this is repeatedly stated in the essay on pages 187 and 188, and is factually shown on page 288, where the quality of the soil is included in the calculation. But it was and still is my opinion that when the same soil and the same treatment are under discussion, then the altered manure power (as the only altered factor) alone is to be credited with the change in yield.

It was now my intention to present the influence of manure power first for a single soil type and for a determinate treatment, then to determine the effect of manure on other soil types, and only later, when these data were available, to attempt whether from the different effect of manure on different soil types the law by which the soil exerts its influence could be represented.

I freely admit that your method, from the very beginning considering soil and manure in constant connection with each other, may have certain advantages; but I also believe that for a third observer the comparison of what has been accomplished by both different methods may be of essential benefit.

It was my intention that this writing should at the same time give a history of the development and gradual advancement of my ideas. The first half of the essay is therefore printed almost verbatim from the manuscript I wrote in 1806. What was still indeterminate and too isolated therein was to be more precisely determined and connected with other subjects in the continuation. This has in the second half of the essay been able to occur only partially, and so in this fragment the proposition "that manure power alone is the measure of fertility" may easily appear as the predominant tendency. But precisely to this circumstance I owe the astute objections and remarks that you have communicated to me on this subject — and so I am very satisfied with it.

Moreover, I wished to make only the results of the soil's influence the subject of my investigation, and I freely confess that it lay neither in my plan nor in my calling to investigate the cause, or the manner and mode, of this influence: your interesting investigations are the first and only ones we possess on this important subject.

The word "primal power" (*Urkraft*), chosen by you for power, seems to me so apt that it cannot be replaced by any other. In the word itself lies a determinacy that could not be given by any definition.

You say: "The more humus and the more lime in the soil, the more carbonic acid gas is produced. The lime continually attracts what is given off, just as through the leaves and roots of the plants oxygen and nitrogen are alternately absorbed and resorbed." These four lines say a great deal and appeal to me enormously. It seems to me necessary that you communicate this view to the public soon — if only separately — so that a one-sided view, according to which the entire business of the soil consists in giving off its humus to the atmosphere, does not become predominant.

However much I agree with you about the essence of the primal power, and however much I rejoice in the enrichment of our knowledge in this respect, I cannot reconcile myself with Wulffen's formula — which you still use — if this is to express the concept of primal power. Wulffen's formula very well expresses the activity of the soil, but in my conviction cannot at all designate the essence of the primal power; rather, it frequently directly contradicts it.

Since, according to all experience, the decline of successive crops is greater on sandy soil than on good loamy soil — or sandy soil gives up a greater portion of its richness to the rye harvest than loamy soil — to express this in the formula, the numerator of the first factor must be raised for sandy soil. According to your own and certainly correct theory, good loam has a much higher primal power than dry sandy soil. The factor that is supposed to designate primal power must therefore be higher for the former soil than for the latter — and so it stands here in precisely the inverse proportion to the factor for activity, and therefore one and the same factor cannot simultaneously designate both primal power and activity.

I am very eager to hear your remarks and comments on this.

#### J. H. von Thunen to von Voght
*Tellow, February 22, 1824*

From your goodness, my esteemed patron, I may hope that you will not reluctantly receive my communications, even when these come so seldom — and I cannot possibly let Herr Staudinger depart without bringing myself to your remembrance through a few lines.

Through your last letter the disputed points have been settled, as far as this can be done through a mutual exchange of ideas, and I therefore must not return to them. But I must express my thanks for the call: "not to despair of time, and to leave no enterprise undone from fear of not being able to complete it." This call, from you and at your age, could not remain without effect on me, and I have begun an experiment that can only yield results after several years.

A piece of uniform field of 16 square rods is divided into 4 parts:

a) is manured in the usual manner,

b) is left unmanured,

c) shall, during an entire rotation, when the grain is in blossom, be mown green,

d) shall remain fallow throughout the entire rotation, the field being turned over whenever it begins to show green.

This experiment, if successfully carried through, must in the second rotation give us a contribution to answering the questions:

1) What is the ratio of the exhaustion caused by a crop mown in blossom to that of a crop ripened?

2) Does the field become richer or poorer through continued cultivation and constant exposure to the air?

Should such an experiment fit into your plan and you wish to undertake it, the comparison of the final results of both experiments could not fail to benefit science.

The essays of Doctor Spalding in Gustrow (who signs himself X and frequently appears in the Mecklenburg Annals as an opponent of the statics) gave me the first idea for this experiment. Herr X maintains that the humus, made soluble through the cultivation of the soil, rises in gaseous form and is then either absorbed by the plants that happen to be on the field, or, if the plants are not present, blown away by the wind; that therefore the exhaustion of the soil is caused by the mere cultivation, and by no means by plant growth.

However little I agree with this hypothesis, and however easy it is to set another line of reasoning against it, it nevertheless seems to me very difficult to defeat it with undeniable facts. This, however, reveals a weak side of our previous knowledge. The natural laws relating to agriculture we know almost exclusively from the observations that a farming operation directed toward money-making permits. As soon as something is at issue about which nature cannot speak in such profit-seeking operations — such as the effect of a several-year fallow — we can combat the opinion only with opinions, instead of defeating it with facts. For the further progress of science, experimental farms are therefore indispensable — and we must gratefully acknowledge that you have given your farm this direction.

In the penultimate issue of the Annals, Herr X has retracted his earlier view on exhaustion, and this almost grieves me, since the statics has thereby lost an interesting opponent and this hypothesis its representative.

For the communication of your experiments on potato cultivation I tender my most obliged thanks. These experiments are interesting to me through the results they yield — and at the same time give me the agreeable confirmation that you continue with equal untiring activity to work for our science.

Especially important to me is your remark that when the capacity for yield rises above 720 degrees, the yield then no longer stands in direct proportion to it — since through observation of the local operations I have been led to a similar assumption. I am thereby prompted to call into question once more the proposition "that the yield for low degrees of fertility stands in direct proportion to the humus content." I was fortunate enough to find a formula that brought unity to my observations and which I therefore still value very highly. The correctness of the formula certainly cannot be tested from one standpoint — and I would very gladly submit what I have written down on this to your judgment; but unfortunately this is so brief and incomplete that in this form it is not intelligible or communicable.

Nevertheless, I cannot refrain from laying before you the results of this formula. On 1,000 square rods of field, when the activity and quality of the soil remain equal:

For a yield of 40 Berlin Scheffel, the humus content is 179 degrees, the exhaustion of the rye about 1/9.
For 80 Scheffel: 400 degrees, exhaustion about 1/5.
For 100 Scheffel: 939 degrees, exhaustion about 1/11.
For 120 Scheffel: 720 degrees, exhaustion about 1/6.
For 140 Scheffel: 1,159 degrees, exhaustion about 1/8.

The lodging of the grain and the resulting limitation of the yield are not yet taken into account in this formula.

Should this or a similar formula one day prove true, a great number of phenomena that we now explain from a changed activity of the soil would find their explanation in the changed humus content.

The statics would thereby become extremely difficult in its application, but if nature does not act according to such simple, easily comprehensible laws, we shall have to accommodate ourselves to investigating even its complicated laws.

#### J. H. von Thunen to von Voght
*Tellow, March 10, 1826*

My long silence stands in the strongest contrast with the interest that your last letter has inspired in me. But I would only tire you with the reasons for this long silence, which lie partly in my personal, partly in my business circumstances, and I therefore proceed directly to the answering of your interesting letter.

The theory that you set forth concerning the effect of crops mown green on the soil and its richness has occupied me vividly and lastingly.

Involuntarily a view diametrically opposed to yours was vividly recalled to my memory. I absolutely cannot remember to whom this view belongs, where I read it, or from whom I heard it; but it gives, placed beside yours, occasion for very interesting comparisons, and you will therefore permit me to communicate this view to you here in detail.

Until the blossoming of the plant, all its organic parts — leaves, stalk, and root — are in the highest activity: the pores of the leaves as well as those of the roots are open; the former take up gases and moisture from the atmosphere, the latter take up humus from the soil. With the completed blossoming, however, the plant changes its entire appearance: the leaves turn yellow, shrivel, and in this condition are visibly no longer suited for the uptake of atmospheric substances. Less visible is the change that occurs with the root; but the plant is an organic whole, of which one part cannot die while the other retains its full vital activity.

What the leaves are above the earth, the fine absorbing roots are below it; as the former wither, so too these die, and indeed when we take plants out of the earth, we see that they have all the more absorbing roots the younger they are. After the completed blossoming the grain swells, but the stalk bleaches and loses, in the same measure as the grain increases, its nourishing juices; and when the ripening is completed, the stalk — which, cut at the blossom, would have given a very nourishing livestock feed — has sunk to an almost nutritionless, nearly mere fiber material of straw. What the grain has gained in nutritive substance, the stalk has lost; ripening is therefore nothing other than a transformation of the nourishing juices of the stalk into mealy grains.

From this it follows that the plant lives until the blossoming from the atmosphere and the soil, but afterward from the juices already taken up into itself; that therefore not the seed formation but the formation of the plant body exhausts the soil.

From this is further explained why plants grown in pure earth without humus, merely through water and air, up to the blossoming, can produce no seed: for the stalk is then, instead of nourishing juices, filled with watery moisture, from which no seed grain can arise.

For this view of the unnamed author and against your view speaks:

1) The fact that some plants that never come to seed formation, for example cabbage, exhaust the soil very severely, since the land that has borne cabbage requires a strong manuring to replace the exhaustion caused by the production of this plant;

2) That in the cereal crops mown in blossom, almost as much nutrition for livestock is already contained as after ripening in grain and straw together.

Against this unnamed author and for your theory speaks, on the other hand:

1) The extraordinarily great effect that you have had from green manuring;

2) The experience that winter grain sown after vetches turns out the worse, the further the vetches had advanced in ripening.

Since the unnamed author is not present and cannot defend himself, you will permit me now to become his advocate and to say for him what he could or would reply according to his theory.

a. I grant the great effect of green manuring as a fact; but from this it by no means follows that the crops plowed under green enrich the soil, that is, return to it more than they have taken from it. We know that different kinds of manure possess a very different degree of solubility or capacity for transition, as already appears from the comparison of sheep-fold manure with strawy cow dung. The vegetable manure arising from crops plowed under green is, however, perhaps the most soluble of all, and is wholly consumed by the next harvest. Suppose that of the stable manure only 1/4 is capable of transition, while the green manure is wholly consumed by the first crop, and the green manure shows itself in the first crop equal to the manuring of 4 cartloads of stable manure: then the effect of the green manure is only equal to that of one cartload of actually consumed stable manure; if the production of the green crop has cost the soil one cartload of stable manure — which according to my hypothesis just developed is entirely possible — then the crop plowed under green has, despite the deceptive appearance, produced no enrichment of the soil whatsoever. Not the success on the next crop, but only the comparison of the sum of replacement with the sum of exhaustion in a farming operation in a steady state can give the decision here; but observations of this kind we have virtually none at all.

b. That winter grain after green-mown vetches turns out better than after ripened ones is true; but after the former the field can be cultivated for a longer time than after the latter, whereby a difference in soil capacity is produced which, even at entirely equal richness, must have a great difference in yields as a consequence. This experience can therefore also decide nothing.

c. But even granted that the crops plowed under green return to the field far more than their production cost, and this I shall have to grant if Herr Joubert has successfully carried out his project announced in 1820 of maintaining a part of his field in equal strength without any stable manure, solely through green manurings: even this is still no refutation of my theory, and no confirmation of the opposing theory according to which the plant up to the blossoming not only does not exhaust the soil but actually enriches it. For from this it would only follow: 1) that the green fodder cut in blossom and made into hay loses a part of its nourishing substances in drying, and still more in passing through the animal stomach; and 2) that in the preparation of manure through fermentation, a large part of the fertilizing substances is volatilized and lost, while when the green crops are plowed under, the fermentation takes place in the soil itself and the developed substances are absorbed by the soil.

The latter has been almost proven by Gazzeri, the former has at least been widely conjectured. It would be an inestimable gain for science if the controversy over the effect of green manuring contributed to bringing these points to evidence.

d. Experience teaches that plants enjoying one and the same atmosphere nevertheless have very different growth, depending on whether they find more or less manure and humus in the soil. But if plants live entirely from the atmosphere until the blossoming, how do the defenders of this theory then explain that the growth of plants is constantly promoted by the increase of humus, indeed up to a certain point stands almost entirely in proportion to it? The assumption that the manure acts here as a stimulant on the vital force of the plant, that it can then take up more gases from the atmosphere through the heightening of its vital force — this is at least so artificial that my opponent has the burden of proving it so.

So far the unknown author.

The subject seems to me so important that I cannot refrain from drawing some consequences from the theory of the unknown, from whose agreement or contradiction with known facts the correctness or unsustainability of this theory must most readily appear.

If all the sap from which the seed is formed is already contained in the plant at the time of the completed blossoming, then the nutritive mass that the seed of the ripened plant supplies must be the measure: a) for the exhaustion of the soil that the plant's production causes; b) for the nutritive mass that this plant at the time of blossoming and made into hay contained.

Ad a. All cultivators of the statics, with the exception of Burger, are agreed in taking the measure of nutritive substance contained in the seed as the measure of the exhaustion of the soil. Here there is agreement.

Ad b. If from a field planted with rye, which would yield 2 cartloads of ripe rye, one half is mown in blossom and, made into hay, produces a product of 1,800 pounds of hay, then the ripened half will, besides the straw, bring approximately 600 pounds of grain. With timothy grass, by contrast, the ripened part will bring only about 200 pounds of seed when the green-mown part yields 1,800 pounds. If the seed yield is to be the representative of the nutritive mass contained in the hay, and if we take — since the ratio of nutritiveness between rye and timothy seed is unknown to us — the weight of the seed as the norm, then one cartload of rye hay would have to contain as much nutritive substance as 3 cartloads of timothy hay. This contradicts all experience.

The disproportion becomes even greater when we compare rye hay and clover hay in this way.

In my own opinion, therefore, neither the seed formation nor the formation of the plant body alone is to be credited with the entire exhaustion we observe when the soil has borne a ripened crop. But in what proportion is the total exhaustion divided between the two?

I myself have hitherto assumed that of the entire exhaustion caused by the ripened cereal crops, 1/3 falls upon the formation of the plant body and 2/3 upon the seed formation — and the phenomena I have had occasion to observe can be brought best into agreement by this assumption. But this opinion is always based only on observations subject to error, not on facts.

The statics demands, however, a firmer foundation, and if it is to gain influence on practice and enter into life, the dispute over the effect of green-mown crops on the richness of the soil must be settled, and the differences of opinion on this matter resolved.

This uncertainty is oppressive, indeed intolerable to me, and I do not wish to occupy myself further with the cultivation of the statics if I cannot attain on this point at least a degree of clarity satisfying to myself.

All the more gratifying is it therefore to me, and all the more meritorious does it appear, that you have communicated your view on this to the public. Your view has thereby become the subject of general attention and collective consultation, and I hope that the united efforts of many will soon succeed in bringing light into this intricate matter, especially if several are thereby prompted to question nature through experiments.

This is the fruit of the statics, of which Herr von Wulffen says in his vigorous language: "The occupation with the statics must lead us to one of two results: either we find the truth, or we recognize the error."

I would add: the statics tolerates no errors that are in contradiction with one another, and directs attention from inessential subjects to the point where decision is needed.

Under these circumstances, I look forward to the outcome of the experiments I mentioned in my previous letter with intense expectation. So far, this experiment, continued only 2 years, has not been disturbed by any unfavorable circumstances, and since my crop rotation in the present cycle permits no further continuation, I shall receive the first result this year.

Regarding the weight ratio of the ripened and the grain cut in blossom, these two years have already yielded a result.

In the year 1824, plot No. 2 gave in ripened wheat and straw: 180 pounds. Plot No. 4 gave in wheat hay mown in blossom and standing in shocks until the bringing in of the ripened wheat: 162 pounds. In 1825, plot No. 2 gave in barley sheaves: 108 pounds; in barley hay: 90 1/2 pounds.

Remarkable here is the small difference in weight between the ripened and the green-mown wheat. In 180 pounds of wheat sheaves there are normally 60 pounds of grain and 120 pounds of straw; the green-mown stalks, fully dried, weighed 162 pounds; the straw only 120 pounds — thus the loss in weight of the stalks during ripening is 42 pounds. Where have these 42 pounds gone?

My letter has already reached such a length that I must not go into answering the other points in your substantial letter this time.

But I must yet tell you how much it delighted me that you have finally decided to communicate to the public a part of what you have accomplished for science in a series of essays.

You have had the goodness to communicate to me the draft of several of these essays in manuscript. Among those I did not yet know, the one on the low grain prices interested me the most. Of everything I have read so far on this important subject, this essay is the most thorough.

From what I have had occasion to observe in my neighborhood, your purpose — "the spreading of knowledge of the statics" — will not be missed. Among other things, I hope that thereby the young Pogge of Striesenow will be won for the statics. He is an excellent experimenter who can do much for science if his experiments are directed toward the extension of the statics.

Herr Staudinger has communicated to me some of your remarks and comments on my manuscript "The Isolated State," which were very interesting to me. It would be of great importance to me if you would let me have these in greater scope.

#### J. H. von Thunen to von Voght
*Tellow, December 30, 1829*

Receive first my thanks for the communication of your work: "Flotbeck's High Culture." With sympathy and joy I have learned from it that after so long and laborious exertion you have reached the goal of your striving, that at the evening of your life you can look back with satisfaction on the result of your efforts — which falls to the lot of only a few.

On the other hand, your communication that you will close your practical career with this year has filled me with heartfelt concern for your health and serenity. But of course you had to make a break in your life; you had to cease collecting if what was collected was not to be lost to the world; and so this decision too will bring gain for science, if only your health does not suffer from the changed way of life.

The careful, almost garden-like cultivation of the Flotbeck soil described in your work, we may certainly not imitate here, because the greater product would not cover the increased labor costs. But this is really not at all the point; what advances science is gain, even if for the time being no application at all can be made. Euclid's Elements may have been regarded as idle speculations by some of his contemporaries — and what use has posterity made of them!

I always turn ice-cold in an agricultural conversation when I find that only what directly brings in something is an object of interest.

Even though your cultivation method as a whole can be applicable only in a very limited circle, individual results of your investigations — for example, those on the advantages of finer pulverization of the soil, of shallow covering of the seed, etc. — are of almost universal value for practice. For one can, without changing the form of farming, under fortuitously favorable circumstances — for example, after a rain — achieve the pulverization of the soil through a single rolling and thus at very small cost; one can through a single forward drawing with the harrows prevent the too-deep covering of the seed grains, etc.

This leads me to another interesting question: The higher the richness of the soil, the less carefully the cultivation needs to be, to bring the grain to the maximum yield. One can therefore, if one manures the soil more heavily, save on cultivation costs; one can, with more careful but more expensive tillage, save manure and yet in both cases have equal grain yield. Does one now obtain the maximum grain yield most cheaply through a raising of soil richness or through a more expensive cultivation of the soil? Or is this maximum on one and the same soil a variable quantity depending on cultivation, so that, for example, with ordinary cultivation it cannot be raised above 11 grains through any increase in soil richness, while with more careful tillage it could be raised to 12 grains?

If the latter were not the case, I would have to believe that for you in Flotbeck, where manure is cheap and labor costly, it would be more advantageous to obtain the maximum grain yield through stronger manuring instead of through increased cultivation.

In your latest communication, you have had the goodness to let me see an extract from several letters written by you. These letters seem to me to contain precisely what I should like to know about Flotbeck, and I very much wish that you would bring them to general knowledge through publication. It would be especially welcome to me and, I believe, also to the public, if you would communicate the yield of grain and of the other crops for at least the last 10 years, better still from the beginning of your farming. One would thereby be able to survey the successive rise of Flotbeck's cultivation at a glance.

The ninth letter in particular is of great interest to me. To know what each crop, each operation brings in and costs — this is the highest, but — since truthfulness and accuracy are indispensable conditions — hard to reach goal of all agricultural bookkeeping. But once the goal is reached, all errors in the farming lie clearly before one's eyes; they must disappear and the farming must become consistent.

That grain cultivation in the vicinity of a large city must be connected with loss has long been my opinion from the conclusions in "The Isolated State," and it was an agreeable confirmation of this opinion to learn from your letter how all grain crops in your case belong to the losing crops.

In the vicinity of a large city, grain cultivation is therefore an evil, and one would abandon it entirely if other reasons did not make it necessary. One of these reasons is, as you have already mentioned, that one cannot grow clover without grain. (But can clover not also be sown under green-mown mixed grain?) A second reason may lie in the fact that the nature of the crops requires a change of fruits, and that this change cannot be achieved through nothing but profit-making crops alone. It would accordingly be an interesting investigation, not yet broached, to show and state in numbers how, with ever-further limitation of the loss-making grain crops, one finally arrives at a point where further restriction of grain cultivation no longer increases but diminishes the net return of the entire farming. Once this point is found and the farming regulated accordingly, the grain crop must be credited for its influence on the next crop, or rather on the entire farming, and if this element is included in the bookkeeping, in a perfectly consistent farming no more loss-making crop should be found.

But how far is science still from being able to solve such a problem, while we see that in practice everyone with ease indeed does not solve this knot, but at least cuts through it.

While in regard to grain cultivation there is perfect harmony between your experiences and the result of my investigations, the two form a decided contrast in regard to rapeseed cultivation. In the Isolated State, namely, rapeseed cultivation is banished to a great distance from the city, even behind the circle of grain cultivation; while your experience shows that rapeseed cultivation is very profitable even in the circle of free farming, quite near the city.

Whence arises this contrast? Perhaps because rapeseed is profitable only when it yields the maximum; but for this maximum a high soil richness is required, which can almost only be achieved where manure can be purchased cheaply, that is, near the city. Or have I perhaps — which seems to me probable for several reasons — assumed the exhaustion caused by rapeseed far too high?

Your kind remark that you would, if you only had time, like to translate "The Isolated State" into English and French has delighted me; for even though your time is far too valuable for this ever to be carried out, this contains the proof that you set value on this work, and this recognition from you is at once rewarding and encouraging for me.

Your method in the statics of agriculture has obtained a new interest for me, and I therefore scarcely need to add that I am very ready to communicate to you the desired data on the Tellow operations, if you believe this subject is worth claiming your time.

#### J. H. von Thunen to A. P. Thaer
*Tellow, Autumn 1839*

There is perhaps no more worthy, more beneficial occupation than this: "to hunt down one's own errors." If we have found their source, we are not only freed from these errors but also secured against similar confusions in the future.

We are led to such reflections when we find ourselves in disagreement with significant men, and quite especially I feel myself challenged to this in the face of a Wulffen.

I have therefore in the past summer immersed, indeed almost submerged myself in the study of von Wulffen's manuscript, and have attempted to set forth the reasons for my differing views in the appended remarks. Despite the exchange of ideas that has taken place since that time, in the most essential points between von Wulffen and myself there remains the same difference of opinion. I have therefore once more subjected my expressed views to a serious examination, but have not been fortunate enough to discover the error in them.

The fundamental cause of the deviation of both your and my views from those of Herr von Wulffen is this: that Herr von Wulffen connects two different concepts with the word "richness" (*R*), understanding by this expression at one time a part of the substance, and then secondly the productive capacity of the soil, and now does not acknowledge the decomposition of *R* — in the latter sense — into the two factors *Q* and *M*.

That equal quantities of manure (material, replacement), applied to different soil types, do not yield equal produce until complete consumption, is known to all practical farmers. At the same time, this influence of the soil (the quality) can be determinately ascertained and stated in numbers in farms that are in a steady state.

When we design static tables for different soil types, we must multiply a given manuring, for example of 5 cartloads per Morgen, on sandy soil perhaps by 2, on moderate soil by 2 1/2, on clay soil by about 3, in order to convert the manure into degrees of richness. The multiplier then designates the quality of these soil types.

In my opinion, Herr von Wulffen will not be able to proceed otherwise when designing static tables. Why, then, do we not wish to include in the basic formulas of the statics what is universally recognized, what we ourselves apply in the static calculation, and what is so simple and easily understandable? I do not know.

As a reply to von Wulffen's manuscript, I must pose the counter-question: What deficiency, disadvantage, or error arises from taking the equation *R* = *QM* as the basis in the statics?

Without being clear on this, I would be able to contribute only obstructively, not constructively, to a joint building.

I therefore cannot agree with von Wulffen's formula for the exhaustion, already for the reason that the formula leaves it undetermined whether *R* = *M* or = *QM*. Also, if we set *R* = *QM*, I do not see its necessity.

I am in complete agreement with you that this formula will find no acceptance with the public; but least of all can I approve that from this formula, which itself still requires proof, the quality of the soil — which presents itself so simply from experience — is derived.

Excellent, on the other hand, I find the classification of soil types. Only the disadvantage remains that a field yielding 9 hundredweights of wheat or 10 hundredweights of rye, and which, because 9 hundredweights of wheat have a higher value than 10 hundredweights of rye, is in reality planted with wheat and counted as wheat soil, belongs according to this classification to the rye soil. But this perhaps cannot be avoided.

It is indeed very gratifying for me to see from your communications that your and my views in the statics closely coincide in the main points. Only in one point is there an essential deviation in our views.

In the notes to von Wulffen's letter you say: "*C*, the consumption of richness, is measured by the decrease in richness. From *C* arises the harvest (*E*), but with a greater or lesser loss (*A*): *C* = *E* + *A*; *E* = *C* - *A*." However, I believe that this difference of opinion could soon be reconciled through an oral discussion.

Just as the activity of the soil, measured by the relative decline of successive harvests under equal preparation of the soil, is not dependent on the chemical and physical properties of the soil alone but is essentially conditioned by the cultivation method — is different in three-field farming than in Koppel farming and than in fruit-rotation farming — so too the quality of the soil, in the sense in which I take this word, is not bound solely to the physical properties of the soil but is at the same time dependent on the cultivation method. With this meaning of the word "quality," there may indeed be a loss in the transition from material to richness, but from richness itself there is no further loss, since the entire loss is already expressed by the degree of quality. To be sure, it would be very desirable if we could represent in the general quality and in the general activity the share that soil, cultivation, and crop rotation each individually have in it. But in the first foundation of the statics, in my opinion, this task does not yet belong.

At the assembly at Potsdam you made the highly interesting remark that in fruit-rotation farming a greater product arises from the same material than in farming systems with pure fallow. My investigations had indeed led me on trails pointing to similar views; but so fully developed and definitively stated I have found these views nowhere; they have therefore constantly hovered in my memory and occupied me.

In my economic life, this year marks an epoch, as I have now lived on the estate of Tellow for 30 years, and thus have a 30-year experience before me. In compiling the results that these 30 years yield, I have involuntarily, almost against my will, returned to the static investigations. For what is the statics of agriculture other than the expression of the laws of nature with regard to agriculture over a longer series of years? Through these investigations your above-mentioned remark has been fully confirmed for me; but they have also led me to the view that the quality of the soil is indeed raised on sandy soil by fruit-rotation farming, but not on every soil; that clay soil in Koppel and three-field farming can even attain a higher quality than through fruit-rotation farming.

How fruitful the statics must become for practical agriculture when it has matured to the point where it can determine and state in numbers for all soil classes the influence of the different farming systems on quality!

## Section 6

### Shorter Essays

#### 1. Reflections on the Present Time in Relation to the Low Price of Grain
*(Written in Winter 1826)*

When in the year 1819 the cheapness of all agricultural products began, everyone believed that this was an ordinary, soon-passing fluctuation in the price of things.

But when thereupon one year of disappointed hope followed another, people began to investigate the causes of this change, and, so far as I know, thirteen different causes have been identified; but the all-clarifying passage of time has now revealed the nullity or insignificance of most of these reasons, and after a seven-year experience lies before us, it may be easier to separate the essential from the inessential.

The terrible natural events of the previous winter destroyed the winter crops over a large stretch of the most fertile soil, and after this happened, the wheat price sinks as low as never before.

This apparently incomprehensible coincidence teaches us how deeply the evil is rooted, and with astonishment and alarm we become aware what great and comprehensive causes must underlie it.

From other investigations, which cannot be communicated here, I must now borrow and state in advance the following results that emerge from them.

**a. Law for the Price Determination of Grain.**

In every state isolated by decree or by nature, the price of grain in its principal marketplace must be so high that the farmer for whom the delivery of grain to market is most costly — because he either cultivates the poorest soil or lives farthest from the marketplace — whose production, however, cannot be dispensed with for satisfying demand, receives just enough to cover the production and transport costs of the grain. The price determined in this manner we call the natural price.

**b.** This poorest estate yields, after deduction of the cultivation costs and the interest on the capital invested in buildings and other objects, no surplus at all, or the land rent of this estate equals zero. All better estates — which either have a more fertile soil or lie closer to the marketplace — receive for their grain the same price as the poorest estate, and therefore yield a net surplus from which the land rent arises.

**c.** When several states stand in free commercial intercourse with one another, the richest grain-requiring state — for us, England — determines the grain price of all countries that supply it with grain.

The circle from which this rich state draws its grain requirement we shall, for brevity, call the grain territory of that state.

**d.** When within the grain territory of the rich state other independent states lie, then in these, even when they neither import nor export grain, the grain price (under free trade) is entirely governed by the grain price of the rich state.

Now if the rich state that has hitherto determined the grain price of all other countries closes its ports against the import of foreign grain, then in the other states the grain price sinks to the price that is natural to each state when it stands isolated by itself.

The level of the natural price in the various countries — each considered individually as isolated — depends on the ratio in which the demand for domestic consumption stands to the extent of cultivated acreage and the degree of its fertility.

The production of grain becomes the more costly, the poorer the soil on which it is grown. My calculations yield the following on this subject:

The production costs of one Berlin Scheffel of rye amount, when the field yields per 100 square rods:

- 10 Berlin Scheffel: approximately 21 Schilling Gold
- 9 Scheffel: approximately 23 Schilling
- 8 Scheffel: approximately 26.4 Schilling
- 7 Scheffel: approximately 31.5 Schilling
- 6 Scheffel: approximately 41 Schilling
- 5 Scheffel: approximately 47.2 Schilling

The taxes to the state are not included in these production costs, and if the return on the land is not to sink below zero, the selling price must exceed the production costs sufficiently for the taxes to be covered thereby.

Now if in a country of small extent, which contains several small but no large towns (where, therefore, the distance from the marketplace is nearly equal for all estates), only grain for domestic consumption is to be grown, then, if the soil that yields 10 kernels (10 Berlin Scheffel per 100 square rods) is available in such quantity that demand is already satisfied by its production alone, the natural price of rye per Berlin Scheffel is 21 Schilling Gold (exclusive of the amount of taxes and transportation costs to the marketplace).

But if the production of this soil is not sufficient, and to satisfy demand the soil of 9, 8, and 7 kernels' yield must also be brought under cultivation, then the natural price must rise to 31.5 Schilling, because the soil that yields only 7 kernels then just barely receives compensation for production costs and at a lower price cannot be permanently cultivated.

In all countries that formerly exported grain to England and are now excluded from the London market by the Corn Bill, the grain price must — if no other markets can be found — sink to the natural price of these countries. This natural price is, however, especially for Mecklenburg, where only about a quarter of the entire population lives in towns, extremely low, and on all poor and indeed even on all soil types of middling quality, the cultivation of grain is at this price attended with loss.

This explains why grain prices on the Continent stand so low; but it does not explain how England, through the great decree of power — "I will have no foreign grain" — can free itself from the need for foreign grain.

The magnitude of a country's grain production depends less on the size of the cultivated area than on the richness of the soil in plant nutrition, or on the quantity of humus* contained in the soil.

*[Footnote: By humus, however, von Thunen does not mean all combustible substances that may be found in the soil, such as wood and heather roots, meadow and swamp mold, etc., but he restricts the meaning of the word "humus" to the residues of earlier manure applications and of the turf decomposition of a two- or at most three-year fallow. According to this definition (cf. Isolated State, Part 1, p. 57), all mineral constituents required for the nourishment of our cultivated plants are present in the humus (cf. ibid., p. 75). In the year 1821 we find the same definition in the Mecklenburg Annals, p. 168, in these words of von Thunen's: "The soil contains in 100 square rods 10 cartloads of manure" means: the effect of the humus contained in the soil equals the effect of 10 cartloads of manure.]*

Now it is well known that earlier English agriculture was directed primarily toward cattle fattening, that a large part of the most fertile soil remained as pasture for livestock, and that even of the cultivated acreage one half bore livestock feed such as turnips, clover, and so forth. The farming was therefore evidently an enriching one, that is, a farming system whereby the humus content of the soil increased from year to year.

Agriculture is to be regarded as a machine whereby the humus of the soil is converted into grain, and it is in the power of the farmer to carry out this conversion to a greater or lesser degree, that is, to produce a greater or lesser quantity of grain.

The conversion of a seven-field rotation into a six-field rotation already brings about — not permanently, but for a time — an increase of grain production of 16 percent. Since England, even in the years when it imported grain, is said never to have drawn more than one twenty-seventh of its entire requirement from abroad, it evidently needed only a slight alteration of the previous farming system to produce this one twenty-seventh itself.

Now there are three cases to distinguish:

1) English agriculture is in general, even after this alteration, still an enriching one. In that case, England will be able to dispense with grain imports in the future as well.

2) English agriculture is now on the whole a stationary one, that is, one that maintains itself at the same level of soil richness. If this is the case, then England needs no grain imports for its present population, but given its enormously rapidly growing population, it will again need supplies from abroad for the additional population, because without an increase in soil richness no lasting increase in grain production can take place.

3) The English farmers have, enticed by the high grain prices, converted the previously enriching system into an exhausting one.

This case is the most probable, because through the Corn Bill the correct relationship between the price of grain and that of animal products has been completely distorted. While the import of wheat is only permitted at the price of 80 Shillings per quarter, and thus is practically entirely prohibited, the import of wool, hides, tallow, butter, and cheese has been allowed against payment of moderate, in part very small, duties. If one wished to help English agriculture essentially and permanently — though at the expense of all other branches of industry — one should have prohibited not only the import of grain but also that of all animal products; but it was too plainly obvious that this would immediately bring about the ruin of the factories and manufactories working in wool and leather, whereas with the artificial raising of the grain price the necessary increase in wages entails an equally certain, but less visible and slower, withering of the prosperity of English factories.

In all richer countries with high land rent, the net return from livestock raising is in any case very small. If now in such countries the grain price is further artificially raised (which, partly through the increase in wages and partly through the increase in land rent, makes animal products more expensive), while the price of animal products, if these are exposed to competition with the less cultivated foreign countries, tends to fall rather than rise, then the net return from livestock raising must not merely vanish but be converted into loss.

The Corn Bill has therefore made grain growing more profitable for the English farmer and livestock raising unprofitable. The extension of grain growing and the curtailment of livestock raising is, however, connected with a diminution of soil richness.

Since now in all occupations man is guided by intelligent self-interest and should be so guided, and since furthermore agriculture in England is almost entirely in the hands of farmers (tenants), who have a far lesser interest in the preservation of soil richness than the proprietors, I believe I am not mistaken in assuming that the Corn Bill will convert the formerly enriching system into an exhausting one, or has already done so.

But if this is the case, then England's high grain production is a passing phenomenon; the harvests will, despite all extension of grain growing, diminish from year to year, and England will then later fall into a dependence on foreign countries for its grain supply such as never before. With the diminution of soil richness, the foundation of England's prosperity will be shaken, and the Corn Bill can in this way become the grave of England's preeminence. People will then not comprehend how agriculture, despite all encouragement, could nevertheless have declined.

But just as the accumulation of humus in the soil is not the work of a few years, so too the diminution of it can become visibly apparent only after a series of years.

But now — one may further ask — would grain have maintained the price it had in the period from 1790 to 1820, if the grain trade with England had remained completely free and no Corn Bill at all had existed?

In England the grain prices would then not have risen so high, the English farmers would not have extended grain growing so far, consumption would have remained greater at lower prices, and England would then have continued to need imports.

According to the law stated above, the grain price in London would then have been so high that the most distant estate whose production was still necessary for satisfying demand would just have received payment for the production and transport costs of grain. If, for example, the grain shipped on the Vistula to Danzig had still been required for supplying London, then the price at which the Polish farmer can deliver grain to Danzig, combined with the commercial and transport costs of grain from Danzig to London, would have determined the price of grain on the London market. If, on the other hand, the grain arriving at the port of Konigsberg had still been required for satisfying London's demand, then the price in London would have had to be higher by as much as the freight from Konigsberg to London exceeds that from Danzig to London. In Mecklenburg the grain would then have been worth as much more than in Konigsberg as the difference in freight to London amounts to.

The question, "what level grain prices would have maintained under completely free trade with England," thus leads to the other question, from what distance grain would have had to be brought for supplying London.

This question cannot be decided theoretically, but the experience of the last six years provides data for answering it.

Let us now consider how the destruction of wheat by rust in 1820 had no effect at all on the wheat price, how the crop failure of 1822 was far from able to raise grain prices to the earlier average levels, how the destruction of the harvests of the most fertile stretch of Germany and Holland is accompanied by a fall in grain prices, how finally all the waste of grain caused by the low prices is unable to diminish the stocks: we must then arrive at the conviction that an excess of production over demand prevails.

This excess of production can have a twofold cause: it can namely have been brought about 1) by a series of very fruitful years, 2) by the increased cultivation of the soil.

The years 1821, 1823, and 1824 have been of such rare fruitfulness as former times scarcely knew them singly, much less in succession. Incontestably great is the share that these fruitful years have in the present grain surplus, and for a long time I believed that they were the main cause of the cheapness of grain; but the phenomena of recent years have unfortunately given me the conviction that all other causes, including the fruitful years, have been merely contributing causes, that the main reason for the surplus lies in the too rapidly increased cultivation of the soil. I say "unfortunately," for among all the causes adduced to explain the cheapness, there is none that is so difficult to remove, that promises so long a duration of the present state, as the one stated.

Let us now assume that with this advanced production the grain trade with England had remained free and that England's grain requirement had been the same as in the period from 1790 to 1820; then grain would not now, as formerly, need to be drawn from the interior of Russia for supplying London. Already the ports of the Baltic Sea would have been able to supply the greater part of this requirement, and Danzig would perhaps have been the most distant port from which grain was still shipped to England. The London market would then no longer have had any effect on the countries lying beyond this circle, and grain prices in Mecklenburg would indeed have been higher than now, but still very far below the earlier average prices.

But the simultaneous rise in soil cultivation in almost all countries of Europe cannot stem from a coincidence; rather, a universally operating cause must underlie it.

Whoever understands the inner nature of agriculture, whoever has grasped how the level of the grain price conditions the appropriateness of a higher or lower farming system and the greater or lesser grain production, finds a wholly sufficient explanation of this phenomenon in the high grain prices that prevailed from 1790 to 1819 in all countries of Europe.

Harder to understand is why this inevitable consequence of the high grain prices remained absent so long, showed itself only so late, and this can only be explained to some extent by the insecurity of property during the war, by the uncertainty whether what the farmer devoted to the improvement of his soil would also benefit him.

Thus in the unnaturally high prices of the past period lay the germ of the present unnaturally low prices.

The grain that finds no consumer has no value; therefore no more must be produced than is needed. But how is this quantity to be measured, to be determined in advance?

Even the official standing at the head of a state, whose vantage point already affords a wide overview and to whom every inquiry is available, would seek in vain to determine how large the grain sowing must be in order to satisfy next year's demand. Even the great merchant, who maintains connections in all parts of the world, deceives himself only too often — even after the completed harvest — about the ratio of the harvest and the stock to demand. How then shall the individual farmer, whose view is confined to a small area of the earth, how shall he be able to gauge demand correctly and adjust his production accordingly?

Badly, very badly would it stand with the provisioning of nations with foodstuffs, if this were to be left to the foresight of governments or of farmers.

But fortunately the price of a commodity — and this may be one of the most splendid effects of money — brings to general knowledge what otherwise no one could fathom. If namely the price of a commodity or product rises above the production price, this is a sign that demand is not being satisfied by production, and the producer's own interest then stimulates him to multiply his commodity. If on the other hand the market price falls below the production price, this indicates that more is being produced than is wanted and needed, and the producer must then — if he does not wish to be ruined by his own efforts — curtail his production.

In this situation we Mecklenburgers now find ourselves, and with us almost all farmers of northern Germany.

For six years the extremely low price of grain has indicated to us that an excess of production prevails, and yet we continue — with few exceptions — to farm in the accustomed manner. It has come to the point that a rich harvest — formerly regarded as a blessing of heaven — now fills the farmer with cares and anxiety. We squander our capital, we waste the sweat of the laborer, to bring forth a surplus of grain that cannot be consumed by human beings but serves as nourishment for rats, mice, and grain weevils.

Shall this condition continue still longer, shall the exhausting struggle among the producers be carried on until one half of them succumbs and, from lack of means, lets the land lie waste?

From the governments no relief is to be expected, for every artificial raising of the grain price — for example, through the establishment of magazines — would only prolong the evil; for it is precisely through the low prices that the farmer must be compelled to desist from the foolish undertaking of producing grain that no one wants, no one can use.

Only in the hands of the farmers themselves lies their salvation. If they curtailed production to the extent that it came into equilibrium with demand, then in a single year the cheap prices could be converted into appropriate ones.

But how is an agreement among all farmers to be brought about?

The low price of grain is the bond that encompasses all, that speaks to each in a language intelligible to him.

If only every farmer would give up the cultivation of soil that does not pay the cultivation costs, if only everyone would cease to work where he not only receives no wages for his labor but must even pay for the privilege, then the surplus would vanish, the low prices would be converted into appropriate ones, and the pressure that now weighs on all farmers would come to an end.

But unfortunately the majority of farmers are in complete ignorance about the amount of the production costs of grain.

Some even have the ruinous principle of wanting to make up for the shortfall in income caused by low grain prices through increased grain cultivation and enlarged grain sales. Inasmuch as the farmers who follow this unfortunate principle prolong and increase the misfortune that rests on the entire class of farmers, they diminish — through the production of grain that does not pay the costs — their income instead of increasing it; inasmuch as they exhaust their soil through the extended grain growing, thus diminishing the value of their property, they destroy at the same time a part of the national capital.

This cannot be a matter of indifference to the state, and if the governments could succeed in enlightening the farmers about their true interest, they would thereby become the benefactors of the people, instead of, through tariffs on the import of foreign grain and through other artificial measures, merely increasing the delusion of the farmers and prolonging their misfortune.

In Holland and Prussia, the duties on imported grain have not raised the grain price. This becomes comprehensible when one considers that through this governmental measure the hope of a rise in prices has been awakened among the domestic farmers, and these have thereby been induced to extend grain cultivation.

The domestic production, increased beyond the level of demand, causes prices not to rise even after the cessation of foreign supply. Inevitably, however, the grain price in the grain-exporting countries sinks even lower than before. This again excites concern in the restricting state; to keep foreigners out of the domestic market, the duties must be raised once more — and so the evil escalates of itself, until finally in the restricting state the soil, as a consequence of the grain-growing encouraged by legislation, is exhausted, and now the state needs a permanent and, compared with former times, significantly increased grain supply from abroad in order to feed its inhabitants.

Whence may the hostility come that the governments now almost universally display against the import of foreign grain?

According to Adam Smith, the labor devoted to agriculture pays more than in factories, because nature cooperates in it, and for this reason agriculture yields, besides the restitution of wages and the profit on capital, also a land rent.

It seems to me that the present statesmen of Europe — whether they are conscious of it or not — pay homage to this view of Adam Smith and act accordingly.

If it were well founded that the labor devoted to agriculture had a greater success than that directed to factories because nature cooperates, or if, as Say thinks, the mere distribution of land among proprietors were already sufficient to produce a land rent, then governments could indeed do nothing more salutary and useful for their states than to prohibit the import of foreign grain and thereby compel their subjects to divert capital and labor from other occupations, which yield only wages and interest, to agriculture, which besides wages and interest yields a further surplus.

Why must what is so clear in the individual case, that everyone grasps it correctly without mental effort and acts accordingly, be so unclear in the theory of political economy and thereby lead to such ruinous measures?

Almost nowhere except in the vicinity of large cities, where manure is cheap to buy, does the raw soil devoid of all humus have any value. The value of the soil arises in the great majority of cases from its humus content and falls or rises with the lesser or greater content of humus. Now no grain can be grown without a part of the humus contained in the soil being consumed thereby. Every state that exports grain exports at the same time a part of its humus capital and delivers it to the state that receives the grain. What the importing state pays for the grain above the production costs is the compensation for the humus consumed in its production.

On rich soil, under ordinary conditions, grain growing yields a far higher land rent than livestock raising (through England's Corn Bill an unnatural alteration of this relationship has at this moment occurred, which, however, precisely because it is unnatural, cannot be lasting and cannot be taken as the norm).

My calculations, which are based on the high grain prices from 1790 to 1820, show that with a yield of 10 kernels, 1,000 square rods planted with grain give a land rent of 22.31 Thaler Gold, while 1,000 square rods of equal richness used as pasture yield only 5.07 Thaler land rent.

The conversion of grain land into pasture land is very easy and lies within the farmer's power. What could then, in that period when grain commanded so high a price, move the farmer to draw the high return from only a part of his field, while being content with a small return from the other part?

Without doubt only the consideration that the extension of grain growing over the entire field would result in an exhaustion of the soil and a depreciation of his estate's value.

The land used for pasture yields, besides its small monetary return, a production of manure; the land used for grain growing yields a high monetary return, but at the expense of the humus capital contained in the soil.

To find the total return of the pasture, the value of the manure produced would have to be added to its monetary return; to find the truly net return of the grain land, the value of the consumed manure would have to be deducted from the monetary return. Only after this is done does it become apparent which use of the soil has been the truly most advantageous.

If now the return of the grain land and the pasture land, calculated in this way, is in equilibrium, then the sensible farmer refrains, despite the enticing monetary gain, from converting the pasture land into grain land.

In this practice there already lies the acknowledgment that grain is not purchased merely through the expenditure of labor and capital, but also requires the sacrifice of a part of the humus capital contained in the soil; that therefore through the price of grain not only the expenditure of labor and capital but also the value of the humus consumed by the grain must be compensated.

While this is so clearly evident in practice that the simplest farmer acts accordingly, it appears to be entirely unknown to the science of political economy and to the governments. At least no trace of this knowledge appears in the conduct of governments.

The value of humus depends on the level of the grain price and is considerably higher in countries where the grain price is high than in countries where it is low.

For this reason humus has a far greater value in England than in Mecklenburg and Poland.

The transfer of humus from the poor country to the rich one would therefore be attended with great profit. But humus cannot be extracted from the soil and also cannot be physically transported from one country to another, because the transport is attended with too great costs.

But if one converts the humus through vegetation into grain, which through consumption is again converted into humus, then this transfer is possible.

The money that a nation spends on foreign grain is not lost after the grain is consumed; the material for new production has remained. The soil has become richer, and the national wealth has received an increase. The money spent on foreign manufactured goods, by contrast, leaves behind after the consumption of the goods nothing that serves to promote national wealth.

In every country the greatest part of the national wealth consists of landed property. The value of land depends, however, not on the size of the area but on the content of the soil, that is, on the humus content.

The farmer who has drawn a surplus of 1,000 Thaler from his estate but has diminished the value of the humus capital contained in the soil by 2,000 Thaler has not become richer but poorer.

What holds for the individual estate must also hold for the entire state, for the land of the state consists of the sum of the individual estates.

If England prohibits the import of foreign grain and induces its farmers through artificially raised grain prices to extend grain growing, it increases its monetary wealth (by saving the money for foreign grain), but its humus capital is diminished to a greater degree, and just as with the individual farmer the exhaustion of his estate, despite the initially much increased monetary income, leads to his ruin, so too with an entire nation the extended grain growing at the expense of humus capital must lead to the impoverishment of the people.

The first effect of a general diminution of humus capital in an entire nation has the appearance of a brilliant flourishing: money, manufactured goods, and agricultural products are in abundance; but after some years, when the exhaustion of the soil becomes visible, the abundance vanishes and the delusion ceases. One has converted a part of the humus capital into money, grain, and goods, and while one has clearly before one's eyes the increase of these, one does not perceive the decrease of the humus capital and rejoices over the increase of national wealth while impoverishment already follows hard on its heels.

Does not England from the year 1819 to the present offer a speaking example of this picture?

If the ministers could include the humus capital in the reckoning of national wealth, if they could perceive its decrease and increase just as the individual farmer becomes aware of it through the change in the yield of his soil, they would immediately abandon the system of exclusion against foreign grain; but since they regard the value of the soil as a constant quantity and judge it only by the size of the cultivated area, they must necessarily be led to false measures.

Not in the extension of the cultivated area, only in the accumulation of humus capital lies the means by which England, in the event of a Continental blockade, can produce its grain requirement for several years on its own. The cheaper, however, the foreign grain is to be obtained, the lesser the costs at which the enlargement of the humus capital is achieved, and the more advantageous the grain import is for the state.

#### 2. On the Improvement of Communication or Side Roads
*(Written Spring 1837)*

The construction of chaussees has considerably increased the need for better communication roads. On the country roads the usual load of a four-horse wagon was formerly 6 sacks of wheat, but now, when one regularly loads 12 sacks on the chaussee, one demands of the horses a greater effort on the stretch to the chaussee and commonly loads the wagon with 8 sacks. Smaller estates that drive to town with 2 wagons but keep only 3 teams of horses are particularly motivated to do so.

But in order to save the time that reloading the grain causes, some estates that do not lie far from the chaussee drive with a full team and the full load of 12 sacks on the side roads to the chaussee.

By law, the side roads cannot be held to such high standards as the highways leading from town to town, which are subject to the control of the road inspection commission. These highways, however, in wet winters frequently fell into a condition where the horses could no longer pull 6 sacks, and one had to content oneself with a load of 5 sacks. On the highways, however, on the one hand the swampy or clayey places have for the most part already been filled in with stones, and on the other hand the clayey constituents of the soil have been more or less washed out and swept away by rain, while the coarser sand particles remain behind and cover the surface. Neither of these is the case on the communication roads, and with equally heavy loading of wagons and equal frequency of traffic, these therefore become impassable much sooner than the highways.

If now, however, the communication roads are, as is now happening, driven with heavier loads than were formerly carried on the highways, then such a road is made impassable in incredibly short time during wet weather. One then chooses a second road lying further back to reach the chaussee, and when this is made impassable, a third, fourth, and so forth. This now has the disadvantage that some estates, even when they lie only one mile from the chaussee, must make a detour of half to one mile to bring their grain to the trading town, that the chaussee at times becomes almost unreachable, and that upon the estates through which the communication roads pass a burden is imposed in the repair and maintenance of these roads that far exceeds the upkeep of an old highway.

The worst part, however, is that the interest of those who have to maintain such a communication road on their estates stands in direct contradiction with the interest of the community. For the worse the individual keeps his roads, the more he keeps the wagon traffic away from himself, the more he shifts the burden onto others — and since these communication roads are not subject to the control of the road inspection commission, and therefore no external compulsion to repair them exists, it is foreseeable that they will deteriorate from year to year.

If now the benefit that the chaussees can incontestably afford the country is not to be largely lost again, then either a legal regulation must be introduced that no one may drive on the side roads with a heavier load than 6 sacks of wheat during wet weather, or cheaper means than have hitherto been applied must be found for the improvement of the side roads.

To be sure, it would be desirable if all side roads could be converted into stone roadways or even merely gravel chaussees; but a closer examination shows that this, even if the costs could be raised, would be a waste of national capital.

If one estimates the construction costs of an 18-foot-wide stone roadway at 30,000 Thaler, the annual maintenance costs at 800 Thaler, and the collection costs of the road toll at 100 Thaler per mile, then at an interest rate of 4 percent, 2,100 Thaler must be raised through the road toll if the construction capital is to earn interest.

At our present chaussee tariff of 4 Schilling for a four-horse loaded wagon and 1 Schilling for the empty wagon, the chaussee must therefore, to raise that sum, be traversed annually by 2,100 times 48/5 = 20,160, or daily 55 wagons in both directions, if the interest on the construction capital, the maintenance costs, and the collection costs are to be covered.

But if one foregoes interest on the construction capital — as is necessary if chaussees are to be built in Mecklenburg at all — then the maintenance and collection costs can be covered if the road is traversed annually by 900 times 48/5 = 8,640, or daily by 24 wagons in both directions.

Since in Mecklenburg, apart from the already paved roads, few roads will be found on which such a frequency of traffic occurs, the further construction of such costly improved roads must very soon reach its limits.

But if one makes the stone roadway only so wide that every point of it can be driven upon, about 10 to 11 feet, and combines with it a summer road, if one allows a gradient of one-fifteenth, and if one, sacrificing the long straight lines — but with the least possible deviation from the main line of direction — avoids mountains and swamps, then such an improved road may be built for 15,000 Thaler per mile and, with less traffic, maintained for 400 Thaler annually.

Here the maintenance and collection costs together amount to 400 + 100 = 500 Thaler, and these can be raised if the chaussee is traversed by 500 times 48/5 = 4,800 wagons annually in both directions.

For inexpensive chaussees of this kind a wider circle of application then opens up, and in particular such an improved road from Tessin to Rostock, extended backward perhaps to Gnoien, might be suited to the circumstances of this region.

The costs of constructing a gravel chaussee are very much dependent on the distance from which the material must be brought. If one reckons the construction costs on average per mile at 7,500, the annual maintenance costs at 200 Thaler, then on such an improved road 300 times 48/5 = 2,880 wagons will have to travel annually if the maintenance and collection costs are to be covered.

Since the collection costs of the road toll always remain equally large, whether the road is heavily or lightly traveled, in these costs themselves lies the impediment to the improvement of less traveled roads. On a road that is traversed by only 960 wagons annually, the collection costs already consume the entire income from road tolls, and for the maintenance of the road nothing remains.

From joint-stock companies, therefore, even with significant support from the state, nothing at all is to be expected for the improvement of lightly traveled roads.

This subject can, however, be considered from yet another and higher viewpoint, namely with regard to the benefit that the chaussees, compared with the costs they cause, afford the state as a whole.

If one imagines that an entire region up to the trading town belongs to a single owner, then this owner will everywhere where the benefit outweighs the costs build chaussees and improved roads and thereby increase his income.

In reality, however, the interests of individuals, split and frequently opposed by the division of property, hinder the execution of such useful operations. In the state, considered as a whole, and in its sovereign, the separated and opposed interests are reunited into one great and beautiful whole; what promotes the welfare of the whole is its purpose.

In the present case we can therefore regard the great estate owner as the representative of the state's purpose and pose the question: when and where will he build chaussees and improved roads?

According to a calculation communicated in the Mecklenburg Annals (Vol. 16, p. 670), derived from experience, the transport of one Last of wheat on ordinary highways costs 1 Thaler 42 Schilling per mile. On the chaussee the load is doubled — 12 sacks instead of 6 sacks — and if we leave aside the other circumstances that come into consideration, the transport costs sink from 1 Thaler 42 Schilling to 45 Schilling per Last and per mile. For a wagon onto which 4/7 of a Last is loaded, the saving in transport costs therefore amounts to 45 times 4/7 = 33 and 3/7 Schilling, or approximately 7/10 Thaler.

The chaussee affords the further advantage that one can cover the same distance with the doubled load in less time, that one can transport the grain at any convenient time, and so forth. On the other hand, however, when one seeks to reach the chaussee by the shortest possible route on the side roads, the way to the trading town is lengthened. But this is not the place to enter into a detailed calculation on these points.

Retaining the above-stated rates for construction costs and maintenance costs of the different types of improved roads, the following calculation results.

**a. Chaussee with 18-foot-wide stone roadway:**
Construction costs per mile 30,000 Thaler at 4 percent interest: 1,200 Thaler. Maintenance costs: 800 Thaler. Total costs: 2,000 Thaler. For each loaded wagon traveling on the chaussee, 7/10 Thaler in transport costs is saved per mile. To cover the expenditure of 2,000 Thaler, therefore 2,000 divided by 7/10 = 2,857 wagons are required. That is, benefit and costs of the chaussee balance when it is traversed by 2,857 wagons in both directions.

**b. Chaussee with single stone roadway of 10 to 11 feet width:**
Interest on construction capital 15,000 Thaler at 4 percent: 600 Thaler. Maintenance costs: 400 Thaler. Sum: 1,000 Thaler. These are covered when the chaussee is traversed by 1,000 divided by 7/10 = 1,428 wagons.

**c. Gravel chaussee:**
Interest on construction capital 7,500 Thaler at 4 percent: 300 Thaler. Maintenance costs: 200 Thaler. Sum: 500 Thaler. Assuming that on the gravel chaussee 10 sacks = 5/7 Last of wheat can be loaded per team, benefit and costs of the installation balance when the road is traversed by 500 divided by 7/100 = 1,064 wagons.

The collection costs of the road toll naturally fall away entirely here: for it would be absurd if the great estate owner wished to collect a road toll that he himself paid and then took back in, and thereby imposed on himself a permanent expenditure through the collection costs.

But equally purposeless and fruitless is the collection of the road toll for the state, as the embodiment of the whole: for if one abstracts from the small contribution that foreign travelers make to it, the state, just like the great estate owner, obtains through the road toll no real income but rather imposes on itself a tax through the collection costs.

**Comparison and summary.** If the maintenance costs of an improved road and the salary of the toll collector are to be covered by the road toll, then the construction is limited:

1) of a chaussee with 18-foot-wide stone roadway to areas in which the chaussee is traversed annually by 20,160 or more wagons;
2) of a chaussee with single stone roadway of 10 to 11 feet width: 4,800 wagons;
3) of a gravel chaussee: 2,880 wagons.

The benefit of a chaussee compensates the interest on the construction capital and the maintenance costs when the chaussee is traversed:

1) with an 18-foot-wide stone roadway: by 2,857 wagons;
2) with a 10 to 11 foot wide stone roadway: by 1,428 wagons;
3) with a gravel chaussee: by 1,064 wagons.

Even if the assumed rates for construction costs of the different kinds of improved roads, for their maintenance costs, and so forth are by no means to be regarded as fixed, the result remains, even when one varies the rates by 50 percent in either direction:

that if the principle is followed that the road toll must cover the maintenance costs and so on, a large part of the country in which the benefit that the improved roads afford exceeds the interest on the construction capital and the maintenance costs must forego the benefit of stone chaussees, and that on less traveled roads even the less costly construction methods cannot be applied under this condition.

If the ingenious idea of Herr J. Pogge — communicated earlier in the Mecklenburg Annals — could be realized, according to which not only the construction capital but also the annual maintenance costs would be covered by a voluntary contribution, proportionate to the extent of use, from everyone who uses the chaussee, then the country would be spared the burden of the collection costs of the road toll, and at the same time the possibility would be opened of constructing improved roads everywhere where the benefit outweighs the costs.

But the principle prevailing in business life of exploiting circumstances to one's advantage as far as the laws permit, and of seeking, beyond the benefit in which all share, also a personal advantage, does not allow such a voluntary agreement to come about.

Only through the mediation of the state and its sovereign, in whom the interests separated in private life — the personal and that for the common good — again become one, could such a measure be carried into execution.

This could happen, for example, if the maintenance costs of the improved roads were declared a state burden and the amount thereof were raised through an equally distributed levy. Justice requires, however, that if all are to bear an equal burden, all should also receive as nearly equal an advantage as possible in return. This would, however, only be the case if improved roads suited to local conditions were spread over all regions of the country.*

*[Footnote: When in a great monarchy provinces of very unequal population and unequal wealth are united, and the chaussees are built and maintained at the expense of the state, it is entirely consistent to construct the improved roads only in the most populated regions on the most frequented roads, because only here does the benefit outweigh the costs. But since the costs are borne jointly by all citizens of the state, the richer provinces are given to and the poorer ones taken from. Perhaps this is a contributing cause of the impoverishment of East Prussia.]*

The condition that the road toll must cover the maintenance costs, however, impedes precisely the spread of improved roads in regions with less traveled roads — and so here too obstacles stand in the way of executing that proposal.

But even if these obstacles were somehow overcome and it were brought about that everywhere where the benefit exceeds the costs appropriate improved roads could be built, still, according to the foregoing calculation, the construction of the least costly improved road, the gravel chaussee, is only expedient where the road is traversed annually by 1,064 wagons.

Now there are, however, very many side roads that are traversed by only 200, 100, or even, at the boundary of the chaussee territory (that is, of that area of land from which grain is transported on the chaussee), only by the wagons of a single estate.

If one wished to convert even such roads into improved roads, the construction capital would earn only 3, 2, or 1 percent interest, and since the capital would have to be withdrawn from other occupations in which it earns 4 percent, such an operation would lead not to the enrichment but to the impoverishment of the country.

We thus arrive through our investigation at the unpleasing result that the ordinary side roads, even with the application of the least costly construction method, cannot and should not be converted into improved roads.

Although the number of wagons with which each individual side road is traveled appears insignificant, yet since so many side roads lead to the chaussee, the distance that all the wagons from the chaussee territory cover on the side roads when transporting grain to the trading town is at least as great as the distance that these wagons travel on the chaussee itself — assuming that the chaussee has no branch lines.

The benefit that the entire chaussee territory derives from the improved road therefore reaches, in respect of the saving in transport costs, hardly half the benefit that it would afford if the chaussee touched every estate. The other advantage, however, that the chaussee affords — that one can transport grain on it at any convenient time — is largely lost for the majority of estates through the poor side roads.

If on the one hand the chaussee with poor side roads cannot attain its full usefulness, but on the other hand the construction methods hitherto applied for road improvement cannot, on account of their costliness, be carried out on side roads, then the purpose can be achieved only through the discovery and application of improvement methods that cost so little in their execution that even the lightly traveled side roads repay the costs through the benefit — with the cooperation of legislation.

#### 3. Value Calculation of a Muck Pond

In 24 years approximately 1 foot of muck is produced in the firm dry state. This yields on 100 square rods of 256 square feet: 256 cubic feet, or 16 two-horse carts; on 1,000 square rods therefore 1,600 carts. Five carts are equal in mass to 1 cartload of manure; in value I reckon 10 carts = 1 cartload of manure.

1,600 carts are therefore equal to 160 cartloads of manure. 160 cartloads of manure produced in 24 years gives an annual manure production of 6 and 2/3 cartloads per 100 square rods.

If one reckons 1 foot of muck in 30 years and 100 carts of muck in value equal to 8 cartloads of manure, then 1,600 carts of muck equal 128 cartloads of manure in 30 years; this gives for 1 year an annual manure production of 4 and 8/30 cartloads.

A field of 10,000 square rods requires every 6 years a manuring of 500, thus annually 83 and 1/3 cartloads. 100 square rods of water supply annually 4 and 8/30 cartloads; for 83 and 1/3 cartloads approximately 2,000 square rods are therefore required.

**Present value of such a muck pond, if it has just been dredged:**

If one reckons the value of the muck after deduction of dredging costs at 1 Schilling per cart, then 100 square rods yield after 30 years a return of 1,600 Schilling = 33 and 1/3 Thaler. The present value of this is approximately one-quarter, or 400 Schilling; this makes per square rod 4 Schilling.

But if one has 30 ponds, each of 100 square rods, with 1-year, 2-year, 3-year up to 30-year muck, then 1 pond can be dredged annually and 30 times 100 = 3,000 square rods of water supply a regular annual net return of 1,600 Schilling. The capital value of all 30 ponds together is then, at an interest rate of 4 percent, 25 times 1,600 = 40,000 Schilling. This makes per square rod 13 and 1/3 Schilling.

## Section 7

### Foundations for the Investigations in "The Isolated State"
*Tellow 1810-1815 and subsequent years*

This is the longest section, comprising detailed agricultural data and calculations from the Tellow estate. It is organized into four main parts:

#### A. Costs of Various Agricultural Operations

Detailed cost calculations for all agricultural operations performed at Tellow during 1810-1815, based on precise labor records and cost accounting:

- **Plowing (Haken):** Costs per 1,000 square rods for different types of plowing — fallow, stubble, and deep plowing. Average costs calculated from five years of records.
- **Harrowing (Eggen):** Costs per 1,000 square rods under various conditions.
- **Sowing:** Labor costs for broadcasting seed, calculated per 1,000 square rods for different crops.
- **Manure Hauling:** Costs of producing, loading, transporting, and spreading manure, broken down by distance from the farm buildings. The calculation includes the costs of feeding the horses used for transport.
- **Harvest Costs:** Detailed costs of cutting, binding, shocking, carting, threshing, and winnowing grain. Separate calculations for rye, wheat, barley, and oats.
- **Grain Transport to Market:** Costs per Scheffel per mile for transporting grain to the market town, including the time of man and horse.
- **Grain Prices:** The 14-year average prices used in all calculations: rye at 1.29 Thaler Gold per Berlin Scheffel, wheat, barley, oats, and rapeseed at their respective averages.

#### B. Yield Calculations for Seven-Field Rotation

Detailed calculations of the yields from a seven-field rotation system (the Koppelwirtschaft) on both rye-quality and wheat-quality soils at Tellow. For each field in the rotation (fallow, first grain, second grain, third grain, pasture years), the expected yield is calculated based on the soil's richness, the cultivation costs, and the manure applied.

The calculations show the raw yield, deduct seed, straw for livestock feeding, and all costs, to arrive at the net yield per 1,000 square rods. The results are compared with actual yields from the Tellow records.

#### C. Costs and Yields of Various Crops

Detailed cost and yield calculations for each crop grown at Tellow:

- **Oats:** Cultivation costs, expected yields on different soil qualities, net return.
- **Peas:** Similar calculations, noting the beneficial effect on subsequent crops.
- **Tobacco:** High labor costs but high value per unit area.
- **Rapeseed:** Cultivation costs and returns, noting the heavy manure requirement.
- **Potatoes:** Extensive calculations including planting, cultivation, harvest, and the value of potatoes both as food and as livestock feed. The analysis includes the costs of the "Hollanderei" (large-scale dairy operation with 60 cows), showing how potato cultivation integrates with dairy farming.
- **Flax:** Cultivation costs and the value of both fiber and seed.
- **Clover Seed:** The costs of establishing and harvesting clover for seed, and the residual benefit to subsequent crops.
- **White Clover, Buckwheat, Mixed Grain:** Brief calculations for minor crops.

**Feed Utilization:** Detailed tables showing the feed value of different crops and crop residues, and how they are allocated among horses, cattle, sheep, and pigs.

**Dairy Calculations:** Comprehensive accounts of a 60-cow dairy operation (Hollanderei), including:
- Milk production per cow per year
- Butter yield from both sweet and sour cream
- Value of skim milk for calf and pig fattening
- Total revenue and costs of the dairy operation
- Net return per cow

**Sheep Farming:** Costs and returns of keeping sheep, including wool production, lamb sales, and the value of sheep manure.

**Hay and Straw Value:** Calculations determining the value of hay and straw based on their contribution to livestock feeding and manure production.

#### D. Summary of All Yields from Tellow 1810-1815

A comprehensive summary table bringing together all the individual crop and livestock calculations:

- Total raw income from all sources
- General farm expenses (administration, buildings maintenance, insurance, road repair, etc.)
- Total net income
- Breakdown by source: grain sales, livestock sales, dairy products, wool, etc.

**Manure Balance:** Calculations showing the extraction of soil nutrients by each crop versus the replacement through manure, allowing an assessment of whether the farm is maintaining, depleting, or enriching its soil capital.

**Labor Compilation (1810-1820):** Detailed records of all labor performed at Tellow over the decade, organized by type of operation:
- Total man-days and woman-days for each agricultural operation
- Seasonal distribution of labor
- Horse-days for each operation

**Editor's Notes on 1840-1860:** H. Schumacher provides supplementary notes on changes in farming practices at Tellow between 1840 and 1860, including the introduction of purchased feed and fertilizer (guano, bone meal), and changes in crop rotation.

**Grain Yield Tables 1800-1860:** Tabular data showing the annual yields of wheat, rye, barley, and oats from 1800 to 1860, providing a 60-year record of agricultural productivity on the "classic soil of Tellow."

**Detailed Dairy Economics:** Additional calculations on milk production, butter yield, the economics of sweet versus sour milk processing, calf fattening, and pig fattening from skimmed milk.

## Mathematical Appendix

### The Discovery of the Formula for the Mean Distance

Von Thunen presents the mathematical derivation of the formula for the mean distance from all points within a given area to a central point, using integral calculus.

The derivation proceeds through several cases:

1. **Right Triangle:** Finding the mean distance from all points within a right triangle to one vertex, using double integration.

2. **Acute and Obtuse Triangles:** Extending the method to general triangles by decomposing them into right triangles.

3. **General Polygon:** Any polygon can be decomposed into triangles, so the formula extends to arbitrary shapes.

4. **Circle:** The mean distance from all points within a circle to its center, yielding the result that the mean distance = 2/3 of the radius.

5. **Pyramid Volume Method:** An elegant alternative derivation using the fact that the volume of a pyramid equals 1/3 times the base area times the height, which provides a geometric interpretation of the mean distance formula.

This formula is used in Part 1 of The Isolated State to calculate transport costs from an estate to the city, which is fundamental to the entire theory of land rent.
