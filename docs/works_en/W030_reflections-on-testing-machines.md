XX.

Report on the sheep show held on June 6, 1838, in Güstrow.

(By Mr. Dr. von Thünen of Tellow.)

Regarding this year's sheep show, the following announcement had been issued in the public newspapers.

Sheep Show at Güstrow on June 6, 1838.

With reference to the announcement issued by Count von der Osten-Sacken (printed in the second issue of the Schwerin Advertiser) concerning the animal show in general, the following resolutions are hereby brought to the public's attention, which were adopted at the assembly of sheep breeders held on April 25 under the direction of Count von der Osten-Sacken, and which shall serve as the standard for this year's sheep show.

1. The body weight of a buck to be used for calculation shall be determined in the following manner:

The standard weight of a buck after shearing is set at 100 pounds. If the buck weighs more than 100 pounds, then for every 2 pounds of excess weight, 1 pound is credited, so that a buck weighing 120 pounds in the shorn condition is calculated at 110 pounds. Conversely, if the buck weighs less than 100 pounds, then for every 2 pounds of deficit weight, 1 pound is added, so that a buck of 70 pounds is reckoned at 85 pounds.

Under these provisions, the following wager was proposed and accepted.

A wager of 1 Louis d'or per entry on the Merino buck whose one-year fleece per 100 pounds of body weight has the highest monetary value.

At the same time, it was resolved that the winner of this wager shall receive a premium of 150 Reichsthalers in gold, in addition to the entry fees insofar as these are not absorbed by expenses, and that a second premium of 100 Reichsthalers in gold shall be awarded to the buck that comes closest to the winner.

3. Among the bucks competing in this wager, the one that is recognized by a majority vote of the competitors as the most excellent breeding ram in all qualities shall receive a premium of 50 Reichsthalers in gold.

No one may vote for his own buck.

4. Yearling bucks are excluded from this wager.

5. The competitors in this year's wager shall declare on their honor and conscience the previous year's shearing date of the bucks they have entered.

6. The bucks that competed in last year's wagers shall be brought to this year's animal show, and those competing this year shall be brought to next year's animal show and put on display. With regard to next year's sheep show in 1839, it was resolved:

a) that only such bucks may compete for premiums as were shorn at this year's sheep show and stamped with the addition of the year;

b) of the sorted and appraised fleeces, each competitor in the wagers shall henceforth deliver a sufficiently large sample of each grade to the directorate.

Registrations for this year's wager are to be made by May 31, inclusive, to Mr. Court Secretary von Dadelsen in Güstrow.

Mr. Domain Councillor Denker of Mirendorf has had the kindness to undertake the arrangement and management of the business relating to the erection of the sheep shed and the placement of the sheep, and the competing gentlemen are to address themselves to him in this matter.

Since only a single day is allocated this year for the sheep show, the shearing, and the buck auction, the bucks competing in the wagers must be placed on the rampart at Güstrow by June 6 at 7 o'clock in the morning at the latest.

Tellow, April 27, 1838.

J. H. von Thünen,
as Second Chief Director of the
Mecklenburg Patriotic Association.

In previous years, the sheep brought to the show had to camp under tents, which provided insufficient protection against the weather, did not allow adequate separation of the sheep, and offered no arrangement for feeding the animals. This year, however, under the direction of Domain Councillor Denker of Mirendorf, a permanent sheep shed was built that remedied all these inconveniences and, through the appropriateness and practicality of its arrangement, received universal approval.

The show was opened by presenting the bucks that had competed in last year's wagers individually for the public's inspection. Before each buck, the results of the examination by the assessors in Leipzig were then displayed, including the fleece weight in the factory-washed condition, the grade, the price of the wool, and the total fleece value, as well as the assessors' judgment on the properties of the wool in general — all presented and read aloud to the assembled sheep breeders, and finally the fineness grade of the wool according to Mr. Jeppe's measurement was also communicated.

In this way, even those sheep breeders who had not themselves participated in the wagers could, through their own observation, compare their judgment of the value and quality of the wool with that of the expert assessors, and obtain instruction that is otherwise only laboriously attainable after sustained study. A general interest in this instructive show was also evident, and there is no doubt that through it, correct knowledge of wool can spread rapidly and in wider circles throughout Mecklenburg.

First, wool samples were taken from each buck at positions No. 1, No. 3, and No. 5 (according to Jeppe's wool sample chart). Then the bucks were numbered, weighed, and — in order to verify their identity in the following year — branded on the horn.

Thereupon, the determination of the winner of the wager, which was to be decided by majority vote of the competitors themselves, was undertaken.

For this purpose, divisions of bucks were formed, each division placed on a table, the most excellent buck selected, and finally the selected ones were placed side by side to determine the winner.

The result was as follows.

First division. Bucks No. 1, No. 2, No. 3, No. 4, and 5. Bucks No. 2 and 3, from Schlieffensberg and Schwandt, which both received an equal number of votes, were declared the best.

Second division. Bucks No. 6 through 10, inclusive. Buck No. 8 from Ivenack received the most votes.

Third division. Bucks No. 11, No. 12, No. 14, No. 15, and No. 18. Bucks No. 14 and No. 18, from Basedow and Gorschendorf, received the most votes, with equal numbers between them.

Fourth division. Bucks No. 17, No. 20, No. 21, No. 22, and No. 23. The majority of votes went to Buck No. 17 from Roggow.

Fifth division. Bucks No. 19, No. 24, No. 25, No. 26, and No. 28. Among these, Buck No. 26 from Tellow received the most votes.

Thereupon, the 7 bucks that had been declared the most excellent in their respective divisions — namely No. 2, No. 3, No. 8, No. 14, No. 18, No. 17, and No. 26 — were placed together, and after long and careful examination by the competing gentlemen, Buck No. 18 from Gorschendorf received 2 votes and No. 17 from Roggow received 5 votes.

The buck No. 17, belonging to Mr. Pogge of Roggow, was accordingly declared the winner.

The same buck had also won the 150 Reichsthaler premium the previous year.

This wager, decided by the competitors themselves, found much approval and aroused lively interest, partly because the decision was reached immediately, but mainly because everyone — including non-competitors — could compare and critically assess their own views on the value of the wool and animals against the decision rendered. This wager becomes especially instructive because one later learns the judgment of expert assessors on the fleeces of the same animals, thus offering a comparison of the sheep breeders' views with the verdict of the wool merchants.

It also cannot fail that in such a collectively reached decision, the views of sheep breeders become more aligned with one another, so that the goal of their efforts in sheep breeding converges, and consequently the wool produced in Mecklenburg will in time become more uniform — which has a considerable, hitherto insufficiently appreciated influence on the price of wool brought to a small market frequented by few buyers.

This wager is furthermore especially useful, indeed necessary, to prevent the determination of the winner based on the highest fleece value from exerting too great an influence, and to provide a counterweight to that determination. For if a fleece contains an ounce of twisted wool, this reduces the value of the fleece in the wool merchants' assessment only by a trifle. But if that ounce of twisted wool grew on the back ridge of the buck, then such an animal is — because this defect is inherited by offspring in increasing measure — completely useless for breeding, no matter how great its fleece value may be.

Fleece value is an important, but not the sole, criterion in judging a breeding ram.

After the decision of this wager, the bucks were shorn clean under the constant supervision of the competitors, the fleeces were individually weighed, placed in a bag, and the bags sealed by Notary Holsten with the notarial seal.

The specification of the body weight of the bucks can be found in Appendix C, that of the previous year's shearing date — given on honor and good faith by the competitors — in Appendix D, and the fineness grade and length of the wool according to Mr. Jeppe's measurement in Appendix E.

On the afternoon of June 6, the announced buck auction took place. After 7 bucks had been sold at prices of 10.5 to 20.5 Reichsthalers per head, no further buyers could be found, although bucks from most of the distinguished breeding flocks in the country had been brought to auction, and for the most part very good animals.

Since the buck auction in the previous year had also yielded an equally unfavorable result, one can no longer regard its failure as a mere coincidence, and the question arises: whether the timing was poorly chosen, or whether the entire arrangement bears something about it that the public may rightly find objectionable.

The first buck auctions, attempted at the founding of the animal show, were bound to fail because individual flocks submitted so many bucks — including poor ones among them — that demand was far exceeded and the price sank to the level of a wether's price.

But in 1836, when Hofrath von Wedemeyer was the director of the sheep show, he introduced the still-maintained rule that no sheep breeder may bring more than 6 bucks to auction.

Through this restriction, overcrowding has been prevented, and since the reputation of the flocks depends substantially on the animals displayed before a large audience in Güstrow, it is in the interest of sheep breeders to bring only excellent bucks to auction.

The buyer now finds here in a small space bucks of various strains side by side, and can, saving the time- and money-consuming journeys to the estates that raise breeding rams for sale, select the buck that is most homogeneous with his flock. The condition of the animals in full, fully grown wool also makes correct assessment possible, whereas in autumn, when buck purchases frequently take place, the ungrown short wool provides insufficient characteristics for judging its value, and one frequently obtains an animal whose fully grown wool does not at all meet the expectations one had formed.

All circumstances thus seem to combine to make this institution truly useful for the country.

Guided by this view, several members of the Patriotic Association, acting in the spirit and purpose of this association, have felt obligated, despite the unfavorable results and the danger and costs associated with transporting the bucks, to bring bucks to the Güstrow auction every year.

Since the outcome has remained persistently unfavorable, however, there must be reasons for the low participation and against the arrangement. Since the reporter, despite his attention devoted to this subject and his inquiries, has been unable to discover and ascertain sufficient reasons, he takes the liberty of requesting those who know these reasons to make them publicly known, so that either what causes offense may be remedied, or if this cannot be achieved, so that those who make annual sacrifices in the belief that they are promoting something useful may no longer be induced to do so, and so that what is either impractical or untimely may finally be abandoned.

The bags in which the fleeces had been placed had received the numbers of the bucks. But so that no one might know the number of his own fleece, the previous numbers were erased while all competitors were absent, in the presence of Domain Councillor Sibeth, merchant Jeppe, and Notary Holsten, and different numbers were substituted. The protocol enclosed as Appendix A, which provides further information on this matter, was sealed by each of the three gentlemen and then deposited with Mr. von Dadelsen.

Baron von Maltzahn of Zettemin, who did not compete in the wagers, had also submitted a fleece with the request that it be washed and appraised together with the other wool fleeces, at his expense. This fleece was designated as No. 27.

The question — at what type of wash and at what degree of cleanliness of the wool the producer obtains the highest monetary amount for a given wool — is almost no less important than the question: which sheep strain yields the highest wool value with equal washing.

The buyers criticize any impure wool and frequently say that they would pay a considerably higher price if the washing were better. Weary of this criticism, about 15 years ago several Mecklenburg sheep breeders began to wash their sheep with soap and now brought a completely clean, white wool to market.

The criticism did cease, but the buyers paid only 5 to 10 percent more for this clean wool than for ordinarily washed wool, while the soap washing had resulted in a loss of 10 to 15 percent in shearing weight. Thus, the clean washing resulted in a very considerable loss for the producers. At first, it was believed that the buyers only failed to pay an appropriate price for the clean wool because they were unfamiliar with soap washing and feared disadvantages for the wool — and one therefore hoped for a soon-to-come favorable change in the price ratio between clean and dirty wool. But the fulfillment of this hope was postponed from year to year, the disproportion in price remained persistent, and after one finally became convinced of the futility of one's hope, the soap-washed wools have now disappeared from the market.

It was initially very difficult for the producers to understand why fat and dirt, despite the increased transport costs they cause, were paid for together with the wool — because the buyers gave either no explanation at all, or only inadequate ones.

Only later did it become generally known that the fat in the wool is necessary for its conservation, that defatted wool must be processed immediately, and if this cannot happen, loses very significantly in value during prolonged storage.

Removal of the dirt while preserving the fat thus appears to be the goal that must be pursued in wool washing, in order to obtain the highest monetary amount for a given quantity of wool. This, however, cannot be achieved through ordinary hand and immersion washing, and to the extent that one seeks to preserve the fat, a smaller or larger portion of the utterly useless and detrimental dirt also remains in the wool.

Now, if every residue of fat — important for the conservation of the wool — is inevitably accompanied by a residue of dirt that diminishes value — partly through more difficult factory washing, partly through increased transport costs — then there must be some degree of cleanliness of the wool at which the advantage of a further addition of fat is exactly offset and compensated by the disadvantage of the accompanying addition of dirt. The washing by which this degree of cleanliness is attained is at the same time the one at which the producer obtains the highest revenue for his wool. The determination and specification of this degree is therefore of great interest to every sheep breeder. This task, however, is too difficult to be brought to a decisive result by any individual, and is therefore rather a matter for the entire association.

The directorate of the sheep show would, in my view, earn merit if it initiated wagers directed solely at the most profitable wool washing, and then at future decisions on the wager for the highest fleece value, set the assessors the twofold task: first, to appraise the fleeces solely according to the quality of the wool, without regard to the washing, and second, to examine and estimate the difference in value of the fleeces that — assuming equality of wool fiber — arises solely from the different washing. From the multiplication of both appraisals, the relative value of the fleeces compared to one another would then emerge, and from the pure weight of the fleeces to be determined by bag washing, compared with the experts' assessment directed at the quality of the washing, it would become apparent which washing is most profitable for the producer.

Spray washing is said to have far more than hand and immersion washing the property of removing dirt while conserving the wool fat, and thereby being particularly advantageous for the producer. But while the spray-washed wool is praised and sought after by some wool merchants, it is criticized and avoided by other buyers — and thus the public remains in uncertainty on this matter as well.

Mr. Pogge of Roggow has now, in order to get to the bottom of the best type of washing, conducted very interesting experiments with 6 sheep washed in different ways.

In order to entirely eliminate the inequality of the wool and allow only the difference in washing to come into play, Mr. Pogge had half of each animal — taking the spine and middle of the belly as the dividing line — washed in a different manner, and then each half shorn separately. Mr. Pogge applied spray washing, soap washing, and immersion washing, and thus the influence that these different washing methods, with identical hair, exert on the quality and value of the wool is now before our eyes.

Mr. Pogge had the kindness to present the 12 half-fleeces on which the experiment was conducted for the public's inspection, and it was resolved to send these fleeces along to the assessor to be chosen for deciding the wagers, for appraisal.

The assessment of these has also been received simultaneously with the assessment of the wool fleeces. The reporter refrains, however, from drawing conclusions and inferences from this, because Mr. Pogge himself can undoubtedly present and develop the instructive results far better than the undersigned — who therefore hereby requests Mr. Pogge to communicate his procedure in the experiment and the results obtained from the assessment to the public in a supplement to this report.

Mr. Clauß of Pirna, well known as a wool expert and writer on sheep breeding, had, at the reporter's suggestion, the kindness to undertake the factory washing, sorting, and appraisal of the wool fleeces.

The fleeces were shipped to Pirna in October 1838 and returned in February 1839 with the enclosed appraisal protocol — jointly drafted and signed by Messrs. Clauß, wool merchant Wilkens, master craftsman Meissner, and cloth manufacturer Querner — attached as Appendix B.

In the appraisal protocol, however, several arithmetic errors were found, and since absolute accuracy was required here, Mr. Court Secretary von Dadelsen, at the undersigned's urgent request, had the kindness to reformulate the entire calculation.

In printing the protocol, this revised calculation has now been incorporated in place of the original.

The undersigned then prepared the calculation enclosed as Appendix C regarding the normalized body weight, and thereupon Mr. Court Secretary von Dadelsen prepared the main calculation enclosed as Appendix D, showing the fleece value of each animal reduced to the normalized body weight.

After these preparations for determining the winner had been made, Count von der Osten-Sacken issued an invitation to the competing gentlemen and friends of advanced sheep breeding for an assembly in Güstrow.

This assembly took place on April 20, 1839, and was opened by Count von der Osten-Sacken with a presentation on the purpose and significance of the sheep show and the associated competition for premiums.

After the legality and impartiality of the procedure in determining the winner had been recognized by the assembly, the sealed protocol on the exchange of numbers was opened, and it emerged:

1. that the buck whose fleece received No. 3, and which according to Appendix D has the highest fleece value among all bucks per 100 pounds of normalized body weight, namely 3.9255 Reichsthalers (3 Reichsthalers 44 Schillings), belongs to Count von Plessen of Ivenack.

2. that the buck with fleece number 9, which at 3.7789 Reichsthalers (3 Reichsthalers 37 Schillings) fleece value per 100 pounds of normalized body weight stands closest to the winner, was entered by the now deceased Mr. Lange of Jürgenshof.

Accordingly, Count von Plessen was awarded the first premium of 150 Reichsthalers, and the heirs of Mr. Lange were awarded the second premium of 100 Reichsthalers.

The names of the owners of the bucks (who had remained entirely unknown to the assessors) were then written next to the fleece numbers in the appraisal protocol, and each fleece was then inspected while the grade, the assessment, and the fineness degree of the wool were read aloud.

The assembly universally acknowledged that the factory washing arranged by Mr. Clauß was the purest and most uniform that we have had to date, and that the sorting of the wool had been carried out with care and expertise. It was rightly lamented, however, that the assessors had made no subdivisions within the main grades, whereby the finer differences in the value of the wools go unnoticed and cannot assert themselves in the assessment.

Since the measurement register of merchant Jeppe, enclosed as Appendix E, indicates not only the fineness grade but also the length of the wool, both in the natural and in the stretched condition, it offers, through comparison with the grade into which this wool has been placed, material for interesting and instructive reflections — and it emerges from this that the length of the wool is almost as important a factor in determining the grade as its fineness.

Tellow, November 10, 1839.

J. H. von Thünen.
