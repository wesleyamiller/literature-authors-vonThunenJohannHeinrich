# Report on the Testing of Agricultural Machines and Instruments

*Original: Notizen über den Hafen-Pflug (1839)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

Report on the testing of agricultural machines and instruments which took place on the 23rd and 24th of May.

The following were exhibited this year:

1) The Scottish plow, made entirely of iron, which won a prize at a trial in Scotland.

2) The knife harrows invented by Captain Carr.

3) The Scottish rhomboidal harrows.

4) The Scottish seed harrow.

5) A plow with double, movable moldboards for hilling potatoes.

6) A triple-share implement for cleaning potatoes, with an attached hoe.

7) A wagon with English patent axles.

All instruments from No. 1 to No. 7 inclusive were exhibited by Captain Carr of Tüschenbeck.

8) The Alban sowing machine.

9) The Sibeth sowing machine.

10) The Hane sowing machine.

11) The seed plow or seed coverer invented by Dr. Wertheimer.

12) A Scottish double plow.

13) A poorly manufactured example of the Belgian plow.

14) The Ribnitz hook plow.

15) The Tellow hook plow.

16) The Fliche ripper and scarificator.

17) The Stein grain-cleaning machine.

18) A potato slicer.

19) A chaff-cutting machine with flywheel.

The direction of this affair was entrusted this year to Privy Councillor Denker of Mierendorf and the undersigned by the Main Directorate of the Mecklenburg Patriotic Society.

The Carr instruments and the Wertheimer seed coverer were first put into operation.

The soil of the field on which the testing took place was mild, and had already been loosened by an earlier cultivation.

The Scottish plow delivered excellent work; deep and shallow, wide and narrow furrows were drawn by it with equal perfection. The device by which the width and depth of the furrows is regulated differs from that of the other plows known to me, and appears to be far more precise and less variable than that of the Small and Morton plows. The reviewer must confess that he has never seen a more perfect plow; Captain Carr also greatly preferred this Scottish plow to the Morton plow, which had hitherto been considered the best in England. The plow required, at a furrow depth of 6½ inches and a width of 13 inches, 3 hundredweight of force, and at a furrow 9 inches deep and 14 inches wide, 4 hundredweight of force.

Only the Belgian plow can perhaps challenge it for rank. Since we may now hope to obtain a properly constructed Belgian plow this year, it will be very instructive at the next year's testing to see the plows regarded as the best by the two nations standing highest in agriculture working side by side, and to compare them.

The Carr knife harrows are of extraordinary effectiveness: they chop up and thoroughly work through the surface of the soil, so that almost every particle of it is touched.

The rhomboidal harrows, with vertically positioned, non-cutting tines, break up the clods of earth on the field previously worked through by the knife harrows.

The Scottish seed harrows are similar to the latter, only the tines stand even closer together, so that the tine tracks are only about 2 inches apart, and the field takes on the appearance of having been hoed.

These three types of harrows have, through their rhomboidal shape and through the fact that two harrows of the same kind are always connected together, the particular advantage that the tine tracks fall close together while the tines themselves stand far apart, and are less clogged by pieces of sod than our ordinary harrows.

From a single trial on mild and friable soil, one cannot yet draw conclusions about the effectiveness of these harrows on other soil types in various conditions; but the value of these harrows has long been established, and they have already spread from Tüschenbeck with universal approval over part of western Mecklenburg. Stones, too, provided they are not present in too great a quantity, are said to be no absolute obstacle to their use, as I have been assured by Mr. Schwarz of Steinhagen, who has used these harrows for a considerable time.

Whether the plow with double movable moldboards for hilling potatoes has an advantage over our hook plow, which is very well suited for this purpose, seems doubtful to me, and can probably only be determined through a longer comparative application of both instruments.

The triple-share for cleaning potatoes, on the other hand, is probably preferable to the Flottbeck paring plow.

Captain Carr has made all of these instruments available to the Patriotic Society for the mere reimbursement of the cost of materials — and thereby at a price at which they could not be produced here — and indeed under the condition that they remain in Güstrow as models, and that in copying them, no supposed improvements be made within one year.

Captain Carr has not only borne the costs of the long transport of the instruments, but has also brought his farm manager and his lead plowman along to Güstrow, thereby depriving his own estate of two persons who are almost indispensable for a considerable period of time.

This striving, at the sacrifice of one's own interest, to be useful to others and to spread what is good in a wide circle, calls for our heartfelt thanks and our respect, and the General Assembly of the Patriotic Society has awarded Captain Carr the gold medal as a token of recognition of his merits.

Captain Carr has also given us the hope that each year at the time of the livestock show he will bring the newest instruments invented in England to Güstrow. In this way we would, by the shortest and for us most convenient route, become acquainted with the inventions of the nation most outstanding in this field, and could immediately appropriate their fruits for ourselves.

Since at the same time an expert in the field of mechanics, Dr. Alban, has undertaken to have these instruments copied and manufactured under his supervision, the possibility has been opened of obtaining these instruments true to the original and of equal quality — which was previously unattainable — and in an important branch of agriculture, to which attention had still been dormant until a few years ago, a new era is beginning.

The sowing machine recently invented by Dr. Alban received universal and undivided approval, both for its performance and for its mechanism.

As on the dial of a clock, a pointer, which one moves with ease, indicates and determines how many square rods a bushel of grain or a pound of clover seed is to be sown over.

By means of another crank, the sowing mechanism can be instantly disconnected from the revolving wheel, so that when the machine continues moving, no more seed is scattered.

The machine is the work of an ingenious idea; it impresses through its clever yet simple mechanism, and the invention does Mecklenburg honor.

Regarding the durability and robustness of the machine, not visual inspection but only experience can instruct us.

The Sibeth sowing machine has already been tested earlier, and a report about it has been printed in the Annalen.

We find everywhere that perfection never, or very rarely, arises all at once, but that intermediate steps and stages lead slowly toward it.

Privy Councillor Sibeth retains forever the merit of having been the first inventor in this field in Mecklenburg — and what is especially important, through his invention attention was directed to this branch of knowledge, and thereby the impulse for improvements and further inventions was given.

The Hane sowing machine also deserves honorable mention. Mr. Manufacturer Hane modified the English brush machine, originally designed for fine seeds, about five years ago so effectively that all types of grain can now be sown with it, and as a result of this improvement, the machine has been widely and usefully employed in both Pomerania and Mecklenburg.

By the General Assembly of the Patriotic Society, Dr. Alban and Privy Councillor Sibeth were awarded the gold medal for their inventions, and Mr. Manufacturer Hane was awarded the silver medal for the effective modification of the English sowing machine, as tokens of recognition of their merits.

The Wertheimer seed plow, an entirely new and original instrument, is intended for the shallow covering of seed.

Our hook plow, with all its other merits, is nevertheless a very deficient instrument for covering summer seed; for on the one hand it covers the seed 3 to 4 inches deep with earth, whereas a covering of 1 to 2 inches would in most cases be far more beneficial to the growth of the plants, and on the other hand the power of two draft animals is used where, at a furrow depth of 2 inches, half a horse-power would suffice. Here, then, deficient work is combined with a waste of power. With advancing cultivation and especially after the removal of stones, a well-designed seed plow will become an ever greater necessity. Dr. Wertheimer will therefore earn a merit if he continues to devote his attention to this subject and, through continued improvements, gives the instrument the greatest possible perfection.

The instrument draws — if the reviewer remembers correctly — three furrows, which together have a width of 2 feet, at once, and required 3½ hundredweight of draft force in loose soil and 4 hundredweight in firm soil. The seed plow had, however, only just been completed, and there is no doubt that the inventor will succeed in significantly reducing the required draft force.

The Scottish double plow has two moldboards, of which one hovers in the air while the other works in the earth. At the end of the furrow, both moldboards exchange their function, making it possible to plow back along the same furrow. The instrument delivered far less good work than the plow exhibited by Captain Carr, and required, at a furrow depth of 6 inches and a width of 11 inches, 5½ hundredweight of draft force. Since the resetting of the moldboards at the end of the furrow requires at least as much time as turning toward another furrow three ridges away, and since the plow not only requires great draft force but is also very expensive, it was incomprehensible to all how such an instrument could have found approval in England and even won a prize.

Captain Carr gave us the following explanation on this matter:

"In England and Scotland, hills are cultivated that are so steep that the horses cannot walk beside each other but must be harnessed one in front of the other, both walking in the furrow, and on such hills this double plow is used."

In Mecklenburg, the cultivation of such steep hills would be associated with great loss, and if in England such land is still capable of cultivation, this gives us a vivid picture of the effect of the English Corn Bill, which, through highly elevated grain prices, makes possible and even profitable the cultivation of steep hills as well as of poor sandy soil that would be worthless here.

On the other hand, we also recognize from this that, once this condition exists in England, completely free grain trade would turn whole stretches of cultivated land back into wasteland and render thousands of families breadless; that we therefore must never hope for or expect a duty-free importation of grain into England.

Time did not allow the remaining machines and instruments listed at the beginning of the report, most of which had already been exhibited in earlier years, to be put into operation and subjected to an equal testing.

The exhibition and testing of the agricultural implements aroused general interest this time, and the assembly that attended it was large and distinguished. It was especially gratifying and encouraging that His Excellency the Minister of State von Lützow honored this testing with his presence and directed his attention to it.

Even His Highness Duke Gustav gave this subject his interest, as His Highness inspected the machines and instruments exhibited in the wool warehouse on the last day of the livestock show.

Tellow, in July 1839.

J. H. von Thünen, Dr.

The prizes and distinctions proposed by the deputations for both divisions of the trade exhibition were all approved by the General Assembly of the Patriotic Society on July 3 of this year and accordingly dispatched to the persons concerned by the Main Directorate.
