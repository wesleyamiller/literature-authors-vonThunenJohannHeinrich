It is very much to be regretted that Mr. Staudinger, who owns Eiderstedt sheep — long renowned for the quality of their wool — did not present a ram for this wager. The question:

"By which sheep breed can ordinary spinning wool be produced with the greatest advantage, and which breed yields the finest spinning wool?"

is gaining ever greater importance for Mecklenburg, since spinning wool is now already so scarce and therefore so expensive that one probably receives just as high a return for feed through ordinary country sheep, and soon perhaps even higher, as through middling Merino sheep.

That question, however, can only be decided through the compilation and careful comparison of animals of different breeds.

Among the animals presented by Mr. Pogge was a two-year-old ewe that had resulted from the mating of a Merino ewe with a Dishley ram. The wool of this sheep measures 4.5 degrees Köhler according to Mr. Jeppe's measurement. It is to be hoped that Mr. Pogge will present not only this sheep next year, but also its sire and dam, and that the wool of all three animals will then be measured.

This would provide a contribution toward solving the problem:

In what ratio does the fineness of the wool of the offspring stand, in the mating of animals with unequal wool fineness, to that of the two parents?

Surely there is no randomness here — when one considers not individual specimens but the totality of offspring — but rather lawfulness; and it is very striking that since the invention of wool meters, these instruments have not been used to solve that problem. Formerly it was believed that the fineness of the offspring's wool would stand midway between that of the father and the mother — and from this it was calculated that in the improvement of country sheep through Merino rams, the wool of the fourth generation would already nearly equal the quality of Merino wool. Experience has shown how erroneous this view was.

But even supposing that the wool fiber of the offspring truly held the mean between the wool fiber of the father and the mother: it is still not determined whether such a division refers to the diameter, or to the cross-sectional area, or to the cubic content of the hair.

Suppose the wool fiber of the Merino rams used for improvement measures 50 degrees Grawert = 3.3 degrees Köhler, and the wool fiber of the country sheep to be improved measures 100 degrees Grawert = 2.3 degrees Köhler; then the wool fiber of the offspring has the following fineness grades:

2nd Generation: 62.5

The advances in the refinement of wool are thus far smaller when the division takes place according to the cubic content than according to the diameter of the hair.

According to my experiences and observations, even when all lambs reverting to the mother are carefully culled, it is only the wool of the 7th or 8th generation that equals the Merino wool, or comes so close that one can no longer distinguish it.

But if the careful culling of reverting lambs is neglected, then the wool produced through improvement probably never equals Merino wool.

My experience thus accords far more with the assumption made under C than with that made under A.

But will the reverse procedure — the mating of Merino sheep and their descendants with rams of the country breed — also proceed so slowly in coarsening? Will it likewise take until the 8th generation before the wool equals country wool?

If this were not so, if it were found on the contrary that, for example, in four generations the Merino sheep is transformed into a country sheep, while the transformation of the country sheep into a Merino sheep requires eight generations, then the following would emerge:

that the climatic influences which, acting freely in our regions, produced the coarse-wooled country sheep, are still at work today;

that nature continually works against the further refinement, and even the preservation of the fine herds in their present condition, in our regions;

that we must form stud herds from those individual specimens which — as sports of nature, according to laws unknown to us — surpass both father and mother in the fineness of wool, in order to overcome the counteraction of nature;

that even herds which are not to advance further in the fineness of wool still require rams from a finer stud, merely to maintain themselves at the same level;

that in the stud herds which raise breeding rams, the highest fleece value of the sheep must not be the ultimate goal.

Since the conclusions drawn here rest on a still unproven — though probable — premise, they also have only the value of conjectures; but they are also only meant to draw attention to the importance of solving the problem stated above, and to encourage the gentlemen sheep breeders to continue experiments in retrograde breeding and to communicate the results obtained.

It is well known that in the determination of the value of wool, not only fineness but also the crimp, the uniformity of the hairs among themselves, the uniformity of each individual hair from tip to base, the staple length, the softness, the luster, and several other properties are taken into consideration.

In the judgment of the wool dealer, then, the value of all these qualities is expressed in their combination.

The measurement of wool with the Köhler wool meter, by contrast, gives only a single element of value determination — the fineness — and even this only at one point of the hair, the middle.

The fineness grade therefore can never be the measure of value for individual fleeces.

If, on the other hand, we compare whole masses of wool, whole grades with one another, then the other properties of the wool nearly balance out, and fineness regains its full significance. This is demonstrated by the numerous Hoffmann measurements, which show that in the various grades, a nearly perfectly regular gradation in the diameter of the wool fibers takes place.

It must therefore be possible, in every case where the appraised value of the wool does not harmonize with its fineness grade, to identify the cause of the deviation.

A large portion of the value-determining properties of the wool expresses itself in magnitudes, and is thus measurable, such as: number of crimps per inch of length, uniformity or nonuniformity of the hairs among themselves, difference in the fineness of the wool fibers at the tip and the base, durability tested by suspended weights, and so forth.

The more accurate the judgment of the assessor, the more a certain lawfulness must be recognizable in the judgment.

Since we had the good fortune this year of having an outstanding wool expert as assessor, it would be very instructive if, on the samples where appraisal and fineness grade diverge the most, everything that is measurable, countable, and weighable were further investigated.

It is only a pity that measuring with the Grawert wool meter is so laborious and requires such great practice if the result of the measurement is to inspire confidence, and that neither with the Köhler nor the Grawert wool meter can the ends of the wool fibers be measured with reliability.\*

Tellow, December 1833.
