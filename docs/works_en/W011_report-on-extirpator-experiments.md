# Report on Some Experiments Conducted with the English Extirpator

*Original: Bericht über einige mit dem englischen Extirpator angestellte Versuche (1814)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

In the spring of 1812, I sowed a piece of oats using the English eleven-footed extirpator. A large portion of the oats remained lying on top; and due to a drought that set in, these grains did not germinate. Nevertheless, just as many plants came up on this piece as on the adjoining piece that had been harrowed in the usual manner. I can only explain this phenomenon by the fact that with our ordinary harrowing, a large quantity of grains must likewise remain behind. The young crop stood as uniformly as if it had been planted; regular rows were not at all discernible. In its growth, the oats did not distinguish themselves from those harrowed in the usual way.

In the autumn of 1812, I had a piece of rye stubble harrowed for sowing, smoothly harrowed, sown, and then had the seed incorporated with the extirpator. This rye distinguished itself upon emergence by an extraordinarily uniform and dense stand, and it was evident that I could have saved a third of the seed quantity. In its further growth, I noticed no difference, neither in the autumn nor now.

This spring I have once again made a small trial with oats, the result of which I shall only be able to fully assess at the forthcoming harvest.

I am convinced that the Fellenberg extirpator has great advantages for incorporating seed grain. I would certainly acquire one immediately if the many stones on my fields had not always remained too great an obstacle for me, on account of which I have broken a pair of irons with almost every attempt.

Tellow, the 1st of July, 1813.

Thunen.

Brief as this report is, and as much as one might wish it somewhat more complete, one will certainly not fail to recognize the keen observer, the sound judge, and the indefatigable experimenter. These experiments also confirm what I have recounted in the preceding pages, namely that on land worked with the extirpator, more plants come up than on land cultivated with the ordinary harrow. At the same time, this experiment refutes the quite incorrect opinion that some of our farmers still hold against the Fellenberg extirpator: that it merely churns up the soil and does not turn it over as our harrow does.
