# Report on the Sheep Show Held on June 6, 1843, in Gustrow

*Original: Bericht uber die am 6ten Juni 1843 zu Gustrow stattgefundene Schaafschau (1844)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thunen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

IX.

Report on the sheep show held on June 6, 1843, in Gustrow.

By Dr. von Thunen of Tellow.

For this year's sheep show, the following conditions and regulations, which were published in the public papers, served as the standard:

Sheep Show in Gustrow on June 6, eventualiter on June 7, 1843, at 7 o'clock in the morning.

The Sheep Show Committee has passed the following resolutions, which shall serve as the standard for this year's sheep show.

For premiums, a total of

300 Thaler in gold

has been set aside.

The distribution of premiums and the determination of winners shall proceed according to the following principles and regulations:

1. All rams entered for competition shall be divided into 2 categories: combing-wool rams and cloth-wool rams. Rams whose wool, measured at position No. 9 on Jeppe's wool sample chart, is 2 inches or more in length shall be counted as combing-wool rams.

After the separation has been made, both categories shall be presented one after the other, and the most excellent rams shall be selected from each, specifically in such number that the selected rams constitute one third of those presented. For each of the selected rams, a premium of 10 Thaler in gold shall be paid.

The selection and decision shall be made by majority vote of the competing sheep breeders, none of whom may vote for his own ram.

For this competition, however, no more than 10 premiums of 10 Thaler in gold each are set aside, and if more than 30 rams compete, only 10 may be selected and rewarded with a premium.

2. After the winners from each of the two categories have been determined in this manner, these winners shall be presented together and the best ram selected from each category. Each of the two principal winners shall receive a premium of 20 Thaler in gold. The decision shall be made as in No. 1, by the competitors.

3. Then both principal winners shall be presented together, and for whichever breeding ram is recognized as the most excellent by majority vote of the competing sheep breeders, a premium of 30 Thaler in gold shall be awarded.

4. A premium of 100 Thaler in gold shall be awarded for the ram whose one-year fleece, per 100 pounds of normalized body weight, has the highest monetary value.

The normalized body weight is found by adding 100 pounds to the weight of the ram after shearing and dividing this sum by two. The normalized body weight of a ram weighing 120 pounds is accordingly (120 + 100) / 2 = 110 pounds, and that of a ram weighing 70 pounds is 85 pounds. Under this procedure, whatever the ram weighs more or less than 100 pounds is counted only at half.

5. Furthermore, the following are offered:

a. A premium of 10 Thaler in gold for the ram that, at position No. 9 (Jeppe's wool sample chart), has the finest wool according to measurement with the Kohler wool meter. In the case of equal fineness, the quantity of wool shall decide.

b. A premium of 10 Thaler in gold for the ram whose one-year fleece in the unwashed state has the highest weight, without regard to breed, fineness, or body weight.

c. A premium of 10 Thaler in gold for the animal, whether ram, ewe, or wether, that has the greatest body weight after shearing.

6. For each ram entered in the competition, regardless of whether it is competing for all or only one premium, the owner shall pay 5 Thaler in gold as an entry fee.

7. Every sheep breeder has the right to enter up to 4 rams in the competition for premiums.

8. Only those rams shall be admitted to the competition that were shorn and branded at the previous year's sheep show in Gustrow. An exception to this is made only for No. 5a and No. 5c. Yearling rams are entirely excluded from the competition.

9. The rams that were entered in the competition for premiums the previous year must be brought to this year's sheep show, and those that compete this year must be brought to next year's sheep show and put on display.

10. All competing rams shall be presented with unwashed wool.

11. An attempt shall be made to bring about the decision on the main premium of No. 4 of 100 Thaler already during the animal show week.

12. After all sheep show business has been concluded, a ram and sheep auction shall take place, provided there are interested parties.

13. All animals to be presented at the sheep show must be on the ramparts of Gustrow no later than June 6, at 7 o'clock in the morning, where the necessary arrangements will be made.

14. The shorn fleeces shall be displayed for viewing in a suitable locale during the duration of the animal show. At the same time, Mr. Jeppe will then, upon request, show his very instructive wool sample charts of the fleeces of Australian wool that were exhibited in Stuttgart, and provide the necessary explanations thereof.

With regard to the sheep show expected in the coming year (1844), the following has been determined:

a. That for all premiums to be awarded at that time, only such rams may compete as were shorn and branded with a sequential number at this year's sheep show;

b. That from the appraised fleeces, each competitor must hand over a sufficiently large sample to the directorate.

Registrations -- in which the number of animals to be entered for competition, for shearing, and for display must be indicated -- are to be sent postage-free by June 1 to the Secretary of the Court, Mr. von Dadelsen, in Gustrow.

At the same time, it must be indicated whether the entrant will bring his own shearers or whether he wishes that such be provided by the directorate. In the latter case, 8 Schilling are to be paid for each ram to be shorn.

Charlottenthal and Tellow, April 30, 1843.
C. W. Engel.
J. H. v. Thunen,
as current chairman of the Sheep Show Committee.

After wool samples had been taken from all competing rams, the procedures for determining the winners with regard to the remaining premiums were undertaken, consisting of:

the shearing of the presented rams,
the weighing of the shorn rams, and
the weighing of the unwashed fleeces.

The weight found here for the fleeces could not, however, serve as a guide, since some fleeces were moister than others, and the fleeces were weighed again in a dried state by the appraisers.

The result of the final weighing of the fleeces, as well as the body weight of the rams, is recorded in the attached Table D.

The shearing took place under the supervision of some of the competing gentlemen, who inspected each shorn ram and made three classes with regard to the quality of the shearing, the degree of closeness of the shear and the greater or lesser number of cut wounds found on the animal forming the basis for classification.

For the rams of the first class -- the best shorn -- the shearers received, in addition to the day's wage, a premium of 12 Schilling; for the rams of the second class, 8 Schilling were paid; and for those of the third class, 4 Schilling per head. In total, 7 Thaler 40 Schilling in premiums and 6 Thaler 16 Schilling in day wages were paid out to the shearers.

Since competition and the prospect of reward simultaneously spur good work, this arrangement can become a school for training skilled shearers, who can in turn become the teachers of others -- provided that the shearers are not drawn from a single estate but from many estates.

The fleeces were then handed over to Mr. Merchant Jeppe and Mr. Factory Inspector Mantius, who kindly undertook the appraisal of the fleeces.

Remarks on the preceding report.

A. About 12 years ago, a premium was offered for the most wool-rich ram that, without regard to breed or wool fineness, would carry 5 pounds of clean wool in 365 days. The premium seemed easy to win, since one heard of many rams in Mecklenburg that carried 8 to 10 pounds of washed wool. Unexpectedly, however, all rams presented, even from the most recognized wool-rich flocks, fell far short of the stated goal. When it had been clearly demonstrated that there was no ram in Mecklenburg that carried 5 pounds of clean wool, the standard weight was reduced from 5 to 4 1/2 pounds of clean wool. Even here, many efforts to obtain the premium failed, until finally a ram with coarse quarta wool appeared that had a few Loth over 4 1/2 pounds of wool.

Who could have guessed at that time that scarcely a decade later, a ram would appear at the animal show that, with a mean fineness of 2 1/2 and the grade I Prima, carried 4 pounds 7.05 Loth in 361 days, and thus 4 1/2 pounds of clean wool in a full year.

This is a gratifying proof of the power given to man even over animal nature, and shows us that the limit of perfecting animals for human purposes is not yet discernible.

Since this outstanding animal, No. 28, combines with wool quantity and wool fineness also the other desired qualities, such as good staple structure and so forth, it is very striking that in the visual inspection competition for the premium of 20 Thaler, ram No. 28 received only one vote, while ram No. 26 received 5 votes.

The competitors thus decidedly gave preference to ram No. 26, and the reporter must confess that he considered this decision correct and that he would have voted the same way had he been a competitor. The reporter, however, simultaneously expressed the view that ram No. 28 would be the winner in fleece value, and was a witness as the owner of ram No. 26 declined a bet offered to him by the owner of ram No. 28 regarding the highest fleece value. The result of the appraisal has fully confirmed this prediction, as according to Appendix D, ram No. 28 has a fleece value of 4 Thaler 18.03 Silbergroschen, but ram No. 26 has a fleece value of only 4 Thaler 9.37 Silbergroschen per 100 pounds of normalized body weight.

Even more striking is the superiority of ram No. 28 over ram No. 26 with regard to the fineness of the wool, as the mean wool fineness of the former ram is 2 1/2, while that of the latter ram is 3 1/8.

If we on the one hand regard the highest fleece value, as determined by experts, as the decisive factor in premium distribution, and on the other hand nevertheless give preference to a ram of lesser fleece value, there appears to be a contradiction in this. But one should never tolerate such a contradiction within oneself; rather, one should bring to clarity and consciousness the reasons upon which it rests.

I therefore take the liberty of proposing and recommending the following questions for discussion at next year's sheep show:

1. On what grounds do the sheep breeders give preference to ram No. 26 over ram No. 28?

2. Although ram No. 28 has a good staple, it is nevertheless inferior in this regard to ram No. 26, which, in proportion to the length of the wool, has an excellent wool staple.

If this were then the reason for the higher ranking of ram No. 26, this would in turn lead to an investigation of the question:

a. What value does good staple structure actually have?

b. To what degree can excellent staple structure compensate for inferiority in wool quantity, fineness, and fleece value?

c. We know that the regular growth of the wool, the uniformity of the hairs among themselves, and the uniformity of the diameter of the individual hair from base to tip generally corresponds to the regularity of the staple structure; accordingly, the staple structure offers us a characteristic by which, even without precise examination and without deeper knowledge, we can form a superficial judgment about those wool properties. Has not the ease and convenience of such an assessment led us to ascribe too great a value to staple structure?

In the careful appraisal of wool by experts, however, all wool properties are themselves examined, and the staple structure loses its validity when it does not harmonize with the wool properties. Thus the correctly appraised fleece value appears as the higher criterion, and the question arises whether the sheep breeders must not subordinate their superficial judgment, founded on staple structure, to this criterion.

If, nevertheless, the staple structure is to have a validity that outweighs the decisive fleece value, then almost no other reason for this remains than that a good staple is the sign of constancy -- that the descendants of well-stapled animals will more closely resemble their parents than those of poorly stapled ones.

Is this a principle drawn from experience, or an accidentally prevailing, as yet unexamined opinion?

If the sheep show is to fulfill its purpose and be worth the costs devoted to it, then it must contribute to resolving these and similar questions.

B. For this there is almost no other means than the realization of the proposal made many years ago by Mr. Pogge of Roggow: to judge rams by their performance, that is, by their offspring, and to extend the premium distribution to include this criterion.

3. The appraisers, in their assessment, give ram No. 28 the designation "worthy of attention," but give ram No. 26 the designation "worthy of special attention," thus ranking the latter higher than the former.

Which properties of the wool are it, then, that establish this superiority, and why does this superiority not express itself in the value of the wool, which is set at 85 Thaler for the former but at 80 Thaler per Prussian hundredweight for the latter?

3. For some years now, interest and participation in the sheep show have been declining more and more.

What is the cause of this, and how can it be counteracted?

In part, the diminished interest probably stems from the fall in wool prices and from the significant reduction in the monetary yield of sheep farming.

But if it is a fact that among different breeding rams there is a difference of 1 to 2 Thaler in the value of their fleeces, and if through the continued use of these rams a difference of 2 Thaler per head can appear in the wool revenue of entire flocks, then it would be a failure to recognize one's own interest to wish to withdraw one's attention from this matter.

Indeed, given the malleability of the sheep for human purposes and the constant changes in the value of the various wool grades, an institution through which the most profitable sheep breeds and the most excellent animals in the various breeding flocks are identified and brought to the knowledge of the public seems to be indispensable, if the sheep breeding of the country is not to take a direction detrimental to the revenue of estates.

Another question, however, is whether the current organization of the sheep show meets the demands by which the participation of the broader public can be engaged. At the founding of the sheep show, the mere wish to become acquainted with the various sheep breeds of the country could serve as motivation for participation and attendance. With the knowledge once gained, however, this incentive falls away.

Since no scientific discussions are connected with the sheep show, and all deliberations and activities are limited to determining the winners, while the public is informed of the results through the Annals, the circle of participants narrows more and more to the number of sheep breeders who enter rams for competition.

This deficiency could, however, be remedied if on the day after the show the sheep breeders were to unite in an assembly in which the problems that sheep breeding still presents, and which constantly renew and multiply with every invention in the machines for processing wool and with every extension of sheep breeding to distant, hitherto uncultivated countries, would be discussed; in which, furthermore, the interesting and instructive wool sample collections from all regions of the world, which Mr. Merchant Jeppe brings and displays each year, would be contemplated with calm and leisure; and in which, finally, the fleeces of the competing rams would be subjected once more to an inspection and examination of all their wool properties, with the participation of knowledgeable wool merchants and sorters.

For the present purpose, it would be advisable to announce, together with the sheep show notice, some of the questions to be discussed, to which every participant in the assembly could then add other questions, especially those relating to the fleeces at hand.

C. The appraisers have not, as was formerly customary, based their appraisal on the market prices of wool from the previous year, but have retained the prices adopted at the fleece exhibition in Doberan as approximating the average price. This is certainly more correct than the former procedure, in which the momentary, highly variable market conditions served as the measure of decision. Nevertheless, these adopted prices should not permanently serve as the standard in the future, because the ratio of average prices of the various wool grades is also subject to change. Perhaps it would be most appropriate if in the future we based the prices for the various wool grades on the average of the prices of the preceding 5 years. This would at least avoid the two disadvantages of treating the last year either as the sole standard or as carrying no weight at all in the balance.

In any case, we always obtain in this manner only information about which sheep breed, which individual thereof, has been the most valuable in the past.

What we actually strive to know, however, and in which we have a vital interest, is the answer to the question:

Which type of wool must we produce in order to obtain the highest return on our feed in the near future, or which sheep breed will be the most profitable for us in the future?

The grain prices of the future can be estimated with some certainty from the average price over a longer period of the recent past; but the situation is different with the price of wool and especially with the ratio of prices of the various wool grades to one another.

With the perfection of navigation, the freight costs of wool relative to its value have become so small that wool from the most remote countries on earth can be brought to the European market and can compete with the wool produced here.

In Australia, South Africa, and South America, there are still immeasurable expanses of uncultivated but grass-rich land suitable for sheep pasture, and in these countries the production costs of wool are much lower than here, for the following reasons:

1. The land is to be had almost for nothing, and the land rent, which constitutes a part of the production costs of wool, is here almost zero;

2. The climate in these southern countries is so mild that vegetation continues throughout the entire year, whereby the costs of cultivating and harvesting winter feed for the sheep are saved;

3. Because of the mild weather, the sheep can remain outdoors the entire year, and the costs of building and maintaining sheep barns are eliminated.

With such advantages, we can probably not withstand the competition with these countries, and since wool production in them has already increased to an enormous degree over a quarter century, and a limit to the increase is not yet foreseeable given the immeasurable pasture areas hitherto unused, colonial wool -- once its production has exceeded England's demand -- can appear on our German markets in a perhaps not distant future and, through its low price, drive our wool from those markets.

This would indeed open a bleak prospect for our sheep breeding and thus for one of the most important sources of income of the country -- if, that is, the southern countries could produce all types of wool used in the factories. If, however, this is not the case, then it is clearly evident that we must, in wool production, avoid competition with colonial wool and turn to the production of such types of wool as the southern countries cannot produce.

The greater warmth of the climate, the difference in feed, the constant remaining outdoors, and the lack of intelligence and care with which sheep breeding is conducted cannot be without influence on the wool of the sheep brought from Europe to the southern countries.

According to what has come to my knowledge and seems credible to me, the wool of the sheep transplanted to Australia becomes longer and softer there, but at the same time also more brittle and loses its regular growth. Also, Australian wool, as far as I know, is used almost exclusively for combing-wool fabrics and is either entirely unsuitable for cloth manufacture or suitable only when mixed with European wool.

Accordingly, it is not cloth wool but only combing wool that is threatened by the competition of colonial wool.

Now, the majority of Mecklenburg sheep breeders have turned to the production of combing wool, and indeed with great profit until now.

Does it not lie in the interest of the Mecklenburg sheep breeders, then, to voluntarily abandon this path before they are forced to do so by many years of losses?

In order to be able to answer this question in the affirmative, another question would first have to be decided.

Mecklenburg combing wool distinguishes itself through strength and vigor, and thus possesses precisely those properties that colonial wool lacks. Will not Mecklenburg combing wool, for this very reason, remain indispensable even with an overabundance of colonial wool and retain an appropriate price, however low the price of colonial wool may fall?

It is evident how important for Mecklenburg's sheep breeders is clarification and understanding on this point, how they can, guided by a correct foresight in their practices, protect themselves from great future losses.

My proposal therefore is: to grant annually from the animal show treasury a certain sum for the collection of information on the aforementioned point, on the course of the wool trade, on the state of sheep breeding in other countries, on the inventions in manufacturing that affect the future price of wool, and so forth -- and then to commission a knowledgeable man with the collection of these inquiries.

For Mecklenburg, the execution of this proposal may promise a more favorable outcome than for many other countries, since we have a man who combines expertise and wide-ranging connections with indefatigable activity and a self-sacrificing enthusiasm for this field, and who would surely be glad to undertake this business.

The person commissioned with this business would then have to deliver, each year at the assembly to be connected with the sheep show, a detailed report on the results of his inquiries and especially on all points that presumably suggest a change in the price ratio of the various wool grades.

In this way, in the opinion of the reporter, the sheep show would not only regain its former interest but could also exert a more fruitful influence than before on the sheep breeding of the country.

J. H. v. Thunen.

Appendix B.

Most respectful memorandum.

In most respectfully forwarding the enclosed papers, namely:

1. The appraisal protocol of June 7 of this year,
2. The value determination table,

I take the liberty of adding the following remarks.

1. The prices of the grades, which depending on the condition of the fleeces have a range of 10 Thaler per hundredweight, are consistent with this year's Breslau prices, if one considers that the latter apply to fleece wools in shearer's bundles, while we are dealing here with sorted grades.

The Breslau prices are well suited to serve our purposes, since at no other market does the quality of the wool and the gradation of the grades express itself as precisely through price as in Breslau.

2. Since the animals entered in the competition had become more or less wet due to the persistent rain on the day of shearing, I had them all dry at uniform temperature, then removed all foreign matter from the fleeces and weighed them on a very accurate scale. Only then were the samples taken for bag washing. This procedure, most necessary for a correct determination, had the usual consequence that the fleeces that had been wet lost weight in this cleaned state, while some that had remained dry had even gained slightly.

Rostock, July 1843. F. W. Jeppe.
