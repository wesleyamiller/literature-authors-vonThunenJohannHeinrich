## Grain Yield of a Field at Tellow That Has Not Been Fertilized for Twenty Years

A lecture delivered by Dr. J. H. [von Thünen] in the Section for Agricultural Accounting at Doberan.

As a supplementary appendix to the lecture presented in the section:

The experience communicated here, together with the frequently made observation that the raw earth thrown up from newly dug ditches becomes fertile after the passage of several years, combined with the results of my investigations into agricultural accounting regarding the connection between soil quality and soil activity, have produced the following view in me:

Just as, with respect to temperature and moisture content, there is a constant interaction and a striving toward equalization between air and earth — so that moist soil gives off water to the atmosphere, while dry soil absorbs moisture from the air — so too, with respect to the content of plant-nourishing gases — chiefly ammonia and carbonic acid gas — there is a striving toward equalization. The humus-rich soil, especially freshly manured fields, releases plant-nourishing gases into the atmosphere, while soil exhausted by many grain harvests absorbs them back from the air. The contact of the field with the atmosphere, brought about by tillage, is therefore depleting for rich soil, but enriching for poor soil. With continued cultivation of the soil with exhausting crops and without the addition of manure, a state must eventually be reached in which there is an equilibrium between what the soil receives from the atmosphere and from the roots and stubble of grain left behind, and what the grain harvests take from it.

If the field does not lack the mineral substances necessary for the thriving of plants, then the average yield of this field without any manure will remain constant — the steady state has been reached.

Depending on the physical characteristics of the soil, its water-holding capacity, and the nature of the layer of air hovering above it, the grain yield of different soil types in the steady state will vary greatly. Without manure application, clay soil in the steady state will give a much higher yield than sandy soil.

At Tellow, the piece of field designated for exhaustive cropping yielded, in its tenth sowing without fertilization, still 10.3 Berlin bushels of rye per 100 square rods — equivalent to 12.15 Berlin bushels of rye per Magdeburg morgen — and the yield of this piece, compared with the yield of the entire field from the same area, amounted to 81 percent.

A friend who inspected this rye with me last year gave me the inspired advice to enlarge this piece — advice I intend to follow, not because I believe the result will be equally favorable, but because it promises important insights for science. For surely the yield of a piece lying far from the woods, and also that of the entire field, would be much lower if manure application were omitted.

The cause of the wonderfully sustained fertility of the aforementioned field piece must therefore lie not merely in its physical properties, but in its location and surroundings.

This field is now almost entirely enclosed by woodland, which leaves an opening only to the southwest. The gases rising from the neighboring fields, especially from the manured fields, are carried to the aforementioned field piece through this opening, but are prevented from escaping by the surrounding woodland. Thus the lower layer of air here is much richer in plant-nourishing gases than on the open field. I have been unable to find any other explanation for this almost miraculous phenomenon, at least up to now.

If chemistry is to give us clarification here, then not merely the topsoil and subsoil — the analysis of which Dr. Schulze of Eldena has kindly undertaken — but also the layer of air hovering above must be investigated.

The aforementioned field piece has, apart from some strips cut off from it in the year 1835, a loamy sandy soil that one may class as barley soil of the third quality, and had formerly borne very good, though not unusually heavy, grain. Since this piece was already surrounded on three sides by woodland, and was also bordered on the fourth side by a strip of field fit only for planting, I resolved in the year 1827 to sow it likewise with pines, but first to exhaust it through continued grain cultivation without manure, to the point where it would have no further value as arable land.

This piece was initially about 1,100 Mecklenburg square rods (9½ Magdeburg morgen) in size, and after the sandiest part was cut off in 1835, it still had a size of about 950 square rods (8 Magdeburg morgen).

The field had been marled in 1815, had received mold application in 1821, and in the period from 1821 to 1827 had borne crops like the adjacent field: 1st fallow with manure, 2nd rye, 3rd oats, 4th pasture, 5th pasture, 6th pasture.

*Note: It was not only this piece but also adjacent fields of similar character that received mold application, and this, as the boundary line between treated and untreated fields showed, never produced a significant effect.*

Since the year 1827 this field piece has been managed in the following order:

In the years 1834, 1840, and 1841, the grain from the field piece under consideration was carted to separate storage and threshed separately. The harvest amounted to:

- In 1834, rye: 8.23 bushels per load
- In 1840, rye: 9.13 bushels per load
- In 1841, oats: 15.09 bushels per load

The threshing yield per load in the years 1834 and 1840 thus does not differ significantly from the average threshing yield.

Average of these 5 harvests: 72 : 100

After this field piece had already borne six grain sowings following the last fertilization given in 1821, the five grain sowings that followed still delivered an average yield that, per 100 square rods, equaled 72 percent of the yield of the entire regularly manured field.

Most remarkable, however, is the fact that in the last rotation no decline in yield is visible compared with the preceding one, since the rye in 1834 brought about 80 percent, and in 1840 brought 81 percent, of the yield of rye on the rest of the manured field.
