# Remarks on the Mecklenburg Hook Plow

*Original: Bemerkungen über den mecklenburgischen Häfen (1837)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

## Notes on the Hook Plow

By Dr. von Thünen at Tellow.
(With an accompanying lithographic drawing.)

The experiments made with preference for the plow over the course of more than 20 years to displace the hook plow by introducing Small's and Bailey's plows had remained fruitless. It had been found that the hook plow, crude and rough as it generally is in construction, and as wasteful of energy as it is in some of its functions -- such as breaking loose the clod of earth, turning the soil over on both sides, and so forth -- nevertheless runs more lightly, that is, requires less draft power, than the best plows; furthermore, it had been found that the work of the hook plow, although so deficient in individual respects, in its final result -- the good preparation of the soil for sowing and its influence on the size of the harvest -- at least equals, and perhaps surpasses, the work of the plow.

On the other hand, the deficiencies of the hook plow, partly with respect to the waste of draft power in individual aspects, and partly with respect to the quality of work in detail, were so clear and evident that it long remained incomprehensible to the undersigned how that result could come about. Only later did he arrive at the conviction that the wedge-shaped penetration of the hook plow into the earth, with respect to the lightness of draft, and the crumbling of the soil, with respect to the good preparation of the field, are of such decisive importance that thereby all deficiencies which the hook plow actually has in comparison with the plow are canceled out or even outweighed. Connected to this was the investigation of whether it might not be possible to remedy the deficiencies of the hook plow without giving up its essential advantages.

After a prolonged period of attention devoted to this subject, it seemed to the reporter probable:

1) that by extending the hook share toward the field side, the furrow could be cut horizontally;

2) that the hook share, if instead of sloping down on both sides it formed a flat surface, could be made high enough on the land side to cut through the strip of earth rather than break it loose, thus taking the place of the coulter;

3) that if the moldboard consisted of a flat surface and formed an acute angle with the hook crummel instead of a right angle, the falling of the earth toward the land side could be prevented and perhaps a turning over of the strip of earth could be produced;

4) that by covering the moldboard with a cast-iron plate and by using two iron sole-plates -- one under the beam and the other on the land side of the beam -- whereby the earth would come into contact everywhere only with iron and nowhere with wood, the friction would be so considerably reduced that the turning of the much enlarged mass of earth resulting from the horizontal cut would nevertheless require no additional draft power.

In the spring of 1834, after many trials and modifications, such a hook plow was completed which, although it still had various imperfections -- for example, it had only a forged, less smooth, iron moldboard instead of a cast one -- nevertheless clearly showed that the idea was realizable.

It was decided to run this hook plow through the entire summer alongside and next to the ordinary hook plow fitted with a forecarriage and drawn by horses, so that a daily comparative test could be conducted and the workers themselves could form a judgment about the worth or worthlessness of the instrument.

During this summer, the result showed that the horses pulling the ordinary hook plow could not keep pace with those pulling the hook plow, and that the latter were at the end of the summer in at least as good condition as the former, even though a much larger mass of earth had been turned over with the hook plow, since broader furrow slices were taken with it and these, at the same depth as the hook furrow on the land side, were cut horizontally.

Encouraged by this success, I had several hook plows manufactured in the spring of 1835 and now had the moldboards covered with cast-iron plates.

During this summer, one ordinary hook plow was still to remain in use alongside the hook plows to continue the comparison between the two; but the farmhands constantly quarreled over who should take the hook plow, since none of them was willing to work with the ordinary one, and therefore another hook plow was acquired.

Since then, only one hook plow working over both hands, with a forecarriage, has remained in use here, with which:

1) the headlands are plowed, because here it is preferable to throw the earth away from the ditches rather than toward them; and

2) the last furrows of the plow ridges are plowed out, because the hook plow, like the regular plow, made too wide an open furrow there.

In addition, when covering potatoes by plowing, the belly hook plow is used to avoid trampling them, in which the horses do not tread in the furrow. For all other plowing work, however, the hook plow alone is employed.

## The Work of the Hook Plow

The hook share and the moldboard plate lie in one plane, and together they form a doubly inclined surface. The earth lifted by the hook share is pushed onto the moldboard during the draft, and since this moldboard has an inclination to the left side, it falls off again. In this process, the part of the earth strip closest to the open furrow falls back down immediately behind the hook share, while the more distant part of the earth strip rises higher on the moldboard and, since the moldboard is wider at the top, is pushed further to the left before the earth falls off. This produces a rotation of the earth strip around its own axis, and thus here, through the mere falling from an inclined plane, the turning of the earth is accomplished -- something for which the English plows expend so much draft power through the curved moldboard.

In sod and in firm stubble fields, this turning of the earth, with good guidance of the hook plow, is complete. In loose, already cultivated soil, all the earth lying on top is indeed covered by the earth brought up from below, but some earth crumbles back into the furrow. To an eye accustomed to clean plowwork, this would probably be objectionable, although in result it is probably without disadvantage. The furrow, however, remains open enough to afford the plowman and the draft animals a comfortable walk.

Since the earth rises high on the smooth moldboard and then falls from above, the field acquires through cultivation with this instrument an extraordinary looseness, which is not produced to this degree even by the hook plow.

On sandy soil, this could become disadvantageous if one does not give one fewer pass than with the other instruments.

If the field is in a dry condition, the earth not only comes to rest loosely but is also greatly crumbled by falling from the moldboard. In moist conditions, however, on loamy soil the cohesion of the earth is too strong to be broken by the fall from the moldboard; here the hook plow forms clods which, although not firmly pressed as in plowing, are nevertheless larger and more cohesive than with the ordinary hook plow.

That the ordinary hook plow crumbles the soil more in wet conditions probably has its reason in the fact that it lets the earth fall on all sides, and that the friction on wood is far greater than on smooth iron. In the seed furrow during such wet weather as in the autumn of 1836, the ordinary hook plow therefore delivers a field better prepared for sowing than the improved hook plow.

The hook plow cuts the earth strip horizontally, just like the regular plow, and leaves no earth falling back on the land side.

In the position in which the hook plow is held during work, the upper tip of the right side of the hook share is raised 9 1/2 inches above the bottom of the furrow, and for furrows up to this depth, the hook share cuts the earth strip loose and takes the place of the coulter. For a deeper plowing than 9 1/2 inches -- which is quite unusual here -- the instrument in its present condition is therefore not suited. By extending the hook share toward the right side, however, this deficiency could probably be remedied.

## Performance in Various Conditions

In very firm soil, the hook plow penetrates at least as well as the ordinary hook plow, and its progress there is steadier, its work much better. In the summers of 1835 and 1836, all field roads created by manure hauling were broken up with it.

The hook plow has shown the unexpected property that, at a given setting, it penetrates to the same depth in mellow and in firm soil and is not lifted, as the ordinary hook plow is, when it strikes a hard subsoil. At a furrow depth of 6 inches, it therefore brings up some raw earth on the loamy hilltops with shallow topsoil, which then receives its own separate manuring. According to the observations of the reporter, the loamy hilltops, which almost always suffer from drought due to their sloping position, can be given only a momentarily heightened but not an enduringly high fertility through repeated heavy manurings without deepening of the topsoil. A deepening of the topsoil, whereby the field acquires the ability to absorb and retain a larger quantity of water and manure, seems here alone to lead to the goal. The mentioned property of the hook plow is therefore very welcome here; on estates that with uneven soil have a shortage of manure, it might appear disadvantageous.

Also for breaking up level meadows that have been covered with earth and are strongly turfed, the hook plow is used here with success. For this work, however, the sharpness given to the edge of the hook share by the blacksmith is not sufficient; rather, the hook share must be ground sharp on a grindstone.

The hook plow does not turn the furrow in the meadows as well as the Small plow; also, individual sod pieces fall back into the furrow (which, however, also occurs with the Small plow when a very skilled operator is not present), and therefore a woman must follow each plowman to set these pieces right.

On these meadows, however, the hook plow breaks up a much larger area with no greater exertion of the horses than the Small plow. The sod strips turned by the hook plow lie hollow, are in this position very exposed to the attack of the harrows, and for 2 years strongly turfed meadows have been made mellow and ready for sowing with rapeseed through 4 passes in a single fallow year.

With the hook plow, when it goes 6 inches deep, furrow slices of 13 inches Lubeck measure = 12 inches Rhenish measure in width are generally taken.

The furrow slice is cut completely horizontally, and at the width of 13 inches, no trace of ridges remains standing.

The cross-section or profile of the furrow then amounts to 6 x 13 = 78 square inches.

When the furrow of the ordinary hook plow is 6 inches deep on the land side, the earth on the field side is cut only 4 inches deep; the average furrow depth is thus 5 inches. If no ridges are to be left standing, the hook plow at this setting can only take furrow slices of 12 inches in width, and this only in spring when the hook shares have not yet been worn down and retain their full width. The profile of the furrow slice then amounts to 5 x 12 = 60 square inches.

## Draft Power Tests

To determine the draft power required by various tillage implements, experiments with a dynamometer were conducted here in the spring of 1835 in the presence of several farmers. The result seemed to indicate that the hook plow and the ordinary horse hook plow with forecarriage required equal draft power when the furrow slice of the former measured 72 square inches and that of the latter 60 square inches in profile.

Since, however, the dynamometer itself was out of order and these experiments were conducted only once rather than in various states of the field, no further conclusions can be drawn from them.

Far more decisive on this matter is the condition of the draft animals when they work continuously with one instrument.

In this respect, it has been found on the hilly field here, consisting partly of loamy soil, in the summers of 1835 and 1836, that the horses which daily went before the hook plow -- with which furrow slices of 5 to 6 inches depth and 12 to 13 inches width were taken -- did not lose weight but remained approximately in the same condition.

## Comparison of the Day's Work of the Hook Plow with That of the Ordinary Hook Plow

The statements about the day's work of the hook plow diverge from one another to the highest degree, which probably stems mainly from the following causes:

1) The condition of the field: whether the soil is loamy or sandy, hilly or level, intersected by many ditches or not, contains many stones or is free of them, and so forth.

2) The manner of plowing: whether broad furrow slices are taken and ridges left standing, or whether the plowing is done cleanly, and whether one plows deep or shallow.

3) The manner in which the plowed area is determined: whether one measures on individual days how many square rods the hook plow has broken up, or whether one keeps a record of how many hook plows were required for breaking up an entire field and then divides the total square rods of the entire field by the number of hook plows used.

When the first method is applied, regular pieces with long turns are already selected to facilitate measurement; the workers, informed by the preparations that a measurement will take place, are spurred on by competition and the frequent presence of the overseer to push the horses hard and at the same time to take the widest possible furrow slices with ridges; the overseer naturally chooses for such trial measurements fine weather when he is troubled neither by heat nor rain -- and from the combination of all these favorable circumstances come the results on which the claims one not infrequently hears are based, that the hook plow, even on loamy soil, daily accomplishes up to 400 square rods (namely when ridges are left standing).

Since, however, there are not always long turns but headlands and wedge-shaped pieces must also be plowed, the soil often hardens and makes plowing more difficult, competition and supervision are generally lacking, the horses also cannot continuously work under such strain, since time is lost through moving from one field section or from one field to another, through the breaking of a hook plow, as well as through the transition to other work when plowing is interrupted by heavy rain, and since finally the weather is not always fine but frequently impedes and delays the work of men and draft animals through great heat or rain: the comparison with what a hook plow has accomplished on average across the whole field generally shows a difference of 20 to 30 percent.

The statements based on such individual measurements are, in scientific terms -- since they contradict what is actually accomplished in total -- completely worthless and lead, when applied to yield estimates, calculation of required draft animals, determination of the net return of a crop, and so forth, not to truth but to error. Moreover, since with the hook plow, as soon as one does not plow cleanly but leaves ridges standing, furrow slices of any desired width can be taken, the statement of the plowed area, without precise indication of the width and depth of the furrow slices, decides nothing whatsoever about the quantity of work the hook plow performs.

If a comparison between the ordinary hook plow and the improved hook plow with respect to the quantity of work performed is to be made, only the figures determined by the second method may be used as the basis.

From the estate Tellow, the reporter has before him a ten-year labor account from an earlier period, kept by himself. Since this account covers such a long period of time, and the journal as well as the extracts, compilations, and calculations are all in one hand, a communication of the results concerning plowing may not be without value and interest.

It must first be noted, however, that during that time almost always clean plowing was done, that is, no ridges were left standing, which is also why even in the breaking, fallow, and turning furrows, the furrow slices were not taken wider than 12 inches Lubeck measure.

The sum of the field area plowed in the 10 years from Midsummer 1810 to Midsummer 1820, divided by the sum of the hook plows used for this purpose, yields the following day's work of one hook plow for the various furrow types:

- Breaking furrow: 158 square rods. Furrow depth measured on the land side approximately 5 inches, width of furrow slice on average 10 1/2 inches. (The sod breaking is done either entirely in the short autumn days, or if not, the work is reduced to autumn days.)
- Fallow furrow: 258 square rods. Depth of furrow 5 inches, width 11 1/2 inches.
- Turning furrow: 251 square rods. Depth 6 inches, width 12 inches.
- Seed furrow for winter grain: 220 square rods. Depth 4 1/2 inches, width 10 1/2 inches.
- Breaking winter grain stubble: Depth 5 inches, width 12 inches.
- Turning furrow in spring: Depth 6 inches, width 12 inches.
- Covering barley: Depth 4 1/2 inches, width 10 1/2 inches.
- Breaking summer grain stubble: Depth 5 inches, width 11 1/2 inches.
- Covering oats: 266 square rods. Depth 4 1/2 inches, width 10 1/2 inches.

During the period in question, oxen were still kept at Tellow, though not all the plowing was done by them but partly also by horses. According to the individual observations made on this subject, the ox-drawn relay hook plow accomplished somewhat more land in the long summer days but less in the short autumn days than the horse-drawn hook plow. For the entire duration of the plowing season, the work output seemed to be approximately equal.

The Tellow field is, as already mentioned above, hilly and consists of approximately two-thirds wheat soil and one-third rye soil; it is also fairly frequently intersected by ditches, though not to an unusual degree.

On level ground, the hook plow requires far less draft power than on hilly ground; on sandy soil less than on loamy; and on the former, ridges can be left standing and broad furrow slices taken without harm. If there are few or no ditches, the work is greatly advanced by long turns. Now, if all these favorable circumstances combine on a field and the soil is at the same time free of stones, the day's work of a hook plow can rise considerably higher -- not merely according to individual measurements but also on average across entire fields -- than what has been found for Tellow. I do not, however, have accounts from such estates in hand to be able to state the ratio. On the other hand, I possess from two estates with good soil similar accounts as from Tellow, though only for individual furrow types -- and in these, the result shows little deviation from what was found for Tellow.

## Results of the 1836 Records

Now, to determine the quantity of work accomplished with the hook plow on average across entire fields, in the summer of 1836, after a long interruption, records on plowing were kept again for the first time. This yielded the following day's work of the horse-drawn hook plow:

- Breaking furrow in the autumn days: 244 square rods. Depth of horizontal furrow 3 1/2 inches, width 12 1/2 inches.
- Fallow furrow: Depth 4 1/2 inches, width 12 inches.
- Turning furrow: Depth 6 1/2 inches, width 13 inches.
- Seed furrow for rapeseed: Depth 4 1/2 inches, width 12 inches.
- Seed furrow for wheat and rye: Depth 4 1/2 inches, width 12 inches.
- Breaking winter grain stubble: Depth 6 inches, width 12 1/2 inches.
- Turning furrow in spring: Depth 6 1/2 inches, width 13 inches.
- Seed furrow for barley (broadcast): Depth 4 1/2 inches, width 12 inches.
- Breaking summer grain stubble: 240 square rods. Depth 6 inches, width 12 1/2 inches.
- Seed furrow for oats: 327 square rods. Mostly broadcast. When broadcast, depth 4 1/2 inches, width 12 inches. When covered by plowing, 3 1/2 inches depth, width 12 inches.

Here I must note, however, that in the summer of 1836 the diary on plowing was not kept by me personally, and I cannot vouch for the complete accuracy of the account. Since, however, I have personally observed the size of the day's work on many field sections, and these observations nowhere contradict the result of the account, and since I know the record-keeper well and his character inspires my confidence, this account has credibility for me.

If one now sets the day's work of the ordinary hook plow everywhere equal to 100 in order to arrive at an easier overview, the following ratio results:

| Furrow type | Hook plow | Improved hook plow |
|---|---|---|
| Breaking furrow | 100 | 154 |
| Fallow furrow | 100 | 122 |
| Turning furrow | 100 | 130 |
| Seed furrow for winter grain | 100 | 144 |
| Winter grain stubble | 100 | 123 |
| Summer grain stubble | 100 | 130 |
| Seed furrow for barley | 100 | 129 |
| Seed furrow for oats | 100 | 123 |
| Turning furrow in spring | 100 | 123 |
| **Average of the 9 furrow types** | **100** | **129** |

This result, according to which the day's work of the hook plow in area relates to that of the ordinary hook plow on average as 129 to 100, speaks extraordinarily in favor of the hook plow and far exceeds the expectation which the reporter held during its construction.

Perhaps, however, over the average of several years, the day's work of the hook plow will prove somewhat lower, because over a longer period, dry years occur in which the soil is more hardened than in this year.

A further circumstance also comes into consideration here, namely that the field itself has undergone a change. In the period from 1810-20, the loamy hills were still in their raw state, very hard in drought and difficult to plow; now they are for the most part heavily covered with muck and thereby made mellow. As far as this can be determined by estimation, I would assume that given the present state of the field, the day's work of an ordinary hook plow would be about 5 percent greater than in the former period. The ratio between the improved hook plow and the ordinary one would accordingly be 129:105, equal to 123:100, and taking into account the dry years with hardened soil that occur over a longer period, I believe I can now assume as an average that the day's work of the hook plow relates to that of the ordinary hook plow as 120 to 100.

If one adds up the above-stated depth and width of the furrows for the 9 main furrow types, the result is:

- Average depth: Hook plow 5 2/7 inches, width 11 inches.
- Improved hook plow: 5 2/7 inches, width 12 1/2 inches.

The profile of the furrow slice of the hook plow is thus 6 2/7 x 12 5/7 = 62.6 square inches.

When the furrow of the ordinary hook plow is 5 2/7 inches deep on the land side, the depth on the field side is 3 2/7 inches, the average depth thus 4 2/7 inches, and the profile is 4 2/7 x 11 3/7 = 45.5 square inches.

At equal width of furrow slices, and thus also equal plowed areas, the mass of earth broken up by the ordinary hook plow and the improved hook plow is in the ratio of 4 2/7 to 5 2/7, or 100 to 125.

The ratio of furrow width between the ordinary and improved hook plow is 11 7/10 to 12 7/10 = 100:110, or the furrow slices of the latter instrument are on average 10 percent wider than those of the ordinary hook plow.

This, however, by no means explains how the day's work of the improved hook plow can be 26 percent greater than that of the ordinary hook plow. But if such a ratio does in fact present itself in reality, then the hook plow must possess still other properties, or there must be circumstances and arrangements in its use that advance the work so greatly, and it is well worth the trouble to seek these out and learn to understand them.

## Explaining the Greater Productivity

1) The furrow drawn by the hook plow is so level and wide that draft animals and plowman can walk comfortably in it, instead of having a difficult and tiring walk in the pointed, half-collapsed furrow of the ordinary hook plow. A person is no more fatigued by a walk of 6 miles on level firm ground than by 4 miles on plowed field. When plowing with oxen in a continuous circle so that one animal always walks on plowed field, that animal visibly loses weight. With the first hook plow used here, it was found -- as already mentioned -- that the horses pulling the ordinary hook plow could not keep pace with those pulling the improved hook plow, even though the latter instrument broke up a mass of earth at least 20 percent greater. The speed of draft and the fatigue of the draft animals therefore depends not solely on the size of the load to be drawn, but perhaps equally on the difficulty the animals must overcome in walking.

It is remarkable that this is so little heeded in Mecklenburg that even where plowing is done with horses, the belly hook plow -- in which one horse walks on the plowed field -- is still frequently used. But if the reporter is not mistaken, it is precisely in the use of this instrument that the reason lies why in recent times on so many estates plowing with horses has been abandoned and oxen have been acquired again.

2) Since the hook plow leaves no ridges standing and cuts the furrow slice horizontally, it is entirely unnecessary to plow crosswise, that is, across the first-drawn furrows, at the second working. This produces a significant gain in two ways: first, one can select the longest turns that the field allows, and second, the time-consuming measuring and furrowing of new ridges is saved, since one can plow into the furrow left by the earlier tillage and there lay the center ridge of the new section.

3) The Mecklenburg hook plow is manufactured by the wheelwrights on the estates, who are not accustomed to any precision, not according to fixed dimensions but by eye; at least, the reporter found the ratio between the length and height of the moldboard, and consequently also the angle that the moldboard forms with the beam, to be different on every hook plow. For the hook share too, there is no fixed measure, and the blacksmith gives it size and form at his pleasure. With this mode of origin, it is natural that hook plows made by the same craftsman turn out very unequal and sometimes work well but more often poorly. Now the hook plow has the peculiarity that the operator has great control over it, and that a faulty hook plow can still be made to deliver tolerable work through skilled handling; but since this is connected with unceasing exertion for the plowman, he seeks to relieve himself of it through continuous corrections to the hook plow. From this arises the adjusting of the hook plow, which occurs so frequently and requires such an expenditure of time that when 10 or 12 hook plows are in the field, it seldom happens that one of them is not standing still for adjustment. If one could precisely state the time that adjustment takes away daily and calculated this over the entire duration of plowing, one would be astonished at how costly it is that one shuns the expense of having the instrument made by a skilled craftsman. If one reckons the time spent on adjustment at one-quarter of the working time, the working days of the hook plow annually at 120, and the duration of the hook plow at 5 years, this amounts to the day's work of 30 men and 60 horses.

The improved hook plow, by contrast, is far less in the operator's control; incorrectly set, it does not work at all; correctly set, however, very comfortably for the operator. Since the hook plows which the reporter owns all have the same dimensions, taken from a particularly well-working specimen, the main cause of adjustment is eliminated; however, this would not last long -- since everyone who cannot plow believes the fault lies not with himself but with the instrument -- if everyone had the freedom to make changes at will.

It is therefore forbidden here for any plowman to adjust anything on his instrument except what concerns the width and depth of the furrow. If his hook plow does not work well, he must turn to the lead plowman and plow with his horses while the lead plowman puts the instrument back in order. The sharpened hook shares coming from the smithy are also all fitted by the lead plowman, since so much depends on their correct setting. To prevent the interchanging of hook shares, the number of the hook plow to which it belongs is stamped on each hook share. In this way, adjusting has almost entirely ceased, and the time that was formerly wasted on it is now almost entirely devoted to work.

One further modification that has been made, which has a not insignificant influence on the quantity of work, must be mentioned here. Previously, the hook shares were, in accordance with the general custom, re-plated only once a year, namely in spring, and since a constant wearing away of the iron occurs through use, the hook shares became increasingly narrower over the course of the summer, so that in autumn, during the seed and breaking furrows, only furrow slices of 10 1/2 inches in width could be taken if no ridges were to remain. The shares of the improved hook plow, on the other hand, are re-plated twice, namely once in spring and then again before the harvest, and the blacksmith must each time restore the normal form precisely determined by a model. With the improved hook plow, therefore, broad furrow slices can still be cleanly plowed even in late autumn. The effect of this modification, however, is already expressed in the above-stated width of furrow slices and does not come into further consideration here.

The accelerations of work mentioned under points 2 and 3, however, take place at the expense of the horses' rest time; the most essential and decisive factor in the comparison of the two instruments is therefore whether the horses, when they perform that quantity of work with the improved hook plow, are not more strained than formerly when using the ordinary hook plow.

In this respect, the result here in the two summers of 1835 and 1836, according to my observations and the testimony of all the plowmen, has been that the horses which pulled the hook plow throughout the summer are in autumn in rather better, or at least equally good, condition as in the earlier years when using the ordinary hook plow.

## Comparison with the Plow

The advantage of comfortable walking and the possibility of taking long turns, the plow shares with the hook plow, and if the plow did not require more draft power than the ordinary hook plow, a considerably larger area would have to be plowable with it than with the latter, which, however, according to observations so far -- except in sod breaking -- is by no means the case. Also, the experiments with the dynamometer conducted here in the spring of 1835 showed that both the Morton and the Small plows, at equal profile of the furrow slice, required significantly more draft power than the ordinary horse-drawn hook plow. These experiments, however, are not conclusive owing to the imperfection of the dynamometer used.

Indeed, no one would think of splitting wood with a wedge having the shape of English plows, because obviously a greater force is required for that than when using a wedge with flat side surfaces. Should not a similar difference, in favor of the straight wedge, exist in splitting a strip of earth?

The moldboard of the English plows has received its artfully curved, even backward-bent form in order to produce a turning of the cut earth strip. But if, as with the hook plow, the turning of the earth strip can be achieved by the mere falling from an inclined plane, then the lesser expenditure of force that the straight wedge requires compared to the curved one is a net saving.

Besides the wedge-shaped penetration, the hook plow has the following properties that reduce resistance:

1) The plow body comes into contact with the earth only through two narrow strips of iron, 1 1/2 inches wide, whereby the friction compared to the plow, which presents a much larger contact surface to the earth, is greatly reduced.

2) The hook share, in cutting the earth strip from the land side, penetrates at a much more acute angle than the coulter of the plow and therefore requires less draft power.

3) This cutting side of the hook share is immovable in its position relative to the beam and plow body, while the coulter is easily displaced, causing the earth to be split doubly -- once by the coulter and again by the plow body itself.

In German plows, the moldboard generally forms a flat surface; but here the transition between share and moldboard is not mediated at all; the earth strip, when it leaves the share, must be driven up against the vertically standing wall of the moldboard, and these plows therefore -- and because they generally have a wooden moldboard causing more friction -- require far more draft power than the English plows.*

*) Bailey's treatise on the construction of the plow according to mathematical principles -- translated into German under the pompous title "The Best Possible Plow!" -- has through its mathematical calculations an appearance of reliability and definitiveness. Upon closer examination, however, one finds that most calculations are based on the proposition that the plow strip is an inflexible mass joined into a solid block which, like a brick, no longer exerts pressure on the moldboard once its center of gravity is supported. This, however, almost never occurs in practice and therefore permits no application to ordinary conditions. The form of Bailey's moldboard, however, was found by a purely empirical route and merely produces the most uniform possible wearing of all parts of the moldboard, which is a very subordinate advantage. The main task -- "to find a form of moldboard at which the turning of the earth strip takes place with the least possible expenditure of force" -- has not even been attempted to be solved through mathematical calculus.

Among all plows known to the reporter, the Belgian plow, with its beautifully rising share and its only slightly curved moldboard, seems to require the least draft power. This plow has been in use here only for a short time, however, because the craftsmen did not know how to rebuild it.

## Comparison with the Ordinary Hook Plow

The ordinary hook plow shares with the improved hook plow the splitting of the earth strip by means of a straight wedge, and the question now presses itself forward: how can such a much greater quantity of work be accomplished with the latter without the draft animals being more strained?

The main advantages that the improved hook plow has in this respect are:

1) that it cuts the earth strip rather than breaking it loose;
2) that through the cast-iron moldboard and the two strips of cast iron attached to the beam, the earth everywhere comes into contact only with iron, never with wood, and friction is thereby very considerably reduced;
3) that the horses have a comfortable and secure walk in the horizontal, open furrow.

It would be interesting and instructive if the share that each of the three stated circumstances has of the total effect could be determined and specified. If one knew, for example, the influence that the iron moldboard has on reducing friction, and it were found that the saving in draft power were worth far more than the cost of acquiring and maintaining it, then it would be expedient to cover the moldboards of the ordinary hook plows with iron plates as well. If it were known how much more the horses are strained by walking in the furrow, it could be determined whether it might not be advantageous to replace the belly hook plow with the hook plow with forecarriage, even though the latter undeniably requires more draft power than the former.

For such a sharp separation, however, data are still lacking, and the following can only be regarded as a preliminary attempt.

From a series of experiments conducted in earlier years at Flottbeck with the dynamometer during plowing in sandy soil, it was found that the Small plow, equipped with a coulter, required on average of 19 recordings one hundredweight of draft power for every 28.3 square inches of plow strip profile. Then the coulter was removed, and now on average of 16 recordings, one hundredweight of draft power was required for every 24.6 square inches of profile. For 28.3 square inches, therefore, 28.3/24.6 = 1.15 hundredweight was required. At equal profile size, the ratio of required draft power between the plow with coulter and the one without was thus 1:1.15, or 100:115. Removing the coulter therefore increased the resistance by 15 percent.

Now, it cannot be demonstrated that the increase in resistance from breaking the earth loose with the ordinary hook plow, compared to cutting the earth with the improved hook plow, stands in the same ratio as with the Small plow without coulter to the one equipped with a coulter; but no case is known to me that would be more analogous, and I therefore cite it here. Since, however, breaking the earth loose in mellow soil and in shallow plowing requires little expenditure of force, I believe I may not reckon more than 10 percent additional required draft power for this on average across all furrow types.

The resistance arising from the friction of the earth on the moldboard and on the sole of the hook plow or plow depends essentially on the greater or lesser roughness of the parts that come into contact with the earth. If the earth is completely dry, the friction on smoothly planed or use-polished wood may not be considerably greater than on cast iron. But as soon as the earth is moist, it adheres firmly to wood -- but not to iron -- and since earth then rubs on earth, the resistance through friction is increased to a very high degree and the progress of the instrument is made uncertain at the same time. Insofar as mere observations can be decisive here, and insofar as a calculation made about the force required to push a mass of earth up an inclined plane is applicable here, I would assume that by covering the moldboard and sole with cast iron, the resistance that the hook plow offers during draft is reduced on average over the whole year by one-quarter, or at least by one-fifth.

If one accepts these figures provisionally as correct, until they are more precisely determined through manifold experiments with the dynamometer, it is thereby explicable how, with equal expenditure of force by the draft animals, a so much larger area can be broken up with the improved hook plow than with the ordinary one. Since, however, at equal plowed area, the mass of earth that the improved hook plow breaks up is on average one-quarter larger than that of the ordinary hook plow because of the horizontally cut furrow slice, and the one-quarter enlarged mass of earth requires at least a one-eighth increase in draft power, the two cited causes alone are not sufficient to explain the greater work output of the improved hook plow; rather, still other favorably operating circumstances must be present.

One such favorable circumstance is that the horses have a much more comfortable walk in the horizontal, open furrow than in the pointed, half-collapsed furrow of the ordinary hook plow, and that the horses, now walking on level ground, can pull a larger load at the same speed or an equal load at greater speed than on uneven ground, without being more strained.

Other reasons the reporter has not yet discovered, and he must therefore assume that the last-mentioned circumstance is of greater influence than is commonly believed. The workers too, who can best observe this, place great value on it, and an intelligent farmhand, whom I recently asked whether, if he considered only his horses, he would rather work with the ordinary hook plow or the improved one, answered: "Then I would take the improved hook plow, because the horses have such a comfortable walk with it."

Walking on plowed field, however, is much more tiring than walking in the hook plow furrow, and if this circumstance proves to have such great influence on the performance of the draft animals, this might well prompt an examination of whether, with the yoke hook plow -- in which one draft animal walks on the plowed field -- far more is lost in speed of draft than is saved in draft power by the hook plow not requiring a forecarriage with this mode of hitching. That oxen in a short yoke, pulling on a chain, can walk before the hook plow with forecarriage as well as before the plow in the furrow, surely admits of no doubt.

The forecarriage can, however, be dispensed with not only by the hook pole laid in the yoke, but also by an arrangement similar to that of the English plows.

That the hook plow could be converted into a swing plow while retaining the essential parts of its construction had seemed probable to the reporter for some time, and after multiple attempts, one was completed last spring; however, it does not take furrow slices as wide as the hook plow. This swing hook plow probably requires even less draft power than the hook plow and is at the same time, owing to the saving of the forecarriage, nearly half as cheap.

Since, however, with swing plows the direction of the plow is changed by every irregular movement of the horses and the draft is made unsteady, the farmhands therefore shy away from driving the horses, and these become accustomed to a slow pace; also, every small stone in the path deflects the plow from its course. I therefore do not consider the use of swing plows advantageous for the conditions here. Only in countries where the workers are accustomed from youth to handling them, where skill in plowing is considered a matter of honor among farmhands, and where at the same time the soil is entirely free of stones -- there the swing plow may have the advantage over a good wheeled plow.

On the other hand, the attempts to convert the hook plow by laying a long pole into a yoke hook plow have so far always failed. However, I must confess that I have devoted little attention to this mode of hitching. The experiment here had only the purpose of making the hook plow also applicable to covering potatoes by plowing, and thereby making the ordinary hook plow entirely superfluous.

## Harrowing the Field Broken Up with the Hook Plow

The reporter hoped that through the complete cutting and turning of the sod, which the hook plow accomplishes, the work of harrowing in the breaking and fallow furrows would be greatly reduced. But as much as the reporter's expectations regarding the quantity of work that the hook plow accomplishes have been exceeded, his hopes in this regard have been disappointed.

The sod broken up in the autumn of 1835 with the hook plow was, although the clover had come up poorly, nevertheless very turfed and tough, and -- what here otherwise almost never occurs -- in several places strongly overgrown with couch grass.

In the spring of 1836, the two-horse harrows -- whose tines, admittedly, were already very blunt -- could not tear loose and break through the sod furrows, which had settled and compacted during the winter. When the fallow furrow was given crosswise, the sod strips were divided into squares which, however, since they were over a foot wide and long and had mostly been completely turned over by the hook plow, once again could not all be torn loose and broken up by the harrows. The field therefore had to be partly turned twice, and nevertheless the couch grass and grass roots were by far not as completely destroyed in the fallow as had been the case in all previous years.

Here it must be mentioned, however, that the rainy weather of this summer, and especially the autumn, contributed substantially to this, and that on many fields that were worked only with the ordinary hook plow, the fallow field likewise did not become clean.

In any case, however, it has been found that the harrows customary here are not capable of penetrating and breaking up the broad, flat-lying sod strips. The reporter is therefore now having triangular harrows with forward-curved cutting tines made, which are fastened by screws and can be sharpened at any time, and hopes that these will accomplish the cutting and breaking up of the turf.

The observation presents itself here of how closely linked one thing is with another in the customary farming system, how every change has another change as its necessary consequence.

Just as in the affairs of state, the introduction of what is truly better can become ineffective or even harmful if everything that stands in relation to it -- but is difficult to recognize in advance -- is not simultaneously changed, so it is also in trades and in agriculture.

In mellow soil, harrowing presents different phenomena. The harrows penetrate deeply into the loosened soil and bring the clods and whatever rootwork may still be found in the topsoil to the surface. If the earth was moist and compact during breaking, the hook plow produces larger clods than the ordinary hook plow, and the harrow and roller then have more work in breaking them down. If, on the other hand, the field is broken up with the hook plow in a dry but not yet hardened condition, the field acquires an almost garden-like appearance; the work of harrowing is then limited to mere leveling and sinks to insignificance.

If the field has been deeply plowed with the hook plow in dry conditions, the horses sink deeper into the very loosened soil than after plowing with the ordinary hook plow, making the work of harrowing more tiring for the horses.

In the comparison between the ordinary hook plow and the improved one, the following questions further present themselves:

1) Will not the soil worked with the ordinary hook plow, because it presents a larger surface to the atmosphere, also reach a higher degree of activity and therefore, given equal richness of soil, yield a higher return in the first harvest than the soil worked with the improved hook plow?

2) Does not a larger portion of humus escape through volatilization as the activity of the soil increases, and does not a large part of the manure -- especially sheep manure -- which the ordinary hook plow buries so very imperfectly, escape in gaseous form into the air? Will not, therefore, the field worked with the plow or the improved hook plow be richer in plant nutrients in the second and third rotation than the one always cultivated with the ordinary hook plow?

3) Is it a matter of indifference for the thriving of plants whether the soil is loosened to 6 inches in some places and only 4 inches in others, or whether the entire topsoil is uniformly worked to a depth of 6 inches? Is it furthermore indifferent whether the manure and humus are mostly concentrated in the upper 3-4 inches of the topsoil, or uniformly distributed through the entire topsoil?

4) Should not the great looseness that the hook plow imparts to the soil be utilized to save a pass with certain crops? Should not, in particular, the seed furrow after a preceding deep turning furrow be replaced by covering the seed with the extirpator, or even with harrows that have deeply penetrating tines?

The answering of these questions requires longer experience. For the present, I would be able to give only conjectures but no facts, and so the discussion of this subject must be deferred to a future time.

If an inherently dry subject has been treated here with such detail, this can find its justification only in the importance of the matter. Now, in every country the work of plowing constitutes a considerable part of the national labor, and every saving therein therefore has an essential influence on the increase of the national income.

## Cost Analysis

In the 10 years from Midsummer 1810 to Midsummer 1820, the estate Tellow used in total for plowing: 9,042 men, 3,638 horses, and 28,895 oxen, calculated on a daily basis. On average, therefore, 904.3 hook plows per day were used annually.

According to the calculation communicated in the Mecklenburg Annals (16th volume, p. 667), the costs of plowing with a team of four horses, including the farmhand, are:

a. In spring and summer: 1 Reichsthaler 15.4 shillings, plus a helper: 2 shillings. 2 hook plows cost daily: 1 Reichsthaler 37.4 shillings. This makes for one hook plow: 42.7 shillings.

b. In autumn, after completion of the sowing: 1 Reichsthaler 33 shillings, the helper: 2 shillings. 2 hook plows cost: This makes for 1 hook plow: [calculated below].

Of the plowing work, approximately two-thirds falls in spring and summer and one-third in autumn. 2 hook plows in summer cost: 1 Reichsthaler 37.4 shillings. 1 hook plow in autumn costs: 32.6 shillings. 3 hook plows cost together. This amounts for the daily work of one hook plow on average over the whole year to 39 1/8 shillings.

Since 904.3 hook plows per day were used in the year, the total costs of plowing for this estate amount annually to 904.3 x 39 1/8 = 741 Reichsthalers.

If now, according to experience so far, the day's work of the improved hook plow relates to that of the ordinary hook plow in area as 120 to 100, or as 6 to 5*, then through the application of the improved hook plow on this single estate, 1/6 of 741 Reichsthalers = 123 Reichsthalers 24 shillings are saved annually on the costs of plowing.

*) Where one generally plows with ridges and takes furrow slices 15 inches wide, the improved hook plow naturally cannot break up a larger area than the ordinary hook plow, but only deliver better work.

The size of the arable land (which has been successively changed through forest plantings) averaged during that period 158,800 square rods. The costs of plowing therefore amounted per Last of arable land (of 6,000 Mecklenburg square rods = 51 Magdeburg Morgen) to 741 x 6/158.8 = 28 Reichsthalers, and the saving effected by the improved hook plow is 1/6 x 28 = 4 Reichsthalers 32 shillings.

Whether, on the other hand, the costs of harrowing the field broken up with the improved hook plow will also be permanently higher, even after the adoption of more suitable harrows, than with the ordinary hook plow cannot yet be stated with certainty. It is probable, however, that with the width of the plow strip, the work of harrowing required to break them up also increases.

## Practical Advice on Introduction

Even though the experiences communicated here speak in favor of the improved hook plow, I cannot advise a sudden transition from the ordinary hook plow to the improved one, but must rather warn against it.

On the occasion of the introduction of the hook plow on other estates, my old coachman said: "If the people don't want it, the masters will accomplish nothing," and experience has already confirmed this many times.

The handling of the hook plow is indeed easier, the walk behind it more comfortable than with the ordinary hook plow, and anyone who has become familiar with handling a correctly set instrument of this kind will not voluntarily exchange it for the ordinary hook plow again. But at first glance, the workers, especially the horse servants, are alarmed by the great mass of earth that the hook plow turns over. Because the instrument breaks up so much earth, they think it must be hard on the horses, and through a curious but not uncommon chain of reasoning, the opinion is immediately elevated to a fact, and the opinion itself then proved again by appeal to the fact.

If the director of the farm does not have the time or interest to devote his attention primarily to this matter during the initial introduction, or if he does not have the expertise to overcome the difficulties that undeniably present themselves at first, then the instrument wanders into the rust chamber.

Also, continuous attention and supervision must be directed at the blacksmiths, who find it inconvenient to maintain the hook share constantly in the prescribed form; left to themselves, they gradually return to the customary and for them more convenient form of the old hook share, and the instrument then becomes unusable. That the blacksmiths should receive a small compensation for the greater precision of the work is only fair; here the blacksmith receives for this and for the twice-yearly re-plating of the hook shares one bushel of rye per hook plow.

To win the workers over to the instrument and to prepare for its general introduction, it seems necessary first to let one hook plow work during a summer alongside the other ordinary hook plows.

The single hook plow, however, does not then show the advantages that its application on a large scale provides; the farmhand who operates the hook plow will not outpace the other plowmen but will use the lightness of draft to spare his horses; the long turns cannot be preferentially selected but the hook plow must also be used crosswise, and so forth. If the single hook plow is used in the sod breaking among the other ordinary hook plows, it can even become disadvantageous, since the sod strip turned by the hook plow is almost impenetrable for the ordinary hook plows during cross-plowing, or at least requires excessive exertion of the draft animals.

I would further advise, at the first application of the hook plow for the preparation of the spring sowing, not to plow deeply either the turning furrow or the seed furrow, since with deep plowing -- even when no deepening of the topsoil takes place -- too large a part of the lower topsoil containing little humus is brought to the surface and remains in contact with the air too short a time before sowing. But if the field has already been deeply broken once in autumn or in the fallow, a deep turning furrow in spring -- if only it does not exceed the limit of the topsoil -- will do no further harm.

## Specification of Some Main Dimensions of the Hook Plow

It is not my intention to provide a complete description of the hook plow, since this, even accompanied by the best drawings, would almost never suffice to have a specimen manufactured accordingly. I must therefore presuppose knowledge of the ordinary Mecklenburg horse hook plow with forecarriage, and will give only the dimensions of those parts of the hook plow that differ from those of the ordinary one.

Beam, crummel, hook pole, and handle are joined together in the same manner as with the ordinary hook plow and must lie in one plane.

If this frame -- the beam downward -- is set up vertically on a level floor and the hook pole is cut to a length of 6 feet, the lower tip of the hook pole must be 3 feet above the floor; the upper tip of the endpoint of the handle is usually given a height of 3 feet 2 inches; this, however, is not a fixed measure, since it must be changed according to the size of the plowman if the handling is to be comfortable for him.

The mortise in the crummel, into which the tenon of the moldboard fits, must be placed at a height such that a perpendicular dropped from the point where the moldboard touches the crummel above to the floor measures exactly 18 inches. The length of the moldboard, which needs to be only 10 to 12 inches wide, must be exactly 30 inches; from this, the lower part is cut to form the tongue onto which the hook share is slid, which must have a length of 10 inches and a width of 6 inches.

The beam is cut at the front so that the endpoint of the 30-inch-long moldboard resting on it touches the floor. The angle that the moldboard and the sole of the beam form with each other under this condition is 37 degrees.

The length of the beam is indeterminate, since it depends on the greater or lesser curvature of the hook crummel; it must be 4 inches.

The moldboard has, as already mentioned earlier, an inclination to the left side, and it forms with the plane laid through the crummel and beam an angle of 76 degrees.* This inclined position is given to the moldboard by cutting the tenon of the moldboard, which fits into the mortise of the crummel, at an angle so that it forms the angle of 76 degrees with the moldboard itself.

*) In the position in which the hook plow is held during work, with the hook share making a horizontal cut, the plane laid through the crummel and beam forms (according to the average of several measurements) an angle of 67 degrees with the horizon. If one extends the plane of the moldboard until it meets the horizon, it forms with it an angle of 180 - (76 + 67) = 37 degrees, thus precisely the same angle that the moldboard and beam form with each other. At this angle of inclination, the sides of the right-angled triangle that results when a perpendicular is dropped from the endpoint of the inclined plane have the rational ratio 3:4:5.

Since the magnitude of the twofold inclination of the moldboard was not derived from theory but was found only after almost innumerable trials and modifications -- only that inclination at which the work became most perfect was adopted and retained, and only then was the magnitude of the angles of inclination sought out -- it is indeed striking and remarkable that for both inclinations of the moldboard -- forward and to the left side -- one and the same angle results, and that this angle is precisely the one at which the sides of the right-angled triangle have a rational ratio to one another.

When the tenon of the moldboard is fitted into the crummel, the lower end of the moldboard is connected to the beam by a nail; this nail, however, must be strong and long, for if it gives way, the plow breaks. A screw might be even more suitable here.

The cast-iron moldboard plate is then screwed to the moldboard with 4 screws.

The form and dimensions of the moldboard plate, as well as of the hook share, are shown in the accompanying drawing.*

*) It is remarkable how sensitive the instrument is to every change in the width of the moldboard plate. The first, broader moldboard plate that was cast threw the earth too far away and made the draft heavier; a second, narrower one let the earth fall too soon and gave no open furrow; and only the moldboard plate cast according to a fourth model met the requirements.

The line h.d. from the tip of the hook share k. to d., the point where the upper side of the moldboard touches the middle of the crummel, shows what position the moldboard and hook share must have relative to the beam and crummel.

The most difficult part is cutting the tongue onto which the hook share is slid so that the moldboard and hook share obtain the correct position. By following the prescription below, however, this is achieved.

To the left of d. and 2 1/2 inches from it, another point r is taken; from this point, a straight line is drawn to the middle of the tip of the beam. This line, which bisects the line k.l. and stands perpendicular to it, gives the direction and at the same time the center of the tongue, which is then cut 10 inches long and 6 inches wide.

The hook share, with its head 14 1/2 inches high, projects, when it is slid onto the 10-inch-long tongue, 4 1/2 inches above the moldboard. If it lay flat against it from the start, its tip would penetrate too deeply into the earth; a small wedge must therefore first be inserted, which is removed again when the hook share has lost some of its length through wear.

When the hook share has been fitted, the frame is bent over to the left side so that the left side of the hook share touches the floor everywhere and supports the plow itself. In this position of the plow, the tip c of the hook share must be 9 1/2 inches and the tip q of the moldboard 19 1/2 inches above the floor. If in the manufacture of the instrument all the stated dimensions have been precisely followed, this is at the same time the position in which the hook plow is held during work. Then the lower sharp edge of the beam, on which the plow rests in this position, is cut away far enough that a surface 1 1/2 inches wide is formed, which fits flush everywhere against the level floor. A groove is then cut into this surface, 1 1/2 inches wide and 1/2 inch deep at the back but tapering to nothing at the front; into this groove, the sole plate, which is 2 feet 10 inches long, 1 1/2 inches wide, 1/2 inch thick at the back and 1/4 inch thick at the front, is screwed in.

The other edge on the land side of the beam is blunted in the same manner but vertically, and grooved along its entire length 1/4 inch deep and 1 1/2 inches wide, and then covered with an iron strip of the same length and width as the sole plate but of uniform 1/4-inch thickness.

The two iron strips then project 1/4 inch above the wood, and of the entire plow body, only these two narrow strips of iron come into contact with the earth.

The ordinary forecarriage of the horse hook plow is retained for the improved hook plow, except that the discs on which the yoke rests are placed vertically instead of strongly overhanging.

The chain connecting the forecarriage with the plow is wrapped around the crummel and forms a knot that can be shifted. If the knot is on the right side of the crummel, this pulls the plow over toward the land side; on the other hand, the mass of earth resting on the moldboard pushes the plow over toward the field side. Both forces thus act against each other, and through a skillful positioning of the knot of the chain, it can be arranged that both forces balance each other and the plowman can guide the instrument without exertion. The deeper the plow goes, the greater the mass of earth pressing on the moldboard, the more the knot of the chain must be pushed upward on the right side of the crummel to counterbalance the pressure of the earth.

The hook share must be set so that a line bisecting the underside of the beam, if extended, meets the tip of the hook share. This is an indispensable condition for the regular running of the instrument. Since, however, the center of the beam is not exactly discernible after the sole plate has been fitted, this line must be drawn beforehand and its endpoints marked by driven-in nails.

### Note

The master joiner Geest at Tenze, postal station Gnoien, manufactures the hook plow on his own account for 11 1/2 Reichsthalers Mecklenburg currency. I therefore request those gentlemen who may be inclined to acquire a hook plow to apply not to me but directly to the said master joiner. Until now, the hook plows manufactured by him have for the most part first been tested here, and the defects found in his work have then been remedied by him before the instrument was dispatched. As long as the orders do not accumulate too much, I will gladly continue this, but cannot permanently promise it.

In case the use of the hook plow should spread significantly, it would be desirable for a skilled craftsman in each of the larger cities of Mecklenburg to devote himself exclusively or chiefly to the manufacture of this instrument; for only through competition can good work and fair prices be secured. I will gladly give such a craftsman, at his request, whatever closer information and instruction may be desired.

On the other hand, I absolutely cannot recommend having this instrument manufactured by estate wheelwrights, who as a rule work only by eye and not according to precisely specified measurements, in order to save costs. I am so firmly convinced that the cost saving in this point is a waste that I no longer have my own wheelwright -- who after all manufactured the first specimens of the hook plow himself -- make any more; and he himself does not trust himself to work according to such precisely specified dimensions.

Moreover, I have also found that in finer work, the skilled craftsman produces in one day as much and better work as the estate wheelwright, accustomed to manifold, rough, and imprecise work, does in 3 days, so that the cost saving is more apparent than real.

If 120 days a year are worked with a hook plow and the day's work of the hook plow, according to the calculation demonstrated above, costs 39 1/8 shillings, then the costs of the work performed with one hook plow in a year amount to 120 x 39 1/8 shillings = 98 Reichsthalers 16 shillings. If through the incorrect or imprecise construction of the instrument the quantity of work, or even the quality and value of the work, is diminished by one-tenth, this causes an annual loss of nearly 10 Reichsthalers.

## Concluding Remarks

In North America, plows are manufactured on a factory scale according to the best models, whereby alongside the quality and uniformity of the product, the lowest possible price is also achieved.

Since progress in agriculture, as in manufacturing, goes hand in hand with the improvement of tools and machines, and is in many respects conditioned by them, the progress of the Americans would have been very much slower if they had begun their cultivation of the soil with the crudely and imprecisely made tools that are still customary in a large part of the Old World -- though not in England and Belgium.

The class of tradesmen devoted exclusively to the manufacture of agricultural implements does not yet exist in Mecklenburg, unfortunately, but must first be developed.

Some trades that naturally belong in the countryside have been forcibly squeezed into the towns in our country. On the other hand, the trade of manufacturing agricultural implements, which is now scattered across the entire countryside, belongs, in my conviction, in the towns, because only with a larger market area can several persons devote themselves solely to this business, because the craftsmen who must work together on it live together there, and because only through competition can good work and fair prices be obtained.
