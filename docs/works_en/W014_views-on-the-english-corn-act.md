# Views on the Effects of the English Corn Act on Mecklenburg

*Original: Ansichten über die Wirkung der englischen Korn-Acte auf Mecklenburg (1819)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

By virtue of the English Corn Act, no foreign wheat may be sold in England unless the quarter exceeds 80 English shillings (approximately 2 Thaler, 40 florins per Rostock bushel).

With completely free corn trade, after a rich harvest the corn price will fall everywhere, and the farmers, or in their place the merchants, will store part of the harvest in expectation of higher prices during future poor harvests.

The English Corn Act has entirely changed this relationship. In abundant years, England grows as much corn as it needs, and foreigners are then excluded from the English market.

Whereas formerly the English farmer as well as the Mecklenburg farmer would store part of a rich harvest, now the Englishman can sell off his entire harvest; but the Mecklenburger, insofar as he produces corn for England, must store his entire harvest. If, under these changed circumstances, the Mecklenburg farmer were still to refrain from all speculation and bring his entire harvest to market every year, then such a large capital would need to be available in the hands of the merchants as would suffice to buy the entire surplus of a harvest and let it sit for one or several years. Such a large trading capital, however, cannot be expected in Mecklenburg: for, firstly, the corn trade has hitherto not required such considerable outlay, and secondly, the capital devoted to the corn trade has been greatly diminished through large losses in the previous year.

If, under such circumstances, the farmers nonetheless insist on selling the entire surplus of their harvest, then the corn price must fall so low that the trading capital suffices to buy the entire marketable harvest.

The very low price brought about in this way is, however, neither natural nor grounded in European conditions.

Through the Corn Act, England's need for corn from abroad does not cease, and England will — at least for some decades yet — still need the corn of those countries that have hitherto produced for the English market; only with the difference that it now takes nothing in abundant years, but in years of its own poor harvests takes away at once the accumulated stock of several harvests. England will then, however, not merely pay us the previous average prices, but must also pay the costs of multi-year storage: for only on the condition that agriculture remains just as profitable as before can foreign countries produce as much corn for England as they have done hitherto. This is also already evident from the fact that at the time when the sale of foreign wheat in England is permitted, the price of corn is nearly double our average price; and we must therefore receive more than that average amounts to.

Later, the trading capital will certainly increase through greater participation of foreign merchants in the local corn trade and will become adequate to the demand. But so long as this has not yet occurred, it remains of the utmost importance for the farmer to store his corn and wait for a more favorable conjuncture. When the price for perfectly good, sound corn has fallen to two-thirds of the average price, storing the corn can bring the farmer no loss, but rather considerable gain; for experience has taught that corn never remains persistently below the average price, but always rises again to it and above it.

This requires, for each farmer, a disposable capital in order to be able to forgo the income from corn for one or two years. This capital could easily have been formed from the surplus of the three past abundant years. But with the acquisition of this capital, not all difficulties are removed: for firstly, there is seldom enough storage space on a single estate to store such a large quantity of corn, and secondly, all estates that are five or more miles from the trading town are compelled — because otherwise their teams would have nothing to do in one year, yet in the following year could not transport the harvest of two years — to cart their corn every year. The only expedient remaining, therefore, is to make an arrangement with a merchant in the trading town, according to which the merchant provides the necessary storage space for a reasonable price and undertakes the supervision of the corn for a fixed commission. There is reason to fear, however, that the merchant, finding the business too trifling, will not agree to such an arrangement with individual estates.

It would therefore be desirable that several farmers enter into an association and jointly declare that they are prepared to conclude such contracts.

If in this way the business becomes large enough for the merchant, and through the agreed terms he can draw a somewhat larger profit from it than when he purchases corn on behalf of foreign merchants, then there can be little doubt that one merchant or another will be found willing to conclude such contracts.
