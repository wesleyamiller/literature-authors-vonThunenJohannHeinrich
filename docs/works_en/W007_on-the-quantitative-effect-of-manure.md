# On the Quantitative Effect of Manure and on the Exhaustive Power of Crops

*Original: Ueber die quantitative Wirkung des Dungs und über die Aussaugungskraft der Gewächse (1821)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

## Foreword

Since this first section of a work — which I may perhaps not complete for some years yet — constitutes, to a certain extent, a self-contained whole, I take the liberty of presenting this fragment to the readers of the Annals for their examination and judgment.

## First Section

### General Principles and Views

#### Section 1

The experience of all times and all countries teaches that grain, when cultivated on the same spot for several years in succession, declines in yield from year to year; but that this field, when it is then manured, thereby regains its original fertility.

From this we must conclude:

1) that manure serves as nourishment for the grain;
2) that the soil is deprived of a portion of its manure content through the production of a grain harvest, and that these crops therefore possess the ability to exhaust the soil, that is, to rob it of its nutritive substance.

#### Section 2

If for two grain fields, of identical physical composition of the soil and sown with the same type of grain, all forces and circumstances acting upon vegetation are equal, and only the manure content of the fields is unequal: then the difference in yield of the two fields can only result from the difference in their manure content. The greater yield of the one field is the effect of its greater manure content; and if one can express this in numbers, then the quantitative effect of manure is thereby determinable.

If, for example, a piece of arable land of 200 square rods, which has entirely uniform soil, is divided into two equal parts, and one half is then treated with 5 cartloads of cow manure at 2,000 pounds each, while the other half is left unmanured, and then both parts are tilled and sown uniformly and simultaneously: then the greater yield of the manured piece is the effect of 5 cartloads of manure.

Suppose now that the experiments, continued over several years, had produced the result that the yield of the unmanured piece averaged 6, and that of the manured piece 9 Berlin bushels of rye: then we would say: 5 cartloads of manure produce, on this soil and with this cultivation of the field, 3 Berlin bushels of rye.

#### Section 3

Since the unmanured piece has yielded 6 bushels, and since 5 cartloads of manure are required for the production of 3 bushels, one can further conclude that the nutritive substance contained in the soil itself is equal to 10 cartloads of manure, and we can now express the manure-power of the soil in numbers.

The expression "this soil contains 10 cartloads of manure" must not, however, be taken literally. If one were to separate the humus from the earth precisely by some chemical operation and weigh it, its weight would be quite different from what we assume here. Manure is not a substance that remains the same for long; the process of fermentation, the expulsion of certain gases, the absorption of others, continues uninterruptedly; moreover, manure is capable of various degrees of moisture, and one cannot at all expect that the ratio of solid parts to water in the humus should be the same as in freshly applied manure. "This soil contains 10 cartloads of manure in 100 square rods" means: the effect of the humus present in the soil is equal to the effect of 10 cartloads of manure.

*) A Magdeburg Morgen is equal to 118 Mecklenburg square rods. — Note by the author.

#### Section 4

What fraction of the manure present in the soil is consumed by a crop, and how great the quantity is that this crop required for its development, emerges from the decline in yield of the crop following it.

If the same crop, for example oats, is cultivated on the same spot two years in succession, then — if weather, cultivation, and other influences are the same in both years — the lesser yield of the second oats is the consequence of the exhaustion that the soil suffered through the production of the first oats. In a convertible husbandry system that grows oats twice in succession, one could, through a precise separation of the first from the second oats, determine how great the exhaustive power of oats is. A three-field system that manures once every six years, and thus has one manured and one unmanured fallow, would, by comparing the rye grown in the unmanured fallow with the rye harvested on this spot three years earlier in the manured fallow, show how great the exhaustion caused by the first rye and the first oats together is.

Suppose one had found in this manner that the production of a rye harvest costs the soil one-fifth of its manure-power, or that the exhaustion of rye equals one-fifth of the power present in the soil: then from this, in connection with the preceding propositions, one can determine how much manure a rye harvest of a given size has consumed.

If according to Section 2 the soil which yields 9 Berlin bushels of rye possesses a manure-power of 15 cartloads, and the production of rye costs the soil one-fifth of its power, here thus 3 cartloads: then 9 bushels of rye have consumed 3 cartloads of manure, and in one cartload of manure there is thus nutritive substance for 3 Berlin bushels of rye.

#### Section 4 (continued)

Since both the observations and the experiments specifically undertaken for the investigation of these laws cannot be made in a single year, the weather, which acts so powerfully upon vegetation, can give the observations and experiments, in different years, quite different results. The influence of the weather is so inconstant that, for example, the rye in the unmanured fallow can give a higher yield than the rye standing on this spot three years earlier in the manured fallow.

Given the perpetual involvement of such an inconstant force, one might easily be tempted to believe it is a futile endeavor to seek out the laws by which the size of grain harvests is determined.

The great irregularity of the weather, however, disappears when we compare longer periods with one another.

"As the effect of the weather was in the past, so it will also be in the future" is a principle that tacitly underlies all purchase prices, leases, and yield assessments, and without which no valuation of an estate would be possible.

When the experiments on the effect of manure and on the measure of exhaustion have been continued over a long series of years, the result that emerges from the average of these experiments has attained the degree of certainty of which agriculture is everywhere capable.

In the course of this work, the weather will not be considered according to its influence on a single year, but rather according to the aggregate influence that it exerts over a long series of years; it thereby becomes a constant, unchanging force, through which alone a scientific treatment of agriculture becomes possible.

#### Section 5

The grain types that are similar in their entire structure — wheat, rye, barley, and oats — will, according to analogy and observation, exhaust the soil, for equal masses of harvest, in proportion to their nutritive value relative to one another. This ratio of nutritive value is indicated by the average prices of the grain types — with which the chemical analyses and the experiments in distilling agree fairly precisely.

When one compiles the prices from several places and over a longer period, they approach the following ratio:

For rye : wheat : two-row barley : oats approximately as shown.

Accordingly, the field is as much exhausted by the production of 100 bushels of wheat as by the production of 133 bushels of rye; 100 bushels of barley are, in their effect of exhaustion, equal to 75 bushels of rye, and 100 bushels of oats equal to 50 bushels of rye.

#### Section 6

The ratio in which the exhaustive powers, or the ability to appropriate a portion of the manure present in the soil, stand among the various grain types relative to one another, results from:

1) the size of the yield that a soil of given manure-power produces of each grain type; and
2) the relative value of the grain type.

Let a field be divided — one that is equally well suited by its physical properties, its preceding crop, and its prior cultivation for the production of wheat, rye, barley, and oats — into four parts equal in size and quality, and let each part be sown with one of the named grain types: then the yield — when this experiment has been continued for several years — will show how much the same manure-mass produces of each grain type.

Suppose the following average yield had resulted from these experiments:

Wheat: 100 bushels; Rye: 100 bushels; Barley: 120 bushels; Oats: 175 bushels.

Then the value of the yield, reduced to rye, is: 100 bushels wheat at the ratio of 4:3 equals 133 bushels rye; 100 bushels rye equals 100; 120 bushels barley at 3:4 equals 90; 175 bushels oats at 1:2 equals 87.5.

For a soil that loses one-fifth, or 20 percent, of its manure content through the production of a rye harvest, the exhaustive power is accordingly:

Of wheat: approximately 26.6 percent;
Of barley: approximately 18 percent;
Of oats: approximately 17.5 percent.

#### Section 7

When in a farming operation the harvested grain and the straw from which this grain was threshed are each weighed separately, and this is continued over several years: then one can subsequently, from the average that these measurements have given, calculate the straw yield from the grain yield.

Such a ratio has only been reported in more recent times, by the late Senior Commissioner Meyer and Councillor Karbe — but only in individual experiments. Karbe found that 152 pounds of straw come with one Berlin bushel of rye.

#### Section 8

We owe likewise to Senior Commissioner Meyer the first figures on the quantity of manure that results from the droppings when fodder is consumed by livestock.

According to his experiments and observations, 1 pound of straw yields, through feeding, 2.3 pounds of manure.

Thaer assumes the weight increase for hay and straw to be equally high, namely at a ratio of 2.

However varied the ratio between grain and straw may be, modified by soil and farming system, and however much the ratio between fodder and manure is altered by the type of livestock, by the intensity of bedding, and by the time of manure removal: there must nevertheless necessarily exist such a ratio for every locality, because everywhere the yield of grain, of straw, and of manure can be expressed in numbers, and in this statement itself the ratio is already established.

#### Section 9

The exhaustion that the soil suffers through the production of a cereal crop cannot be fully compensated by the straw of that crop, when it is converted into manure and returned to the field.

The proof of this proposition is provided by the three-field system, which, for as long as it has been practiced and as widely as it has spread, has at no place and at no time been able to sustain itself — that is, to maintain itself continuously at the same level of manure-power — through the manure obtained from the straw alone; but on the contrary always requires an external manure supplement through grazing livestock and through meadows, in order to establish the balance between exhaustion and replenishment.

From this proposition, which is of universal validity, it follows:

1) that one cannot obtain straw without exhausting the field more than the straw is able to replenish;
2) that in a hitherto existing farming operation, an increased sowing of grain and thereby increased straw yield — despite the stronger manuring and the elevated grain yield in the first years — must inevitably lead to the exhaustion of the soil.

The purpose of our investigation is, however, directed at defining this general proposition more precisely, and stating the measure both of the exhaustion and of the replenishment.

If we also apply the numerical ratios used hitherto as examples, we can, at least for one crop — rye — state this measure.

According to Section 3, the soil loses 100 cartloads of manure through the production of 300 Berlin bushels of rye.

According to Section 7, 192 pounds of straw are obtained with one bushel of rye, so with 300 bushels, 57,600 pounds of straw.

According to Section 8, 1 pound of straw produces through feeding 2.3 pounds of manure, which for 57,600 pounds of straw gives 132,480 pounds of manure. Since we have assumed the cartload of manure at 2,000 pounds, the replenishment from the straw amounts to 66.24 cartloads. The exhaustion is 100 cartloads; therefore the field, even when the manure from the straw is returned to it, still loses 33 cartloads through the production of 300 bushels of rye.

Regarding the value and significance of the numbers used here, I believe I owe an explanation.

The investigation of the laws according to which nature acts, and their presentation, must necessarily precede the determination of their effect in individual cases. The numerical ratio — the magnitude of the effect for a specific case — is not yet given by the knowledge of the natural laws in general, but must first be found and developed from reality itself, before it has practical validity.

I would therefore have avoided all numerical statements in this section devoted to the presentation of principles, had I not believed that this was necessary for the elucidation and illustration of the ideas.

#### Section 10

**Empirical proposition.**

When a field that has hitherto borne grain is now sown with clover and grass and laid down to ley or as pasture for livestock: then this field, when it is broken up again for grain cultivation after one or several years, has a higher fertility than it had when it was laid down.

We must conclude from this: that the exhaustion that the field suffers through the production of grass and pasture clover is not as great as the replenishment that the field receives, partly through the grasses and herbs grown on the pasture, which are returned as manure after being consumed by livestock, and partly through the grass and clover roots remaining in the soil.

Experience further teaches that the increase in fertility through lying fallow is greater the greater the grass and clover yield of the ley was. Since, for a given soil and under otherwise equal circumstances, the grass and clover yield, as well as the grain yield, is determined by the manure-power present in the soil, we may assume that the increase in manure-power that the laying down to pasture produces stands in proportion to the magnitude of the manure-power that the field had when it was laid down to pasture.

In order to learn the grass yield that a field produces, one must first determine through experiments how much grass a cow consumes daily, and then note for how many days the cow finds nourishment on the ley.

From the grass yield found in this manner, the quantity of manure that the ley receives through grazing can also be calculated.

In a convertible husbandry on upland soil, the pasture sown with clover and grass provides the highest grass yield in the first ley year as a rule; in the second ley year the grass growth already diminishes somewhat, and it becomes progressively less in the third and fourth year. An exception to this rule occurs only when the sown seed has not germinated and the soil is left to natural grassing, in which case the later ley years have the advantage over the first.

Now, that here, with progressively increasing manure-power of the soil, the size of the yield nevertheless decreases with each year, is mainly due to the following two causes:

1) The soluble humus present in the soil at the time of laying down to pasture is successively absorbed by the plants and used for the formation of their roots and stalks; the process whereby the undissolved, that is, the humus not yet prepared as nourishment for the plants, is made soluble, can, because of the exclusion from the external air, either not take place at all, or only imperfectly, and so in the later ley years, as the soluble humus of the soil diminishes, the grass growth must also diminish. Through a multi-year pasture, the soluble humus that was initially in the soil can probably be almost entirely consumed; but this is more a transformation of the humus than an actual exhaustion, since the entire topsoil is now permeated by a web of grass roots, which, when brought to decomposition by breaking up the field, in turn provide excellent nourishment for all crops.

2) The older a pasture becomes, the more densely and firmly the topsoil settles together and the more impermeable it becomes to moisture. The old leys therefore commonly suffer from drought, and it takes a very sustained rain before they are soaked through to the depth of the topsoil. A downpour that penetrates the loosened topsoil of arable land runs off the firm and even surface of the old ley without moistening the grass roots. Also, the very firm soil probably provides no favorable growing conditions for the finer grasses and for the clover varieties.

For our purpose, the answer to the question "by how much is the manure-power of the field increased by laying down to pasture?" is particularly important. We can arrive at this knowledge if, through experiments, or in their absence, through manifold observations, we compare the size of the harvests that the field gave before being laid down to pasture with those it gives after the breaking up of the ley.

According to my observations and according to the experiences communicated to me by others, I have adopted the following proposition:

**The exhaustion that the field suffers through the production of grass and clover is replenished and compensated by the clover and grass roots remaining in the field; all manure, however, that arises from the plants grown on the ley through grazing by livestock, is pure gain and increases the manure-power of the soil.**

But since the humus of the soil before laying down to pasture and after breaking up the ley is no longer in the same condition, and therefore can also enter into the calculations in a different ratio: observations of this kind are subject to many deceptions; and we must therefore regard this proposition as problematic until we can test the correctness of this proposition by applying the numerical ratios found for a convertible husbandry to the three-field system, and answer the above question decisively.

#### Section 11

The influence that the fallow exerts on the manure-power of the soil must be considered from two essentially different points of view: first, insofar as the fallow effects a real increase of manure; second, insofar as it brings the humus already present in the soil to a higher degree of effectiveness.

An increase in manure-power occurs because, during the fallow year, grass and weeds grow on the field, which, through plowing under or through grazing, return more to the field than they took from it.

This increase must necessarily vary greatly according to the cultivation of the field, according to the variety of field implements, according to the time of manure removal, and according to the greater or lesser purity of the field from weed seeds.

If the plow is used for breaking up the ley in autumn, the grass growth in the fallow is extremely insignificant; if the breaking up is done by the harrow, however, a portion of the ley sod remains exposed to the air and continues to vegetate undisturbed until the harrows come to this field. In the latter case, under otherwise equal circumstances, the grass growth in the fallow and the increase in manure-power will follow the grass growth of the preceding pasture year.

Let a field that has borne oats be divided into two equal parts; let one half, after the oats have been harvested, be harrowed three times and then sown with rye in the same autumn; let the other half remain as fallow, and be likewise harrowed three times the following summer, but not manured, and then sown with rye: the yield of the fallow rye will significantly exceed that of the stubble rye, perhaps by 2 bushels per 100 square rods. If one now wished, as in Section 2, to infer from the yield the manure-power and say: the fallow increases the manure-power of the soil to the extent that 2 bushels more rye grow — for which, according to Section 2, a manuring of 3.33 cartloads is required — then one would fall into a great error. For if the manure-power of the soil had been so significantly increased by the fallow, then the oats, which is sown after both the fallow rye and the stubble rye, would also have to show this; but the oats — for which, since the other circumstances are equal, the inference from the yield to the manure-power does apply — will (provided the fallow was not necessary because of the field running wild) show that the fallowed piece has no advantage in manure-power over the other.

As we now see that the same manure-mass in the soil produces larger or smaller harvests depending on the preceding cultivation of the soil, we must assume that the manure is capable of different degrees of effectiveness.

Of all preparations for winter grain, the fallow produces the highest effectiveness of the manure, and if, for example, the manure after the fallow is brought to the condition that one-fifth of it can pass into the rye, then with the cultivation that the stubble rye receives, perhaps only one-seventh of the manure present in the soil can be absorbed by the rye.

According to Thaer's teachings, the humus does indeed contain the nourishment for the plants, but the humus itself cannot pass directly into the crops in its solid state; rather, it is through the combination that it enters into with oxygen that the directly nourishing substances — carbonic acid gas and extractive matter — are first developed from it, which are soluble in water and are absorbed by the plant roots.

From this, the phenomena presented by the fallow can now be very well explained. The humus can combine with oxygen only when it is brought into direct contact with the atmospheric air. The production of extractive matter will therefore take place more completely and in greater measure the longer the loosened soil is exposed to the air, and the more this happens during the warm season, which promotes the chemical process.

Probably the greater part of the extractive matter present in the soil is exhausted through the production of a crop, and it must then, through renewed cultivation, be formed again from the available humus before a second sowing can thrive on this spot. For the size of the next harvest, the quantity of developed extractive matter is therefore far more decisive than the humus content of the soil.

It seems accordingly that, in order to designate the degree of fertility of a soil, we should state its content of extractive matter and not its content of humus.

For a given farming system, however, the cultivation of the soil is itself already determined and not to be regarded as arbitrary; there will therefore also exist for this system a definite, unchanging ratio between the quantity of humus and of extractive matter, and when the former is designated by degree, the latter is thereby also given.

For a different farming system, which does not maintain a pure fallow, the quantitative ratio between humus and extractive matter undoubtedly changes. The winter grain will here, from the same manure content of the soil, give a lesser yield. For the summer grain, however, through thorough cultivation of the soil, the manure can, in every farming system, be made equally effective; probably just as effective as is achieved through fallow cultivation for the winter grain.

I must further note that I have in view only the humus that has arisen from the residue of earlier manurings and through sod decomposition on the field that has been cultivated for centuries.

The greater or lesser production of extractive matter from the humus is influenced not only by the farming system, but also by the physical composition of the soil and a great number of other circumstances — whose consideration we must defer to another occasion. For example, in clay soil, which often clumps into clods that are sealed against the action of the air, the formation of extractive matter cannot proceed as well and as quickly as in sandy soil, where every particle can be brought into contact with the air.

In nature, no force acts in isolation; rather, the product that it strives to bring forth is always modified by the counteraction or cooperation of other natural forces.

If we wish to determine the influence of one force through experiments, we must leave all other acting agents unchanged and increase or decrease only that one in degree: the product that we obtain always remains a joint product of all influencing forces; but the change that occurs in the product is then attributable solely to the change of that force, and the magnitude of its effect thereby becomes recognizable to us.

In agriculture, the size of the harvest is the joint product of a very large number of factors. If one now wishes to learn the influence of one factor, for example the manure-power of the soil: one must necessarily regard the other factors as unchanged, and the change in the size of the harvest is then the consequence of the change in manure-power. In the same manner, one could choose another factor, for example the influence of the soil, as the subject of investigation, and one would then have to regard all other factors, including the manure-power of the soil, as constant quantities.

When we, in the course of this work, treat manure-power as apparently the sole determinant of the size of harvests: this does not happen because we fail to recognize the influence of the other agents; but because we regard them as at rest, or rather as continuously and uniformly acting, and now the effect of manure-power alone can come into consideration.

When, on the other hand, we express the magnitude of the effect of manure in numbers, and now attribute validity to this numerical ratio only for the specific case; this is by no means because we regard the laws according to which manure-power acts as limited to this case. On the contrary, the tendency of this force is everywhere the same; but since it can only bring a product into being with the cooperation of other forces, the product cannot remain the same when the cooperation changes.

#### Section 13

In order not to complicate the overview of the propositions communicated so far, I have let them follow one another without interruption; I now add some further remarks, and will attempt to answer the objections that first presented themselves to me.

Against the assumption that the exhaustion of the soil depends on the size of the harvest obtained from it, one can raise the following objection:

"If a larger grain harvest exhausted the soil more than a weak one, then the crop following the larger harvest would have to be worse than that following the smaller harvest; but we usually grow better barley when the preceding winter grain turned out well than when it failed."

Indeed, to the question whether barley thrives better after a strong winter grain harvest than after a weak one, I have almost always received an affirmative answer, and only two farmers have communicated to me an opposite experience.

This question must, however, be analyzed to be properly understood.

The land that generally bears better winter grain also has more manure-power. If one field contains 150 cartloads of manure, another 100 cartloads, then the rye takes from the first field 30, from the second 20 cartloads of manure. Despite the greater extraction, the first field still retains a surplus of 40 cartloads of manure, and it is very understandable that it now has a great advantage in the summer grain. But most farmers answer the question in this sense.

If, however, through poor cultivation or through very unfavorable weather, a soil that has the manure-power to produce 9 bushels of rye in the normal case now gives only 6 bushels, will the barley following it then turn out better than if the rye had given 9 bushels?

I believe, by all analogy, that this must be affirmed; the following two cases can, however, despite the correctness of the proposition in general, produce a different result:

1) A soil that is very full of weed seeds usually bears, in the places where the rye is missing, other plants. Instead of the rye consuming the manure here, this is now done by the weeds, and the following crop cannot gain from the failure of the rye.

2) The stronger and denser a crop stands, the less the field can become infested with couch grass, and the more excellent the preparation of the following crop will be. Perhaps also the influence of the atmosphere on such a densely shaded soil is stronger; the humus can then perhaps combine better with the oxygen, and thereby the production of extractive matter can be promoted.

If this case occurs on a very rich soil — which, for example, contains 200 cartloads of manure per 1,000 square rods — then the stronger exhaustion can be outweighed by the higher effectiveness of the remaining humus.

Suppose this field gives, instead of its usual average yield — appropriate to the manure-power — of 120 bushels, now 150 bushels of rye; then the field is deprived of 50 instead of 40 cartloads of manure; there thus remain in the field 150 cartloads. If now, through one of the cited causes, the effectiveness of the manure is increased by one-fifth: then the following harvest is as large as if the field had contained 180 cartloads of manure.

The exhaustion of the soil can therefore be greater, even when the immediately following crops appear to prove the contrary. The effect of the exhaustion shows itself later, but then all the more strikingly.

#### Section 14

"Let two grains of wheat be planted, or two potatoes of apparently equal quality, at the same time next to one another in entirely uniform soil. One seed grain can produce a luxuriant, the other a stunted plant; both enjoy the same weather and the same nourishment, and yet the one plant produces perhaps three times as many grains as the other. It is with plants as with animals: one individual thrives, another is stunted on the same nourishment. Just as one breed pays better for its feed than another: so too will one variety of plant — for example, Hasselburg rye — give a greater yield from the same manure-power of the soil than another — for example, Mecklenburg sand rye. Since the growth of the plant depends on the organization of the seed grain, the nutritive mass cannot provide a standard for the thriving of the crops."

I answer this as follows:

Hasselburg rye gives, from the same nutritive mass in the soil, a greater yield; but this yield again stands in proportion to the manure-power. A soil of 150 cartloads of manure content will give one and a half times as much yield as one of 100 cartloads; but one cartload of manure produces more grain of this variety than of that one. If, for example, one cartload of manure produces 4.5 bushels of Hasselburg rye but only 3 bushels of sand rye: then we conclude that a field which yields 90 bushels of sand rye contains not 150, but 180 cartloads of manure.

The numbers are therefore changed, but the laws remain the same.

#### Section 14 (continued)

With a very great richness of the soil in humus, the ratio between manure-power and yield can no longer be the same as on a soil of medium fertility.

If a very concentrated manure-mass still bore grain in the same proportion, one would have to be able to grow as much grain on a manure heap as on a large area over which this manure-mass were distributed. But for the growth of plants, besides nourishment, an appropriate growing site is required; a soil that contains too much humus is too loose for most plants, and the humus then becomes not merely ineffective, but also harmful.

Every plant furthermore has a certain measure of size or strength that it cannot exceed, even with an abundance of nourishment and under the most favorable other circumstances. For every crop, a certain amount of humus is therefore required in order to attain the perfection possible according to its nature. If one now further increases the manure-power of the soil that this quantity of humus already possesses: then the manuring will not affect the yield at all, since the crops, even before the manuring, found as much nourishment as they could absorb.

The yield of cereal crops is, however, also limited to a certain point by the fact that, with very luxuriant growth, they do not remain upright but lodge. Vegetation does not entirely cease after lodging; but the grains then shrivel, and the yield of the lodged grain thereby becomes less than that of the standing grain on soil of lower manure-power.

Cabbage, potatoes, rapeseed, and some other crops that are not subject to lodging require, to give the highest possible yield, greater manure-power of the soil than the cereal crops can tolerate.

For the two reasons — that first, the absorptive capacity of the plants is limited, and that second, with too luxuriant growth the cereal crops lodge — the manure-mass accumulated in a very rich soil cannot therefore deliver as great a grain yield as if the same quantity of manure were distributed over a larger area.

On the other hand, for a soil that has not yet reached the fertility at which the grain lodges upon it, the manure-power alone is decisive for the yield; that is, the grain yield remains the same whether the same manure-power is distributed over a larger or smaller area — over 1,000 or 2,000 square rods.

In order to test the correctness of this proposition — which I adopted hypothetically fifteen years ago, and which indeed required confirmation through experience — I have since then, with unremitting attention, observed the phenomena in nature in this regard, and these observations have more and more convinced me of the agreement of this proposition with reality. But I have also attempted to explain the phenomena that experience offered me in another way: I devised a formula according to which the manure was absorbed all the more strongly by the plants the less of it was contained in the soil; but I found that this formula in its application led to contradictions with reality.

The degree of fertility at which the field begins to produce lodged grain is, however, not as close as one commonly imagines. One does find in wet years, on fields of low average yield, individual lodged spots; but these lodged spots are then always in the low-lying areas, which either have a much greater manure-power than the surrounding soil, and if harvested separately would give a much greater average yield, or which consist of a spongy meadow soil in which the plants have no support. But here we are speaking only of a firm soil, uniform in its texture and at every point of equal manure-power — which, once it has attained the fertility at which the grain lodges upon it, now also produces lodged grain over its entire surface.

Rye that yields an average of 6 to 7 bushels per 100 square rods can indeed be knocked down by hail, downpour, or storm; but this affects the rye of 3 bushels yield equally, and does not result from too luxuriant growth.

When I first conceived the idea in 1806 of expressing the manure content of the soil and the exhaustion by crops in numbers, I followed almost entirely the principles that are presented in the foregoing.

Through a fifteen-year testing, the fundamental principles themselves have not been essentially altered — as is evident from what I wrote down at the time; all the more, however, the numerical ratio that I had assumed, and the view I held regarding the value and general applicability of the same.

To find the numerical ratio, there are two paths: one either assumes the numbers hypothetically and tests how they agree when applied to a given farming operation, and then modifies them until one can bring the phenomena of this and several other farming operations into agreement with them; or second, one derives them from the experiences and data that the management of an estate over a longer period provides.

Since at that time I entirely lacked my own experience, I had no other choice than to take the first path and assume the numbers hypothetically. I could not, to be sure, expect to achieve complete agreement with reality; but I did hope, through continued corrections, to come ever closer to this goal.

But I had overlooked the difficulty of obtaining accurate and comprehensive data from other farming operations, and so I soon found my hope disappointed. In 1810 I abandoned this path entirely and took the second, which, though slow, had to lead more surely to the goal. But since I had also found that my hypothetically chosen numbers did not provide satisfactory agreement with reality — which showed itself especially in the application to the three-field system — and I considered the correctness of the numerical ratio to be an entirely indispensable requirement if this theory was to become practically useful — I resolved to communicate nothing of these views to the public until I had tested the principles through direct application to my own farming operation and derived the numerical ratio from my own experience.

The plan for the management of the estate Tellow — which I took over in 1810 — arose entirely from this theory; and this afforded me the advantage of constantly comparing what the theory promised with what reality delivered.

The success of this farming operation has now convinced me even more firmly that these theoretical views can be of very essential benefit to practical agriculture.

With respect to the numerical ratio, my calculations have, during ten years, been constantly directed at recording what could lead to knowledge of the same. Nature, however, makes this very difficult through its great complexity, and in a farming operation that must not be an experimental farm, many a lesson that nature gives cannot be grasped and utilized.

The result of these investigations can therefore — despite all earnest striving for completeness and accuracy and all the effort expended upon it — not satisfy the demands that science must make; but I may consider it a significant approximation to the truth.

Since my time does not permit me to continue the calculations at their previous scope any longer, and since I have in general little hope of further perfecting the numerical ratio from my individual standpoint, and thus what has been accomplished already contains what is attainable for me: a further postponement of publication would also be without benefit.

I shall now, in the second section, present the numerical ratio found for a given locality — the estate Tellow. But in order to be able to utilize the experiences that the management of an estate offers, and to apply them correctly to our purpose, we require knowledge of certain propositions, which I shall now communicate here.

#### Section 15

**Principles.**

1) If on an estate, during a very long series of years — in which the weather is to be regarded as a constant magnitude — the farming has remained entirely the same in crop rotation, tillage, and every other respect, and if the yield on this estate becomes smaller from rotation to rotation: then this can only result from a diminution of the manure-power of the soil, which was brought about because the exhaustion amounted to more than the replenishment that the field received back.

2) If, in the opposite case, the yield increases with each rotation, then the replenishment is not entirely consumed by the exhausting crops; a part of it remained in the soil and brought about the increasing harvests.

3) If, however, on such an estate the yield remains continuously the same: then here replenishment and exhaustion are in balance, and the harvested grain mass has — if the grain was the only exhausting crop — consumed precisely as much plant nourishment as the replenishment that the field received back amounted to.

If one expresses the magnitude of the exhausting forces — the quantity of harvested grain — in bushels of rye, and the magnitude of the replenishing forces — manuring, pasture, fallow — in cartloads of manure: then from this it follows how much manure has been consumed and withdrawn from the field through the production of one bushel of rye.

#### Section 16

**Theorem.**

A farming operation whose crop rotation is in itself exhausting — that is, which does not produce within itself as much manure as the exhausting forces consume — but which then receives through meadow hay an annual manure supplement of a determinate size: such a farming operation approaches, in yield and in manure-power, a certain point that is determined by the size of the manure supplement, and remains, once this point is reached, in a persistent, no longer changing state.

**Proof.**

Let the manure-power of the soil at the beginning of the first rotation, in the fallow, equal x cartloads. Let the effect of the exhausting crop rotation be such that the manure-power sinks in one rotation from x to rx cartloads — where r must then be a fraction. Let the invariable manure supplement equal a cartloads.

The second rotation therefore begins with rx + a; the manure-power at the beginning of the rotation is to that at the end of the rotation as x is to rx, or as 1 is to r. The second rotation, which begins with rx + a, will therefore at its end contain (rx + a)r = r²x + ra. To this the supplement a is again added, and the third rotation therefore begins with r²x + ra + a.

In this manner one finds the manure-power at the beginning of the:

- 1st rotation: x
- 2nd rotation: rx + a
- 3rd rotation: r²x + ra + a
- 4th rotation: r³x + r²a + ra + a
- nth rotation: r^(n-1)x + r^(n-2)a + r^(n-3)a + ... + ra + a

**First case: when (1-r)x > a.**

Then the manure-power decreases with each rotation; and one finds the magnitude of the loss brought about by the:

- 1st rotation: (1-r)x - a
- 2nd rotation: r((1-r)x - a)
- nth rotation: r^(n-1)((1-r)x - a)

Since (1-r)x - a is a positive quantity, the manure-power of the soil is also diminished with each rotation; but since r is a proper fraction and every higher power of a fraction is an ever smaller quantity, the magnitude of the loss also decreases with each successive rotation, progressively and indeed in a geometric progression. The question now arises: how far can the manure-power of the soil be diminished by this crop rotation, and whether there is at all a limit to this diminution? The greatest decrease in manure-power that can be produced by this crop rotation occurs when we assume n, or the number of rotations, equals infinity.

For n = infinity, however, r^(n-1) = 0, because r is a fraction; consequently the loss that the nth rotation produces is also zero.

An infinitely continued application of the exhausting crop rotation can therefore diminish the manure-power of the soil only down to a/(1-r) cartloads; and for this power the loss through the crop rotation becomes zero, that is, the farming operation then remains in a persistent state.

**Second case: when (1-r)x < a.**

Then the manure-power increases with each rotation. The increase is then for the:

- 1st rotation: a - (1-r)x
- nth rotation: r^(n-1)(a - (1-r)x)

For n = infinity, r^(n-1) = 0, and thus (1-r)x + a = 0 at the limit.

The limit of the possible increase of the manure-power is therefore the same as the limit of the decrease: a/(1-r) cartloads.

**Example.**

Let the manure-power of the soil in the fallow equal 150 cartloads per 1,000 square rods. Let the crop rotation, left to itself, lose in one rotation one-tenth of the power with which it began.

Let the manure supplement amount to 10 cartloads for each 1,000 square rods of fallow.

The 1st rotation begins with 150 cartloads;
the loss is one-tenth, thus 15;
there remain 135;
add the manure supplement of 10.

The 2nd rotation begins thus with 145;
loss one-tenth of 145 = 14.5;
there remain 130.5;
manure supplement 10.

The 3rd rotation begins with 140.5;
loss one-tenth = 14.05;
there remain 126.45;
supplement 10.

The 4th rotation begins with 136.45;
loss = 13.645;
there remain 122.805;
manure supplement 10.

The 5th rotation begins with 132.805.

If one continues the calculation in this manner, an ever more diminished manure-power will result.

Once, however, the power has diminished to 100 cartloads, the loss of one-tenth equals 10;
there remain 90;
add the manure supplement of 10.
The following rotation begins again with 100.

In this case, 100 is therefore the limit that the manure-power continually approaches and that can never be exceeded. One finds this limit by dividing the manure supplement — here 10 — by the manure loss fraction of the crop rotation — here one-tenth.

But if, with the same crop rotation and the same manure supplement, the power of the soil were only 80 cartloads, this would increase with each rotation and approach unceasingly the limit of 100.

**Applications.**

**I. How does it come about that the so exhausting three-field system has existed for centuries on estates that have a small hay yield, and still continues to exist?**

The three-field system solves this problem by diminishing the grain yield to the degree where the loss that the crop rotation produces is compensated by the small manure supplement.

If, for example, we had found for a certain soil that the three-field system loses one-tenth of its manure-power in each rotation, and the manure supplement through outside grazing and meadow hay amounted to only 5 cartloads for each 1,000 square rods of fallow: then this farming operation must sink in manure-power to 50 cartloads per 1,000 square rods, or to a yield of 5 grains (3 Berlin bushels per 100 square rods); but once arrived at this point, it will remain unchanged in its yield for centuries.

By contrast, a three-field system of 9 grains yield would require a manure supplement of 15 cartloads for each 1,000 square rods of fallow in order to maintain itself at the same level of yield.

**II.** Until the most recent time — when a complete transformation has been brought about through marling — only those estates in Mecklenburg that had a large hay yield were in a high state of cultivation. One could, with few exceptions, infer the grain yield of the arable land from the ratio of hay yield to the size of the arable land, and thus take the meadows as the standard for the fertility of the soil. The question now arises: **how could the hay yield of an estate become so decisive for the manure-power of the soil?**

This phenomenon can be fully explained by the uniformity of the farming operations. In the division into fields, one usually took into account only the quality of the soil, and very often placed the estate with little hay into the same exhausting crop rotation as the estate with much hay.

Suppose now that it had been found, for a given soil, that a six-field convertible husbandry with three grain crops loses in one rotation one-fifteenth of the manure-power it had at the beginning: then the estate with a manure supplement of 6 cartloads will remain standing at 6 times 15 equals 90 cartloads per 1,000 square rods, or at a grain yield of 5.7 bushels, while the estate with the same soil, but with a manure supplement of 10 cartloads, will rise to a power of 10 times 15 equals 150 cartloads, and then delivers a yield of 9 grains.

The level of yield and manure-power to which an estate rises is determined solely by the ratio that exists in the crop rotation between the exhausting and the replenishing forces. Since we can, by reducing the grain sowing and by increasing the ley, give the replenishing forces the preponderance in any farming operation: the highest cultivation of the arable land is also attainable on every estate, even one that has little or no meadow hay; though this is admittedly associated with more difficulties and greater costs than on those estates that possess much meadow hay.

**III. What are the consequences of marling, if we regard the increase in fertility brought about by the marl as if it had arisen through a newly added manuring?**

Of all the hypotheses proposed regarding the effect of marl, the one that attributes a fertilizing power to the marl probably provides the most reassurance about the consequences of marling.

If we now — since there is still no theory that has received universal agreement and that satisfactorily explains all the effects of marl — base our investigations on this hypothesis, and then develop the future results of marling from the above theorem: we can be assured that if, even under this hypothesis so favorable to marling, great caution is still necessary, then under the assumption of another hypothesis this caution is not superfluous, but must be increased.

Although we here regard the effect of the first marling as an increase in the manure-power of the soil, we must nevertheless, as experience teaches, establish the essential distinction between manure and marl: that a repeated manuring always exerts the same favorable effect on the fertility of the soil, while the marl is capable of increasing the fertility of the soil only once — or at least only after a very long interval a second time. A field once marled and subsequently exhausted cannot therefore be restored to vigor by a second marling, but only by intensified manuring.

Suppose now that a farming operation whose manure-power equals 100 cartloads per 1,000 square rods, whose crop rotation loses one-tenth of its manure-power in each rotation, which has a manure supplement of 6.67 cartloads for each 1,000 square rods of fallow, and which gives a grain yield of 6 bushels, is brought by marling to a yield of 9 grains, which presupposes a manure-power of 150 cartloads — what will the consequences of marling be?

**First case: if the crop rotation is not changed after marling.**

The first rotation begins with a manure-power of 150 cartloads.
The loss amounts to one-tenth = 15;
there remain 135;
the manure supplement amounts to 6.67.

Manure-power at the beginning of the 2nd rotation: 141.67.

Here the same phenomenon appears as in the example given above: the manure-power decreases with each rotation, but cannot sink lower than 15 times 6.67 = 100 cartloads. This farming operation therefore gradually returns to the yield it gave before marling.

Before marling, the farming operation was in a persistent state; after marling it declines because the manure supplement is sufficient for a grain yield of 6, but not for one of 9 bushels. To maintain the increased yield continuously, the regular manure supplement would have had to be increased from 6.67 to 10 cartloads.

**Second case: one cultivates the same area with grain after marling, but now grows wheat instead of rye.**

Since wheat exhausts the soil more than rye, the sum of the exhausting forces is now increased; the replenishing forces are rather diminished than increased. The crop rotation therefore becomes more exhausting than it was before.

If it had been found, for example, that through the introduction of wheat cultivation the loss in one rotation had been increased from one-tenth to one-twelfth: then the manure-power at the beginning of the first rotation is 150 cartloads; the loss of one-twelfth = 12.5; there remain 137.5; the manure supplement 6.67.

The second rotation begins with 144.17.

The limit of the sinking is here not at 100, but at 12 times 6.67 = 80; and only when the manure-power of the soil has fallen to 80 cartloads does the farming operation reach a persistent state.

**Third case: one increases the grain sowing after marling, resulting in an increase of the exhausting and a decrease of the replenishing forces.**

Through this change, let the exhaustion that the crop rotation suffers in one rotation have risen to one-fifteenth.

The manure-power of the soil after marling is 150 cartloads;
the loss of one-fifteenth = 10;
there remain 135;
the manure supplement 6.67.

The second rotation begins with 141.67.

If this exhausting operation is continued, its sinking cannot end at 100, but only at 15 times 6.67 = 66.67 cartloads, or at a yield of 4 grains.

In those three-field systems that have only a small amount of meadows relative to the size of the arable land, the more distant arable land of each field usually remains uncultivated for lack of manure, and then bears only one grain crop in 3, 6, or 9 years. As soon as the straw harvests become larger through marling in such a farming operation, and the manure mass therefore also increases, a portion of the more distant arable land that lay idle merely for lack of manure is also manured and incorporated into the regular rotation. The enlarged grain sowing then again increases the straw and manure yield, and so through marling all previous outlying land can disappear, and the entire arable area can be subjected to the regular three-field system.

This enlarged grain sowing explains quite sufficiently why the marling of three-field systems has as a rule become so ruinous; why we have so many tales of fields being "marled out"; and whence the proverb "marling makes rich fathers but poor sons" has arisen.

But if, instead of applying the marl, one had purchased so much manure that the entire field received a newly added manuring of 5 cartloads per 100 square rods, and then permanently enlarged the grain sowing: then after many rotations the field would likewise be exhausted or "marled out." One therefore need not seek in the marl any peculiar force by which the marling-out is brought about; rather, this exhaustion or marling-out — which can occur with purchased manure just as well as with marling — is brought about solely by an inconsistent procedure.

**Fourth case: one reduces the grain sowing after marling and increases the pasture.**

Suppose that one had thereby reduced the exhaustion so much that the loss in one rotation amounts to only one-fifteenth instead of one-tenth: then for a manure-power of 150 cartloads,
the loss of one-fifteenth = 10;
there remain 143.33;
the manure supplement 6.67.

The second rotation begins again with 150 cartloads.

In this case the yield would remain perpetually the same, and one would now, through the sacrifice of a part of the grain sowing, have permanently established in the field the increase in fertility brought about by the marl.

#### Section 17

**Empirical proposition.**

On the estate Tellow — and to a lesser degree on almost every estate — there are found, in a soil whose average yield before marling was 8 grains, individual spots of poor physical composition and with a very permeable subsoil, whose average yield amounted to at most 4 grains. These spots must, on account of their location among the other arable land, and on account of their small extent, have always received the same manuring and the same cultivation as the surrounding good arable land.

Since the estate Tellow had, until the year 1800, a very exhausting farming system — the four-field system with 3 grain crops — and since, according to the theorem in the preceding section, every soil that is subjected to an exhausting crop rotation ultimately reaches the persistent state: here too, both the good arable land and the poor spots must have come very close to this state.

According to Section 15, we find the quantity of manure consumed through the production of one bushel of rye when, in a farming operation maintaining itself at the same level of power, we set the sum of the exhausting forces equal to the sum of the replenishing forces.

If we now apply this calculation to the present case, then the manuring and the replenishment from pasture and fallow on the good soil have been consumed by the rye harvest of 8 grains and by the two following summer crops — whose yield is determined by the size of the winter grain harvest. From the detailed calculation it then emerges that on this soil, one cartload of manure has been consumed through the production of 3.1 Berlin bushels of rye. On the poor spots, the same manuring and the replenishment from pasture and fallow — which here, however, amounts to only half of what the good arable land gives — has been consumed by half the grain production of the good arable land. From the detailed calculation we then find that on these spots, one cartload of manure was sufficient as nourishment for only 2 bushels of rye.

From this experience there now emerges the remarkable fact: that on the one soil, the same grain harvest consumes more manure than on the other, and that the one soil, through the yielding of an equal manure-mass to the plants, delivers more grain than the other soil.

The property of the soil of delivering a larger or smaller harvest from the same manure-mass yielded to the plants, I call the **quality** of the soil, and I designate its degree by the number of Berlin bushels of rye that are produced on this soil through the exhaustion of one cartload of manure. Thus in the present case we ascribe to the good arable land a quality of 3.1°, and to the poor spots a quality of 2°.

The difference in the quality of the soil shows itself, if not in such a stark gradation, yet no less decisively, in the comparison of entire estates. The estate that has a sandy rye soil will, with equal crop rotation and equal manure supplement, never rise to the grain yield that the estate with a fine barley soil attains. The same manure supplement will there be consumed by a smaller, here by a larger grain harvest; and precisely this marks the difference in the quality of the soil.

For wind-blown sand the quality equals 0°; for the finest wheat soil on the uplands 3.3° — perhaps 3.5°; between these two endpoints lies, in innumerable gradations, the ordinary arable soil; but I doubt whether anyone anywhere considers worth cultivating a soil whose quality does not exceed 2°, when it lies in a contiguous area.

For a scientifically precise assessment of the soil, the knowledge of its quality seems to me indispensable; but it will be very difficult to discover the characteristics by which the quality of the soil is determined and recognizable. With equal situation and equal subsoil, it appears to me to be most closely connected with the clay content.

In any case, a wide field remains open here for future research.

The explanation of the interesting phenomenon that the same plant requires a greater or smaller quantity of nourishment for its development depending on the nature of the soil must be left to the chemists and physicists; we hold to the fact itself and seek to develop the results that follow from it for practical agriculture.

Tellow, February 1821.

J. H. von Thünen.
