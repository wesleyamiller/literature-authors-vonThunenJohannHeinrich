# On an Expanded Cultivation of Commercial Crops in Mecklenburg

*Original: Ueber einen erweiterten Anbau der Handelsgewächse in Mecklenburg (1823)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

(Submitted from the Teterow District of the Mecklenburg Patriotic Association.)

Under free trade, every country will grow those products which are most suited to its climate, its geographical location, and its entire local conditions — that is, those which it can supply more cheaply than other countries.

Such a product has hitherto been grain for Mecklenburg, and with the money that Mecklenburg received for its surplus grain, all products that other countries supplied more cheaply for whatever reason could be obtained in exchange.

But since nearly all nations have closed their ports to our grain, this relationship has fundamentally changed. As our grain sinks to worthlessness, we no longer possess the means to purchase these products from other countries.

If, at the previous average prices of grain, its cultivation was more profitable than that of commercial crops, then now — should the prohibitive system succeed in pressing the average price of our grain down to one-half or two-thirds of its former level — the cultivation of commercial crops will be far more profitable than that of grain.

Our attention must be directed especially toward those crops which have hitherto still been imported from abroad, and for which we therefore find a secure market within the country itself, independent of the whims of foreign nations.

But everything now depends on our producing these crops of the best quality and at the lowest possible cost, in order to withstand competition with foreign countries.

It is therefore very important to investigate whether the advantages that some countries have over us in the quality and cheapness of commercial crops are grounded in climate and soil, or whether they merely stem from a better method of cultivation that we can learn and adopt.

1. Tobacco.

Whence comes the great superiority of American tobacco? Is it grounded in the climate, the soil, the seed, or the method of treatment?

In the middle part of the North American republic, the climate is no warmer than ours, and if those regions grow better tobacco than we do, this must be due to causes other than climate.

I have read in some book that the best American tobacco is grown in the ashes of soil on which the wood and the turf have been burned.

But since the operation of burning can be performed on any site — except on peat soil — only once, the production of superior tobacco would have to be gradually declining already, which does not seem to be the case.

We know, however, that even in Mecklenburg tobacco varies greatly in quality from place to place; that sandy soil produces better tobacco than clay soil; that heavily manured and especially heavily folded soil yields a heavy but foul-smelling tobacco; that it is not a matter of indifference from which quality one takes the seed. If experience itself reveals such great differences here, may we not hope that through continued observation we might arrive at means by which we produce a tobacco far superior to any grown here before, and equal to or at least approaching the American?

Is there no book in which the cultivation of tobacco in America is truly and accurately described? Such a description would greatly facilitate and advance our investigations.

If one were to trench a sandy soil deeply so that it could hold a greater mass of plant nourishment, and then enriched it through several years of repeated manuring so that it could yield a good tobacco harvest without being freshly manured for the tobacco crop: would the tobacco produced in this manner not far surpass all that has been grown here before in quality?

Such an operation was too costly for our previous circumstances. But since, on the one hand, marling has greatly increased the richness of our soil, and on the other hand, with persistently low grain prices, grain cultivation must necessarily be curtailed, the manure thus employed might perhaps pay for itself handsomely through the higher price of the tobacco produced.

2. Linseed.

We still buy Riga linseed. But can we not produce linseed of the same quality if we treat it just as the Russians do? For the climate in northern Russia can scarcely be more favorable than ours.

Has it been confirmed that linseed, when dried in a baking oven, or when intimately mixed with cow dung and left to lie for a year, then yields a much stronger flax harvest?

According to a very credible report, the Riga merchants buy a great deal of linseed in the Rhine regions. Most probably they sell this seed again as Riga seed, and this would then be proof that the production of better linseed is not tied to the Russian climate and Russian soil.

3. Hemp.

Hemp cultivation and the processing of hemp are not unknown here; but despite this, hemp cultivation has declined more and more in recent years — proof that we do not grow it as cheaply as the Russians supply it to us. The reasons for this are probably the following:

1) Russian hemp commands a significantly higher price than the local product;

2) the yield of hemp here is far lower than in Russia and in other countries.

Since we can well rival northern Russia in climate and soil, the disadvantage in which we stand relative to Russia with respect to hemp cultivation can only be grounded in the deficient preparation of the soil and in the poor treatment of the product itself. We must learn both — and we shall then be able to hold our own against Russian hemp in price and perhaps even drive it from other markets.

The Russians give hemp the water ret; we give it the dew ret — this we can change.

Hemp shows by the structure of its roots that it must penetrate deep into the earth if it is to thrive. We must therefore trench the soil deeply, and we have, if only we curtail grain cultivation, manure enough to enrich the deeper subsoil.

4. Clover Seed.

Significant sums still leave the country for clover seed. Since the fields have been marled, it lies within our power to extend clover cultivation and seed production over as large an area as we wish. Why then do we not grow at least as much clover seed as is consumed in Mecklenburg itself, and why should we not be able to supply it so cheaply that foreigners are thereby driven from our markets?

5. Caraway.

The demand for caraway in Mecklenburg is considerable on account of the many distilleries, and of this only a small portion is produced within the country; the greater part is purchased from abroad.

The caraway grown in southern Germany commands a higher price than the local variety because it is said to be more potent. If this is well founded, it will be difficult to expand the cultivation of this crop and to withstand competition with foreigners. Nevertheless, several farmers who grow it assure us that it pays its place well, and it would be desirable for experiences and precise calculations on this matter to be communicated.

6. Hops.

Since hops thrive very well in Mecklenburg and, as far as I know, are of the same quality as the foreign variety — provided one has a good type of hops — it would be very easy, through an expanded cultivation in the often all-too-spacious gardens, to produce the country's entire requirement.

Now, if the diminished grain market already makes the cultivation of commercial crops necessary, this is rendered no less imperative by the transformation that has recently taken place in our agriculture.

Even earlier, at harvest time our convertible husbandry system lacked sufficient working hands. The same number of people who could be employed with field work throughout the year was not sufficient to harvest so large an area sown with grain within the proper time, and a frequent, often considerable loss was the consequence. Through marling, this disproportion has now been very greatly increased; harvests have perhaps grown by a quarter, and harvest labor has risen in the same measure; on the other hand, the work of tillage has not increased, for rich soil requires not more but often fewer plough furrows for its preparation than poor soil. If we were now to keep no more laborers than we can employ throughout the year in our ordinary grain economy, the increased production would be largely lost again through grain losses at harvest. We must therefore engage people who help only at harvest and who find employment and earnings during the rest of the summer, and for this purpose certain commercial crops — especially those requiring frequent hoeing — offer us a favorable opportunity.

In order to complete the great operation of marling an entire set of fields within a few years, the number of day-laborer families has been increased on most estates, and the saying that where there is work and wages, people soon appear has been strikingly confirmed here. But when in a few years the marling in Mecklenburg will be completed, these additional workers will, under our present grain economy, find work only during the harvest, and an incalculable misery — of which, sadly, some traces are already visible — will be the inevitable consequence.

It is important to recognize a threatening evil in advance, because preventive measures are then still possible, and both the state and every private individual are under the strict obligation to consider means by which the population that has risen so suddenly can be provided with bread and work in the future as well. The establishment of new factories, combined with the cultivation of commercial crops — which would then mutually support each other — appears to be the only comprehensive remedy. But factories need, at their inception, if not outright promotion, at least some protection, or at the very least the removal of the obstacles that stand in the way of their flourishing.

Through low grain prices, through the increased richness of our soils, and through the surplus of working hands, the point in time at which the cultivation of commercial crops becomes possible and advantageous for Mecklenburg has now decidedly arrived; but it cannot be denied that through our ignorance of the better methods of cultivating these crops, and through the complete unfamiliarity of our day laborers with the necessary work involved, substantial obstacles still stand in the way of their initial introduction and dissemination. Our grain cultivation has become what it is today only after slow improvements continued over centuries, and has still not reached the highest level of perfection. If we wish to learn the far more refined cultivation of commercial crops by this same slow path, we may expect no success in this branch. For since we cannot be protected against competition with foreigners by any import prohibitions on foreign products, we shall be able to hold our own against them and drive them from our market only through a method of cultivation as good and as cheap as theirs.

But if we could attract from those regions where the cultivation of commercial crops has long been practiced in the highest perfection one or a few small farmers to Mecklenburg, we would enrich ourselves with the long experience of those countries, and our laborers would learn the techniques of processing, which can be acquired almost only through observation and hands-on practice.

A gift of a few thousand square rods of land and some ready money for setting up the farm would perhaps already suffice to induce a Belgian farmer to settle in Mecklenburg. But one would absolutely have to give him good soil, of high fertility and with sufficient meadows; for it can be rigorously demonstrated that on poor and on lean soil, intensively high cultivation cannot sustain itself and yields a lower net return than the labor-saving extensive system of farming.

If the Patriotic Association were better endowed, it could, by devoting its revenues to this purpose, earn a lasting merit for the entire country; but its means are too limited even to think of such an undertaking.

Should, however, the highest state authorities give their approval to this proposal and be inclined toward its execution, then not only would the country's agriculture be generally much advanced, but also in pecuniary terms the initial, not very substantial outlay could in time bear exceedingly rich fruit. For the example of a Belgian style of cultivation could, over time, not fail to influence the local parceled peasant and smallholder farms; one would come to know the value and the high productivity of which such parcels are capable, and then the crown estates could be leased at far higher prices than at present, when the entire yield must be drawn from a meager grain cultivation and a very mediocre livestock husbandry.

J. H. von Thuenen.
