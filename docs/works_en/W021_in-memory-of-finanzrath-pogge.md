# Remarks on the Treatise of the Chief Forester, Baron von Stenglin, "On the Effect of Burnt Marl"

*Original: Zum Andenken an den verstorbenen Finanzrath Vogge (1832)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

The author of that treatise was kind enough to share it in manuscript with Count von Schlieffen and myself, and I take the liberty of appending the following remarks to it.

When I first read General Beatson's system of agriculture in the German translation, with its pompous but false title, I regarded the celebrated effects of burnt clay, if not as charlatanry, then at least as delusion. But when last summer I became acquainted with the publication of the Chief Forester, Baron von Stenglin, "On the Application of Burnt Marl," I became very attentive to this subject. We have come to know too many wonderful effects of marl not to be inclined to consider it capable of an even higher efficacy under changed conditions. Moreover, the author communicated in that publication experiences regarding the application of burnt marl which placed its great effectiveness — under the conditions prevailing at Gelbensande — beyond doubt. But where a fact is incontestable, it matters very little whether one can explain it, that is, reconcile it with other already known facts, or not. It now seemed very probable to me that the clay which General Beatson used for burning was calcareous, although he does not mention this important circumstance, and it should therefore properly be called clay marl. Fertilization with burnt lime, however, has long been customary in England, and in some regions of the country takes place in every rotation just as regularly as the application of animal manure.

Last summer I had the pleasure of making the personal acquaintance — most valued to me — of the Chief Forester, Baron von Stenglin, and having been invited by him to Gelbensande, I traveled there at the beginning of August with Count von Schlieffen from Sulz.

At Gresenhorst, where the Chief Forester had come to meet us, we saw the first sample of the effect of burnt marl.

A cottager from Gresenhorst had fetched a cartload of burnt marl from Gelbensande, spread it thinly over his field, and heavily covered the remaining part of the plot with raw marl. The oats on the entire plot had lodged, and I could perceive no difference between the oats after burnt marl and those after raw marl. The cottager assured us, however — and his whole demeanor bore the stamp of uprightness and truthfulness — that the oats after burnt marl had, before lodging, visibly distinguished themselves by greater height. (Note No. 1.)

Nevertheless, I must confess that my attention here was directed far less at the burnt marl than at a spectacle of another kind — one might say of a higher nature.

Several cottagers from Gresenhorst had assembled to show us their grain on the field they had made arable. When we stepped onto the reclaimed field, we first saw a plot of oats that filled me with astonishment. The oats were very tall in the stalk, had large panicles with full kernels, and had laid down under the weight of their own gravity; I could scarcely recall having seen such fine oats on the best soil during this summer. The adjoining rye was also very strong in the stalk, but, as was almost universally the case this year, did not have fully laden ears. As we walked further, we found all the oats of nearly equal strength to those first seen. The sight of the beautiful grain could well gladden the eye of the farmer; but nevertheless the eye was soon drawn away from the grain and turned to the people through whose diligence it had been produced. They expressed the liveliest joy at being able to show us such fine grain. When we directed a question at one of them, they all answered at once, and in the eyes of all there spoke contentment and cheerfulness at the success of their laborious work. But what these people had accomplished we only fully recognized when we came at last to a piece of land that had not yet been made arable, and we now beheld the soil in its original condition. It was low-lying, boggy ground covered with tussocks. Count von Schlieffen made the remark, to which I had to give my full assent, that this soil would not repay the costs of reclamation, and that it would be a waste of human energy to transform it into arable land by carting in earth. We shared this remark with the cottagers and gave them the advice to make the plot into a meadow instead. Our suggestion, however, made no impression on them at all, and one of them answered: "If the gentlemen come back in a year, it will already look different," and then added mischievously: "The Chief Forester had better have a good watch kept, or else the cottager N. N. (to whom this plot belongs) will steal the earth from him," pointing with his hand at the sand thrown up from a newly dug ditch.

In fact, we now learned for the first time that the greater part of the reclaimed former forest land, on which we had seen the fine grain, had first received an arable topsoil through a thick layer of hauled sand, and that the cottagers had fetched the sand partly from a distance of sixty rods and more — with the help of their single horse.

Blessings upon the diligence of these people!

On the way back, one of the cottagers said openly and in a firm voice:

"If the Chief Forester had not taken up our cause and procured this forest land for us, there would now be thirty more scoundrels in the district."

These people had formerly been settled as cottagers in Gresenhorst but had been able to find as little work there as in the Grand Ducal forests, and since the land allotted to them was far from sufficient to feed them, they may have fallen into the bitterest destitution, until through the mediation of the Chief Forester von Stenglin, the higher Chamber and Forestry Board leased them the aforementioned forest land for a number of years (if I am not mistaken, twelve years).

The cottagers then expressed contentment with their present situation, but concern about the future when, after the lease expired, they would have to give up their forest field, which was destined for reforestation.

This concern is probably unfounded; for if no higher state purposes stand in the way — and what could those be here? — there can surely be no doubt that the highest authorities of the land, when informed of the situation of these cottagers and their almost unparalleled exertion to support themselves in an honest way, will permanently grant them this field — or rather the spot where they had carted topsoil — and give it to them on a hereditary lease. A ten-year uncertainty and anxiety spread over thirty families, however, destroys too much human happiness for one not to wish most fervently that the permanent possession of this field be secured for them even now. (Note No. 2.)

We now took leave of these good people; but my thoughts lingered with them for a long time. From what a small parcel of land — I said to myself — can a family live contentedly and happily! How worthy of respect are these people who, merely to earn their bread honestly, do not shy from toil, exertion, and privation, and undertake improvements on a leased plot that yield not half, and in part perhaps not a quarter, of the ordinary day-laborer's wage! What a fine feeling the consciousness must afford of having rescued thirty families from misery and moral corruption! But herein lies at the same time a serious warning not to settle cottagers in places where they can find no supplementary income. For if no philanthropist comes forward to take up their cause, or if circumstances later permit no remedy, the physical and moral ruin of the people, and the endangerment of security and property throughout the surrounding area, is the inevitable consequence of such an ill-considered settlement.

Having arrived on the Gelbensande fields, we first saw a piece of land that had been reclaimed only a few years before and had been sown with oats this spring; but it now bore not oats, but buckwheat, for only along the ditch edges did narrow strips of oats appear. The whim of the soil, to bear not the sown crop but another one of its own preference, gave us no favorable opinion of its natural character. The sown oats had, in fact, perished, and in their place the grains left from the previous year's buckwheat crop had come up.

The next morning we first inspected the Beatson agricultural implements, about whose value and usefulness the Chief Forester will hopefully inform us himself; then a very practically arranged hay scale, by means of which the hay given daily to the cattle is weighed with almost no loss of time and no increase of labor. Thereupon we went to a pasture enclosure lying close to the farmstead, which was very densely grassed and showed a vigorous growth. The field here, as on the adjoining rye enclosure, was laid in beds only six feet wide. The field is altogether very level, to all appearance only slightly above sea level, and suffers greatly from wetness. The soil itself consists of a mixture of sand and peat, to which remnants of old tree roots are admixed. Given this character and situation of the soil, the narrow beds appear to be a condition of its cultivation, without which at least no winter grain could be grown.

Quite peculiar, however, is the manner of constructing the narrow beds. Since their creation by hoeing on the part of the laborers requires great precision and involves many difficulties, and since the beds would be partly destroyed again by harrowing in the seed, the Chief Forester has the field plowed level — that is, without beds — sown, and the seed harrowed in; then, with a water-furrow plow that has long wings on both sides, a furrow is drawn every six feet. As the earth from the furrows is pushed by the long wings toward the center of the strips, the narrow beds are thereby directly formed.

I did not see the water-furrow plow itself, but it certainly deserves a more detailed description and illustration; for there are probably still many areas that are considered unfit for grain cultivation because of insufficient slope and excessive wetness, but which could be converted into fertile arable land by this method of cultivation. On our upland soils we shall naturally spare ourselves such work. But the thought did occur to me whether on very rich soil the construction of such beds might entirely prevent or at least greatly reduce the lodging of wheat; for we see everywhere that wheat next to drawn water furrows lodges later than the rest.

The rye on the adjoining enclosure had already been mowed and stood in shocks. The rye stubble was uncommonly dense with young clover, and only very rarely have I seen so fine a clover stand on our best upland soil in autumn.

We then drove to the more distant part of the field and came to the cow-marl pit, where the compost that the Chief Forester mentions in the aforementioned publication is prepared.

The preparation of compost by mixing and covering manure with ordinary topsoil comes at a high cost due to the expense of carting in the earth and the greatly increased cost of the subsequent manure removal, and will hardly pay under ordinary circumstances. (Note No. 3.) At Gelbensande, however, where the compost is prepared from humus-rich earth that is available everywhere in the ground, and where the humus particles not only absorb the gases escaping from the manure but also, through contact with the manure itself, undergo fermentation and are transformed into mild, plant-nourishing humus — the situation may be different; and certainly the state of cultivation of the field is thereby improved.

We then entered the plot that the Chief Forester has described in more detail in his publication, pages 13 and 14. Of this plot, one part was treated with burnt marl in the spring of 1829, and the other part in the autumn of the same year with ten loads of manure per one hundred square rods.

In the summer of 1830 the entire plot bore rye, and now — in the summer of 1831 — oats. We now directed our full attention to the boundary between the manured section and the section treated with burnt marl. With respect to the size and strength of the oats, we could perceive no definite difference between the two parts; certainly, however, the oats after burnt marl did not fall behind those on the manured field. With respect to color and time of ripening, we found a difference similar to that usually found between grain on marled and unmarled fields — even though here both parts had previously been treated with raw marl. The oats after manure were already close to ripeness and showed a yellowish color everywhere, while those after burnt marl still had many green stalks and panicles. The oats were on the whole good, though nowhere near as strong as those we had seen at Gresenhorst.

It is a remarkable fact that burnt marl still had just as strong an effect in the third crop as a manuring of ten loads per one hundred rods. Such an effect of burnt marl may, however, occur only on very humus-rich soil, and would probably not be repeated on ordinary arable land.

Very striking, and perhaps leading to new insights, is the phenomenon that burnt marl has such a great effect on already marled land, while experience almost universally teaches that a second application of raw marl — when the first marling was sufficiently heavy — remains without effect.

From there we went to the kilns and observed the process of burning the marl, then drove through a part of the very extensive Gelbensande forest, where everything we saw was orderly and without gaps, and then on the way back passed a field of oats, part of which had been treated with burnt marl. Sharper eyes than mine could already discern from a distance the difference in the oats in favor of the burnt marl.

Concerning the mode of action of burnt marl and its more general applicability, I can after these observations not yet express a definite opinion, but only voice some conjectures:

1) The acid present in the soil is neutralized by both raw and burnt marl; but where the soil contains very much acid, the neutralization will be more completely effected by burnt marl, because it is applied in greater quantity.

2) Burnt marl probably decomposes plant fibers and insoluble humus to a higher degree than raw marl.

3) The burning, or rather the roasting, of the marl imparts to the lime contained in it the ability to attract carbonic acid gas and moisture from the atmosphere, and as the lime gives off to the plants the carbonic acid thus formed, it serves as a vehicle for the nourishment of plants from the atmosphere — at least the phenomenon that burnt marl also has a favorable effect on already marled soil that still contains lime leads to this conjecture.

If this latter effect of burnt marl were the predominant one, then it could be applied in abundant measure on any soil without danger of exhausting the field; one could obtain richer harvests through it without the soil having to contribute anything to nourish this increase in yield.

As long, however, as this is not settled by prolonged experience, I consider it advisable to apply burnt marl only on small areas. But this caution must not divert our attention from the subject; it is rather to be wished that in many regions of the country experiments with burnt marl be conducted on the greatest possible variety of soil types. It deserves particular consideration that the English — at least on several types of soil — have found a regularly repeated fertilization with burnt lime advantageous, even though this fertilization is very costly. What the English accomplish with burnt lime, we can perhaps achieve at much lower cost with burnt marl.

Sinclair says in his Principles of Agriculture, page 273 of the German translation:

"Many farmers spend ten shillings per acre annually (approximately one and a third Thaler per one hundred square rods) on liming their lands, and still find their account in it. The beneficial influence of this on the thriving of green crops is alone sufficient. These can indeed also be obtained with the help of much and strong manure; but experience has always shown that with the application of calcareous substances, a far smaller quantity of animal and vegetable substances was required to achieve the same purpose."

On page 274, however, Sinclair adds the warning rule: "Lime should never be repeatedly used on weak or lean soils, except mixed with a compost manure; and even in this exceptional case, the fields so treated should be immediately laid down to grass."

At Gelbensande, where the soil contains such a surplus of humus that the decomposition and removal of a part of it improves the texture of the soil and increases its productive capacity, the application of burnt marl on a large scale cannot lead to the exhaustion of the soil, but can only be beneficial. However, near the farmstead there is a more elevated, very sandy piece of arable land with little humus — and if the Chief Forester kindly continues to share his experiences with the public, this plot will first provide us with instruction about the mode of action of burnt marl and the ultimate result of its application.

The soil at Gelbensande, as well as the purposeful method of cultivation applied to it, deviates so much from ordinary conditions in Mecklenburg that it was very interesting and instructive for me to become acquainted with both.

The initial reclamation of this soil is very costly; for here too, as at Gresenhorst, a part of it had to be heavily covered with sand in order to make it productive. Then its further enrichment through compost preparation, through application of burnt marl, and so forth, is associated with costs that are unknown elsewhere.

Under these circumstances, the question frequently pressed upon me: whether such reclamations are profitable for an entrepreneur who must bear the initial cultivation expenses alone but enjoys their fruits only during his lifetime, or not?

However that may be, it is certainly true that through such enterprises a capital accrues to the state that did not previously exist, and at the same time new sources of sustenance are opened for the working class; that is, the work of the entrepreneur — even if not for himself — is beneficial for the whole, and it is all the more meritorious the less self-interest was the guiding motive.

I feel further compelled to mention here an institution founded by the Chief Forester, Baron von Stenglin: namely the provident fund for the forest workers in the Gelbensande forest inspectorate.

As far as I recall, the arrangement is approximately as follows:

Every forest worker contributes half a Schilling for every Thaler he earns, and in addition one Schilling weekly, to the provident fund.

The administration of the fund, under the supervision of the founder, is entrusted to a number of stewards elected by the workers from their own ranks.

The contributions are thrown into a chest, to which each steward affixes a lock but keeps the key, so that the chest cannot be opened except when all the stewards are assembled.

Quarterly, under the direction of the founder, assemblies of the stewards are held, at which the fund is audited and a record is kept of the state of the fund as well as of what has occurred in the districts of the stewards.

From this fund, the widows of workers receive support, as do the workers themselves when accidents or illness temporarily, or old age permanently, render them unable to earn their bread — support that is greater or smaller according to the degree of their need. The question of whether someone qualifies for support, and how much he shall receive, is decided by the stewards alone.

This institution can become extraordinarily beneficial in its consequences, both for the moral education of the people and for their happiness.

Nothing promotes the moral corruption of the people more than when diligence and thrift, along with laziness and wastefulness, lead to one and the same result: "begging or suffering want in old age."

If the wage is so low that the worker, with the greatest exertion during his vigorous years, can save nothing for the needs of later age, and must then live on the grace and charity of others, then the interest in saving is lost, and courage and desire for exertion — for which no reward beckons in the future — disappear.

On the other hand, the more and the better legal institutions provide for the subsistence of the poor, the more the worker's incentive to diligence and thrift slackens — and under such circumstances we see those enormous poor taxes arise under which even England's prosperity nearly succumbs.

An institution such as that established at Gelbensande, by contrast, places the conduct of every individual worker under the scrutiny of all the others. All workers have an interest in ensuring that none among them, through laziness, disorder, drunkenness, and so forth, becomes dependent on support — and the scrutiny exercised in this manner is infinitely more effective than any that emanates from the authorities.

As proof of how beneficially this institution has affected the morality of the workers, I learned the following:

Two members of the institution had been guilty of a field theft and were thereupon expelled by the other workers with the declaration: "that they could only tolerate honest people among them."

A splendid trait — from which it is evident what moral development the common man in Mecklenburg is capable of.

That this fine act was already a fruit of the provident fund, however, I must doubt; for even though I fully acknowledge the beneficial effects of this institution on the morality of the people, I cannot believe that this institution could already at its inception produce so difficult a fruit. According to my previous observations on the character of the workers, I must rather assume, to explain this fact, that a just, benevolent, and provident treatment of the workers had long preceded the establishment of the provident fund.

What share the worker should have in the product of his labor, what the natural wage of labor should be — this law has not yet been discovered. For the widely held view that "the natural wage of the worker equals the sum of his necessary living requirements" is drawn from reality, but has by no means been proven by rational arguments. What is natural will indeed one day become reality; but perhaps only after millennia — and we must not give ourselves the illusion of believing that what presently takes place at an imperfect stage of development is already the pronouncement of the law of nature itself.

However the still deeply veiled law of nature may read, this much we can know with certainty: that it contradicts the purpose of nature when a person who spends his best years in strenuous labor and lives frugally must, in old age with declining strength, suffer want or live on the charity of others.

Everything, then, that contributes to resolving this contradiction is salutary for humanity, and among these I count the aforementioned provident fund.

To be sure, the small contribution that can be given at the present low level of wages is far too insignificant for the higher goal — "to secure for the diligent and thrifty worker an old age protected from want, free from care, and independent" — to be fully achieved thereby; but for now it is sufficient if only the principle for improving the condition of the workers comes to life.

One might object to this provident fund: "If the worker can save something from his wages, he does not need to throw the saved Schilling into a locked chest; he can keep it himself, and thus gradually accumulate a small capital for his later years."

But we must not overlook that the worker, with what he has saved with difficulty, can procure comforts that border so closely on necessities that it is truly not surprising if he succumbs to the temptation to spend the saved Schilling.

But even without adducing reasons, this objection is refuted by the simple fact: "that the worker — with the rarest exceptions — without such a provident fund accumulates no capital."

In this regard, the workers must — not forever, but at the present stage of their development — be regarded as minors, and it is certainly a fine fulfillment of duty when the person placed higher by rank and education takes care of the best interests of the minors.

Tellow, January 1832.

J. H. von Thunen.
