# Proposal for the Establishment of a Deposit Note Bank in Mecklenburg

*Original: Vorschlag zur Errichtung einer Deposital-Zettelbank in Mecklenburg (1831)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

## 1. Proposal for the Establishment of a Deposit Note Bank in Mecklenburg.

(Examined by Herr von Thünen at Tellow.)

**Editor's Foreword.**

At the most recent autumn meeting of the Teterow District, Herr von Thünen at Tellow read aloud a review of the publication by Herr Chief Steward von Jasmund: "One Million. Free of Charge and Interest-Bearing. A Memorandum for the Illumination of the Matter." This subject aroused such particular interest among the gentlemen present that they universally expressed the wish to see it recommended to the Most Honourable Main Directorate for examination and subsequently to all districts for their assessment. Herr von Thünen undertook to bring forward the expressed wish of the aforementioned district, together with the submission of the memorandum. The Most Honourable Main Directorate, recognizing the importance of the subject, agreed with pleasure to the motion:

1) that the aforementioned publication by Herr Chief Steward von Jasmund be recommended to all districts for deliberation at the next spring assembly, and accordingly

2) that each district should be sent a copy of this publication.

In the gracious directive concerning this point, Herr Count von der Osten expressed the further wish that all the esteemed districts of our association be recommended to submit their expert opinion in such a manner that they would send it to him as a separate appendix with the spring protocols.

As I shall now most obediently comply with the gracious instruction issued to me in this spirit by the Most Honourable Main Directorate, I hasten to bring the discussed memorandum of Herr von Thünen at Tellow to general notice here through printing.

---

In the publication:

"One Million and More,"

Herr Chief Steward von Jasmund has presented the plan for the establishment of a deposit note bank in Mecklenburg in a most lucid manner.

According to the author's calculations, the execution of this plan would provide the country with a net annual income of 30 to 40 thousand Thalers.

Against this calculation, as well as against the author's views, I have nothing substantial to object on the essential points.

However, the execution of this plan faces two difficulties:

1) preventing the counterfeiting of banknotes,
2) avoiding embezzlement in the administration of the bank fund.

But since these difficulties have already been successfully overcome in other states, it should not be difficult to surmount them in Mecklenburg as well.

In any case, the proposal is of such importance that it must not go unheeded. It therefore seems to me useful, indeed necessary, to call forth all possible objections against it, so that if these prove well-founded, the plan may be rejected, but if they do not withstand scrutiny, the matter should not remain at rest but rather come to execution.

In order to hear the voice of the entire country on this matter and to bring about a many-sided examination of the ideas expressed in this publication, it seems to me expedient to bring this subject to deliberation in all districts of the Mecklenburg Patriotic Association, and I therefore take the liberty of proposing to the Most Honourable Main Directorate of the Association:

1) that all districts of the Association be sent a copy of this publication;

2) that all districts be recommended to deliberate on this subject at the next spring assembly, so that a general pronouncement may already be made at the next general meeting, and in case this proves favourable to the plan, an application may be made by the Main Directorate to the Narrower Committee before the next year's Landtag.

If this plan were to come to execution, the Patriotic Association could receive an endowment for expenditures toward public-interest purposes — the lack of which now so often hampers its effectiveness — a school of agriculture and a trade school could be established, and finally canal and road construction could be promoted, without the country contributing anything toward it.

Such a use of the bank's revenue would have an infinitely more beneficial effect on the welfare of the state than if it were applied to the repayment of state debts.

For just as on an individual estate the capital spent on judicious improvements — for example, on marling — yields far higher returns than the customary interest rates and increases the prosperity of the farmer far more than if he had used this capital for debt repayment: so too will the welfare of the state be elevated far more by the judicious public-interest application of a capital than by debt repayment.

In states with a representative constitution, however, it is very difficult to form a fund for such improvements that must arise from the common contribution of all — and improvements that everyone acknowledges as useful often fail to materialize here because agreement on how to raise the costs cannot be reached.

In absolute monarchies, where the sovereign can levy taxes at will and spend the state revenue at his discretion, such a gap between recognition and action does not exist — what is recognized as useful also comes to execution. This explains why such states, as history attests, make more rapid progress each time a wise sovereign appears than do limited monarchies.

This advantage of rapid progress can probably be reconciled with the inestimable benefits that a representative constitution otherwise provides, if only a fund is formed whose revenue is used solely and exclusively for public-interest improvements.

For the formation of such a fund, the proposal of Herr Chief Steward von Jasmund now offers the desired opportunity, and it is especially in this regard that the aforementioned publication seems to me to be worthy of the highest attention.

An increase in the country's revenue by 30,000 Thalers annually may, as long as it falls to unproductive consumption, raise prosperity and population only slightly; but its effect is immeasurable when it is applied to useful public works and to the education of the productive classes of citizens.

What a loss of national income has Mecklenburg already suffered through its bad roads, through the non-utilization of the Elde River, and through the lack of educational institutions for those in trade and industry!

At what level of wealth and population would Mecklenburg now stand if for a century 30,000 Thalers had been spent annually on the improvement of these matters!

Since the author of the above publication has not mentioned an advantage that banknotes have over metallic money in times of enemy occupation — which was already presented earlier by the late Assessor von Blücher — I take the liberty of adding a few remarks on this subject.

During an enemy occupation of the country, the coin supply can be largely exhausted through imposed contributions and plundering.

In such a condition, society is deprived of a part of the advantages that are associated with the use of money. Commerce stagnates and in part reverts, as in a state of nature, to a cumbersome and costly barter trade of goods for goods; the interest rate rises — since with new loans that restrict the interest rate, the laws can easily be circumvented — to an excessive degree, and the nominal value of landed estates sinks so far below their true value that a moratorium becomes necessary, and so forth.

The loss of half the money stock, which in total probably amounts to no more than 3 percent of the national capital, then brings confusion to all relationships and real misfortune upon the country — an effect that stands in no proportion whatsoever to the trifling loss of approximately one and a half percent of the national capital.

The causes of this phenomenon are the following: In the ordinary peaceful circumstances, the use-value of a monetary capital employed in actual commerce is in equilibrium with the returns on capitals invested in agriculture and industry. But if in wartime half the money stock is taken from the country, the use-value of money, which manifests itself through the interest rate on money, rises to nearly double, because with a merely locally — not generally — diminished quantity of money, the price of goods and products, regulated by world trade, remains unchanged, and now the halved money stock is still supposed to mediate the same volume of commerce as before. The returns on capitals that are invested in the construction of new buildings and machines, as well as in improvements in agriculture, are however little or not at all affected by this, and retain — if no other influences take effect — almost entirely their former level. The interest rate on money and the returns on productively invested capital, which formerly coincided at one point, now diverge widely.

Unfortunately, however, the interest rate on money governs the price of estates and immovable property that come to sale, and so from the divergence of the interest rate on money from the returns on productively invested capital, the otherwise puzzling phenomenon becomes explicable: how, with the national wealth almost undiminished, the reduced money stock alone can cause the price of estates to fall by half, and thereby bring misfortune upon many families.

This phenomenon, which has often occurred in history, may also be the reason why a large money stock is considered so influential on the prosperity of a country, and has perhaps given rise to the pernicious mercantilist system — in whose application every nation seeks to wrest metallic money from all other nations. But the increase of the money stock is beneficial only so long as the interest rate on money is still higher than the industrial return on capital. As soon as that point is reached, any further increase becomes ineffectual for the welfare of a people. Scarcity is here an evil, but abundance is no blessing.

The evils with which a country is threatened in wartime through the plundering of its money stock can, through the introduction of paper money to the amount of one and a half million Thalers, not indeed be eliminated, but still be significantly diminished. For paper money has, for several reasons, far less attraction for a foreign enemy than metallic money; it will therefore remain almost entirely in the country even during an enemy occupation, and can thus continue to serve as a medium of exchange. The redemption of the banknotes would have to cease during an enemy occupation for this purpose; but this requires no special provision, because the bank fund held in metallic money will soon be exhausted through frequent presentation of notes in such a case, and the bank's cash payment then ceases of its own accord. The exchange rate of the banknotes will then admittedly fall; but even supposing that it were to sink to 50 percent of the nominal value, 100 Thalers of paper money would still always perform the service of 50 Thalers in coin, and the paper money can nevertheless make a substantial contribution to maintaining commerce in the interior and thus to diminishing the evils described above.

But if the bank remains a pure deposit note bank and does not — which seems advisable to me — engage in the discounting of bills of exchange at all, then the certainty arising from the bank's structure that the banknotes will regain their full nominal value after the war will not allow the exchange rate of these notes to fall far, even during the war.
