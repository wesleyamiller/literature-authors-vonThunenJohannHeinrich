# Remarks on the Treatise on the Effect of Burnt Marl

*Original: Bemerkungen zu der Abhandlung über die Wirkung des gebrannten Mergels (1832)*
*Author: Johann Heinrich von Thünen*
*Translated from German by Claude (LLM)*

---

The author of that treatise was so kind as to share it in manuscript with Count von Schlieffen and myself, and I take the liberty of appending the following remarks to it.

When I first read the farming system of General Beatson in the German translation with its pompous but false title, I considered the praised effects of burnt clay, if not outright charlatanry, then at least self-deception. But when last summer I became acquainted with the writing of the Chief Forester, Baron von Stenglin, "On the Application of Burnt Marl," I became very attentive to this subject. We have come to know too many wonderful effects of marl not to be inclined to consider it capable of an even higher efficacy under altered conditions. Moreover, the author communicated in that writing experiences on the application of burnt marl which place its great effectiveness -- under the conditions prevailing at Gelbensande -- beyond doubt. But where a fact is incontestable, it matters very little whether one can explain it, that is, bring it into agreement with other already known facts, or not. It now seemed very probable to me that the clay which General Beatson used for burning was calcareous, even though he does not mention this important circumstance, and thus it ought properly to be called clay-marl. Fertilizing with burnt lime, however, has long been customary in England, and in some regions of the country takes place as regularly in every rotation as the application of animal manure.

Last summer I had the pleasure of making the personal acquaintance, which I value highly, of the Chief Forester, Baron von Stenglin, and having been invited by him to Gelbensande, I traveled there from Sulz at the beginning of August with Count von Schlieffen.

At Gresenhorst, where the Chief Forester had come to meet us, we saw the first evidence of the effect of burnt marl.

A smallholder from Gresenhorst had fetched a cartload of burnt marl from Gelbensande and spread it thinly over his field, while the remaining part of the plot had been heavily dressed with raw marl. The oats on the entire plot had lodged, and I could perceive no difference between that grown on burnt marl and that grown on raw marl. The smallholder assured us, however -- and his whole manner bore the stamp of honesty and truthfulness -- that before lodging, the oats grown on burnt marl had visibly distinguished themselves by their greater height. (Note No. 1.)

I must confess, however, that my attention here was far less directed at the burnt marl than at a spectacle of another kind -- of a higher nature, I might say.

Several smallholders from Gresenhorst had namely gathered to show us their grain on the field they had brought under cultivation. When we entered the reclaimed land, we first saw a plot of oats that filled me with astonishment. The oats were very tall in the stalk, had large panicles with heavy grains, and had laid down under the weight of their own heaviness; I could scarcely recall having seen such fine oats on the best soil that summer. The adjoining rye was also very strong in the stalk, but had, as was almost universally the case that year, no fully loaded ears. As we continued, we found all the oats of nearly equal strength to those first seen. The sight of the fine grain could well delight the eye of the farmer; but nevertheless the eye was soon drawn away from the grain and turned toward the people through whose diligence it had been produced. They expressed the liveliest joy at being able to show us such fine grain. When we directed a question to one of them, they all answered at once, and in the eyes of all there was satisfaction and cheerfulness at the success of their laborious work. But we recognized what these people had accomplished only fully when we came at last to a piece of land that had not yet been brought under cultivation, and we now beheld the soil in its original condition. It was a low-lying, boggy ground, covered with tussocks. Count von Schlieffen made the remark, to which I had to give my full agreement, that this soil would not repay the costs of reclamation, and that it was a waste of human effort to convert it into arable land by carting in soil. We shared this observation with the smallholders and gave them the advice to turn the plot into a meadow instead. But our suggestion made no impression on them at all, and one of them answered: "If the gentlemen come back in a year, it will already look quite different," and then added slyly: "The Chief Forester had better keep a good watch, or else smallholder N. N. (to whom this piece belongs) will steal the soil from him yet" -- and pointed with his hand at the sand thrown up from a newly dug ditch.

In truth, we only now learned that the greater part of the reclaimed former forest ground, on which we had seen the fine grain, had first received an arable topsoil through heavy application of sand, and that the smallholders had fetched the sand partly from a distance of sixty rods and more -- with the help of their single horse.

Blessings on the diligence of these people!

On the way back, one of the smallholders said openly and in a firm voice:

"If the Chief Forester had not taken an interest in us and obtained this forest land for us, there would now be thirty more rogues in the district."

These people were formerly settled as smallholders in Gresenhorst, but could find as little work there as in the Grand Ducal forests, and since the land allotted to them was far from sufficient to feed them, they may have fallen into the bitterest need -- until through the mediation of the Chief Forester von Stenglin, the aforementioned forest land was leased to them by the high Chamber and Forest Authority for a number of years (if I am not mistaken, for twelve years).

The smallholders then expressed satisfaction with their present situation, but concern about the future, when, after the expiration of the lease, they would have to give up their forest field -- which was designated for replanting with trees.

This concern is probably unfounded; for if no higher state purposes stand in the way -- and what could they be here? -- there can hardly be any doubt that the highest authorities of the land, once they are informed of the situation of these smallholders and their almost unparalleled exertions to earn an honest living, will grant them this field permanently -- or rather the place where they have carted an arable topsoil -- and give it to them in hereditary lease. An uncertainty and anxiety spread over thirty families for ten years destroys too much human happiness for one not to wish most heartily that permanent possession of this field be secured for them already now. (Note No. 2.)

We now took leave of these good people; but my thoughts lingered with them for a long time. "From what a small plot," I said to myself, "a family can live contentedly and happily! How worthy of respect are these people, who, merely to earn their bread in an honest way, do not shy from toil, exertion, and deprivation, and on a leased plot undertake improvement work that yields not half, perhaps in part not even a quarter, of the usual daily wage! What a fine feeling the consciousness must afford of having rescued thirty families from misery and moral corruption!" But herein lies also a serious warning not to settle smallholders in places where they can find no supplementary income. For if no benefactor comes forward to take up their cause, or if circumstances later permit no remedy, then the physical and moral ruin of these people, and the endangering of security and property throughout the surrounding area, is the inevitable consequence of such an ill-considered settlement.

Having arrived on the Gelbensande estate, we first saw a piece of land that had been brought under cultivation only a few years earlier and had been sown with oats that spring; but it now bore not oats but buckwheat, for only along the edges of the ditches did narrow strips of oats appear. The whim of the soil, to bear not the sown crop but another of its own choosing, gave us no favorable opinion of its natural condition. The sown oats had perished, and in their place the grains left over from the previous year's buckwheat stubble had sprouted.

The next morning we first inspected the Beatson farming implements, about whose value and usefulness the Chief Forester will hopefully inform us himself; then a very practically arranged hay scale, by means of which the hay given daily to the cattle is weighed almost without any loss of time and without additional labor. We then went to a grazing enclosure near the farmstead, which was very densely grassed and showed vigorous growth. The field here, as on the adjoining rye enclosure, had been laid into beds only six feet wide. The land is on the whole very level, to all appearances only slightly above sea level, and suffers greatly from wetness. The soil itself consists of a mixture of sand and peaty earth, with remnants of old tree roots intermixed. Given this nature and situation of the soil, the narrow beds appear to be a condition of its cultivation, without which at least no winter grain could be grown.

Quite distinctive, however, is the method of creating these narrow beds. Since laying them out by hoeing requires great precision on the part of the workers and involves many difficulties, and since the beds would partly be destroyed again by harrowing in the seed, the Chief Forester has the field plowed level -- that is, without beds -- sown, and the seed harrowed in; and then, with a water-furrow plow fitted with long wings on both sides, a furrow is drawn every six feet. As the soil from the furrows is pushed by the long wings toward the center of the strips, the narrow beds are thereby directly formed.

I did not see the water-furrow plow itself, but it certainly deserves a more detailed description and illustration; for there are probably many other areas that are considered unsuitable for grain cultivation on account of insufficient slope and excessive wetness, but which could be converted into productive arable land by this cultivation method. On our upland soils we shall naturally spare ourselves such work. Yet the thought occurred to me whether, on very rich soil, the laying out of such beds might not entirely prevent or at least greatly reduce the lodging of wheat; for we see everywhere that the wheat next to the drawn water furrows lodges later than the rest.

The rye on the adjoining enclosure had already been mowed and stood in shocks. The rye stubble was uncommonly thick with young clover, and only very rarely have I seen such a fine clover sward on our best upland soil in autumn.

We then drove to the more distant part of the estate and came to the marl pit, where the compost -- which the Chief Forester mentions in the aforementioned writing -- is prepared.

Compost preparation by mixing and covering the manure with ordinary field soil is expensive on account of the cost of carting the soil and the greatly increased cost of the subsequent manure hauling, and under ordinary conditions will hardly pay for itself. (Note No. 3.) At Gelbensande, however, where the compost is prepared from humus-rich soil available everywhere in the ground, and where the humus particles not only absorb the gases escaping from the manure but also, through contact with the manure itself, undergo fermentation and are transformed into mild, plant-nourishing humus -- the case may be different; and certainly the state of cultivation of the land is thereby improved.

We then entered the plot which the Chief Forester has described in more detail in his writing on pages 13 and 14. One part of this plot was dressed with burnt marl in the spring of 1829, and the other part in the autumn of the same year with ten cartloads of manure per 100 square rods.

In the summer of 1830, the entire plot bore rye, and now -- in the summer of 1831 -- oats. We now directed our full attention to the boundary between the manured section and the section treated with burnt marl. With regard to the size and strength of the oats, we could perceive no definite difference between the two parts; but certainly the oats on the burnt marl were not inferior to those on manured ground. In terms of color and the time of ripening, we found a similar difference to that usually seen between grain on marled and unmarled fields -- although here both sections had previously been treated with raw marl. The oats on manure were already close to ripening and showed everywhere a yellowish color, while those on burnt marl still had many green stalks and panicles. The oats were on the whole to be called good, though by far not as strong as those we had seen at Gresenhorst.

It is a remarkable fact that the burnt marl still had as strong an effect in the third crop as a manuring of ten cartloads per 100 rods. Such an effect of burnt marl, however, can perhaps only occur on very humus-rich soil, and would be unlikely to repeat itself on ordinary arable land.

Very striking, and perhaps leading to new insights, is the phenomenon that burnt marl exerts so great an effect on already marled land, whereas experience almost universally teaches that a second application of raw marl -- when the first marling was sufficiently heavy -- remains without effect.

From there we went to the kilns and watched the process of burning the marl, then drove through part of the very extensive Gelbensande forest, where everything we saw was orderly and without deficiency, and then on the return journey passed by a field of oats, part of which had been treated with burnt marl. Sharper eyes than mine could already perceive from a distance the difference in the oats in favor of the burnt marl.

Regarding the mode of action of burnt marl and its more general applicability, I cannot yet express a definite opinion after these observations, but can only voice some conjectures:

1) The acid present in the soil is neutralized both by raw and by burnt marl; but where the soil contains a great deal of acid, the neutralization will be more completely effected by burnt marl, because the latter is applied in greater quantity.

2) Burnt marl probably decomposes plant fibers and insoluble humus to a higher degree than raw marl.

3) The burning, or rather the roasting, of the marl imparts to the lime contained therein the ability to attract carbonic acid gas and moisture from the atmosphere, and as the lime then gives off the carbonic acid thus formed to the plants, it serves as a vehicle for nourishing plants from the atmosphere -- at least the phenomenon that burnt marl has a favorable effect even on already marled soil which still contains lime leads to this conjecture.

If the latter effect of burnt marl were the predominant one, then it could be applied in generous measure on any soil without danger of exhausting the field; one could obtain richer harvests through it without the soil having to contribute anything to nourish this increase of the harvest.

But as long as this has not been established by longer experience, I consider it advisable to apply burnt marl only on small areas. This caution, however, must not divert our attention from the subject; rather, it is to be wished that experiments with burnt marl be conducted in many regions of the country on as many different soil types as possible. Particular consideration deserves the fact that the English -- at least on several soil types -- have found a regularly repeated fertilizing with burnt lime to be advantageous, even though this fertilizing is very costly. What the English achieve through burnt lime, we can perhaps accomplish with far less expense through burnt marl.

Sinclair says in his "Fundamental Principles of Agriculture," page 273 of the German translation:

"Many farmers spend ten shillings per acre (approximately one and a third thalers per 100 square rods) annually on liming their land, and still find their account in it. The beneficial influence on the thriving of green crops alone is sufficient. These can indeed also be obtained with the help of much and strong manure; but experience has always confirmed that with the application of calcareous substances to achieve the same end, a far smaller quantity of animal and vegetable substances was required."

On page 274, however, Sinclair adds the cautionary rule:

"Lime should never be repeatedly used on weak or lean soils except mixed with a compost manure; and even in this exceptional case, the fields so treated should be immediately laid down to grass."

At Gelbensande, where the soil contains such an abundance of humus that the decomposition and removal of a portion of it improves the texture of the soil and increases its productive capacity, the large-scale application of burnt marl cannot lead to the exhaustion of the soil, but can only have a beneficial effect. However, near the farmstead there is a higher-lying, very sandy piece of arable land with little humus -- and if the Chief Forester kindly continues to share his experiences with the public, this plot will be the first to instruct us about the mode of action of burnt marl and the ultimate result of its application.

The soil at Gelbensande, as well as the systematic cultivation method applied to it, is so different from ordinary conditions in Mecklenburg that it was very interesting and instructive for me to become acquainted with both.

The initial reclamation of this soil is very costly; for here too, as at Gresenhorst, a portion of it had to be heavily dressed with sand to make it productive. Then its further fertilization through compost preparation, through application of burnt marl, and so forth, involves costs that are unknown elsewhere.

Under these circumstances, the question frequently pressed itself upon me: whether such reclamation projects, for an entrepreneur who must bear the initial cultivation costs alone but enjoys the fruits thereof only during his own lifetime, are attended with profit or not?

Whatever the case may be, it is certainly true that through such enterprises the state acquires a capital that did not previously exist, and at the same time new sources of sustenance are opened for the working class; that therefore the work of the entrepreneur -- even if not for himself -- is a beneficial one for the whole, and is all the more meritorious the less self-interest was the guiding motive.

I feel further compelled to mention here an institution established by the Chief Forester, Baron von Stenglin -- namely, the provident fund for the forest workers in the Gelbensande Forest Inspectorate.

As far as I can recall, the arrangement is approximately as follows:

Every forest worker contributes half a shilling from every thaler he earns, and in addition one shilling weekly, to the provident fund.

The administration of the fund is entrusted, under the supervision of the founder, to a number of overseers elected by the workers from among themselves.

The contributions are dropped into a chest, to which each overseer attaches a lock but keeps the key, so that the chest cannot be opened unless all overseers are assembled.

Quarterly, under the direction of the founder, meetings of the overseers are held, at which the fund is audited and a record is drawn up of its condition as well as of what has occurred in the overseers' districts.

From this fund, the widows of workers receive support, as do the workers themselves when they are rendered unable to earn their bread for a time through misfortune or illness, or permanently through the infirmity of old age -- more or less according to the degree of their need. The question of whether someone qualifies for support and how much he shall receive is decided by the overseers alone.

This institution can become extraordinarily beneficial in its consequences, both for the moral development of the people and for their happiness.

Nothing probably promotes the moral corruption of the people more than when diligence and thrift lead to the same result as idleness and wastefulness -- namely, "to begging or to want in old age."

If the wage is so low that the worker, with the greatest exertion during his vigorous years, can save nothing for the needs of later age, and must eventually live on the grace and charity of others, then the interest in saving is lost, and courage and desire for exertion -- for which no reward beckons in the future -- disappear.

But on the other hand, the more and the better provision is made by legal institutions for the subsistence of the poor, the more the worker's incentive to diligence and thrift slackens -- and under such circumstances we see those enormous poor rates arise under which even England's prosperity nearly collapses.

An institution such as the one established at Gelbensande, by contrast, places the conduct of each individual worker under the control of all the rest. All workers have an interest in ensuring that none among them, through idleness, disorder, drunkenness, and so forth, becomes dependent on support -- and the control exercised in this way is infinitely more effective than any exercised by the authorities.

As evidence of how beneficially this institution has worked on the morality of the workers, I learned the following:

Two members of the institution had been guilty of field theft, and were therefore expelled by the other workers with the declaration "that they could tolerate only honest people among them."

A splendid trait -- from which it is evident what moral development the common man in Mecklenburg is capable of.

That this fine act was already a fruit of the provident fund, however, I must doubt; for even if I fully acknowledge the beneficial effects of this institution on the morality of the people, I cannot believe that such a difficult fruit could be produced by this institution at the very moment of its founding. According to my observations of the character of workers thus far, I must rather assume, in explanation of that fact, that a just, benevolent, and provident treatment of the workers had long preceded the establishment of the provident fund.

What share the worker should have in the product of his labor, what the natural wage of labor should be -- this law has not yet been discovered. For the widely held view that "the natural wage of the worker equals the sum of his necessary subsistence needs" is taken from reality, but by no means proven by rational arguments. What is natural will indeed one day become reality; but perhaps only after millennia -- and we must not yield to the delusion that what now takes place at an imperfect stage of development is already the pronouncement of the natural law itself.

However the deeply veiled natural law may read, this much we can know with certainty: that it is in contradiction with the purpose of nature when a person who spends the best years of his life in strenuous labor and lives frugally thereby must, in old age with declining strength, suffer want or live on the charity of others.

Everything, then, that contributes to resolving that contradiction is salutary for humanity, and among such things I count the aforementioned provident fund.

To be sure, the small contribution that can be given at the present low level of daily wages is far too insignificant for the higher goal --

"to secure for the diligent and thrifty worker an old age protected from want, free of care, and independent" --

to be fully attained thereby; but for the present it is sufficient if only the principle of improving the condition of the workers enters into life.

One might object to this provident fund:

"If the worker can save something from his wages, he need not throw the saved shilling into the locked chest; he can keep it himself, and thus gradually accumulate a small capital for his later years."

But we must not overlook that the worker can use what he has saved with great effort to procure comforts that border so closely on necessities that it is truly not to be wondered at if he succumbs to the temptation to spend the saved shilling.

But even without citing reasons, the above objection is refuted by the simple fact:

"that the worker -- with the rarest exceptions -- accumulates no capital without such a provident fund."

In this respect, the workers must -- not forever, but for the present stage of their development -- be regarded as minors, and it is surely a fine fulfillment of one's calling when the person placed higher by station and education provides for the welfare of those who are, in this sense, minors.

Tellow, January 1832.

J. H. von Thunen.
