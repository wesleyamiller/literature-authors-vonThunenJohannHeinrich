LV.
Wool Market and Wool Warehouse

[By Dr. von Thünen of Tellow.]

With regard to the question: "whether it would be more advantageous to concentrate the wool produced in Mecklenburg at one market or to scatter it across several locations," the esteemed District of Rostock has very aptly presented the disadvantages of wool markets in general, but has entirely overlooked the advantages that wool markets provide.

Among these advantages I count most especially the closer connection into which producers enter with wool merchants at the wool markets. The sheep breeder learns here candidly from the buyer the defects of his wool; through comparison of the price received with the price paid for other lots of wool, he can come to know the qualities of wool that are particularly valued and highly paid for, and thus form a goal for his endeavors. Moreover, through the gathering of many producers and the mutual exchange of information that necessarily results from it, the knowledge of individual breeders gradually becomes the common property of all.

What enormous progress has sheep breeding made in Mecklenburg since the year 1818 — the founding of the wool market in Güstrow — not only in the refinement of the flocks, but also in the washing and treatment of the wool?

How meager, by contrast, was the progress in the period from 1800 to 1818 — when fine sheep breeding was already flourishing in other German states — how uncertain all efforts, how deficient the knowledge of sheep breeders?

It is true that people say wool buyers frequently express false criticism and often deliberately seek to mislead producers.

The author has indeed heard individual buyers put forward false views and assertions — for not every wool buyer is a wool connoisseur — but he does not recall that a wool merchant ever deliberately put forward untrue views against him or sought to mislead him. The case may, however, occasionally occur, but given the connection in which sheep breeders stand with one another through the patriotic association, absurd claims of this kind are entirely harmless.

But even assuming that the Mecklenburg sheep breeders no longer needed instruction from the wool merchant — which, generally speaking, cannot be maintained — the personal meeting of sheep breeders with wool merchants remains necessary in the future as well; for through changes in fashion, through new discoveries in machinery, even through the competition of other wool-producing distant countries, the demands made upon wool are subject to constant change. Whoever steadfastly pursues a goal in wool production that is truly correct for the moment can, after a few years, find himself in the position of producing a wool that is no longer in demand in world trade and whose production is entirely unprofitable.

Therefore, the wool markets must remain and must not be converted into wool warehouses. But nonetheless it remains true what has been said in the Rostock opinion regarding the value of wool warehouses.

In order to enjoy the advantages of both, wool market and wool warehouse must therefore be combined with one another.

The Rostock opinion states: that the producer has not always received for his wool at the Mecklenburg markets the price that the prevailing market conditions warranted; that market commerce in general exposes the seller too much to the caprice of the buyers.

This is correct; but the evil lies not so much in the markets as such, but rather in the smallness of our markets, in the dispersal of Mecklenburg wool across three or four locations.

The larger the wool markets are, the greater the competition among buyers and sellers, the more closely the price actually paid approaches the appropriate price — that is, the world price of wool. Indeed, one can say that the markets of Breslau and Berlin regulate the world price of wool.

Things are quite different at our small markets. Here, by the nature of the matter, a local price must form that deviates more or less from the world price.

The large wool merchant can buy 10 to 20,000 stone of wool at the markets of Berlin and Breslau with the same expenditure of time, travel, and living costs as 2 to 5,000 stone at our small markets. For this reason alone, the price at the small markets must be lower than at the large ones. But the situation for the small markets is made worse by the fact that when, by chance, some significant wool merchants appear in greater numbers than usual, a portion of them must depart entirely unsatisfied and has expended time and costs entirely in vain. For the large competition of buyers at the small market does not change the world price of wool, and the large wool merchant will not pay above the appropriate price but rather depart again without buying anything. This may have happened, but it then deters these wool merchants from further attendance at our markets. In fact, the number of large outside wool merchants at our markets diminishes more and more with each year, and they would abandon us entirely if they had not in individual years, when there was great pressure to sell and few buyers, purchased at excessively low prices far below the appropriate level, and thereby been compensated for many a futile journey.

This compensation flows, however, from the coffers of the producers, is taken from them, and is lost to the country — it is therefore entirely contrary to the interest of the whole.

But the evil grows ever worse the more the distribution of wool through the wool warehouse in Rostock and through the house sales reintroduced a couple of years ago increases. The moment when the wool producers will have to pay dearly for this may not be far off.

If the market price of our wool is to always remain close to the appropriate price, then regularly every year at least 50,000 stone must come to market — and in order to counteract the fluctuations that may nonetheless occur, a wool warehouse for the unsold wool must be connected with the market.

In order to bring together the quantity of 50,000 stone of wool, however, there may be only one wool market in all of Mecklenburg, and connected with this market, at the same location, there must be a wool warehouse.
