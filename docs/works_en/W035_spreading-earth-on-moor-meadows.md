## On Spreading Earth on Moor Meadows

"Where water for irrigation of meadows is available, there is no doubt as to which means should be employed to increase their grass yield.

Far less investigated are the means by which meadows that, due to lack of water, are not capable of being irrigated can be brought to a higher state of cultivation.

Previously (in Mecklenburg) such meadows were left to nature and yielded a meager return. About 24 years ago, the late Herr Domänenrath Pogge, highly deserving of agriculture, in conjunction with his eldest son, Herr Pogge of Zierstorf, invented a method for improving such meadows, which was initially crowned with brilliant success and attracted general attention. The procedure consists essentially of the following: the meadow is leveled, completely drained by ditches two and a half to three feet deep, then

covered with earth approximately four inches high, so that the meadow turf is covered everywhere, and thereafter planted with green fodder and undersown clover and grass seed.

In honor of the inventor, this operation has been called "Poggeling of meadows" in Mecklenburg.

The initial success of this improvement, when properly carried out on a suitable meadow, was astonishingly great, almost miraculous and mysterious. The grass yield of the meadows so improved not only equaled but even exceeded the yield of the best clover fields and the finest irrigated meadows. The author has harvested from a meadow that in its natural state yielded only half a cartload of hay per Morgen, in a fruitful year as much as three four-horse cartloads of 1,600 to 1,800 pounds per Morgen. This improvement affected not only the quantity but equally favorably the quality of the hay: when well cured, it possessed such fattening capacity that oxen have been fully fattened with it.

Only later did one recognize the cause of this wonderful effect. Through the four-inch earth covering, the former meadow turf undergoes putrefaction and is at once transformed into direct plant nourishment. The humus, which had become soluble to a high degree, was however bound to be exhausted quite soon, and indeed already in the third harvest, and even more in the fourth, a significant decline in yield became apparent; and one now had to resolve to provide the meadows with compensation through a manuring repeated every two years.

The manurings were initially of extraordinary effectiveness, and the meadows remained thereby for a series of years in their first brilliant condition. Later, however, the effect of the manurings grew weaker and weaker. At least this has been the case on the estate of Tellow.

Since I possess exact records of the costs of Poggeling and their results in various periods and under different treatments for a meadow belonging to the estate of Tellow, situated not far from the farmstead (called Gilwiese), I shall communicate these in the following overview.

This meadow, 2,150 Mecklenburg square rods or approximately 18 Magdeburg Morgen in size, was in its natural state very springy, and bore, with some intermixed grasses and herbs, mostly marsh horsetail (*Equisetum palustre*). In the period from 1813 to 1820 inclusive, the meadow yielded on average 12.4 cartloads of hay per year (according to Appendix B, see under Supplement XIV).

The costs of spreading earth on the meadow, including leveling and sowing, amounted (according to Appendix A, see under Supplement XIV), when the value of a Berlin Scheffel of rye on the estate itself equals 1.3 Thaler Gold — to 234 Thaler 47 Schilling N. This comes to 13 Thaler 2 Schilling N per Morgen, or 15 Thaler 10 Schilling Prussian Currency. The spreading of earth on the meadow took place in the winter of 1820/21. Due to very deficient drainage, in the year 1821 the meadow bore green fodder only in patches, and the hay yield was only 11 cartloads.

In the year 1822, the meadow yielded, although it was not yet fully turfed and the drainage was still imperfect — 32 cartloads of hay.

In the year 1823, a part of the meadow was manured for the first time; in the years 1825 and 1827, however, the entire meadow was manured with approximately 5 cartloads per Morgen.

In the 6 years from 1823 to 1828 inclusive, the average yield of the meadow was 37.7 cartloads of hay. During this period, the highest yield occurred in the year 1823 and amounted to 44 cartloads; the average yield in this period had thus risen to three times the former yield.

In the year 1829, the meadow again received a manuring, and in the years 1831 and 1832 it was even manured two years in succession, though only lightly, with approximately 3¼ cartloads per Morgen.

Despite these repeated manurings, the average yield in the 4 years from 1829 to 1832 inclusive sank to 28.9 cartloads.

The meadow thus yielded only 16.5 cartloads of hay more than it had given in its natural state, which had been purchased at an expenditure of approximately 54 cartloads of manure.

If one now reckons that the manure resulting from the feeding of one cartload of hay is equal in value to 2 cartloads of stable manure, then the additional yield of 16.5 cartloads of hay returned only 33 cartloads of manure, while the meadow had received 54 cartloads.

Under these circumstances, it was clear that the manuring of the meadows could only be continued at the expense of the arable land, and the decision was made to abandon top-dressing entirely.

It was, however, painful to let meadows that had caused so much effort and cost, granted so much joy and awakened such hopes, sink back into their former crude state, and the owner therefore resolved to plough up and fallow a portion of the Poggeled meadows, while another portion, including the Gilwiese, would be strewn with gypsum every year.

When in the year 1833 the Gilwiese was gypsed for the first time, the clover had almost entirely disappeared from the meadow, the sown timothy grass had become sparse, and the native marsh horsetail, which, so long as clover and grass grew luxuriantly, had seemed almost eradicated, predominated again.

I therefore had no hope of a favorable effect from the gypsum, and the result in that year matched my expectations, for the meadow yielded only 29 cartloads of hay. After the second gypsing in 1834, however, the scene changed; the meadow clothed itself with yellow clover (*Medicago lupulina*), yellow vetch (*Lathyrus pratensis*), and bird vetch (*Vicia cracca*) and appeared in an almost never-before-seen splendor. The hay yield was 42½ cartloads. It seemed as if the gypsum had itself called forth the plants upon which it acts and which were not yet present at the time of its application.

Since then, the Gilwiese has been gypsed every year, and in the 7 years from 1833 to 1839 inclusive has given an average yield of 35.5 cartloads of hay.

The meadow thus yielded, in comparison with what it had produced in its natural state, an additional yield of 23.2 cartloads.

The interest on the capital invested in improving the meadow amounts to approximately [unclear] annually; the 10 hundredweight of gypsum used annually cost [unclear]; together 14½ Thaler. Thus 23.2 cartloads of hay were obtained through an annual expenditure of 14½ Thaler.

Up to the year 1839, then, the result of the gypsing was a most favorable one.

Now, however, in the last two years, the growth of grass and clover has declined so greatly despite continued gypsing that only one cutting could be taken, and the yield fell in the year 1840 to 23 cartloads, and in this year has sunk to as low as 16 cartloads.

Is this to be attributed solely to the unfavorableness of the weather, or does the gypsum, with prolonged application, fail in its fertilizing effect, as the stable manure did before?

According to Sprengel's excellent treatise on fertilizers, which gives us so many new insights, 1,000 pounds of clover hay contain only 4.4 pounds of sulfuric acid, while 60 pounds of gypsum contain 34 pounds of this acid.

A harvest of 3,000 pounds of hay per Morgen accordingly consumes only 13½ pounds of sulfuric acid, and in 60 pounds of gypsum there would be nourishment for 2½ clover harvests. Even if a portion of the gypsum dissolved in water is washed away by rain, such a stock of gypsum can ultimately accumulate in the soil that a fresh addition through renewed gypsing remains useless and fruitless, while the soil has become impoverished in other substances that serve as nourishment for plants.

Perhaps a manuring with stable manure would now again be of great effectiveness; probably, however, a marling would already produce a significant result, especially on those spots where sorrel appears. Future experiments will provide clarification on this matter.

In any case, it already seems to be emerging that one must not incessantly apply one and the same fertilizing agent, but must alternate between the application of stable manure, gypsum, marl, and the like, in order to maintain the Poggeled meadows in a high state of cultivation.

My experiments with ploughing up the Poggeled meadows have so far led to no clear result.

The first meadow to be ploughed up was turned in autumn, manured and fallowed the following summer, and in the third year planted with buckwheat, under which clover and timothy seed was sown. The meadow thereupon yielded a significant hay harvest in the next three years, but deteriorated already in the fourth year.

Several later experiments all failed due to a faulty procedure. The fallow was planted with rapeseed already in the first year, and clover and timothy seed was sown at the same time as the early-sown rapeseed. The rapeseed grew

so luxuriantly in autumn that it suppressed not only the clover but also the timothy grass, then largely rotted in spring, yielded a small harvest and poor seed, and left behind a meadow that bore no clover, little timothy, and almost only wild-growing grasses, which in the following years provided a meager yield.

On other estates in Mecklenburg, a better method has in part been employed — for example, oats in the first year, potatoes with manuring in the second, and green fodder with undersown clover and grass seed in the third — and accordingly a more favorable result has been obtained.

It would be desirable for those farmers who have made successful experiments in this regard to bring their procedures and the results achieved to general knowledge.

When one digs new ditches in a boggy meadow and leaves the excavated earth lying on the ditch bank, in the following years scarcely a trace of vegetation appears on this earth. After several years, however, this initially completely barren earth clothes itself with fine noble grasses. From this it follows that the sour, infertile humus is transformed into mild, plant-nourishing humus through the action of the atmosphere. The meadow thus harbors in its bosom nourishment for countless harvests and can become its own nourisher. Through the ploughing up of the meadow and the exposure of the meadow soil to the air, this is partly achieved; but the process of transformation from sour to mild humus proceeds too slowly for this to be fully accomplished in a single fallow year. Perhaps the chemists can suggest to us a not too costly means by which this process may be accelerated; perhaps the strewing of quicklite is already sufficient for this purpose.

The boggy meadow soil, however, is without the admixture of sand or mineral earth unsuitable for the production of clover and many noble grasses — not because it lacks the siliceous earth that serves as nourishment for the grasses, for this is as a rule contained in it for countless harvests — but because its mechanical condition, its great porosity, does not suit the noble plants. For this reason, the spreading of earth on the meadows must always precede if one wishes to have a favorable result from gypsing and ploughing up.

The spreading of earth on meadows has almost fallen out of use, because these meadows, when manured with dung every two years, are not manure-producing but manure-consuming. But if these meadows can be maintained at a high level of yield through the combined but alternating application of gypsum, marl, periodic ploughing up, and the like, and if the natural scientists can indicate means by which, without great expense, the transformation of sour humus into mild humus can be accelerated, then Poggeling will once more become the lever of higher meadow cultivation, and the treasure that lies almost untouched in the great meadow expanses of northern Germany will then come to benefit the world."
