## Part One: Proposal and First Results (Volume 25, pp. 202-212)

### IX. Deepening the Topsoil

Dr. von Thunen of Tellow presented to the general assembly of the Mecklenburg Patriotic Society of 1837 the following proposition:

The resolution of the important question — whether it is advantageous to deepen the topsoil by bringing up a portion of the raw subsoil — requires the conduct of two experiments, to be carried out approximately in the following manner:

**First experiment:** On a level field, a spot is selected where the subsoil is so hard and infertile that no plant roots have penetrated into it; then the topsoil is removed from this spot, and the exposed subsoil is divided into four squares, each of about 2 square rods, with gaps of one foot between them. The first square is dug out exactly 10 inches deep, the second 8 inches, the third 6 inches, and the fourth 4 inches deep, and the earth is carted away. Thereupon the resulting depressions are filled back in with the topsoil previously set aside — and if this is not sufficient, with earth of the same quality from the surrounding field — and finally, at the proper time, the squares are sown without manuring with the crop that the surrounding field bears.

**Second experiment:** On a spot similar in location and soil, perhaps immediately adjacent to the first, the topsoil is likewise removed, and on the raw subsoil four squares are again marked out; then the first square is manured with a quantity of dung equal to 30 cartloads per 100 square rods, the second with 20 cartloads, and the third with 10 cartloads of dung per 100 square rods; the fourth, however, is not manured. Then, after repeated digging to give the soil the proper looseness, all squares are sown at the same time as the surrounding field. At harvest, the yield of each square would be precisely weighed and recorded; a diary would be kept regarding the time of sowing, harvest, and the other tasks involved.

These experiments, continued for 3 years and conducted at two locations in the country by two different experimenters, would give us facts instead of the reasoning we have had until now, and would furnish us with the data to answer the question: whether it is advantageous to consume the actual or supposed surplus of manure through increased grain cultivation, or to invest it in the soil through fertilization of the deepened topsoil.

For the political economist, this subject is perhaps of even higher interest than for the farmer; for as the comparison with Belgium and England shows, it is very probable that if a country with a five-inch topsoil provides subsistence for a population of 4,000 people per square mile, this country with a ten-inch topsoil, under otherwise equal circumstances, can feed at least 6,000 people per square mile. The future prosperity of a state, its wealth, its population, and its power are thus essentially dependent on whether its farmers employ their available manure capital in this or that manner.

For a private individual, conducting the experiments proposed above is too costly; but for a society as numerous as ours, it seems a worthy task not only to be useful through the dissemination of what is already known, but also to promote the investigation of the still unknown and thereby to expand science itself. The question is therefore: whether a portion of the surplus of the main treasury should be applied to the costs of such an experiment?

The general assembly approved the proposal and resolved that the proposed experiments should be conducted at the expense of the main treasury. Dr. von Thunen was requested to undertake the arrangement and direction of the same, to determine the locations at which they should be conducted, and subsequently to report to the Society on them as well as on the results obtained.

Dr. von Thunen accepted this commission, and after the experiments had been carried out — by himself at Tellow, by Count von Schlieffen at Schwandt, by Mr. Berlin at Liepen, and by Mr. Pensioner Meyer at Mandelshagen — he presented the following report on the results to the general assembly of 1840.

The experimental field was fortunately in a sheltered location, and no disturbance from accumulated snow was observed.

Rather, from the beginning of vegetation until harvest, there appeared in the luxuriance of plant growth across the different plots a surprisingly regular gradation corresponding to the heavier manuring and deeper topsoil. Only in plots Nos. 1 and 2, with 10- and 8-inch topsoil, could no appreciable difference in the vigor of the plants be discerned; but in one corner of plot No. 1, from an undeterminable cause, the wheat plants stood thin. This irregularity — the only one that the experimental field presented — is then also the sole reason that the yield of No. 1 falls behind No. 2 by 1 1/2 pounds.

The wheat on the 4 plots with humus-rich earth showed leaf rust after blooming, while the wheat on the plots with raw earth remained completely free of rust.

Tellow, 19 June 1840.

J. H. von Thunen.

### Supplement

At the autumn meeting of the Teterow District on 10 November 1840, Dr. von Thunen was further kind enough to share the following report on the experiment, which had also been continued in this year:

**Yield of the 4-inch topsoil in potatoes:**

[Note: The tabular data here is almost entirely garbled by OCR. The text mentions plots of raw earth 6 inches deep, manured in 1838 with 8 1/2 cartloads per 100 square rods. Each plot contains 2 1/4 square rods.]

Tellow, 9 November 1840.

J. H. von Thunen.

This communication from Dr. von Thunen gave rise at the meeting to a wide-ranging discussion about the vegetative capacity of the soil with and without added humus, since according to the foregoing communication, the deepening of the topsoil yielded a very favorable result, even in relation to the manured topsoil. The more general view, however, was that it would be appropriate to continue the deepening of the topsoil in the long run with an increase in the applied humus, since without a proportionate increase in humus the deeper topsoil would become impoverished.

In connection with this, the observation was made that the thriving of potatoes could be achieved with a smaller quantity of dung if one placed a little short dung individually at each potato when planting. The potatoes would grow more luxuriantly and productively than if the entire field surface were manured. In other regions, poorer people sought to achieve a richer potato harvest in this way; yet it was conceded that the yield of the following crop would again decline if it were not manured anew.

Mayor Reuter of Stavenhagen confirmed from his experience the advantages of deep plowing, adding however that he had brought his field to a very high state of cultivation.

A manuring of 20 cubic feet on 2 1/4 square rods equals 889 cubic feet per 100 square rods. According to several measurements conducted at Tellow, a fully loaded four-horse cartload of dung contains on average 106 cubic feet (Hamburg measure). The manuring of 889 cubic feet thus amounts to 889/106 = 8.38, or approximately 8 1/2 cartloads. 20 cubic feet on 2 1/4 square rods is therefore 8 1/4 cartloads per 100 square rods.

[Note: 106 Hamburg cubic feet are approximately 80 Rhenish cubic feet.]

Strikingly large is the yield of the plots with raw earth on the estate Schwandt. Perhaps the sandy subsoil here was not entirely stripped of humus as the experimental plot. But perhaps also, as Count von Schlieffen surmises, the water-soluble salts contained in the manure, which give the soil no coloration, sank down into the permeable subsoil and now, after the removal of the upper earth, benefit the plants.

If the latter were the case, one could conclude with probability that fields with sandy, permeable subsoil can be deepened without experiencing any disadvantage even in the first crops.

In any case, the need is shown here to have the topsoil and subsoil of the various experimental fields chemically analyzed.

The extraordinarily wet weather of the summer of 1841 disturbed this experiment in two respects:

1. The raw clay soil of plots 5-8 was already in a wet condition unfavorable for receiving the seed at the time of sowing, and was kept in a muddy state by the continuous downpours throughout the entire growing season. The humus-rich topsoil of plots Nos. 1-4, on the other hand, absorbed the water without becoming muddy. This year's yield therefore cannot serve as a norm for the ratio of productive capacity between topsoil and raw clay.

2. On the south and west sides of the experimental field, along the fence, probably as a result of protection against the excessive rain, a stronger vegetation of the oats appeared in a width of approximately 1/2 rod than on the part of the experimental field distant from the fence. This beneficial shelter affected plots Nos. 2, 3, 4, and 5 from one side, No. 1 from two sides, but the remaining plots not at all.

The value of this experiment for science is therefore limited this year to the comparison of the yields of plots Nos. 1, 2, 3, and 4, which enjoyed the protection of the fence in the same manner and to the same degree.
