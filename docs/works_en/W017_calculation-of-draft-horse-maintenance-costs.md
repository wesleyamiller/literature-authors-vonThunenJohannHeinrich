## Foreword

To decide the questions:

"Whether, for the present state of cultivation in Mecklenburg, the conversion of our country roads into paved highways is at all advisable; and in which regions of the country the benefit that the highways provide outweighs the costs of their construction and maintenance?"

the solution of the following tasks seems to me necessary:

1) How high are the transport costs of grain on our present country roads?

2) How high are the transport costs of grain on paved highways?

3) How high must the highway toll be?

4) If, according to the interesting idea of Herr Pogge of Striesenow, the highway is to be toll-free and the costs of construction and maintenance of the improved road are instead to be borne by the estates in proportion to the benefit that the improved road provides them: according to what principles is the contribution of each individual estate to be determined?

Only toward the solution of the first task am I able to offer a contribution for now, and I would not bring this fragment to public attention were I not obliged to comply with a friendly request that has been addressed to me on multiple occasions. *) I have accordingly compiled in the following essay the data that my accounts and experience furnish, and combined them into an overview.

*) I cannot let this opportunity pass without publicly expressing, on my own behalf and on behalf of all members of the Patriotic Society, our most respectful thanks to the highly esteemed contributor for the self-sacrificing and active interest that he tirelessly devotes to the work of the Society and to the flourishing of all national institutions, as this present essay once again demonstrates. If I therefore permitted myself -- although I am well aware that Herr Dr. von Thunen is not only burdened with business but that his health has also been severely affected by strenuous work -- to request the communication of this essay, which is so highly interesting especially at the present time, I hope, in consideration of my good intentions, for forgiveness.

The data required for the solution of the second task I cannot draw from my own experience, and what I know about the matter is insufficient and lacks reliability.

It would be very desirable if someone who has gathered experience on this subject would communicate a similar calculation of transport costs for paved highways, as is here provided for ordinary country roads, or would at least provide information on some main points, such as maintenance costs of the wagon equipment and horseshoeing, the strength of the load averaged over the entire year (thus including the period when the improved road is rendered nearly useless by heavy snowfall); and I believe I express the wish of the Mecklenburg public when I ask foreign agriculturalists who possess this knowledge for their kind communication of the same.

The third task -- "how high must the highway toll be" -- is of great importance.

With a low highway toll, the capital invested in the construction of the improved road yields such low interest that no shareholders can be found, and highway construction then either ceases entirely or is limited to the most heavily traveled roads near the commercial towns.

With a higher highway toll, on the other hand, the shares can yield the customary rate of interest even when the highways are extended further into the interior of the country.

Now, for the estates situated close to the highway, the benefit it provides them is at least twice as great as the amount of the highest highway toll paid anywhere, and for such estates the construction of the highway remains a blessing even at the high toll rate. But since the realization of the improved road and the possibility of extending it into the interior of the country depend on the level of the highway toll, it is precisely in too low an assessment of the highway toll that lies the reason why a large part of the country is deprived of the benefit that the improved road would provide.

On the other hand, however, the increase of the highway toll may only go up to a certain point; if this point is exceeded, the expected increase in revenue turns into a decrease, and at the same time the highway ceases to have a beneficial effect on the country.

If the highway is toll-free, it will be most heavily used, but the revenue from it is zero; with an excessively high highway toll, the improved road is avoided, and the revenue from it is very small.

Both extremes lead to nearly the same result. It does not seem impossible to determine the point at which the maximum of revenue occurs through a mathematical calculation, if all the necessary data were known. But as long as the second task has not yet been solved, the calculation can only be carried out with fictitious numbers that have no practical usefulness.

The idea of Herr Pogge mentioned in the fourth task would, in its execution, besides the saving of the collection costs of the highway toll and the other advantages presented in the 4th quarterly issue of Volume XV of these Annals, pp. 707 ff., provide the very substantial further advantage that highway construction could be extended to less-traveled roads where highway shares would not yield sufficient interest. For even if the highway toll is raised to the point where the revenue from the highway reaches its maximum, this still amounts to only two-thirds at most of the benefit that the country derives from the improved road. Where, therefore, the interest on the construction capital and the maintenance costs of the highway consume more than two-thirds of the benefit that the highway provides, the construction of improved roads on a share-capital basis is impracticable, however beneficially it would still affect the national income. By contrast, if Herr Pogge's proposal were adopted, improved roads could also be built on less-traveled roads where the costs consume three-quarters or four-fifths of the benefit, and indeed these could extend precisely as far as they still produce an increase in national income.

The execution of this idea is unfortunately opposed by two difficulties:

1) that the principles by which the benefit that each estate derives from the highway is to be determined have not yet been ascertained and established, and

2) that it will be impossible to bring so many interested parties voluntarily to a collective agreement.

What is impracticable for the moment, however, must not be given up forever; one should rather keep this view constantly before one's eyes as an ideal, and work unceasingly toward its gradual realization.

How high, then, is the working day of a team to be valued in the different seasons of the year and for the different kinds of work?

According to the greater or lesser importance of the work, the length of the days and the correspondingly greater or lesser quantity of work performed, and the greater or lesser exertion that the work causes the horses, the working day must also be valued higher or lower.

In this regard I now divide the year into four different periods:

1) **The harvest period**, whose length I reckon -- excluding the time during which field work is performed during the harvest -- at on average four weeks.

2) **The summer period**, from the beginning of ploughing to the end of the autumn sowing season, excluding the harvest time. I assume that this period on average begins on the 25th of March and lasts until the 7th of October. From the 25th of March to the 7th of October are 196 days = 28 weeks. For the harvest period, deduct 4 weeks. There remain for this period: 24 weeks.

3) **The autumn period**, from the end of the autumn sowing season to the end of ploughing. I reckon this from the 7th of October to the 18th of November = 6 weeks.

4) **The winter period**, from the 18th of November to the 25th of March = 127 days = 18 weeks 1 day.

- In the 1st period: 3.5 days
- In the 2nd period: ...
- In the 3rd period: ...
- In the 4th period: ...

The working days are accordingly distributed as follows:

- In the 1st period: 4 weeks at 3.5 days = 14 days
- In the 2nd period: ...
- In the 3rd period: ...
- In the 4th period: 18 weeks 1 day at 3.87 days per week = ...

In the whole year: 241.33 days.

The sum of working days actually performed in the ten-year average amounts, as stated above, to 242 days on average, thus agreeing almost exactly with the distribution made here.

I now take the working days during summer at ordinary field work as the unit, and reduce the working days in the harvest and autumn periods to this standard.

In the grain harvest, the work is so taxing for the horses -- through the rapid driving and the length of the time during which work is performed -- that the horses, although they work only 3.5 days in the week, more often lose than gain in strength and flesh. We may therefore not value a week's work of the horses during the harvest lower than during ordinary field work; 3.5 working days in the harvest therefore cost as much as 5.33 ordinary days. Thus 1 working day in the harvest equals 1.5 normal days.

In the autumn period the days are much shorter, the quantity of work is smaller, and the horses are far less taxed than in summer. I estimate accordingly one working day in autumn as equal to 0.75 normal days. Accordingly:

- 14 working days in the harvest at 1.5 = 21 normal days
- 126 days in the summer period = 126 normal days
- 8.14 days in the autumn period at 0.75 = 23.3 normal days
- **171.7 working days = 170.2 normal days**

The costs of a working day for the horses in winter, however, cannot be determined by such a reduction.

One must keep on an estate as many horses as are necessary for the cultivation of the fields, and one must maintain these horses in winter as well and give them the feed necessary for their subsistence, even if no work at all were done with the horses in winter.

To determine, then, the costs of a working day for the horses in winter, one must, in my view, calculate how much in feed, depreciation of the horses, harness, etc. would be saved if the horses did not work at all in winter, and distribute the sum resulting from this calculation over the number of working days that the horses perform in winter.

This saving amounts to:

#### 1) In Feed

How much grain the work horses, when they do not work in winter, must receive alongside the hay they otherwise get in order to remain in the same condition of strength and flesh as they were in autumn, I cannot determine from experience. From the observations made with the cart horses, however, it seems very probable to me that at most half a bushel of oats per team daily is needed for this. When the horses work, the team receives daily 1 bushel 2 measures. Thus per day 10 measures can be saved. For the winter period of 127 days at 10 measures, this amounts per team to 79 bushels 6 measures of oats, which, at 15 shillings per bushel, makes 25 Reichsthalers 30 shillings.

The loss through accidents would certainly decrease by half, and the saving would then amount to: 4 Reichsthalers 24 shillings.

Of the veterinary and treatment costs, at least half would be saved, thus: 1 Reichsthalers 12 shillings.

The work of the horses during winter can be divided into:

1) trips outside the estate, and
2) work on the estate itself.

To the first class belongs the transport of grain; to the second class belongs the hauling of wood -- when the estate has its own supply of firewood -- the hauling of manure, the bringing in of grain stacks, etc.

In almost every winter a time occurs when the roads are so bad that no grain can be transported at all. On the other hand, when the roads are frozen, grain is often transported twice in one week. On an estate lying 6 miles from the commercial town -- as is here taken as the basis of the calculation -- one can generally assume that the team horses are used once a week during the winter period for grain or so-called travel hauls. On shorter days or with bad roads, such a trip of 6 miles including the return journey requires 2.5 days; on longer days in February and March or with better roads, such a trip is made in two days. Over the course of several years one will count approximately as many trips of 2 days as of 2.5 days, and accordingly one grain haul takes on average 2.25 days.

The grain transport during the winter period, which we have taken at 18 weeks, thus occupies the horses for 18 x 2.25 = 40.5 days. The total working time during the winter period we have calculated at 70.33 days. For work on the estate there remain therefore 70.33 - 40.5 = 29.83 days.

The working days of the horses at work on the estate itself must not be equated in terms of costs with the working days during grain transport. The latter are so strenuous for the horses that they lose weight even when transporting grain twice in one week with good roads, while the horses can perform the work on the estate almost continuously without being taxed by it.

In terms of costs, I therefore estimate three working days of the horses during grain transport as equal to five working days at work on the estate itself.

If we now take the days during grain transport as the unit or as normal days for winter, then:

- 40.2 working days during grain transport = 40.2 normal days
- 29.4 days on the estate at 3/5 = 17.7 normal days
- **Total: 58 normal days**

These distributed over 58 normal working days for winter make per day 37.6 shillings, and for one working day on the estate itself: 37.6 x 3/5 = 22.55 shillings.

The costs of the horses with harness amount according to the above calculation in the summer period to 194 Reichsthalers 17 shillings. The depreciation of the implements with which the horses work, and especially the wagons, may also vary in the different seasons of the year. Other things being equal, one would determine the maintenance costs of the implements per working day according to the length of working time, and accordingly a larger share of the costs would fall on the working days in summer than on those in winter. Now, however, in winter on the roads, which are either deep or frozen, the depreciation of the wagons in the same working time is much greater than in summer. This is the case in winter even at work on the estate, as the unshod wheels are very much worn down on the frozen ground.

Since I know no correct basis for this division, I prefer, to avoid arbitrary estimates, to retain the rates found for the average of the whole year also for the different seasons, and thus to assume the depreciation of the implements to be equally high for one working day in the different seasons.

## B. Calculation of the Transport Costs of Grain

For grain transport in winter, a trip to the commercial town 5 miles distant requires on average 2.33 days.

The team costs daily 1 Reichsthaler 15.4 shillings, which makes in 2.25 days: 2 Reichsthalers 46.6 shillings.

The teamster receives 14 shillings daily as food allowance on the trip, which makes in 2.25 days: ... Against this, there is a saving on the teamster's board of approximately 9 shillings, leaving: 22.5 shillings.

Depreciation of grain sacks per trip: 3 shillings.

**Costs of one grain haul: 3 Reichsthalers 24.1 shillings.**

In winter, with bad roads one loads 5 to 5.5 sacks, with good roads 7 sacks of wheat, the sack reckoned at 6.17 Rostock bushels. Over the course of several years, one can take the average winter load at 6 sacks of wheat.

The transport of 6 sacks = 37 bushels of wheat *) thus costs over 5 miles 3 Reichsthalers 24.1 shillings.

*) Since the Rostock bushel contains 5/8 of a Berlin bushel, 37.1 Rostock bushels = 21.4 Berlin bushels. -- Editor's note.

This makes per bushel of wheat: 4.48 shillings.

- Per Last (of 100 bushels): 164.2 shillings

In accordance with the purpose of this investigation, in the preceding calculation only those costs of the grain sale are included under the common designation "transport costs" which depend on the quality of the roads and the distance of the estate from the commercial town.

In addition to the transport costs, the following expenses also fall on the sale of grain:

1) **The commercial or selling costs of the grain.** As a rule, with every grain delivery a steward or overseer is sent along to manage the sale of the grain. The costs thereby incurred are related to the distance of the estate from the commercial town, but very little to the quality of the roads, and depend above all on the size of the estate: for the smaller estate, which sends only two wagons on grain trips, bears in this respect almost as many costs as the larger estate, which sends six and more wagons on trips.

2) **The measuring and loading of the grain** along with the other preparations for the trip. The costs of this work are the same regardless of whether the estate lies near or far from the commercial town.

3) **The straw loss**, which arises because the teamsters do not bring back the straw placed under the sacks but, following established custom, sell it.

If one now wishes to calculate what value the grain has on the estate itself, these expenses must be deducted from the price that the grain commands in the commercial town, in addition to the transport costs. For our present purpose, however, they cannot be included in the calculation.

The calculation of the transport costs of grain, drawn from a single standpoint, cannot serve as the norm for an entire region that uses the same road, but is rather to be regarded only as a single contribution toward the solution of the problem. In every case, however, a calculation undertaken from another standpoint will be facilitated by comparison with the procedure observed here. Nevertheless, it must not go unnoticed that not every variation in the keeping of horses and the strength of feeding entails a corresponding variation in the costs of labor.

Cart horses cost much less to maintain than team horses, but the work performed by them -- since they work fewer days in the year and accomplish less on each working day -- is no less costly than that of the team horses. On the other hand, by keeping larger horses and through stronger feeding, one can achieve that a team of horses transports 7 sacks of wheat instead of 6, or that the horses, instead of once a week, now make three grain deliveries in fourteen days to a commercial town 5 miles distant; but it is by no means certain that this actually reduces the transport costs.

It is an observation made by several writers, and notably also by our late Thaer, that in the estimates of the maintenance costs of draft horses an astonishing diversity is found, but that despite all this diversity, the costs for a certain quantity of work -- for example, ploughing one Morgen of field -- nonetheless differ little from one another.
