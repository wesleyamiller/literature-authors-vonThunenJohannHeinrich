Aurich, December 7, 1836.

The Main Directorate of the Mecklenburg Patriotic Association has received from the latter the expert opinion of the Inner Committee of the Celle Agricultural Society regarding the proposals of Government Councilor von Boddien for the introduction of Mecklenburg agricultural implements, with the request to provide further information thereon. Accordingly, the following remarks are communicated on the subjects raised in the expert opinion:

Re 1. a. and b. of the expert opinion. It is one of the most excellent qualities of the hook plow that it still penetrates very hardened soil that has become inaccessible to the plow. The field roads created by manure carting, which have become so hard that the plow immediately springs out, are here — even on clay soil — broken up with the hook plow, albeit with great exertion of the draft animals.

Sandy soil could indeed become too loose and friable through the constant use of the hook plow, if one always plowed cleanly, that is, left no ridges standing. But since one can take furrows of any desired width with the hook plow, the excessive looseness is prevented by taking wide furrows of up to 12 feet, whereby only about half the earth is stirred and loosened, and the work is at the same time enormously advanced. For breaking up strongly turfed meadows, the hook plow is unsuitable.

Re c. For the seed furrow in autumn, after preceding fallow, the hook plow is excellently suited, especially on binding soil when it is in a moist condition. The hook plow still yields loose earth here when the plow delivers cohering clods, and even when one otherwise also uses plows alongside the hook plow, the plows are then always removed from the field. In full measure, however, this applies only to the horse-drawn hook plow equipped with front carriages. With the ox hook plow, as well as the so-called belly hook plow, part of this advantage is lost through the treading of the draft animals on the already plowed field.

If, however, one does not follow winter grain after clean fallow but after a preceding crop, the hook plow is not suited for single-furrow preparation of the winter sowing, except when one takes furrows of 2 feet in width. Here, as a rule, one will need to give two passes with the hook plow where one pass suffices with the plow; the field will then, however, be better prepared by the two hook plow passes than by the one plow pass.

When covering the spring seed, it is well known to be very important that the seed not be covered deeply but only shallowly with earth. Since the hook plow does not cut the furrow horizontally but goes only about 2 inches deep on the field side while penetrating 4 inches on the land side, and since when turning over the field the sown seeds partly fall onto the shallowly skimmed portion of the furrow, the danger of burying the seed is less when covering with the hook plow than when covering with the plow. Since furthermore the hook plow crumbles the earth far more than the plow, the seed is not so easily covered with clods or large pieces of earth that hinder the germination or sprouting of the young plants. Experience has also taught that plowed-under seed came up later and thinner than hook-plowed-under seed. With a good preparation of the spring sowing, however, when the seed is not to be sown on top but covered, the furrows must not be taken deeper than 3 to 4 inches and not wider than 10 to 11 inches. In this case, however, there is a great waste of draft power, since the hook plow, instead of requiring 2 draft animals, could easily be pulled by one. Nevertheless, where the seed is covered with two-horse plows, this is the case to not much lesser degree. Most unsightly appears the work of the hook plow when breaking up the lea. However, here the appearance is not decisive, but rather quite immaterial, and what applies here is the saying: "by their fruits shall you know them." Now the excellent condition in which, even by the testimony of foreigners, the Mecklenburg fields are found at the end of fallow cultivation shows that by means of the hook plow the purpose of the fallow, despite the imperfect breaking up of the lea, can be fully achieved.

Re d. Quite inaccurate is the statement: "the hook plow has almost entirely gone out of use even in Mecklenburg, and one finds it only in individual areas; in the more cultivated regions, however, only exceptionally on individual estates." — On the contrary, the plow is in use only in the western part of Mecklenburg bordering on Holstein. The dividing line between the region where the plow is predominantly in use and the region where the hook plow predominates runs, according to inquiries made on the spot and according to the notes communicated by the District Directors of the Patriotic Association in western Mecklenburg in response to the inquiries addressed to them, through Grambow, Grevesmühlen, Schönberg in the Ratzeburg district, through Hagenow and so forth toward the Prussian border. East of this line, in central Mecklenburg, the hook plow is the predominant field implement, and in eastern Mecklenburg there are many highly cultivated estates on which not a single plow is to be found, while some estates in this region use the plow partly for breaking up lea and stubble, but retain the hook plow for all other passes.

Since with the hook plow, as soon as one leaves ridges standing, one can take furrows of any desired width, without precise specification of the width and depth of the furrows the statement of the area that one cultivates in a day with the hook plow decides nothing about the quantity of work performed in a day with the plow. If one wanted to draw a comparison between hook plow and plow on this point, one would have to take as the measure the cubic mass of earth that each implement turns over in a given time.

Since among a large proportion of the estate owners in the region around Güstrow the work oxen have already been abolished for several years to their satisfaction, and plowing is done only with horses, the hook plow permits its use with a single horse for the seed furrows, which in this region, which partly has heavy soil, happens frequently. One even considers the heaviest clay soil not properly prepared for sowing unless the last furrow can be done with the single-horse hook plow. This provides not only

2. the advantage that at sowing time one can go to work with twice as many hook plows, but also

3. the very substantial advantage that the seed field, worked with single-horse hook plows, suffers no treading at all from the draft animals, is not trampled firm again, but becomes like dug garden soil, for

4. in this region the seed is also sown on top of the furrow in many places, whereby, according to the reasons clearly developed by the honorable Councilor of State von Voght, that venerable veteran of rational agriculture, the seed is not harmfully buried too deep. This method of sowing on the furrow also finally provides

5. the advantage that one can prepare the field for sowing in advance and use the best weather for the sowing itself, whereas on estates during unfavorable sowing weather the seed lies on top for a long time without being able to cover it with the hook plow.

Furthermore, the hook plow permits the cultivation of the field in all directions and forms no ridges, which with the plow the former is more difficult to accomplish because the latter is not easy to avoid.

Re II. a. and b. Through the Mecklenburg harvesting method, a large quantity of work is accomplished in a short time; but the loss of grain is indeed very considerable. If one wanted to introduce in Mecklenburg, instead of swath-mowing, the cutting of grain with the scythe, or instead of the scythe apply the sickle-hook or even the sickle, the grain loss on the individual area where harvesting begins would become much smaller, but a large part of the grain would then, because the number of workers is limited, become over-ripe or could not be brought in at the right time — and thereby the total loss of grain would become greater than it now is. In countries, however, where one can command any desired number of workers at harvest time at somewhat elevated day wages, this will be richly repaid through the reduced loss of grain.

Re c. Rye and wheat straw are so little battered by the large threshing flail during threshing that it remains fully usable as cutting straw and thatching straw. The straw is carried away in bundles and only the ear-work in trusses. When threshing oats and barley, the sheaves are stood upright; thereby the straw is admittedly bent during threshing, but this causes no loss in fodder value. This straw too is carried away in bundles and not in trusses.

Re III. That flax cultivation in Mecklenburg, compared with that in Hanover, is still very much behind admits of no doubt. However, from the flax grown in Mecklenburg not merely coarse linen is made; rather, one finds in almost all larger households in the countryside fine damask that has been manufactured from Mecklenburg flax.

Re IV. Since pan tiles or concave tiles are considerably more expensive than flat tongue tiles, the assertion "that a roof of tongue tiles would come to stand incomparably more expensive, and would burden the building far more than a pan tile roof" can only be based on the assumption that the tongue tiles must be laid double.

Since, however, the Bohemian method of roofing, whereby the joints between the tiles are filled with lime, has spread more and more in Mecklenburg, the double tile roof becomes more and more dispensable, and a roof laid in this manner is not only cheaper and lighter, but at the same time less of a fire hazard than a pan tile roof made with straw padding underneath.

In Mecklenburg, at least in the eastern part thereof, with extremely rare exceptions all new buildings are roofed with tongue tiles, and concave tiles are almost nowhere to be had at the brickyards unless they are specially ordered.
